The season ends. The robots get packed up, the notebooks get closed — and then most teams never discuss what actually happened. A retrospective fixes that. It turns a pile of memories into a plan for next year.

This guide shows you how to run an effective end-of-season retrospective with your team, what questions to ask, how to document what you learn, and how to hand off insights to future team members.

---

## Why retrospectives matter

Without a structured review, teams repeat the same mistakes year after year. Drive practice starts too late. The Engineering Portfolio gets started in Week 10. The team learns how to run the pre-match alliance meeting — after Worlds.

A retrospective captures that institutional knowledge before it walks out the door when students graduate.

A good retrospective takes **60–90 minutes** and produces:
- A short list of things to keep doing
- A short list of things to change
- Specific action items for next season
- A written record so returning members remember and new members can onboard

---

## When to run it

| Program | Ideal timing |
|---|---|
| **FLL** | Within 2 weeks of final event (qualifier, championship, or season end) |
| **FTC** | Within 1 week of last event; before members disperse for summer |
| **FRC** | During or right after your final event; or the first practice after state/worlds |
| **VEX IQ** | Within 2 weeks of final event |
| **VEX V5 / VRC** | Within 1 week of final event or Worlds |
| **VEX U** | Before final exams; schedule early in the last week of competition season |

Don't wait until fall. Memories fade. Summer break is where lessons go to die.

---

## Who should be in the room

- **All returning students** — mandatory; this is their continuity
- **Graduating seniors / graduating members** — ideally present; their perspective is invaluable before they leave
- **All coaches / mentors** — present but not dominant; let students speak first
- **Parents** — typically not present; this is a team working session, not a debrief for families

Keep it to team members. If you have sponsors or observers, keep them out of this meeting — frank conversation requires psychological safety.

---

## The four-question format

The most reliable retrospective format uses four questions. Answer them in order.

### 1. What went well?

Start positive. This is not sycophancy — it's identifying what to protect and repeat.

**Prompt students with specifics:**
- What worked well at our final event that we should make sure we do again?
- Which part of our robot / code / design held up best under pressure?
- When did we feel most like a team this season?
- What are we most proud of?

**Aim for:** 8–12 items. Capture every genuine contribution — robot performance, judging moments, team dynamics, logistics wins, individual growth moments.

**Watch out for:** Generic answers ("everyone worked hard"). Push for specifics ("we had backup programs for every mission and used them in Match 3").

---

### 2. What would we change?

This is where the real learning is. Not blame — diagnosis.

**Prompt students with specifics:**
- What slowed us down the most during the season?
- What decision, in hindsight, would you make differently?
- What do we wish we'd started earlier?
- What do we wish we'd stopped doing sooner?
- What surprised us about competition day that we weren't prepared for?

**Aim for:** 8–12 items. The coach should contribute here too, not just students.

**Watch out for:** Vague complaints ("we weren't organised"). Ask "what specifically would organised look like?" until you get an actionable answer.

---

### 3. What should we do differently next season?

Turn the "what would we change" list into concrete actions. For each change item, ask: *what specifically would we do differently?*

This produces a draft **next-season action list**. Not a wish list — actual commitments from returning members.

**Examples of good action items:**
- "Start Engineering Portfolio in Week 1, not Week 8"
- "Schedule two driver practice sessions per week from October, not just before the qualifier"
- "Make a pit packing checklist and verify it the day before every event"
- "Elect a dedicated notebook keeper at the first meeting"

**Each action item should have:**
- A clear description of the change
- Who owns it (if known — returning students, coaches, or "first thing at kickoff")
- When it should happen (before season, Week 1, Week 4, etc.)

---

### 4. What do we want new members to know?

This question is often skipped. Don't skip it.

Ask returning members: *If a brand new student joined this team next season, what's the one thing you'd most want them to know before the first meeting?*

Capture these verbatim. They become the **team onboarding brief** — a short document you give to new members at the start of next season.

Common answers:
- "The notebook matters from day one — don't treat it as optional"
- "Every practice you miss, someone else has to cover your part"
- "Judges care about your process, not just your score"
- "It's okay to ask for help — everyone was new once"

## Coach / mentor retrospective (separate or joint)

Coaches should also reflect privately or as a group, separate from the student session. Ask:

- Did I give students enough autonomy? Too much?
- What skills should I develop as a coach for next season?
- What resources (time, money, space, mentors) should I try to secure before next season?
- Did I manage adult-volunteer dynamics well?
- What would I tell myself at the start of the season?

This is optional to share with students, but should be written down. Your notes become part of the [Team Documentation System](/for-educators/team-documentation-system/).

## Documenting the retrospective

Don't just run the meeting — write it down. The output of a retrospective has a half-life: within three months, most people will have forgotten the specifics.

### What to write

A retrospective document should have:

1. **Date and season name / competition year**
2. **Attendees** (names or roles — e.g., "6 students, 2 coaches")
3. **Final record** (e.g., tournament placement, awards won, highest robot score)
4. **What went well** — bulleted list
5. **What we'd change** — bulleted list
6. **Next-season action items** — with owner and timing if known
7. **Onboarding brief** — what we want new members to know

### Where to store it

| Option | Works well for |
|---|---|
| Engineering notebook — final pages | Programs where the notebook is the primary judged artifact (VEX, FLL) |
| Shared drive / Google Doc | FTC, FRC, VEX U — digital-first teams |
| Progress folder in your repo | Teams that use GitHub or a shared git repo |
| `progress/roadmap.md` (if you have one) | Autonomous/technical teams managing backlogs |

The most important thing is that it's **findable next fall** when you're planning kickoff. Don't store it in a chat thread.

---

## Seeding next season's plan

The retrospective is most valuable when it directly feeds your next-season planning. Do this before the document gets filed:

1. **Copy next-season action items into your season calendar template** with rough timing (pre-season, Week 1, Week 4, etc.)
2. **Flag any items that require resources** (money, adult volunteers, new equipment) and surface them to parents / school admin / sponsors early — summer is the right time to ask
3. **Review the onboarding brief** before your first meeting of next season and give it to new members
4. **Schedule your kickoff meeting** while returning members are still available in the spring — it's easier than coordinating in September

## Program-specific notes

### FLL

- Run the retrospective before students leave for summer — FLL teams often lose members to graduation from middle school.
- Include a brief Core Values debrief: *when did we demonstrate GP this season, and when did we fall short?*
- Ask specifically about Innovation Project: research, solution, and presentation are all separate tracks that need separate debrief time.
- If this is a classroom-based team, the teacher should also complete the coach retrospective section and share it with school admin as part of after-action reporting.

### FTC

- Run separate retrospectives for each sub-team (robot, Engineering Portfolio, outreach) if you have time, then combine into a team-wide session.
- Ask specifically about Engineering Portfolio: *did we tell the story of our design process, or did we describe the robot?* Most teams conflate the two.
- Awards debrief: for each award you received or were nominated for, ask what evidence convinced the judges. For each award you missed, what would you have needed?

### FRC

- The retrospective is hardest to schedule because the season ends at different points for different teams (some attend Worlds, most don't). Run it within two weeks of your final event regardless.
- FRC has more specialised sub-teams — consider a quick sub-team retro (mechanical, electrical, programming, Impact/strategy) before the full team session.
- Impact Award debrief is worth its own 30 minutes: review your submission narrative, interview performance, and community outreach evidence against the rubric.
- Ask specifically about the build season: *did we hit bag-day equivalent with a working robot? If not, when should we have pivoted scope?*

### VEX IQ

- Include a Teamwork Challenge debrief separate from Autonomous Coding Skills — they reward different skills.
- Ask about the engineering notebook specifically: *did we make entries after every practice session?* Most teams that struggle at judging point to patchy notebook coverage.
- For younger students (ages 8–10), keep the retrospective shorter (30–40 minutes) and more structured with adult-facilitated prompts rather than open-ended questions.

### VEX V5 / VRC

- Alliance selection is worth a focused debrief: did you scout effectively? Did you know the other teams' capabilities before selection started?
- Skills Challenge strategy: did you allocate time to Skills runs? What was your peak Skills score vs. your first-event score?
- Autonomous Win Point: did you have a reliable auton by your first event? If not, when should that have been locked?

### VEX U

- Two-robot coordination is the hardest operational piece — the retrospective should explicitly review how well Robot A and Robot B worked as a unit at each event.
- Ask specifically about the engineering notebook: university teams often have strong technical depth but weak documentation of decision rationale. Judges want to see *why* you made design choices, not just *what* you built.
- Succession planning: graduating seniors should document their role knowledge before they leave. The retrospective is the right time to identify who needs to be trained for next year.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---|---|
| Skipping it because the season ended badly | That's exactly when it's most valuable — diagnose what went wrong while it's fresh |
| Coach runs the session as a lecture | Coaches facilitate; students generate the content |
| Focusing only on competition outcomes | Robot Game results are an output — focus on the processes that drove them |
| No document written | Verbal retrospectives are better than nothing; written ones are far more durable |
| Running it six months later | After two months, most specifics are gone. Do it within two weeks of your final event |
| Sharing it publicly with sponsors or community | Keep the retrospective internal — frank feedback requires a private audience |
| Treating it as a postmortem for a failed season only | Run it every year, even in your best season — you still want to know what to protect |

---

## Quick-start checklist

Copy this into your notes and work through it after your final event:

- [ ] Schedule the retrospective within two weeks of your last event
- [ ] Invite all returning students and coaches; block 60–90 minutes
- [ ] Run the four-question format in order
- [ ] Document the output: went well / would change / action items / onboarding brief
- [ ] Copy action items into your season calendar with timing
- [ ] Flag resource-dependent items for early parent/admin follow-up
- [ ] File the document somewhere findable for next fall
