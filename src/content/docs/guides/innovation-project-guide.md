The Innovation Project is half of FLL — and for many teams, the half they feel least prepared for. This guide walks you through the whole thing: picking a problem, doing real research, designing a solution, and presenting it to judges without freezing up.

:::note[Season-specific topics]
Each FLL season has a theme that scopes the Innovation Project (e.g., ocean exploration, ancient civilizations, transportation, energy). This guide covers the *process* — it applies to every season. For the current season's specific topic, check the [official FLL season page](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season).
:::

## What judges are actually evaluating

FLL judges look for four things in the Innovation Project:

1. **Problem identification** — Did the team find a real, specific problem within the season theme? "Pollution is bad" is not a problem. "Fishing vessels in our region discard 30% of bycatch because there's no profitable use for it" is a problem.
2. **Research** — Did the team talk to real people who know this problem (experts, affected community members)? Did they find data, not just opinions?
3. **Solution** — Does the solution address the root cause? Is it innovative — meaning it's not just "someone should recycle more"? Does the team understand how it would actually work?
4. **Presentation** — Can the team explain all of this clearly and confidently, in their own words?

Coaches: the single biggest mistake teams make is presenting a problem that's too broad, and a solution that's too vague. Narrow is better. Specific is better.

## Phase 1 — Pick a problem (weeks 1–3)

### Start with the season theme, then go narrow

The season theme is your starting box. Everything inside the Innovation Project must connect to it. Within that box, your job is to find *one specific problem* that your team can actually research and understand.

**Brainstorming exercise (30 minutes):**
1. Write the season theme at the top of a whiteboard
2. Each team member writes 3–5 problems they can imagine existing in that world
3. Circle ones that feel specific and real — not "pollution" but "plastic microbeads from face wash in freshwater fish"
4. Vote to narrow to 2–3 candidates
5. Do a quick web search on each: can you find real data and real people working on it? If yes, it's researchable.

### Pick the one you're most curious about

Judges can tell the difference between a team that picked a problem because it seemed "good" and one that genuinely got interested. Genuine interest wins.

## Phase 2 — Research (weeks 2–5)

### Talk to experts — this is the most important step

FLL explicitly rewards teams that seek out real expertise. An "expert" doesn't have to be a professor — it can be:

- A local professional in the relevant field (veterinarian, engineer, farmer, scientist, chef)
- A person directly affected by the problem (a fisherman, a patient, a community resident)
- A teacher or university faculty member in the relevant subject
- Someone at a local company, non-profit, or government agency working on the issue

**How to reach out:**
- Email is fine. Keep it short: who you are (FLL team), what you're researching, and one specific question.
- Local contacts are often more responsive than famous ones — try your regional university, local hospital, city government, nearby industry.
- Most professionals are happy to spend 20 minutes with a team of kids. Ask!

Document every contact: who you talked to, when, what you learned. This becomes part of your presentation.

### Find data

Opinions are not enough. Look for:
- Statistics about the scale of the problem (how many people affected, how often, what cost)
- Studies or reports from credible sources (government agencies, universities, industry groups)
- Before/after comparisons or case studies from places that tried to solve it

Don't copy text. Write down the key numbers and where you found them, in your own words.

### Engineering notebook

Keep a simple log of your research process — dates, sources, what you learned, what questions it raised. FLL judges may ask "how did you find that?" or "what made you change direction?" Your notebook is your answer.

## Phase 3 — Design a solution (weeks 4–7)

### The solution must address the root cause

If your problem is "kids in our school waste too much food at lunch," a solution of "put up posters about food waste" doesn't address the root cause. Why do they waste it? If the root cause is "portion sizes are fixed and kids can't choose how much they take," a solution of "self-serve stations with smaller default portions" addresses the root cause.

**Root cause exercise:**
1. State the problem in one sentence
2. Ask "why does this happen?" Write the answer
3. Ask "why does *that* happen?" Write the answer
4. Repeat 3–5 times until you hit something that feels like an underlying cause
5. Your solution should address *that level*, not the surface symptom

### Innovation means new — but not science fiction

A good FLL solution doesn't need to use AI or space technology. It needs to be:
- Something that doesn't already exist in the form you're proposing (or that improves significantly on what exists)
- Something that *could* actually work — it doesn't need to be built, but it needs to be physically plausible
- Something the team can explain in detail (materials, how it works, who would use it, what would need to happen for it to exist)

### Prototype or model

Building a physical prototype or model earns points and makes the presentation much more engaging. It doesn't need to work — it needs to show the concept. LEGO, cardboard, 3D-printed parts, craft supplies — all fine.

## Phase 4 — Prepare the presentation (weeks 6–8)

### Structure

You have 5 minutes to present + 5 minutes for judge questions. A simple structure that works:

1. **Hook** (30 seconds) — a story, a surprising fact, or a question that makes judges care about your problem
2. **Problem** (1 minute) — what it is, who it affects, why it matters (use your data here)
3. **Research process** (1 minute) — who you talked to, what you found, how it changed your thinking
4. **Solution** (1.5 minutes) — what it is, how it works, why it addresses the root cause, what makes it innovative
5. **Impact** (30 seconds) — if this existed, what would change?
6. **What we learned** (30 seconds) — one honest thing about what you discovered or got wrong along the way

### Every team member speaks

Judges want to hear from the kids, not watch the coach present. Every team member should have a part to speak, even if it's one sentence. Practice who says what so there are no awkward pauses.

### Practice with questions

The judge Q&A is where many teams struggle. Practice by having a parent or another adult ask hard questions:
- "Why would this be better than just doing X?"
- "Who would pay for this?"
- "How did you find out that statistic?"
- "What would you do differently if you could start over?"

The goal isn't to have perfect answers — it's to be comfortable *thinking out loud* in front of judges.

### Keep materials simple

Judges see dozens of teams. A clear poster, a physical model, and confident kids will stand out more than a complex slide deck read from a screen. Use visuals to support what you're *saying*, not to replace it.

## Common mistakes (and how to avoid them)

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Problem is too broad ("climate change") | Narrow to one specific, local, researchable problem |
| Solution doesn't connect to the research | Go back to your expert feedback — what did they say was actually needed? |
| Only one or two kids present | Assign every member a speaking part, even one sentence |
| Reading from a poster or notes | Practice until kids can say it without reading |
| No expert contact | Reach out to at least one real expert — email a local professional this week |
| Solution is already widely used | Do a quick search: "does [solution] already exist?" If yes, explain what makes yours different |

## What "innovative" actually means in FLL context

Judges don't require a Nobel Prize-worthy invention. "Innovative" in FLL means:
- The team thought of it themselves through their research process
- It's not the obvious first idea someone would have (it shows real thinking)
- It addresses the problem in a way that doesn't currently exist at scale, or improves on what exists

A kid who says "we talked to the school nutritionist, she said the real problem was that kids don't know what's in the food, so we designed an app that tells you allergen info when you scan the lunch tray" — that's innovative. It came from research, it's specific, and it addresses a real gap.
