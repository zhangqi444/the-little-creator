You're new. You have a team (or you're about to). You're not sure where to start. This guide gives you a concrete week-by-week plan for your first four weeks — no experience needed.

The goal is simple: by the end of week 4, your team should be meeting regularly, the kids should have their hands on the robot, and you should have a basic idea of where the season is going.

---

## Before week 1: the three things you actually need

Before your first meeting, make sure you have:

1. **A registered team** — national registration at [my.firstinspires.org](https://my.firstinspires.org), plus your regional PDO registration. See [How to Register for FLL Challenge](/guides/registration-guide/) if you haven't done this yet.
2. **The SPIKE Prime kit** — or confirmation it's on the way. See [FLL Equipment Guide](/guides/equipment-guide/) for what to buy.
3. **The season challenge** — download it free from [firstinspires.org](https://www.firstinspires.org). It includes the field setup guide and game manual.

You don't need to understand the challenge fully before your first meeting. You'll figure it out together.

---

## Week 1: First meeting — get to know each other

**Goal:** Kids meet each other, you establish a basic rhythm, everyone touches the robot.

**What to do:**

- Introduce yourself and let each kid say their name + one thing they're excited about
- Watch the official season reveal video together (it's on YouTube, usually 5–10 minutes)
- Open the SPIKE Prime box together — let kids handle the pieces
- End with: "Next week we're going to try to make the motors move"

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't try to explain the full challenge yet — it's overwhelming
- Don't assign roles or leadership positions yet — you don't know who's good at what
- Don't worry about the competition mat yet

**What to tell anxious parents:**
> "Week 1 is just getting started. We won't know what we're doing until about week 3 — that's completely normal."

**Takes about:** 60–90 minutes.

---

## Week 2: Hands on the hardware

**Goal:** Every kid builds something and makes a motor move.

**What to do:**

- Open SPIKE App on a computer or tablet (free download from [education.lego.com](https://education.lego.com))
- Walk through the "Drive Base" build from the SPIKE Prime building instructions (available in the app)
- Let kids pair the hub via Bluetooth — they can do this themselves with a little guidance
- Have them run a simple block program: drive forward, stop

**Coaching move:** Pair kids up — one builds, one programs. Rotate halfway through. Don't let one kid dominate either role.

**Common problem:** The Bluetooth won't connect. Fix: make sure the hub is charged (press the center button — if it blinks, it needs charging). The USB cable is more reliable for first-time setup.

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

---

## Week 3: Explore the challenge

**Goal:** Kids understand what missions are and try one on the mat.

**What to do:**

- Set up the competition mat (or just spread it out flat)
- Read 2–3 missions from the game manual together — have kids read aloud
- Pick ONE easy mission and try it: drive to the model, do the thing, drive back
- Don't try to score optimally — just complete the physical movement

**Coaching move:** Let the kids pick which mission to try first. Ownership matters early.

**Meanwhile, in the background (you, not the kids):**
- Read the Innovation Project theme section in the season materials
- Think about: what real-world problem in this theme could kids actually research?

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

---

## Week 4: Divide the work

**Goal:** Establish three parallel work tracks and assign kids to each.

FLL has three scored areas. You need kids working on all three. By week 4 you'll have a feel for who likes what:

| Track | What it is | Who tends to enjoy it |
|---|---|---|
| Robot Game | Building, programming, running missions | Kids who like LEGO, coding, tinkering |
| Innovation Project | Research, interviews, designing a solution | Kids who like reading, talking, presenting |
| Core Values | Team culture, how you work together | All kids — this isn't a separate activity |

**What to do:**

- Explain the three areas to the team in plain language
- Ask: "Which of these sounds most interesting to you?"
- Form rough groups — usually 2–4 on robot, 2–4 on Innovation Project, with everyone sharing Core Values
- Set a goal for each group for next week

**Coaching move:** Don't let kids lock in permanently. Roles evolve. The kid who hates programming in week 4 might become your best programmer by week 8.

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

---

## After week 4: what's next

You've now established the basic rhythm. Here's what the next phase looks like:

- **Weeks 5–8:** Robot team tests more missions; Innovation Project team picks a specific problem and identifies an expert to interview
- **Weeks 9–12:** Mock judging sessions; robot scoring becomes more consistent; refine the Innovation Project presentation
- **Weeks 13+:** Tournament prep; final mock runs; pack the robot carefully

For the full timeline, see [Planning Your FLL Season](/guides/season-planning/).

## What you'll probably worry about (and whether you should)

| Worry | Reality |
|---|---|
| "We're falling behind" | Every first-year team feels this. You're probably fine. |
| "The robot doesn't score enough" | A reliable 100 points beats an unreliable 300 points at qualifiers. |
| "The kids are fighting" | Normal. This is why Core Values exists. See [Core Values Activities](/guides/core-values-activities/). |
| "I don't know enough to coach this" | You don't need to. Your job is to keep the team moving, not to know the answers. |
| "Other teams seem so much further ahead" | You're comparing your week 3 to their week 20. They were there once too. |
