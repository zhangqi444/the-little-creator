This guide is the VEX U counterpart to our [first-four-weeks FLL guide](/guides/first-four-weeks/) — a concrete, time-bounded plan for the first month of forming a new VEX U team at a university. By the end of week 4 you should have a registered team, a confirmed faculty mentor, two V5 brain sets on the way (or in hand), the season's game manual read, and a build cadence that fits a college semester.

If you haven't yet, skim [What is VEX U?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-u/) first — it covers eligibility, how VEX U differs from V5RC, and where the program fits relative to FRC and VAIRC.

:::note[Audience]
This is written for the *organising* student (or coach) — the person taking on the work of actually forming the team. Most active VEX U teams are student-led with a faculty mentor in a supporting role.
:::

## Before week 1: confirm you can actually run a team

Three things to verify before you commit:

1. **University buy-in.** You need a faculty or staff mentor (required), and ideally a department or club sponsoring you for liability and equipment storage. Mentor doesn't need robotics experience — many are math, CS, or engineering faculty who say yes because a student asked.
2. **Funding source for two robot sets.** A VEX U team builds and operates **two robots per alliance**, both yours. Budget roughly $2,000–$4,000 for the V5 hardware to start (two brains, two sets of motors, sensors, batteries, structural parts), more if you want custom-machined components. Common sources: engineering department discretionary, student government allocation, dean's office, departmental industry sponsor.
3. **Storage and meeting space.** You need somewhere safe to keep two robots and a partial 12'×12' practice field (you can manage with a half-field early on). A reservable engineering lab is ideal; a corner of a maker space works.

If any of these three is a hard no, that's worth knowing now rather than three weeks in. If they're all "probably yes," you're ready for week 1.

## Week 1: register, recruit, read the manual

**Goal:** Get the team officially registered, build a roster, and orient yourselves to the season.

**What to do:**

- **Register the team on [RobotEvents.com](https://www.robotevents.com)** under the VEX U Robotics Competition program. You'll need to provide the university name, faculty mentor contact, and team contact (you). Registration fee is roughly $150 per team for the season. Each university can register multiple teams.
- **Recruit a starting roster** — 5–10 active students is a healthy starting point. Engineering, CS, math, and physics students are the obvious draws, but VEX U also benefits from product / business students for outreach awards.
- **Confirm your faculty mentor in writing.** Have them sign off on the registration and any safety / lab agreements. RECF requires a mentor for liability and emergency coordination.
- **Download the current [VURC game manual](https://recf.org/teams/competition/vex-u-robotics-competition/)** and the V5RC manual it's an addendum to. Read both this week. The VURC addendum is short but critical — it spells out exactly what VEX U teams can do that V5 teams can't.
- **Skim the [VURC knowledge base](https://vurc-kb.recf.org/hc/en-us)** to bookmark canonical answers to common registration and rules questions.

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't start ordering hardware yet. Read the game first — different games incentivise different mechanical approaches, and you don't want a $400 mistake.
- Don't pick a team captain or formal structure on day one. Let it emerge from week 2-3 conversations.

## Week 2: order hardware, set a meeting rhythm

**Goal:** Hardware is on the way; you have a recurring weekly meeting cadence.

**What to do:**

- **Place the initial hardware order.** At minimum: two V5 brain sets (control system kits including motors and battery), one set of mecanum or omni wheels per robot, the season's field elements (you can practice on a partial field), and the [VEX Education](https://education.vex.com) curriculum if your team is new to V5. Order from [vexrobotics.com](https://www.vexrobotics.com) — VEX U doesn't get the same discounts as the K-12 programs, but RECF grants may apply (check [grants-kb.recf.org](https://grants-kb.recf.org/hc/en-us)).
- **Lock in a weekly all-hands meeting.** University schedules fragment fast; pick a slot that works for the majority and protect it. A 2-hour weekly meeting plus open lab hours is a typical start.
- **Set up team infrastructure** — Discord or Slack workspace, a shared drive for CAD and notebook, a GitHub org for code (private is fine to start). Don't over-engineer this; pick what your team will actually use.
- **Form sub-teams loosely.** VEX U teams typically split into mechanical, programming, and (sometimes) outreach. Don't lock people in — let students try multiple in the first month before they commit.

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't commit to a specific robot design yet. Spend the first month understanding the game, not building.
- Don't skip the engineering notebook. Even informal notes ("we considered X, we picked Y because Z") from week 2 onward make judging at the end of the season vastly easier.

## Week 3: strategy session, basic build

**Goal:** Your team has a shared mental model of the game and has driven a V5 robot at least once.

**What to do:**

- **Run a 2-hour strategy session.** Whiteboard the game's scoring elements, the constraints VURC-specific rules add or remove, and what you think the most leveraged scoring strategies will be. Don't decide your design yet — just build the shared model.
- **Build a basic chassis.** Two students build a stock V5 mecanum or tank drive base. Doesn't need to be competition-ready — the goal is *somebody drove a robot we built this week*. This is huge for morale.
- **Programmers install [VEXcode V5](https://www.vex.com/vexcode)** (and PROS if you're going C++) on personal laptops and confirm they can deploy code to the basic chassis.
- **Start the engineering notebook.** Even a single Notion page with "what we did, what we decided, what we'd do differently" updated weekly is enough.

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't try to build the competition robot yet. Week 3 is about capability, not commitment.
- Don't let the programmers wait for the perfect mechanical design. They can iterate against the basic chassis indefinitely.

---

## Week 4: design review, plan the season

**Goal:** Decide your initial robot direction. Plan the remaining semester.

**What to do:**

- **Hold a design review.** Each sub-team presents what they've learned about the game and what they think the team should build. Discuss tradeoffs out loud. Decide on an initial direction for your *first* robot (you'll iterate; this isn't your final design).
- **Sketch a semester plan.** When are the qualifying tournaments in your region? When is your university's finals week (which will eat capacity)? Reverse-engineer a build cadence from there. Many VEX U teams budget for one major iteration before regionals, a second after, and a third before championships.
- **Apply for any time-sensitive grants or scholarships.** [Girl Powered](https://recf.org/teams/for-participants/girl-powered/) workshops and several RECF-adjacent scholarships have early-season deadlines.
- **Reflect.** What worked this month? What needs to change? Capture this in the notebook — judges love seeing teams that learn from their own process.

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't lock in a final design. VEX U teams that ship one design and never iterate finish mid-pack at best.
- Don't skip the reflection step. The notebook is what separates an Excellence Award finalist from a competent team.

## After the first month

If you've followed this guide you should have:

- A registered VURC team with faculty mentor and roster
- Two V5 brain sets and basic hardware in hand or imminent
- A weekly meeting rhythm
- Loose sub-team assignments
- Game understanding and at least one basic chassis built
- An engineering notebook with month-one decisions captured
- A rough semester plan with target tournaments identified

From here, the season looks like: design iteration → build → first tournament → reflect → iterate → repeat. The [VEX Forum](/resources/vex-resource-map/#vex-forum) is the best place to ask game-specific strategy questions; the [VURC knowledge base](https://vurc-kb.recf.org/hc/en-us) is the best place for rules clarifications.
