:::note[Season topics change; checkpoints don't]
The Innovation Project theme changes every August. These checklists cover the *process* — they apply every season. For the current theme, visit the [official FLL season page](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season).
:::

## Before You Begin — Coach Prep

Before the team starts, the coach should:

- [ ] Read the [official Innovation Project rubric](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season) for this season
- [ ] Read the season challenge overview so you understand the theme boundaries
- [ ] Set a realistic weekly schedule (aim for 1–2 dedicated IP sessions per week)
- [ ] Prepare a folder or binder for documentation (paper or digital — just pick one and use it consistently)
- [ ] Brief parents: the IP takes real research time; family support for expert outreach helps

---

## Week 1 — Problem Discovery

**Goal:** Agree on the real-world problem your team will study.

- [ ] Read the season theme description as a team
- [ ] Run a 30-minute brainstorm: each member names 3–5 specific problems connected to the theme
- [ ] Write every idea on a whiteboard or sticky notes — no judging yet
- [ ] Circle problems that feel *specific and real* (not "pollution is bad" but "plastic from product X in waterway Y")
- [ ] Narrow to 2–3 candidates
- [ ] Quick web search for each candidate: can you find real data and real people? (If yes, it's researchable.)
- [ ] Vote and commit to **one problem** — narrow is better
- [ ] Write a first draft problem statement (Who / What / Why it matters / Why it's unsolved)
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: Can you say in two sentences what the problem is and who it affects?

---

## Week 2 — Problem Statement & Research Plan

**Goal:** Lock in your problem statement and plan your research.

- [ ] Refine the problem statement using the four-component test (see [Deep Dive](/guides/innovation-project-deep-dive/))
- [ ] Check: is this a *problem* or a *solution*? If you can immediately name the fix, go one level deeper.
- [ ] List 3–5 questions you need to answer through research
- [ ] Identify 2–3 types of experts who would know this problem (professionals, affected community members, researchers)
- [ ] Draft outreach emails / scripts — keep them short: who you are, what you're studying, one specific question
- [ ] Send at least 2 outreach messages this week
- [ ] Set up your research log (name, date, what you learned — one row per source)
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: You have a locked problem statement and at least 2 outreach messages sent.

---

## Week 3 — Primary Research

**Goal:** Talk to real people. This is the most important step.

- [ ] Follow up on outreach messages from week 2
- [ ] Complete at least **one expert conversation** (video call, in-person, phone, or written Q&A)
- [ ] Document the conversation: who you spoke with, their role, date, key takeaways
- [ ] If an expert suggests another contact ("you should really talk to…"), follow that lead
- [ ] Look for surprising findings — what did you learn that you didn't expect?
- [ ] Update the problem statement if the expert conversation revealed something new
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: You have spoken with at least one real expert and documented it.

---

## Week 4 — Background Research & Root Cause

**Goal:** Back up your expert insights with data and understand root causes.

- [ ] Find at least 2 credible background sources (news article, research paper, government report, NGO brief)
- [ ] Record each source in your research log (URL, date accessed, what you learned)
- [ ] Run a 5 Whys exercise as a team — start from the surface problem, ask "why does this happen?" five levels deep
- [ ] Write down what you found at the *deepest* level — that's often where a real solution lives
- [ ] If a second expert is available, reach out now
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: You have data supporting the problem and a clear root cause identified.

---

## Week 5 — Solution Design

**Goal:** Design a solution that addresses the root cause.

- [ ] Brainstorm at least 5 possible solutions (quantity first — no filtering yet)
- [ ] Rate each solution on: Does it address the root cause? Is it feasible? Is it innovative?
- [ ] Choose the strongest solution — not necessarily the flashiest, but the most targeted
- [ ] Sketch or prototype the solution (drawing, model, diagram, mock-up, or physical prototype)
- [ ] Identify who would use it and how
- [ ] Ask: what would need to be true for this solution to work in the real world?
- [ ] Check: does your solution directly connect back to the problem statement you wrote in week 2?
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: You have a specific, defined solution that addresses your documented root cause.

---

## Week 6 — Research Plan Document

**Goal:** Assemble your research into a clear document or display.

- [ ] Write or build your Research Plan (see template in [Deep Dive](/guides/innovation-project-deep-dive/))
- [ ] Include: problem statement, research questions, expert sources, data sources, 5 Whys summary, solution summary
- [ ] Every factual claim has a source (name or link)
- [ ] Each team member can explain one section in their own words
- [ ] Review as a team: is the narrative arc clear? Problem → why it's hard → what we found → our solution
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: You have a completed research plan document or display board draft.

---

## Week 7 — Presentation Development

**Goal:** Build a presentation the team can deliver confidently.

- [ ] Decide who presents which section (everyone should speak)
- [ ] Write key talking points — NOT a script to read word for word
- [ ] Practice the 5-minute overview (most judging sessions run 5–10 minutes)
- [ ] Anticipate judge questions (see Q&A preparation table in [Deep Dive](/guides/innovation-project-deep-dive/))
- [ ] Practice answering "How did you find that expert?" and "What surprised you?"
- [ ] Build or finalize any visual display (poster, slides, model, prototype, video)
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: The team has run through the full presentation at least once.

---

## Week 8 — Rehearsal

**Goal:** Make it smooth without making it robotic.

- [ ] Run a full rehearsal with a friendly audience (parents, another adult, sibling)
- [ ] Time the presentation — aim for 5 minutes for the core, leaving room for questions
- [ ] Give each team member specific feedback, not just "good job"
- [ ] Fix the weakest part — often the transition between problem and solution, or the Q&A
- [ ] Second full rehearsal incorporating the feedback
- [ ] Practice explaining the solution to someone who's never heard it — can they understand it?
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: The team can deliver a confident, clear presentation without reading from notes.

---

## Week 9 — Final Polish

**Goal:** Sharpen, not overhaul. Small improvements only.

- [ ] Final review of the research plan document — any typos or missing citations?
- [ ] Confirm the visual display or presentation materials are ready and portable
- [ ] Run one more light rehearsal — no major changes at this stage
- [ ] Brief the team: judges are curious, not scary; they want to hear your thinking
- [ ] Check logistics: what materials are you bringing to the tournament?
- [ ] ✅ Checkpoint: Everything is ready. Stop making big changes.

---

## Tournament Day — Before Judging

**Goal:** Arrive calm and prepared.

- [ ] Arrive early enough to set up and decompress before judging begins
- [ ] Set up your display or materials in the judging room (if allowed by your event format)
- [ ] Quick 5-minute team huddle: each member says one thing they're proud of about the IP
- [ ] Remind the team: if a judge asks a question you don't know, "we didn't research that, but we think..." is a fine answer — honesty about limits is a strength
- [ ] Take a breath. You did real research. That's what judges want to see.

---

## Tournament Day — During Judging

- [ ] Start with the problem statement — frame the problem clearly before going to the solution
- [ ] Name your expert(s) when describing your research
- [ ] When presenting the solution, connect it explicitly back to the root cause
- [ ] Encourage team members who haven't spoken to add their perspective
- [ ] If a judge pushes back on your solution, engage with the question — they're not attacking you, they're testing your thinking
- [ ] End with what you'd do next if you had more time

---

## After the Tournament — Debrief

Whether you advance or not:

- [ ] What did judges respond best to?
- [ ] What questions caught you off guard?
- [ ] What would you change about the research if you had 2 more weeks?
- [ ] What did you learn about the problem that surprised you most?
- [ ] File the research plan, expert notes, and display materials — next year's coach or team will thank you

---

## Quick Reference — What Judges Look For

| Category | Strong signal | Weak signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem** | Specific, data-backed, real people affected | Broad theme ("pollution"), solution stated as a problem |
| **Research** | Named experts consulted, primary + secondary sources | Only Wikipedia or the official theme page |
| **Solution** | Targets the root cause, team can explain mechanism | Generic idea, not connected to research findings |
| **Presentation** | All members speak, answers questions confidently | Read from notes, one member carries all speaking |
| **Innovation** | Genuinely novel approach or combination | Common idea re-described as if new |
