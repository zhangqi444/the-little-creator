Most FLL coaches are parents, not engineers. That's completely fine — FLL is designed for it. But there are moments when having a real engineer, programmer, or subject-matter expert in the room makes a meaningful difference: debugging a motor controller, thinking through an Innovation Project in a professional's field, or just giving kids a glimpse of what STEM careers look like.

This guide covers how to find those people and how to work with them well.

---

## What mentors can and can't do

**FLL rules on outside help:**

FIRST has specific rules about what outside adults can do during competition. The key distinction:

- **At practice:** mentors can teach, explain concepts, demonstrate, and help kids think through problems
- **At tournaments:** the team must build, program, and run the robot themselves — mentors cannot touch the robot or make real-time adjustments during matches

The goal is that kids do the work, not adults. A good mentor helps kids learn how to solve problems; a bad mentor solves problems for them.

**Red flags in a mentor relationship:**
- Mentor builds robot components directly instead of guiding kids to build them
- Mentor writes code instead of explaining concepts so kids can write it
- Kids defer to the mentor instead of making their own decisions
- Parents start using the mentor as a shortcut when things are hard

If this starts happening, a gentle conversation early saves friction later.

## Where to find mentors

### Your immediate network (best first step)

- **Parents on the team:** ask at the first meeting. "Does anyone work in engineering, tech, or a field related to this season's theme?" You'll be surprised — software developers, mechanical engineers, and scientists are hiding in plain sight among FLL parents.
- **Your employer:** many tech companies have STEM volunteer programs. Ask your HR team if there's a matching program or a way to post volunteer opportunities internally.
- **The kids' school:** STEM teachers, the computer science teacher, or a robotics club advisor may be willing to drop in for a session.

### Local organizations

- **IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers):** local chapters often have volunteer programs. [ieee.org/membership/volunteers](https://www.ieee.org/membership/volunteers)
- **Society of Women Engineers (SWE):** runs outreach programs. [swe.org/outreach](https://swe.org/outreach)
- **ACM (Association for Computing Machinery):** [acm.org](https://www.acm.org)
- **Local makerspace or hackerspace:** members are often enthusiastic about working with kids
- **Community college engineering or CS department:** professors and students may be interested in outreach

### FIRST-specific channels

- **Your PDO:** many regional partners maintain a mentor/volunteer database. Ask your PDO if they can connect you with available mentors.
- **FIRST Volunteer portal:** [firstinspires.org/ways-to-help/volunteer](https://www.firstinspires.org/ways-to-help/volunteer) — people who sign up as volunteers often want to do more than just work tournaments
- **Local FRC (high school) teams:** FRC mentors are often experienced engineers who enjoy working with younger kids. A quick email to a local FRC team asking "do any of your mentors have bandwidth to advise an FLL team?" often works.

### Innovation Project subject-matter experts

The Innovation Project requires kids to consult with real experts. This is a great reason to reach out to people in the field your team is researching — doctors, environmental scientists, urban planners, etc.

- **LinkedIn:** direct message to someone with "I coach a team of 10-year-olds working on [topic]" gets a surprisingly high response rate
- **University extension programs:** many have outreach arms happy to connect with K-12 programs
- **Local nonprofits:** organizations working in your IP topic area (food banks for food science themes, environmental orgs for water themes, etc.) often welcome student outreach

## What to ask for

Be specific. "Can you help with our robot" is vague. These asks work better:

- "Could you come to one practice and help our team understand how gyro sensors work? We're trying to fix a drift problem."
- "Our Innovation Project is about water filtration — could you spend 20 minutes on a video call answering questions from the kids?"
- "We'd love a working engineer to come to our first qualifier as a fan and chat with the kids afterward about what STEM careers look like."

**What to offer:**
- A specific time commitment (1 hour, one session, not ongoing)
- Flexibility on when and how (video call, in-person, email Q&A)
- A clear description of what you need — don't make them figure it out
- A thank-you in the engineering notebook and tournament materials

## Making the relationship work

**Onboard them before they arrive:**

- Send a one-page overview of FLL, what the team is working on, and what you need from them
- Explain the "mentors guide, kids do" expectation explicitly — most mentors appreciate the heads-up
- Share the kids' ages and experience level

**During sessions:**

- Have the kids lead the conversation — they ask the questions, they present the problem
- Let the mentor speak to the kids directly, not to you
- Have the notebook keeper take notes — the mentor visit goes in the engineering notebook

**After:**

- Kids write a thank-you note (or a notebook entry) — good Core Values practice
- Let the mentor know how the season went, especially if their advice helped
- If it went well: ask if they'd be willing to come back next season or refer another mentor
