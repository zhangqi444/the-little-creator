Most FLL and VEX teams are not homogeneous. You might have one kid who's been programming since age 8 alongside one who's never touched a computer outside of school. One who thrives in presentations, one who freezes in front of a group. One who builds fast, one who needs step-by-step guidance.

Differentiation means giving every kid something genuinely challenging and genuinely achievable — not the same thing at different speeds.

:::note
This guide focuses on differentiation within a robotics team or classroom group, not individual learning disabilities or accommodations. If you're supporting students with IEPs or 504 plans, work with your school's support services alongside these general strategies.
:::

---

## Why differentiation matters for robotics teams

In a typical classroom, differentiation is about pacing and depth. In robotics teams, there's an additional dimension: **the team's output depends on everyone contributing meaningfully**, not just on each individual learning.

Two failure modes to avoid:

1. **The competence monopoly** — one or two skilled kids do all the real work while others watch and fetch parts. The skilled kids learn less than they could; the others learn almost nothing.
2. **The lowest-common-denominator** — all tasks are simplified to what the least experienced members can do. The team's output suffers; skilled kids disengage.

The goal is *parallel complexity* — different kids working on genuinely different things at genuinely different levels, all contributing to a shared outcome.

---

## Step 1 — Know your kids

Before deciding how to differentiate, observe for a few sessions. For each team member, note:

- **What do they gravitate toward?** (Building, coding, research, presenting, organizing)
- **Where do they struggle?** (Abstract thinking, fine motor, focus, social confidence)
- **What's their experience level?** (Prior FLL seasons, coding background, LEGO experience)
- **How do they work with others?** (Collaborative, independent, dominant, withdrawn)

Don't rely only on self-report — kids often underestimate or overestimate their own skills. Watch what they actually do.

---

## Step 2 — Design roles with built-in depth variation

Instead of assigning identical tasks, design **role families** where the same role has beginner and advanced variants.

### Programming role family

| Level | What they do |
|---|---|
| **Emerging** | Runs existing programs, observes what changes when parameters change, records results |
| **Developing** | Modifies parameters in existing programs (speed, distance, angle); documents changes |
| **Proficient** | Writes new mission programs from scratch; debugs failures independently |
| **Advanced** | Designs the codebase architecture; introduces abstraction (functions, mission selector, constants) |

### Building role family

| Level | What they do |
|---|---|
| **Emerging** | Assembles subcomponents from instructions; maintains parts inventory |
| **Developing** | Builds attachments following a sketch; tests for stability |
| **Proficient** | Designs new mechanisms from requirements ("the arm needs to lift 100g to 15cm") |
| **Advanced** | Iterates design based on test data; makes trade-off decisions (speed vs. torque, weight vs. strength) |

### Innovation Project role family

| Level | What they do |
|---|---|
| **Emerging** | Researches background facts; organizes notes; prepares visual aids |
| **Developing** | Identifies the specific problem; drafts the problem statement |
| **Proficient** | Designs the solution; leads outreach to experts |
| **Advanced** | Structures the overall project narrative; prepares and leads judging presentation |

### Core Values role family

| Level | What they do |
|---|---|
| **Emerging** | Participates in team activities; identifies Core Values moments when pointed out |
| **Developing** | Self-monitors own behavior; gives basic feedback in debrief |
| **Proficient** | Notices and names Core Values moments for the team without prompting |
| **Advanced** | Facilitates team check-ins; designs brief team activities; mentors newer members |

## Step 3 — Assign stretch tasks, not busy work

A common mistake: giving less experienced kids tasks that are "safe" but meaningless — sorting parts, holding things, being a timekeeper. These kids are present but not learning.

Every task should have a **learning edge** — something slightly beyond what the kid can do comfortably right now.

**Signs a task is too easy:** Kid finishes in the first 10 minutes, asks "what next?", or visibly disengages.

**Signs a task is too hard:** Kid stalls completely, asks the same question repeatedly, or copies from someone else without understanding.

**Signs a task is well-calibrated:** Kid makes progress, hits a real obstacle, works through it (maybe with one nudge), finishes with something to show.

---

## Step 4 — Use pair work strategically

Pairing a more experienced kid with a less experienced one works when structured correctly — and backfires when it isn't.

**Structured pair work (works):**
- Assign specific roles: "Jamie drives the robot; Alex watches and calls out what's different each run."
- Give both a job: "Morgan writes the code; Sam tests it and records the results."
- Rotate roles every 20 minutes so the less experienced partner gets time in the "driver's seat."

**Unstructured pairing (often fails):**
- "Work together on this" with no role clarity — the experienced kid takes over, the other kid watches.
- Pairing without observing — the coach assumes it's going well because there's no conflict.

**Pairing for IP and presentations:**
Mixed-experience pairings work especially well for Innovation Project tasks (research, expert outreach, presentation prep) because the work is less technically gatekept and both kids can contribute equally.

---

## Step 5 — Differentiate the Innovation Project specifically

The IP is often where differentiation breaks down — one or two kids who are confident presenters end up carrying the whole thing while others contribute minimally.

Strategies:

**Give every team member ownership of one piece.** Each kid should be able to answer: "I was responsible for ___." Assign specific sections of the research or presentation to specific kids. At judging, every kid should speak.

**Scaffold the research process.** Less experienced researchers benefit from structured templates:
- "Find 3 facts about [problem]. Write one sentence about each."
- "What's one thing our solution does NOT solve? Why?"

**Coach the presentation separately from the content.** A kid who knows the content but struggles to present can practice with you one-on-one, or present to a small audience first before the full judging format.

---

## Step 6 — Handle the "expert kid" problem

Every team has at least one kid who knows significantly more than the others. This is both an asset and a challenge.

**The risk:** The expert dominates. Others defer to them. The expert learns less than they could because they're never challenged; the others learn less because they're not doing.

**Strategies for the expert:**

- **Give them a teaching role with accountability.** "Your job is to get Jamie to the point where Jamie can explain this to me without your help." Teaching reinforces learning and builds team depth.
- **Push them to the next level.** If they've mastered block-based programming, push them to Python. If they can do Python, push them to write reusable functions and document their code.
- **Give them meta-level responsibilities.** Architecture decisions, documentation, testing protocols — tasks that require integrating the whole picture, not just executing one part.
- **Don't let them gate-keep.** If the expert consistently prevents others from touching the robot or the code, name it directly: "Part of your job is making sure everyone gets hands-on time."

## Adapting for different session formats

### Full-group sessions (everyone together)

Use parallel workstreams (see [Practice Session Structure](/guides/practice-session-structure/)). Split by workstream, not by skill — everyone contributes to the same shared goal from different angles.

### Small-group or club sessions (6–10 kids)

Role families work well here. Assign each kid a role at their current level; adjust upward each week as they progress.

### Classroom integration (20–30+ students)

For larger groups, use a **team-of-teams** structure: multiple small teams (4–6 kids each) working on parallel projects. Each team has its own mixed-skill composition. The coach/teacher circulates rather than directing one team.

Assessment: use the [Classroom Assessment Rubric](/for-educators/assessment-rubric/) with role-adjusted expectations — a kid at the "emerging" level in programming should be assessed against emerging-level criteria, not proficient-level.

## Quick reference: differentiation by situation

| Situation | Strategy |
|---|---|
| One kid doing all the robot work | Assign explicit roles; rotate "driver" every 20 min |
| Less experienced kid disengaged | Find their strength area; assign a stretch task there |
| Expert kid bored | Push to next technical level; give teaching/mentoring role |
| IP dominated by confident presenters | Give every kid a specific section to own and present |
| Mixed experience in Core Values | Advanced kids facilitate; newer kids practice noticing |
| Team moving too slowly for advanced kids | Give advanced kids "if you finish early" extension tasks pre-planned |
