Emergencies are rare, but they happen. A student gets hurt. A battery swells. The building loses power. A severe weather alert sounds. In the moment, your ability to respond calmly and correctly depends almost entirely on preparation you did before the session started.

This guide covers the most common urgent situations in robotics programs across all six programs and what to do when they occur. It is not a substitute for first-aid training or your organisation's official safety policy — both of which you should have before running any session.

---

## Before Every Session — The Foundation

You cannot respond well to an emergency you haven't thought about. Before the season starts, do these once:

- **Know your venue's emergency exits and assembly point.** Walk it with your team in the first session.
- **Keep a first-aid kit on-site.** Know where it is. Check expiry dates each season.
- **Have an emergency contact list.** Every student's parent/guardian phone number, printed or offline-accessible — not just in a cloud app that needs internet.
- **Know the address of your venue.** If you call 911, you need to be able to say where you are. Post it on the wall.
- **Know your organisation's incident reporting procedure.** Most school districts, FIRST PDOs, and VEX organisations require written reports for injuries even if they seem minor.
- **Identify any students with medical conditions.** Allergies (especially bee stings / latex), asthma, seizure disorders, diabetes — these affect how you respond to an apparent emergency.

Make sure at least one coach present at each session holds a current first-aid/CPR certification. Many PDOs require this; even if yours doesn't, it matters.

## Injury

### Minor injuries (cuts, scrapes, splinters, small burns from solder/friction)

1. Stop the activity causing the injury.
2. Provide basic first aid from your kit — clean the wound, apply a bandage.
3. Note the time, student name, and what happened. Even minor incidents should be logged.
4. Notify a parent/guardian at pickup (or by phone if more serious).
5. File an incident report if your organisation requires it.

### Significant injuries (deep cuts, head impacts, suspected fractures, severe allergic reaction, loss of consciousness)

1. **Stay calm. Call 911 immediately** if there is any doubt about severity.
2. Do not move the student unless staying in place creates further danger (e.g., fire).
3. If you or another adult is first-aid/CPR certified, apply appropriate care while waiting for emergency services.
4. Send another adult or responsible older student to meet emergency services at the building entrance and direct them in.
5. Contact the student's parent/guardian immediately.
6. Keep other students calm and away from the immediate area.
7. Do not allow students to take photos or video.
8. File an incident report. Even if the student appears fine after the fact, documentation protects the student, you, and your organisation.

### Eye injuries

Take eye injuries seriously regardless of apparent severity — vision damage is not always immediately obvious.

1. Do **not** rub the eye or try to remove debris yourself (except gently rinsing with clean water if a chemical splash occurred).
2. Cover the eye loosely with a clean cloth. Do not apply pressure.
3. Contact the parent/guardian immediately; recommend urgent ophthalmology or ER evaluation.
4. If any chemical is involved, flush with clean water continuously for 15 minutes and call 911.

## Battery Incidents

Lithium polymer (LiPo) batteries — used in FTC, FRC, and VEX programs — carry fire risk if punctured, overcharged, or physically damaged. SPIKE Prime (FLL) uses lithium-ion; VEX IQ uses NiMH or Li-Ion depending on the kit.

### Swollen / puffed battery

A visibly swollen battery is a damaged battery. It should not be used or charged.

1. Do not puncture, compress, or put the battery in a confined space.
2. Place it in a fire-safe container (a metal tin or a dedicated LiPo safety bag) if available.
3. Move it to a well-ventilated area outdoors or near an exterior window.
4. Contact the battery manufacturer or your PDO for proper disposal guidance — most communities have lithium battery disposal guidelines.
5. Document the incident.

### Battery smoke, heat, or fire (thermal runaway)

Act immediately — LiPo thermal runaway can escalate in under a minute.

1. **Do not use water.** Lithium battery fires are Class D / Class B — water makes them worse.
2. If a fire extinguisher is available and you are trained to use it, use a dry chemical (ABC) or CO2 extinguisher to suppress flames.
3. If the fire cannot be immediately controlled: **evacuate the room, pull the fire alarm, call 911.**
4. Do not re-enter the room.
5. If the battery is smoking but not yet on fire and can be safely moved (no flames, no direct skin contact): move it to a metal container and take it outside. If there is any doubt, evacuate first.
6. After any battery fire or thermal event, the area must be inspected before resuming activity.

**Prevention:** Inspect batteries before every session. Never leave batteries charging unattended. Store batteries in a LiPo-safe bag. Never charge a battery that has been dropped or has visible damage.

## Equipment Failures

### Robot stops working mid-session (non-safety)

This is the most common "emergency" — and it is not really one. Stay calm; it is a teachable moment.

1. Power down the robot.
2. Diagnose systematically: battery charge, cable connections, motor/servo ports, software error messages.
3. Refer to the [Robot Troubleshooting guide](/guides/robot-troubleshooting/) for a structured checklist.
4. If the failure cannot be diagnosed in 10 minutes, set the robot aside and switch to a programming, documentation, or strategy task so the session remains productive.

### Electrical smell, sparks, or smoke from electronics

1. Power off and unplug the robot immediately.
2. If smoke continues after power-off, move the robot away from other materials.
3. Do not attempt to repair live electronics.
4. Inspect wiring for shorts, pinched cables, or reversed polarity before any further use.
5. If the component is destroyed, document and order a replacement; do not use components that have been through a short-circuit event without inspection.

### Tool injuries (power tools, soldering irons)

1. Apply first-aid per the injury guidance above.
2. Secure the tool — turn it off, unplug it, set it aside.
3. Do not allow other students to use the tool until the cause is understood and corrected.
4. Review whether that tool is age/skill appropriate and whether supervision was adequate.

## Severe Weather

### Tornado or severe thunderstorm warning (indoor session)

1. Move students to an interior room on the lowest floor — away from windows and exterior walls. Avoid gyms and large open spaces.
2. Account for all students.
3. Do not allow students to go to their cars or leave the building until the warning has passed.
4. Monitor a weather app or official radio for the all-clear.
5. Notify parents if the session end time is delayed.

### Power outage

1. Account for all students immediately.
2. Use phone flashlights to maintain visibility.
3. If the outage is brief and the venue is safe, wait it out.
4. If the outage appears extended or the venue's safety systems are affected (no emergency lighting, no fire alarm power), contact the facility manager and consider ending the session early.
5. Do not allow students to use power tools or charge batteries during an outage — surge risk when power returns.

## Medical Emergencies (Non-Injury)

### Allergic reaction / anaphylaxis

1. If a student has a known allergy and carries an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen), help them use it if they are unable to do so themselves.
2. Call 911 immediately for any suspected anaphylaxis — even after using an EpiPen, emergency care is required.
3. Lay the student down with legs elevated unless breathing is difficult (sit them upright then).
4. Do not give food or drink.
5. Contact the parent/guardian.

### Seizure

1. Do not restrain the student. Clear the area of hard objects.
2. Time the seizure. If it lasts more than 5 minutes or the student does not regain consciousness within a few minutes, call 911.
3. After the seizure: gently place the student on their side (recovery position), stay with them, and speak calmly.
4. Call the parent/guardian immediately.
5. File an incident report.

### Asthma attack

1. If the student has a rescue inhaler, help them access it.
2. Have the student sit upright in a calm, ventilated area.
3. If symptoms do not improve within 10-15 minutes of using the inhaler, or if there is no inhaler available and the student is struggling to breathe: call 911.
4. Contact the parent/guardian.

### Student mental health crisis (severe distress, threatening self-harm)

1. Stay calm. Move other students out of earshot without abandoning the student in distress.
2. Listen without arguing or minimising.
3. If the student expresses intent to harm themselves or others: contact your organisation's support line, call 911, or both — depending on your jurisdiction's protocols and your organisation's policy.
4. Notify the parent/guardian.
5. Do not leave the student alone until a responsible adult takes over.
6. Document the incident and follow your organisation's safeguarding reporting process.

## Suspicious Persons or Lockdown

If your venue initiates a lockdown, or if you observe a suspicious person:

1. Move students away from windows and doors. Lock or barricade the door if procedures allow.
2. Keep students quiet and seated.
3. Turn off lights; silence phones if possible.
4. Call 911 when it is safe to do so. Text 911 is available in many areas if speaking would create danger.
5. Do not open the door until law enforcement has cleared the building.

Follow your venue's specific lockdown protocol — most schools and community centres have posted procedures.

## After Any Incident

Regardless of severity:

1. **Document it.** Date, time, student name(s), what happened, what you did, any witnesses. Written records are important for insurance, incident review, and protecting everyone involved.
2. **Notify the appropriate parties.** Parent/guardian, your organisation's coach coordinator or PDO contact, your venue manager where relevant.
3. **Debrief the team appropriately.** Students who witnessed an incident may have questions or concerns. Acknowledge what happened calmly and age-appropriately without over-sharing.
4. **Review what you would do differently.** Not to assign blame, but to improve. Was the first-aid kit accessible? Did you know the student's medical history? Was the battery properly inspected?

## Program-Specific Notes

| Program | Common incident types | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| FLL Challenge | Scrapes, SPIKE Prime battery heat during extended charging | FLL venues are often schools — know the school's emergency procedures |
| FTC | LiPo battery incidents, REV/servo wiring shorts, eye injury from projectiles during autonomous testing | Always wear safety glasses during robot operation |
| FRC | LiPo / SLA battery incidents, pneumatics over-pressure, high-current wiring shorts, sharp metal fabrication injuries | FIRST FRC requires annual safety training for mentors; review it each season |
| VEX IQ | Minor cuts from snap-together part edges, battery connector issues | IQ batteries are generally lower risk than LiPo; standard first-aid covers most incidents |
| VEX V5 / VRC | LiPo battery incidents (V5 Smart Battery), short-circuits during wiring, robot-motion injuries from powerful drive bases | V5 drive bases are capable of significant force — clear the field before testing |
| VEX U | All VEX V5 risks plus pneumatics (if used), larger robots at higher speeds, collegiate-venue-specific emergency procedures | Two robots on field simultaneously increases collision risk during testing |

## Quick Reference

| Situation | First action | Call 911? |
|---|---|---|
| Minor cut/scrape | First aid kit | No (unless severe) |
| Head impact / suspected fracture | Stop activity, do not move student | Yes if any doubt |
| Eye injury | Cover loosely, contact parent | If chemical involved, yes |
| LiPo battery swelling | Isolate in fire-safe container | No (unless fire starts) |
| LiPo fire / smoke | Evacuate, pull alarm | Yes |
| Severe allergic reaction | EpiPen (if available), lay flat | Yes |
| Seizure over 5 minutes | Recovery position, time it | Yes |
| Asthma, inhaler not working | Sit upright, calm environment | Yes |
| Power outage | Account for students, wait or end session | No (unless unsafe) |
| Severe weather warning | Interior room, away from windows | No |
| Lockdown | Shelter in place per venue procedure | When safe to do so |
| Student mental health crisis | Stay calm, listen, notify parent | If self-harm intent present |
