You just agreed to coach a VEX IQ team. You have a group of kids (ages 8–14), a VEX IQ kit, and a vague sense that there's a competition sometime this fall. This guide tells you what to do in the first four weeks — no robotics experience required.

The goal by week 4: your team is meeting regularly, the kids have built a drive base and made the motors move in code, and you have a rough plan for the season ahead.

---

## Before week 1: the three things you actually need

1. **A registered team** — register at [robotevents.com](https://www.robotevents.com). You'll also need to register your team with your regional RECF affiliate. See [How to Register](/guides/registration-guide/) for the general pattern.
2. **A VEX IQ kit** — at minimum, the VEX IQ Super Kit (Brain, two Smart Motors, IQ Controller, sensors). See [VEX IQ Robot Design](/guides/vex-iq-robot-design/) and the [Equipment Guide](/guides/equipment-guide/) for purchasing advice.
3. **The current season game guide** — download free from [vexrobotics.com](https://www.vexrobotics.com/viqc-current-game) after the season reveal (usually August). If the season hasn't launched yet, start with the Clawbot build and the basics.

You don't need to understand the game fully before your first meeting. You'll figure it out with the kids.

---

## Week 1: First meeting — get to know each other

**Goal:** Kids meet each other, everyone touches the robot hardware, you establish a basic rhythm.

**What to do:**

- Introduce yourself and let each kid say their name + one thing they're excited about building
- Watch the official season reveal video together — it's on YouTube, usually 5–10 minutes, and gets kids excited immediately
- Open the VEX IQ kit together — let kids handle the pieces and identify the Brain, Smart Motors, and Controller
- End with: "Next week we're going to build a drive base and make the motors move"

**What NOT to do:**

- Don't try to explain the full season game yet — it's overwhelming before they've touched the hardware
- Don't assign roles or sub-teams yet — you don't know who's good at what
- Don't worry about scoring or competition strategy yet

**What to tell anxious parents:**
> "Week 1 is just getting started. We won't know what we're doing until about week 3 — that's completely normal."

**Takes about:** 60–90 minutes.

---

## Week 2: Build a drive base and make it move

**Goal:** Kids build a functional drive base and drive it with the Controller.

VEX IQ's standard starter build is the **Clawbot IQ** — a two-motor tank drive base with a claw attachment. The building instructions are built into VEXcode IQ and available at [vexrobotics.com/clawbot-iq](https://www.vexrobotics.com/clawbot-iq-1). You don't have to build the full Clawbot; a simple four-wheel drive base is enough for week 2.

**What to do:**

- Download VEXcode IQ (free) from [vexrobotics.com/vexcode](https://www.vexrobotics.com/vexcode) if not already installed
- Pair kids up — one builds the drive base, one sets up VEXcode IQ on a laptop or tablet
- Builder pair: assemble the two-motor drive base using the Clawbot instructions (pages 1–30 or the "Base" section)
- Coder pair: open VEXcode IQ, plug in the Brain via USB, confirm the Brain appears as a connected device
- Connect: run a simple **Driver Control** program from the template — left joystick forward/back, right joystick turn
- First drive: let every kid drive the robot at least once

**Coaching move:** Pair a confident builder with a nervous one, and a confident coder with a nervous one. Rotate roles halfway through so everyone gets both experiences.

**Common problems:**

| Problem | Fix |
|---|---|
| Brain won't connect to VEXcode IQ | Use USB cable, not Bluetooth, for the first connection. Check that VEXcode IQ has the correct firmware — it will prompt you to update if needed. |
| Motors turn the wrong direction | In VEXcode IQ, open the Devices panel and check which ports the motors are on; swap left/right if the robot turns when you push forward. |
| Brain won't turn on | Fully charge the VEX IQ battery (solid green LED on charger = full). Press the Brain's button once to power on. |

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

## Week 3: Explore the season game

**Goal:** Kids understand what the VIQRC events are and attempt a first element of the game.

**What to do:**

- Set up the competition field tiles (or use a cleared floor with tape lines to mark zones)
- Read 2–3 game challenges from the season game guide together — have kids read aloud
- Identify the simplest scoring element and attempt it: drive to the object, interact with it, return to a zone
- Don't try to score optimally — just complete the basic physical movement reliably

**The two VIQRC event types to explain:**
- **Teamwork Challenge** — two robots cooperate on the same field at the same time (60 seconds, driver-controlled)
- **Autonomous Coding Skills** — one robot, no driver, runs a pre-programmed routine (60 seconds)

**Coaching move:** Let kids pick which game element to try first. Ownership matters early — they'll be more invested if they chose it.

**Meanwhile, in the background (you, not the kids):**
- Read the judging criteria: VIQRC has Design Award and Excellence Award categories. The engineering notebook is the primary judged artifact. Start one now.
- Check your region's qualifier schedule on [robotevents.com](https://www.robotevents.com) — VIQRC qualifiers typically run October through January.

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

---

## Week 4: Divide the work — robot and notebook tracks

**Goal:** Establish two parallel work tracks and give every kid a role.

VIQRC teams work on two main areas. By week 4 you'll have a feel for who likes what:

| Track | What it is | Who tends to enjoy it |
|---|---|---|
| Robot Game | Building mechanisms, programming Autonomous, driving in Teamwork | Kids who like LEGO, coding, building things |
| Engineering Notebook | Documenting the design process, drawing diagrams, recording experiments | Kids who like writing, drawing, organising, explaining |

**What to do:**

- Explain both tracks in plain language — show kids the award categories and explain that the notebook is how the Design Award and Excellence Award are earned
- Ask: "Which of these sounds most interesting to you?"
- Form rough groups — typically 2–4 on robot game, 1–2 on engineering notebook (everyone contributes to both over the season)
- Open the engineering notebook RIGHT NOW. Write today's date. Record what you built this session and why you made the decisions you made. This is page one — starting it at week 4 is already better than most first-year teams.
- Set a specific goal for each group for next week

**The most important thing to say this week:**
> "The notebook is not something we fill out at the end of the season. It's how we show judges the work we did all along. Every build session, every experiment, every time we changed the design — that goes in the notebook."

**Takes about:** 90 minutes.

## After week 4: what's next

By the end of week 4, your team has a driving robot, a started notebook, and knows their roles. Here's what the next phase looks like:

- **Weeks 5–8:** Robot team adds a game mechanism (claw, arm, intake); programming team starts writing a first Autonomous Coding Skills routine
- **Weeks 9–12:** Practice Autonomous runs (aim for consistent completion); run mock Teamwork matches with another team or with two robots on your own; notebook records every change
- **Weeks 13+:** Tournament prep; polish Autonomous; pack and transport checklist; notebook review

For a structured 8-week curriculum with session plans, see the [VEX IQ Curriculum Starter](/for-educators/vex-iq-curriculum-starter/).

## Understanding the competition format

VEX IQ is **uniquely collaborative** among youth robotics programs. Unlike FLL (where teams compete against each other), VIQRC's Teamwork Challenge has you working *with* an alliance partner — a team you're paired with on the day. This means:

- **Practice with different robots** when possible — your Teamwork score depends on cooperating with an unknown partner
- **Keep your robot predictable and reliable** — a simple robot that does one thing consistently is a better alliance partner than an ambitious robot that breaks
- **GP (Gracious Professionalism) is part of the game** — VEX IQ explicitly rewards teams that help other teams; judges notice

See [VEX IQ Tournament Guide](/guides/vex-iq-tournament/) for the full competition day structure.

## What you'll probably worry about (and whether you should)

| Worry | Reality |
|---|---|
| "We're falling behind" | Every first-year team feels this. You're probably fine. |
| "Our Autonomous doesn't work reliably" | Start simple: drive forward, push one element, stop. A consistent simple auto beats an unreliable complex one. |
| "The kids are fighting" | Normal. VEX IQ has a collaborative culture — use it. Ask: "How would you solve this together?" |
| "I don't know enough to coach this" | You don't need to. Your job is to keep the team moving, ask questions, and keep the notebook up to date. |
| "Our robot can't score many points" | At a first qualifier, finishing Teamwork matches without a breakdown is a win. Focus on reliability first. |
| "Nobody wants to do the notebook" | Frame it as the path to awards. Show kids that the team who documented their work wins the Design Award — because they did. |

---

## The one thing most first-year VEX IQ coaches miss

Start the engineering notebook at your very first meeting and keep it updated every session.

Most first-year teams treat the notebook as optional paperwork — something to fill in before the tournament. Teams that win the Design Award and Excellence Award document their decisions *as they make them*: what they built, why, what didn't work, what they changed. Judges can see the difference immediately, and it's nearly impossible to reconstruct authentic process notes after the fact.

You don't need a polished robot at week 4. You do need a notebook that already has four weeks of real decisions in it.
