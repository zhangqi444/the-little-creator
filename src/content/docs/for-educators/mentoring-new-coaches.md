If you've coached a robotics team for two or more seasons, you have something genuinely hard to acquire: knowing what matters when. First-year coaches often can't tell the difference between a normal bad week and a structural problem, between a question worth answering immediately and one to sit with, between a team in productive struggle and a team about to fall apart.

Mentoring a new coach is how that knowledge gets transmitted — and it's one of the highest-leverage things experienced coaches can do for their program community.

This guide is about doing it well.

---

## Why mentoring new coaches is hard

The instinct when you see a new coach struggle is to step in and fix it. You know the answer. You can see what's going wrong. The students are confused, the mentor is overwhelmed, time is short.

Stepping in feels like helping. Often it isn't.

The same principle that applies to coaching students applies to coaching coaches: **if you do it for them, they don't learn to do it themselves**. A new coach who is rescued from every difficult moment remains dependent. A new coach who navigates those moments — even imperfectly, with someone available to debrief afterwards — builds capacity.

Your job as a mentor is to create conditions for the new coach to grow, not to run their team for them.

## What to teach in the first season

First-year coaches have too much to learn all at once. Prioritise ruthlessly. The things that matter most, roughly in order:

### 1. How to tell if a session went well (not whether the robot improved)

New coaches measure session success by whether something tangible was built or fixed. Teach them to also measure:

- Did students drive most of the decisions?
- Were the students engaged and talking to each other?
- Did the team leave with a clear, specific next step?
- Did the coach speak less than 30% of the time?

The robot outcome follows from the session quality. First-year coaches often get this backwards.

### 2. The difference between productive struggle and unproductive struggle

This is the hardest judgment call in coaching. Productive struggle is when a team is confused but engaged — they're generating hypotheses, testing ideas, asking each other questions. Unproductive struggle is when the team is confused and stuck — repeating the same wrong move, losing motivation, starting to disengage or blame each other.

The intervention for productive struggle is almost nothing: stay present, maybe ask one question. The intervention for unproductive struggle is different: narrow the problem, give a hint, reset the environment.

New coaches can't always tell the difference. Walk through specific examples from your own seasons. Let them describe situations and talk through which type it was.

### 3. How not to touch the robot (and when to)

The number one mistake first-year coaches make is picking up the robot, the controller, the keyboard. It signals that the student's hands aren't trusted. It takes the learning moment away. Even when the coach is faster and more accurate, the cost is high.

The only times to physically intervene are:
- Safety (something is about to break or someone is about to get hurt)
- Equipment failure (not a student mistake — actual hardware failure)
- Demonstration by explicit invitation ("can you show me how this works?")

Even then, hand it back immediately.

### 4. How to handle the last 10 minutes before competition

First-year coaches often spend the hour before a competition making one last change. This is almost always wrong. Teach them the rule: **no new code, no new mechanisms after the drive to the event**. The best thing a coach can do in the last 10 minutes is get the team calm, fed, and focused on what they already know works.

### 5. Season arc awareness

First-year coaches often treat the season as a flat timeline — every session equally urgent. Teach them the arc:

- **Weeks 1–3:** Don't rush. Build culture, set roles, get hardware working. Nothing else matters yet.
- **Weeks 4–8:** This is when most of the actual learning happens. Protect it from over-polishing.
- **Weeks 8–10 (qualifier approach):** Reliability over capability. Stop adding, start hardening.
- **After qualifying:** If advancing, reassess scope ruthlessly. One improvement at a time.

## What not to do for them

These are the traps experienced coaches fall into most often:

| Trap | Why it happens | What to do instead |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| Running the team at joint sessions | Easier to model than explain | Debrief after; let them lead |
| Answering student questions directly at their practice | You know the answer | Redirect to their coach: "Ask Coach [name]" |
| Rewriting their session plans | Faster to fix than explain | Mark up the plan with questions instead |
| Taking over during a bad tournament moment | Anxiety; you can see the solution | Stand back; debrief hard afterwards |
| Setting standards they didn't agree to | Experience bias | Co-create expectations at the start of the season |
| Comparing their team to yours | Instinctive benchmarking | Measure them against their own prior week |

The consistent thread: **your mentoring should make the new coach more capable, not more dependent on you being present**.

## What the mentoring relationship looks like in practice

### At the start of the season (before teams meet)

- Sit down for 60–90 minutes. Walk through the season calendar, the registration checklist, and the major decision points (game reveal, scrimmages, qualifier date).
- Share your own coaching philosophy briefly — not as prescription but as example that coaching philosophies exist and are worth having. See [Coaching Philosophy](/for-educators/coaching-philosophy/).
- Agree on the cadence of check-ins (weekly text? debrief after every session? monthly coffee?) and the escalation path (what warrants a phone call vs. what can wait).
- Agree on the boundaries: when are you available to co-attend practice, and when are you not?

### During the season (regular check-ins)

Keep check-ins short and structured. A 20-minute weekly touchpoint beats an irregular 90-minute session. Suggested prompts:

1. What happened this week that surprised you?
2. What's the thing you're most uncertain about right now?
3. Is there a specific situation you want to think through?

Avoid: doing their planning for them in the check-in. You can help them think, but the decisions should stay theirs.

### Before the first tournament

Run a mock tournament day together — even just verbally. Walk through the schedule hour by hour. What time does the team arrive? Who has the robot? Who has the checklist? What happens if the code won't deploy? What do you say to kids who are visibly anxious?

First-year coaches are often underprepared for how different the competition environment feels from practice. The unfamiliarity itself causes lapses in judgment. Walking through scenarios reduces that.

See [Tournament Day Checklist](/guides/tournament-day-checklist/) for a reference they can adapt.

### After the first tournament

This debrief matters more than any check-in during the season. Don't do it immediately after (emotions are high); wait 24–48 hours.

Structure:
1. What went better than you expected?
2. What was harder than you expected?
3. What would you do differently at the next event?
4. What did you observe about your own coaching that you want to change?

The fourth question is the most important and the most easily skipped. Push gently if they try to skip it.

| **FLL** | Trying to win Core Values rather than model it authentically; judging prep starts too late |
| **FTC** | Engineering Portfolio treated as an afterthought; first TeleOp drive delayed too long |
| **FRC** | Scale shock — not anticipating how much time/money/people the program requires; Impact Award planning starts in January, not September |
| **VEX IQ** | Underestimating the collaborative match format; not realising Autonomous Coding Skills runs are time-limited and separate practice |
| **VEX V5** | Treating robot design as iterative from the start (right) but skipping the notebook while doing it (wrong); alliance selection is unfamiliar |
| **VEX U** | Two-robot coordination underestimated; assuming college students don't need structure |

## Handing off: when a new coach no longer needs mentoring

The goal is to work yourself out of a job. Signs a first-year coach has crossed into independent practice:

- They diagnose session problems without prompting
- They de-escalate team tension without your involvement
- They make calls at competition without checking with you
- They can articulate why they made a decision, not just what they decided
- They are starting to notice things you missed

When you see these signs consistently, the relationship shifts from mentoring to peer collegial exchange. Name it explicitly. "I think you're ready to run this independently. I'm still here if you want to think something through, but I'm not going to keep checking in unless you want me to."

That handoff conversation, done well, is one of the most valuable things you can do for a new coach.

## When things go wrong

### The new coach is burning out
Signs: missing check-ins, venting about students, counting down to the end of the season. Don't push through it — the team feels it. Have a direct conversation: what specifically is depleting them? Often it's one solvable thing (a difficult parent, a session structure that isn't working, feeling like they have no support). If it's not solvable in-season, help them get to the end of the season without quitting and reframe the off-season as the time to restructure.

### The new coach's team is struggling significantly
If the team dynamic is genuinely harmful to students — persistent blame, exclusion, coaching patterns that undermine student wellbeing — you may need to intervene more directly. This is the exception, not the rule. Before stepping in: name specifically what you observed, why it concerns you, and ask what the coach wants to do about it. Provide support, not rescue.

### The new coach disagrees with your approach
Good. A new coach who always defers isn't building their own practice. Engage with the disagreement: "Tell me more about what you're thinking." If they're making a values-based choice you disagree with but which is safe and within program rules, let it be. If it's something likely to harm the team, name that clearly and explain why. Then let them decide.

## Quick-start checklist

**Before the season:**
- [ ] Schedule a 60–90 minute kickoff conversation
- [ ] Walk through the season calendar and registration steps
- [ ] Agree on check-in cadence and escalation path
- [ ] Share your contact information and when you're available

**During the season:**
- [ ] Run a short structured check-in each week or every two weeks
- [ ] Attend one practice early in the season (observe, don't lead)
- [ ] Run a mock tournament day walkthrough before their first event
- [ ] Attend the first tournament as a support presence (not as their coach)

**After the first tournament:**
- [ ] Debrief within 48 hours using the four-question structure
- [ ] Identify one specific thing to focus on for the remainder of the season

**End of season:**
- [ ] Run a final season retrospective together (see [Season Retrospective](/guides/season-retrospective/))
- [ ] Explicitly name the handoff when they're ready for independent practice
- [ ] Invite them to mentor someone else next season
