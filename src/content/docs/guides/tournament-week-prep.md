The qualifier is two weeks away. Here's what to do — and what to stop doing.

The two biggest mistakes coaches make in the final stretch: keep changing the robot when it was already good enough, and forget to prep the judging sessions. This guide helps you avoid both.

---

## Week 2 out: stabilize and drill

### Freeze the robot (mostly)

Pick a robot configuration and commit to it. Minor tuning is fine; rebuilding is not. A robot that scores 150 consistently beats one that scores 300 unpredictably.

**What "frozen" means:**
- No new missions added
- No major mechanical changes
- Code changes only to fix specific, reproducible failures

**What's still okay:**
- Adjusting a distance constant by a few degrees
- Tightening a loose axle
- Fixing a color sensor threshold that's wrong on the actual mat

### Run each mission 5 times in a row

For every mission in your run, do 5 consecutive attempts without touching the robot between runs. Track the score:

- **5/5:** mission is solid, move on
- **3–4/5:** acceptable, one more tuning session
- **1–2/5:** needs fixing or cutting from the run

If a mission passes fewer than 3 out of 5 times consistently, seriously consider dropping it from the competition run. Reliable 100 points > unreliable 200 points.

### Do one full mock judging session per track

Run each of the three judging sessions from scratch:
- **Robot Design:** 5 minutes, kids present without you prompting them
- **Innovation Project:** 5 minutes, full presentation + Q&A
- **Core Values:** 5 minutes, team activity + questions

After each, give one piece of specific feedback (not "good job" — something actionable like "you didn't explain *why* you chose that attachment"). Write it down.

## Week 1 out: polish and logistics

### Run mock tournament day

At least one practice this week should simulate the actual competition day:

1. Pack up the robot as if leaving for a tournament
2. Set up in a different room (simulates a new environment)
3. Run all missions in competition order — no do-overs
4. Do all three judging sessions back-to-back
5. Debrief as a team: what went well, what felt shaky?

This is important because **kids perform differently under simulated pressure** than during relaxed practice. Surprises in rehearsal are cheaper than surprises on competition day.

### Confirm logistics with parents

Send a message to all families covering:
- Date, time, exact venue address (and parking notes if you have them)
- What time to arrive (usually 30–60 min before the opening ceremony)
- What to bring (robot, laptop, mat if allowed, lunch/snacks, layers for cold venues)
- What NOT to bring (full build table — usually no room)
- Pick-up time (tournaments often run long; build in a buffer)

### Build your packing list

Pack the night before, not the morning of. Common items:

**Robot and mat:**
- Robot (in a padded container or box)
- Spare parts kit (axles, pins, beams, common attachment pieces)
- Charger for the hub
- Spare charged battery if you have one
- Laptop/tablet with SPIKE App installed and programs saved locally (not cloud-dependent)
- USB cable (Bluetooth is unreliable in crowded venues)
- Printed copy of your programs with mission labels

**Judging:**
- Engineering notebook (original, not a copy)
- Any physical Innovation Project materials (poster, prototype, props)
- Extra printed copies of the IP summary in case judges want one

**Team:**
- Team t-shirts or matching gear if you have them
- Snacks and water — long day, kids get hungry
- Small first aid kit (tournament venues vary)
- A responsible adult who isn't the coach to manage logistics while you're focused on the team

## The week of: day-before checklist

The day before, go through this with your team:

- [ ] Robot runs all missions at the expected score
- [ ] Hub is fully charged; backup battery charged if available
- [ ] Programs are saved and labeled on the device that's going to the tournament
- [ ] USB cable packed
- [ ] Engineering notebook complete and packed
- [ ] IP presentation rehearsed at least once today
- [ ] Team knows the schedule for tomorrow
- [ ] Everyone knows where to meet and when

## Keeping the team calm

First-year teams often arrive at tournament week with anxiety running high. Some things that help:

**Normalize imperfection.** Tell the team explicitly: "Something will go wrong at the tournament. That's true for every team. What matters is how we respond." Teams that expect things to go wrong handle them better when they do.

**Celebrate what's real.** What did the team actually accomplish this season? Name it specifically: "You learned to program from scratch. You interviewed a real scientist. You got the mission to work 4 out of 5 times." That's real regardless of tournament placement.

**Control what you can.** Pre-run checklist, arriving early, having spare parts. These are actions that reduce failures. Focus the team's energy on controllable things, not on worrying about other teams.

**Keep practice short this week.** Tired kids make more mistakes. Shorten sessions, end on a successful run, and send them home with energy left. The last thing you want is a team that burns out before tournament day.

---

## After the tournament (regardless of result)

- Debrief within a day or two while it's fresh — what worked, what didn't, what to do differently
- Celebrate something real (see above)
- If you advanced: start prep for the next level immediately — the gap between qualifier and regionals is often just 3–4 weeks
- If you didn't advance: a good debrief + specific improvements to focus on next season is worth more than any trophy
