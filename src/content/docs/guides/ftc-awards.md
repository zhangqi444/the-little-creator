FTC awards are how teams advance — and how they're recognised for things beyond the match results. The award structure rewards a *combination* of strong engineering, strong outreach, and Gracious Professionalism, with the top award (Inspire) requiring strength across all three.

This guide explains what each award actually measures, which combinations of awards qualify a team to advance, and how a first- or second-year team should set realistic award goals.

:::note[Award lists change]
Specific awards available at any tournament are listed in the current season's [Awards Manual](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/ftc/team-management-resources) on FIRST. This page covers the longstanding award structure, but always check the current Awards Manual for the season-specific list and any criteria changes.
:::

## The headline award: Inspire

**The Inspire Award** is the top FTC award. It recognises a team that exemplifies the FIRST FTC philosophy across engineering, outreach, and Gracious Professionalism — and it qualifies the recipient to advance to the next level (typically state / regional championship, then super-regionals or world championship).

Inspire is **not** a single criterion. Judges look for teams that:

- Have a thoughtful, documented engineering design process (the Engineering Portfolio is central evidence)
- Conduct meaningful community outreach beyond their own team
- Behave with grace, fairness, and respect toward other teams and officials
- Can articulate their season's story coherently in the judging interview

A team can win Inspire without winning matches. Conversely, a team can win every match and not win Inspire — match performance is necessary but not sufficient.

## The judged-only awards

These are awarded by the judging panel based on the interview, the Engineering Portfolio, and behaviour observed during the tournament. No on-field performance required.

### Think Award

Recognises the team whose Engineering Portfolio best captures their **engineering journey** — specifically the *iteration* process. Judges look for: documented design tradeoffs, prototypes that got rejected and why, lessons learned, evidence the team thought through problems rather than copied solutions. The Think Award is essentially "best documented engineering process," distinct from the robot's actual performance.

### Innovate Award

Recognises a team whose robot or workflow shows **creative engineering** — a novel mechanism, an unusual approach to a game element, a clever software solution. Judges want to see something they haven't seen on other robots that season. Often goes to a team that didn't copy a popular design but built something genuinely their own.

### Connect Award

Recognises **outreach to the engineering / STEM community** — partnerships with professionals, alumni networks, mentorship of younger teams, community education events. Strong Connect submissions document ongoing relationships, not one-off photo opportunities.

### Motivate Award

Recognises **team culture, recruiting, and member growth**. Judges look for: how the team brings in new members, how it sustains itself across seasons, how it celebrates wins and losses, what its identity is. A great Motivate team has rituals, traditions, and a clear sense of "who we are." Often the right target award for a team strong on culture but weaker on raw engineering.

### Control Award

Recognises **software achievement** — autonomous routines, sensor integration, computer vision, machine learning, advanced control loops. Submitted via a separate Control Award form (not the Engineering Portfolio). Strong submissions describe *what* the software does, *how* it was tested, and *what data* validates that it works.

### Design Award

Recognises the **mechanical or industrial design** of the robot — elegant solutions, clever mechanism integration, ergonomics, manufacturability. Often goes to teams whose robots look intentional and refined, not bolted together.

### Compass Award

Recognises an **adult mentor** who has had outstanding impact on a team. Nominated by the students. Often handed out at the closing ceremony with the mentor brought up specifically. One of the most emotional moments at any tournament.

### Promote Award

Recognises a team's **video promoting FIRST or FTC** — judged from a video submission. The brief changes year to year; check the season's Awards Manual. Particularly accessible to teams with strong creative / video skills who may not be the strongest at engineering.

## The match-based awards

These are determined by what happens on the field, not by judging.

### Winning Alliance

The two-team alliance that wins the elimination bracket. Both teams on the alliance receive the award. Qualifies the winning alliance to advance.

### Finalist Alliance

The two-team alliance that loses in the finals. Both teams on the alliance receive recognition; depending on advancement slot availability, this can also qualify.

### Dean's List Finalist

Recognises individual students (typically two per event) for outstanding leadership and contribution to FTC. Different from team-level awards — students are individually nominated by mentors and selected from the nomination pool.

## How awards combine to advance

Most tournaments have a small number of advancement slots that go to specific awards. Typical pattern at a qualifier:

1. **Inspire Award** — first advancement slot
2. **Winning Alliance captain** (then the partner) — next slot(s)
3. **Top-ranked qualifying team** not yet advancing — next slot
4. **Judges' picks** based on other awards — remaining slots

The exact advancement criteria are listed in each season's Awards Manual and may vary by region. Check your tournament's advancement matrix before the event.

## What rookies should target

A first-year FTC team is realistically aiming for:

- **Finishing all qualification matches** with the robot operational — surprisingly hard.
- **Being a candidate for Rookie Inspire** (if your region uses that designation). Rookie Inspire is the same criteria as Inspire but limited to rookie teams.
- **Motivate** — strong rookie teams often win Motivate because their story is genuinely interesting (we started a team, here's what we learned).
- **Compass** — your mentor probably deserves this. Talk about them.

A second-year team can realistically target **Think** (documenting your engineering process well is independent of how good the robot is), **Innovate** (one creative mechanism can win this), or **Control** (one strong autonomous routine can win this).

A team should *not* set "we will win Inspire" as a first-year goal — Inspire requires consistent multi-year work. "We will be in the conversation for Inspire by year three" is realistic.
