Season one is about surviving the six-week build season. Season two is about competing with intention — running a better team, building a smarter robot, and presenting a more compelling story to judges. Here's what experienced FRC mentors and student leaders consistently change after their first year.

---

## 1. Start Impact before Kickoff

Year one teams plan to "start Impact in February." By then, the robot is consuming every waking hour and Impact becomes a rushed slide deck assembled the night before the regional.

**What second-year teams do differently:**
- Convene an Impact sub-team at the start of the school year — not at Kickoff
- Begin community outreach activities in September and document them as they happen
- Assign a student Impact lead with authority over the sub-team's schedule and content
- Build a running evidence log (dates, photos, names, measurable outcomes) throughout the fall
- Draft the Impact submission narrative before Kickoff so the only update needed after build season begins is adding the robot's metrics

**The judging reality:** Impact is evaluated on *consistency and depth of outreach over time*, not on the quality of a last-minute pitch. Judges ask follow-up questions that expose teams who rehearsed a script versus teams who lived the work.

See [FRC Impact Award Submission Guide](/guides/frc-impact-submission/) and [FRC Judging Preparation](/guides/frc-judging-prep/) for structure and scoring guidance.

## 2. Scope the robot in week one — and hold the line

Year one teams read the game manual at Kickoff, brainstorm every possible mechanism, and spend weeks 1–3 in feature-creep. Week 5 arrives and nothing is complete.

**The year-two approach:**
1. On Kickoff day, list every scoring action and assign an expected-value score: `points × P(clean execution by week 5)`
2. Cut anything with low probability or high mechanical complexity — especially elements that would require a subsystem you've never built before
3. Define the robot's "minimum viable competition" spec by end of week 1
4. Gate future additions: nothing new gets designed after week 3 unless a core element is already match-tested and reliable
5. Reserve week 5 entirely for driver practice and autonomous refinement — no new mechanisms

**The math:** A robot that scores 50 points reliably beats a robot that targets 90 and delivers 30 inconsistently.

---

## 3. Run real driver practice from week 3

Year one teams driver-practice in week 6 — the week of bag (or ship). Year two teams start in week 3 and accumulate 10+ full-match sessions before the regional.

**What "real" driver practice means:**
- Run full match simulations with a timer (15-second autonomous, 2 min 15 sec teleop, 20-second endgame)
- Score every simulated match and log the score — identify where time is wasted
- Rotate drive team roles early so backup drivers are prepared (driver, operator, human player, coach — all roles should have depth)
- Practice endgame (climbing, parking, balancing) from week 4, both alone and in a three-robot alliance simulation

Driver practice is the most commonly under-invested activity in rookie FRC seasons. One additional 10-match practice block can shift a team from middle-of-the-field to alliance selection consideration.

---

## 4. Build a real pit crew — not just "whoever is available"

Year one pits are chaotic: whoever shows up works on whatever they can reach. Tools get misplaced. The robot takes 45 minutes to repair between matches.

**Year two pit discipline:**
- Appoint a pit lead (student) who owns the pit setup, tool inventory, and repair workflow
- Assign clear roles: electronics tech, mechanical tech, scouting liaison, drive coach backup
- Pack a pit kit with spares for every part that has failed in practice — then double it
- Build a 10-item pre-match checklist the drive team runs before every match (battery charged and fully seated, no loose screws at pivot points, all sensors confirmed, bumper numbers facing out)
- Run a post-match debrief at the pit after every match: what happened, what broke, what to fix before the next match

A well-run pit recovers from a match failure in one queue cycle. A chaotic pit skips matches or sends out a damaged robot.

---

## 5. Invest in scouting — and use the data

Year one teams at alliance selection pick teams they've watched or heard of. Year two teams pick based on data.

**Minimum viable scouting system:**
- Assign 3–4 dedicated scouts who don't also drive or pit-crew during quals (they can't do both)
- Scout every match, not just matches your team plays in — alliance selection requires knowing robots you've never played with or against
- Track at minimum: auto points, teleop cycles per match, endgame reliability, defense capability, connection issues (disconnects, e-stops)
- Before alliance selection: rank teams by consistent output, not peak output — a team that scores 40 reliably is often better than one that occasionally scores 80

**Alliance selection prep:** Know which teams you'd want at ranks 2–6 before the last qualification match ends. When your number is called, you have 45 seconds to decide.

See [FRC Robot Design Guide](/guides/frc-robot-design/) for alliance selection strategy.

## 6. Tell an engineering process story in the pit interview

Year one FRC teams show judges a robot and explain its mechanisms. Year two teams tell a story of how decisions were made.

**What judges are evaluating:** Not "does it work?" — they can see that. They want evidence of engineering process: problem → hypothesis → prototype → test → measurement → iteration.

**What to prepare:**
- A 2-minute robot overview that leads with "here's the problem we needed to solve" — not "here's what our robot does"
- At least two specific examples of design decisions that changed: what you built first, why it failed, what data led you to change direction
- Student-led responses — mentors who jump in and answer for students signal a team that isn't student-led
- Honest answers about what you'd improve in year three: judges respect self-awareness

The most common mistake: a polished pitch that sounds rehearsed and covers no failures. Judges are experienced engineers — they know robots fail; they want to see that your team learns from it.

---

## 7. Treat the engineering notebook as a real-time document

If your regional or district program awards credit for an engineering notebook or portfolio, the same rule applies as in FLL and FTC: retroactive notebooks are obvious and score poorly.

**Year two notebook habits:**
- Open the notebook on the first build session and never close it
- Document decisions, not just outcomes — "we chose chain over belt because…" is more valuable than "we installed chain drive"
- Photograph failed prototypes deliberately — a wall of "it works" photos is less valuable than photos of a mechanism that broke and what the team learned
- Assign notebook duty on a rotating schedule so it doesn't fall to one person

---

## 8. Coordinate with alliance partners before matches — every match

Year one teams treat qualification matches as solo performances. Year two teams arrive at the alliance station 5 minutes early and run a 3-minute alliance coordination.

**What to cover with your alliance partners:**
- Who takes which field zones in autonomous (collisions in auto waste both robots' points)
- Whether anyone is running a defense robot (if so, who do they target, and does it conflict with your plan?)
- Endgame priority: if only one robot can climb, which one has the better shot that match?
- Fallback if autonomous fails: what's the manual recovery?

Alliance coordination at higher-level events (championship, district championship) is considered mandatory by experienced teams. Starting this habit in your second year puts you ahead of many third-year teams.

---

## 9. Plan your whole event schedule in advance — including downtime

Year one teams arrive at the event without a schedule, attend the opening ceremony, then improvise. The day feels chaotic. Students miss critical tasks because nobody knew whose job they were.

**Year two event planning:**
- Create a match-by-match schedule showing who is at the pit, who scouts, who shadows drive team, who handles media/outreach documentation
- Block 30 minutes after alliance selection (if you make it) for alliance strategy meetings
- Build in team meals and a 10-minute debrief each evening
- Assign a student "schedule keeper" who tracks match queue calls and buffers — robots can be late to the alliance station by seconds and forfeit the match

One mentor or student lead should own the event schedule and communicate it clearly the night before.

---

## 10. Keep it student-led — actually

Every experienced FRC mentor eventually has the same realization: the more mentors do, the less students learn, and the less competitive the team becomes in the long run.

**Signs a team is mentor-led (and how to fix them in year two):**
- Mentors make design decisions in the group → rotate to asking "what are our options?" before offering opinions
- Mentors are in the pit during matches → step back; let the student pit lead run it
- Mentors write the Impact application → they can edit, but students must draft
- Drive team coach is a mentor → the coach role is a great first fully-student-run position

**The practical test:** On competition day, if you (as a mentor) had to leave the venue for three hours, could the team function without you? If not, that's your year-two project.

Student ownership also changes judging outcomes. Judges who ask technical questions and get confident, thoughtful student answers score teams significantly higher than teams where the student defers to the mentor standing two feet away.

---

## Summary: the 10 year-two changes

| What most year-one teams do | What to do in year two |
|---|---|
| Start Impact in February during build season | Build Impact sub-team in September, run outreach all year |
| Feature-creep the robot design through week 4 | Scope robot in week one, hold the line |
| Driver practice in week 6 only | Start driver practice in week 3, 10+ sessions before regionals |
| Chaotic pit with no assigned roles | Dedicated pit crew with clear roles, spares kit, pre-match checklist |
| Alliance selection gut-feel picks | Dedicated scouting team + data-driven selection |
| Describe the robot in the pit interview | Tell the engineering process story — failures and all |
| Notebook started week 4 or retroactively | Open notebook on day one of build season |
| No alliance coordination before matches | 5-minute alliance meeting before every qualification match |
| Improvise event day schedules | Match-by-match event schedule assigned in advance |
| Mentors fill the gaps | Students run it; mentors coach from the sideline |
