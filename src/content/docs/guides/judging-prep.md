Judging is where half of FLL happens. Robot scores matter, but they're only one input into whether a team advances. Understanding how judging works — and preparing your team for it — is one of the highest-leverage things a coach can do.

:::note[Official rubrics]
This guide explains the process and offers practical prep advice. For the actual scoring criteria, download the current season's judging rubrics from the [official FLL season page](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season). Rubrics change year to year.
:::

## The three judging sessions

Most qualifying tournaments include three judging appointments, each with a separate panel:

| Session | What's evaluated | Who runs it |
|---|---|---|
| **Innovation Project** | Problem identification, research process, solution design, presentation | 2–3 judges |
| **Robot Design** | Engineering choices, programming approach, team's understanding of their own robot | 2–3 judges (usually engineers or technical volunteers) |
| **Core Values** | Teamwork, inclusion, gracious professionalism, discovery mindset | 2–3 judges |

Each session is typically 5 minutes to set up, 5 minutes to present, and 5 minutes for questions. The team presents; the coach steps back.

## Innovation Project judging

### What judges look for

Judges read the rubric, but they're really trying to answer four questions:

1. Did the team find a *specific, real* problem (not a vague theme)?
2. Did they do *genuine research* — talking to experts, finding data — or just Google a few articles?
3. Is the solution *innovative* — meaning it addresses the root cause in a non-obvious way?
4. Can the team explain it *in their own words*, without reading from a poster?

### Practical prep

- **Every kid speaks.** Assign lines or sections so no one is silent. Judges notice if one kid carries the whole presentation.
- **Anticipate questions.** Common judge questions: "Why did you choose this problem?" "Who did you talk to?" "How would your solution actually get built?" "What would you do differently?" Practice thinking out loud, not reciting rehearsed answers.
- **Know your data.** If a kid cites a statistic, they should be able to say where it came from. "We found it online" isn't good enough. "We found it in a NOAA report about ocean plastic" is.
- **Show your process, not just your conclusion.** Judges love hearing "we originally thought X, but when we talked to [expert], we learned Y, so we changed our solution to Z." It shows real learning.

For a full walkthrough of building the Innovation Project, see the [Innovation Project Guide](/guides/innovation-project-guide/).

## Robot Design judging

### What judges look for

Robot Design judges want to see that the team *understands* their robot — not just that it works. They're evaluating:

- **Design choices** — why did you use this attachment? why this motor placement? what problems did you solve?
- **Programming approach** — how does your code work? how do you handle consistency? what did you iterate on?
- **Team understanding** — can multiple kids explain the robot, or only the one who built it?

### Practical prep

- **Every kid should be able to explain the robot at a basic level.** Even kids who focused on the Innovation Project should know: what sensors does the robot use, how does it drive, what are the main attachments.
- **Prepare to talk about failures.** "We tried X and it didn't work because Y, so we changed to Z" is exactly what engineers say. Judges love it. Don't pretend everything went smoothly.
- **Bring the robot.** Robot Design judging happens with the physical robot present. Bring it in good shape — judges may ask you to demonstrate something.
- **Explain your programming strategy.** How do you run missions reliably? Do you use a gyro? A mission selector? How do you handle inconsistency? Have a kid explain it.

### Common Robot Design mistakes

- Only one or two kids can answer questions — the others look lost
- Describing *what* the robot does without explaining *why* you designed it that way
- Not knowing what sensors you're using or why
- Saying "it just worked" when asked about a design decision

## Core Values judging

### What Core Values actually is

Core Values is not a test. It's an observation. Judges are watching how your team interacts — during a team activity, during the presentation, and even in the hallway. The question they're asking: *does this team actually live FLL's values, or are they just performing them?*

FLL's Core Values are:

- **Discovery** — We explore new skills and ideas.
- **Innovation** — We use creativity and persistence to solve problems.
- **Impact** — We apply what we learn to improve our world.
- **Inclusion** — We respect each other and embrace our differences.
- **Teamwork** — We are stronger when we work together.
- **Fun** — We enjoy and celebrate what we do.

### What the judging session looks like

Core Values sessions usually include a short **team activity** — a physical or problem-solving challenge the judges give the team on the spot. They're not evaluating whether you solve it perfectly. They're watching *how* you work together: Do kids include each other? Does anyone get left out or talked over? How does the team handle disagreement or frustration?

After the activity, judges may ask questions like:
- "Tell me about a time your team disagreed. How did you handle it?"
- "What's one thing you'd change about how your team works together?"
- "Who in your group is the best at [X]? How do you know?"

### Practical prep

- **Don't coach kids to perform.** Kids who've been told "say teamwork things to the judges" come across as rehearsed. Instead, actually *practice* the values during build season — that's what shows up genuine on tournament day.
- **Talk about real things.** In mock sessions, practice answering honestly: a real disagreement you had, a real mistake you made as a team, something you genuinely learned.
- **Include everyone in the activity.** Judges specifically watch for students who get left out. If your team has a tendency for one or two kids to dominate, name that explicitly in practice: "everyone needs to have a voice in this activity."
- **Debrief after practice sessions.** Ask the team: "Did everyone get to contribute? Did we listen to each other?" Make it a habit.

### Gracious Professionalism

This is one of the most-cited FLL values. It means competing with integrity — celebrating your own wins without diminishing others, helping other teams even when it's not required, treating judges and volunteers with respect. It's judged not just in the room but in the pit and at the competition tables.

Simple version to share with students: *"We want to win, and we also want everyone to have a great experience, including our competitors."*

## Before the judging rooms: logistics

- **Know your schedule.** Judging appointments are time-specific. Be at the room 5 minutes early — late arrivals lose setup time.
- **Bring your materials.** Innovation Project poster/model, engineering notebook if your region uses it, robot for Robot Design.
- **Leave the coach outside (or silent).** Judges are evaluating kids. If a coach answers a question directed at a kid, it hurts the team. Practice this.
- **Debrief after each session** — not to fix scores (they're submitted), but to practice reflection: what went well, what would you say differently.

## Awards and what they mean

FLL gives out several awards at tournaments, and advancement rules vary by region. Common awards:

| Award | What it recognizes |
|---|---|
| **Champion's Award** | Best overall — robot performance + judging combined. Top teams advance. |
| **Robot Performance Award** | Highest robot game score |
| **Innovation Project Award** | Best Innovation Project |
| **Robot Design Award** | Best robot design and engineering |
| **Core Values Award** | Best demonstration of FLL Core Values |
| **Motivate Award** | Best team spirit and energy |
| **Rising All-Star Award** | Best rookie team |

Not every team advances, and not every team wins an award — and that's okay. Many strong teams go home without hardware. The judging process is holistic and subjective; judges do their best with limited time. What matters most is that your team leaves having genuinely learned something.
