Print it out, put it in a binder, and fill it in every time the team does research — not at the end.

:::tip[Why a research log matters]
FLL judges specifically look for evidence that your team *did real research* — not just a Google search, but multiple sources, expert conversations, and a process of refining what you found. A filled-in research log is that evidence. It also helps the team remember what they found when they're presenting weeks later.
:::

:::note[Season topics change; the process doesn't]
The season theme changes every August. This template works for every season because it tracks your process, not the specific topic.
:::

## Part 2 — Source Log

Fill in one row for every source your team uses — websites, books, videos, news articles, databases. One source = one row.

**How to use:** Add rows as you find sources. Date each entry. After you discuss it as a team, write down what you actually learned from it — not just "it was about pollution" but "it showed that X% of Y happens in Z."

**Source types:** Website · Book · News article · Scientific paper · Video · Podcast · Government report · Organization publication · Database

:::tip[How many sources do you need?]
There is no official minimum, but teams that impress judges typically have 5–10 distinct sources — including at least one expert interview (see Part 3 below). Quality matters more than quantity: one clear, well-understood source beats five that the team can't explain.
:::

## How to use this at the tournament

- Bring a printed copy in your binder — it is your evidence file
- If judges ask "how did you research this?" point to your source log
- If they ask about your expert, open to Part 3 and walk them through it
- If they push back on your solution, use Part 5 to show you considered alternatives
- Do not read from it — use it as a backup, not a script
