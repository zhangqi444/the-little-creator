Your first tournament day will feel chaotic no matter how well you prepare — so prepare well. This is what you actually need, what the day actually looks like, and how to keep kids from melting down.

## The week before

- [ ] Confirm your arrival time with the tournament coordinator (usually 60–90 min before first match)
- [ ] Know the address and parking situation — tournaments are often at schools with limited lots
- [ ] Review the [Robot Game Rule Book](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season) one more time — focus on the table setup diagram and starting position rules
- [ ] Run at least two full practice runs from start to finish, including carrying the robot to the table and setting up
- [ ] Charge all batteries: SPIKE Prime hub battery, any rechargeable attachments
- [ ] Photograph your robot from multiple angles — if something breaks off, you'll know what it looked like

## What to pack

### Robot and equipment

- [ ] Robot (in a box or padded carrying case — don't carry it loose)
- [ ] Extra SPIKE Prime hub battery (fully charged)
- [ ] Charging cable for the hub
- [ ] USB cable (more reliable than Bluetooth for last-minute program uploads)
- [ ] Your laptop with the SPIKE App installed and your programs saved/backed up
- [ ] Spare LEGO pieces — bring the specific ones most likely to fall off your robot
- [ ] Small flathead screwdriver (for adjusting axle bushings) and spare axles/pins
- [ ] Tape measure (some teams use it to verify consistent starting position)
- [ ] Your physical starting position guide if you built one (a small LEGO block that clips to the base)

### Team materials (Innovation Project and Core Values)

- [ ] Printed Innovation Project presentation materials or physical prototype
- [ ] Engineering notebook or portfolio if your region requires it
- [ ] Any props or models for the judging presentation
- [ ] Backup copies of slides/presentations on a USB drive

### Logistics

- [ ] Snacks — tournaments run long (4–8 hours) and hungry kids are miserable kids
- [ ] Water bottles for everyone
- [ ] Team shirts or matching colors if you have them
- [ ] A team banner or pit decoration (optional, but kids love it)
- [ ] Small first aid kit (bandages, pain reliever)
- [ ] Phone chargers / power bank
- [ ] Cash for concessions (many school tournaments only accept cash)
- [ ] Extra mask (some tournaments still require them)

## What the day looks like

### Arrival and check-in

You'll check in at a registration table and receive your team number and schedule. Keep this schedule visible — it tells you when your robot matches are, when your judging appointments are, and when the opening ceremony is.

You'll be assigned a **pit area** — a table where your team sets up for the day. Most teams keep the robot, tools, and gear here between matches.

### Pit time

Between matches and judging, teams are in the pit area. Use this time to:
- Make repairs (within the rules — judges can't modify the robot after the technical inspection)
- Review programs for the next run
- Watch other teams' matches to learn strategies
- Help younger kids stay occupied and calm

### Technical inspection

Most tournaments include a **robot inspection** early in the day. An official checks that your robot meets the rules (size, materials, one hub, etc.). If something doesn't pass, you have time to fix it before matches start. Don't panic — it's routine.

### Robot matches

FLL teams typically get **three robot match rounds** at a qualifying tournament. Each round: you carry your robot to the table, set it up in starting position, run your program, and collect your score. The highest score from your three rounds counts.

**Between rounds:**
- Note what went wrong (mission didn't complete, robot drifted off course, attachment fell off)
- Decide one thing to fix or adjust — not ten things
- Keep it simple: a small tweak that solves the main problem is better than a big overhaul that introduces new ones

### Judging presentations

You'll have scheduled appointments with three judging panels:
- **Innovation Project** — your research problem and solution
- **Robot Design** — your engineering choices (why you built it the way you did)
- **Core Values** — how your team works together

Judging rooms are usually 5 minutes to set up, 5 minutes to present, and 5 minutes for questions. Coaches step back — this is the kids' time to talk.

### Awards ceremony

At the end of the day, all teams gather for awards. Whether or not your team wins an advancement spot, the ceremony is worth attending. Cheer for other teams — Core Values is watching.

## Keeping kids calm

First tournaments are emotionally intense. Some things that help:

**Lower the stakes in how you talk about it.** "Let's show everyone what we've built" lands better than "we need to score 250 points." Kids perform better when they're excited, not afraid.

**Give everyone a role.** A kid who has a specific job (carries the robot, calls out the starting position, resets the attachment) feels less anxious than one who's just waiting.

**Plan for mistakes.** Tell kids before the day: something will go wrong. That's true for every team. The question isn't whether it happens — it's how you respond.

**Separate robot scores from team success.** FLL judges advancement holistically — a team can advance without having the highest robot score. Core Values and Innovation Project matter.

**Debrief after each match, briefly.** "What did we see? What's one thing we'll try next run?" keeps energy focused rather than spinning on what went wrong.

## If something breaks at the tournament

- Stay calm — the kids are watching how you react
- Go to your pit area and assess what broke
- Focus on the highest-value missions you can still run reliably with the remaining robot
- Ask a robot advisor (usually volunteers at the pit tables) if you need a second opinion
- You can rebuild or repair during pit time — you cannot modify the robot during a match

## After the tournament

Whether or not you advance:

- Thank the volunteers and officials
- Do a quick team debrief — what were we proud of? what do we want to do differently?
- Write down what broke and what you'd fix — your future self will thank you
- Celebrate. This is hard. Your kids did something remarkable.
