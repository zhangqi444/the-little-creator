The FTC Engineering Portfolio is the judged document that drives most FTC awards — Think, Innovate, Design, and Inspire all reference it. It is *distinct* from the engineering notebook, though they're related, and confusion between the two is one of the most common mistakes new teams make.

This guide explains what the Portfolio is, what it isn't, what a strong one looks like, and how to build one without sinking the season into documentation overhead.

:::note[Format and length]
The Engineering Portfolio is capped at **15 pages** (cover page, table of contents, and dividers don't count toward the limit but excessive padding may be marked down). This page covers structure and content; for the exact format requirements check the current season's [Engineering Portfolio guidelines](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/ftc/team-management-resources) in the FTC Team Management Resources hub. Requirements have shifted over recent seasons — always verify against the current Awards Manual.
:::

## Portfolio vs. notebook — the most important distinction

| | **Engineering Portfolio** | **Engineering Notebook** |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Capped at ~15 pages | Unbounded |
| Audience | Judges (single 5–7 min interview) | Internal team + judges as backup |
| Tone | Polished summary | Real-time field notes |
| Created | Mostly at end of season | Continuously throughout season |
| Required at events? | Yes — printed copy expected | Strongly recommended — provides evidence |

**Think of the Portfolio as a 15-page executive summary of the notebook.** Judges read the Portfolio first; they consult the notebook only if they need to verify something or want depth on a specific point.

The Portfolio is what you spend deliberate writing energy on. The notebook is what you maintain incrementally so the Portfolio has material to draw from.

## What a strong Portfolio contains

A widely-used Portfolio structure:

1. **Cover page** — team number, name, logo, season name
2. **Table of contents** — one page; helps judges navigate
3. **Team introduction (1–2 pages)** — who the team is, structure, philosophy, sub-team organisation, where you meet
4. **Season summary (1 page)** — game strategy, key choices, results
5. **Engineering design process (3–5 pages)** — your iteration cycle on each major mechanism: prototype → test → refine → final. Include sketches, photos of dead-end designs, and *why* you rejected them
6. **Robot specifications (1 page)** — final robot dimensions, mass, materials, motor count, sensor list
7. **Software approach (1–2 pages)** — autonomous strategy, key teleop features, computer vision if used, architectural choices
8. **Outreach (2–3 pages)** — events you ran, partnerships, who you mentored, photos, evidence of impact
9. **Strategy + future plans (1 page)** — what's next, how you'll sustain across seasons

These section weights are flexible. A team strong in software might give software 2 pages and outreach 1. A team strong in community work might invert that.

## What judges actually read

In a 5–7 minute interview, judges will:

- Glance at the cover, team intro, and one mechanism case study (~90 seconds)
- Listen to your students present (~3 minutes)
- Ask 2–3 targeted follow-up questions (~2 minutes)
- Flip back to one or two specific pages to verify a claim

So your Portfolio needs to be **skimmable**. Headings should tell the story; the first sentence of each section should give the punchline; details support but don't obscure.

## The single most valuable Portfolio pattern

For every major design decision, document it as a small structured block:

> **Problem:** [What were we trying to solve?]
>
> **What we tried first:** [The first design, with photo]
>
> **Why it didn't work:** [Specific test failure]
>
> **What we changed:** [The fix]
>
> **Result:** [How well the new design works]

These "design iteration" blocks are what win Think Awards. They demonstrate the engineering process in a way judges can verify. Aim for 3–5 of these in the engineering design process section, covering different sub-systems.

## Common Portfolio mistakes

**Padding.** Adding photos, motivational quotes, or repetitive content to fill space. Judges notice; they prefer a sharp 10-page Portfolio over a padded 15-page one.

**No iteration evidence.** Showing only the final design. Looks like the team copied a known good solution. Show the rejected prototypes — that's what proves you did the work.

**Outreach without impact.** Listing events without describing what happened, who benefited, or whether the team continued the relationship. "We visited an elementary school once" is not Connect Award material.

**Software as a black box.** Saying "we wrote autonomous in Java" without describing the strategy, the routines, or the testing approach. Software-strong teams should specifically describe their software architecture.

**No team voice.** A Portfolio written entirely by one adult mentor or one student reads as such. Judges value evidence that multiple students wrote sections.

## When to write the Portfolio

Realistic timeline for a team starting in August:

- **August–October (build season opening):** Don't try to write the Portfolio yet. Maintain the notebook. Capture photos of every prototype and decision.
- **November–December:** Draft the engineering design process sections incrementally as you finalise each mechanism.
- **January (pre-first-tournament):** Draft team introduction, outreach summary, and the polished design iteration blocks. First Portfolio version due before your first qualifier.
- **February–March (between qualifiers):** Iterate. After each tournament, update the Portfolio with new outreach, new design changes, and judging feedback you received.
- **April–May (championships):** Polish for the highest-stakes events. The Portfolio at your championship event should be a meaningfully better document than the one at your first qualifier.

## Notebook backing

If you're going for an award that relies on the Portfolio (Think especially), judges may ask to see the notebook to verify a Portfolio claim. So your notebook should:

- Be dated and chronological (loose-leaf with dates is fine)
- Include rough sketches, test results, and decisions as they happen
- Not be "cleaned up" to match the Portfolio — judges can tell when a notebook has been backfilled

A notebook that's clearly authentic — with bad handwriting, rough sketches, real dates, and dead-end designs — is *more* credible than a beautiful one written the week before the tournament.
