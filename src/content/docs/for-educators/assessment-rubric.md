This rubric is for **classroom and club use** — evaluating student progress during the season, assigning grades, or giving structured feedback. It is distinct from the official FLL tournament rubrics, which are published by FIRST and used by judges at competitions.

:::note[Official competition rubrics]
For FLL tournament judging, download the current season's official rubrics from the [official FLL season page](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season). This classroom rubric is a teaching and assessment tool, not a competition document.
:::

## How to use this rubric

**Pick a focus area per session or week**, not all four at once. Trying to assess everything simultaneously produces noise. One meaningful observation per session, recorded in your gradebook or observation notes, is more useful than a rushed multi-category checklist.

**Assess observable behavior**, not intent or potential. "Tried to but couldn't" and "hadn't tried yet" are different from demonstrated competency. Score what you actually saw, not what you think the student could do.

**Share scores back with students**, with one specific observation and one actionable next step. "You're a 2 on Collaboration today — I noticed you talked over Jamie's idea without trying it. Next time, try asking one follow-up question before proposing your own idea."

**Use the rubric longitudinally.** A student moving from 1 → 3 over a semester is stronger evidence of learning than a student who scores 4 every time.

## Category 1 — Programming

*Applies to FLL (SPIKE Prime Python/Blocks) and VEX IQ (VEXcode Blocks/Python). Adjust level expectations based on how far into the season you are.*

| Score | Descriptor | Observable behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **4 — Proficient** | Programs reliably and iterates independently | Writes programs that complete intended tasks consistently; identifies bugs without help; makes targeted changes to fix specific problems; can explain what each code block or line does |
| **3 — Developing** | Programs with occasional support | Can write basic programs for simple tasks; identifies when something isn't working; needs occasional help debugging or structuring more complex logic |
| **2 — Beginning** | Programs with significant support | Can modify existing programs with guidance; struggles to write from scratch; needs help identifying what's causing unexpected behavior |
| **1 — Emerging** | Not yet programming independently | Watches others program; can run a program but not write or modify one; has not yet attempted to create or change code |

**Assessment prompts:**
- "Explain what this part of your program does."
- "What would you change if the robot needs to go 10 cm further?"
- "Your robot isn't stopping correctly — where in the code would you look first?"

## Category 2 — Building and Engineering

*Applies to both FLL (SPIKE Prime LEGO) and VEX IQ (snap plastic). Adjust expectations for the season stage — early season = simpler builds, late season = refined designs.*

| Score | Descriptor | Observable behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **4 — Proficient** | Designs and builds purposefully with sound reasoning | Makes deliberate design choices and can explain why; tests builds and modifies based on results; troubleshoots mechanical failures independently; build is stable and functional under competition conditions |
| **3 — Developing** | Builds functionally with some reasoning | Can construct a working mechanism with occasional guidance; makes some design decisions with prompting; identifies obvious mechanical problems |
| **2 — Beginning** | Builds with significant guidance | Can follow building instructions; struggles to design from scratch; identifies problems only when pointed out |
| **1 — Emerging** | Not yet building independently | Needs step-by-step guidance for basic assembly; has not yet attempted to make original design decisions |

**Assessment prompts:**
- "Why did you put the motor there instead of somewhere else?"
- "This attachment keeps falling off. What do you think is causing it?"
- "If you were starting over, what would you do differently?"

## Category 3 — Collaboration and Teamwork

*This category assesses how the student works with others, not just what they produce individually. Use observation during normal team activities.*

| Score | Descriptor | Observable behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **4 — Proficient** | Actively contributes to team and includes others | Volunteers ideas and invites others to build on them; actively includes quieter team members; handles disagreement constructively; adjusts own approach when it's not working for the team |
| **3 — Developing** | Participates effectively in team | Contributes meaningfully when engaged; generally listens to others; may occasionally dominate or withdraw but recovers with prompting |
| **2 — Beginning** | Participates with prompting | Waits to be asked for input; may talk over others or disengage without realizing it; responds to redirection |
| **1 — Emerging** | Limited participation in team | Rarely contributes; noticeably excluded from or excludes others from key decisions; conflicts not self-managed |

**Assessment notes:**
- Introverted students often score lower here not because they're poor collaborators but because they contribute quietly. Observe carefully before scoring low.
- High scorers sometimes dominate. A student who does 80% of the work is not a 4 if it comes at the expense of others' participation.

## Category 4 — Project Work (FLL Innovation Project)

*FLL-specific. For VEX IQ teams with no research component, use Category 5 (Problem Solving) instead.*

| Score | Descriptor | Observable behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **4 — Proficient** | Research-driven, original, well-presented | Identified a specific, real problem; can cite sources and at least one expert/external contact; solution addresses the root cause; presents confidently in own words; responds to questions thoughtfully |
| **3 — Developing** | Shows genuine research effort and reasonable solution | Has a specific problem and a plausible solution; some external research beyond Google; presentation is clear if not polished; can answer basic questions |
| **2 — Beginning** | Problem identified, research surface-level | Problem is broad or not clearly connected to the season theme; solution is generic; no expert contact; presentation is scripted or heavily reliant on notes |
| **1 — Emerging** | Project not yet underway or very early stage | Problem not yet chosen or too vague to research; no research activity yet; student cannot describe the project |

**Assessment notes:**
- The Innovation Project is a team product; individual scores here reflect each student's *contribution to and understanding of* the team project, not the project quality itself.
- A student who played a major role in the research but minor role in the presentation can still score 4 if they can speak knowledgeably about the work.

---

## Category 5 — Problem Solving

*Applies to both FLL and VEX IQ. Assess during debugging sessions, design challenges, or any moment the team faces an obstacle.*

| Score | Descriptor | Observable behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **4 — Proficient** | Approaches problems systematically and persistently | When something doesn't work, forms a hypothesis, tests it, and updates based on results; tries multiple approaches; doesn't give up after first failure; can articulate what they tried and what they learned |
| **3 — Developing** | Attempts solutions with some structure | Tries a few approaches; sometimes makes changes without understanding why; persists through moderate frustration |
| **2 — Beginning** | Attempts solutions randomly or gives up quickly | Makes changes without a clear hypothesis; asks for the answer rather than working toward it; becomes frustrated and disengages after one or two failures |
| **1 — Emerging** | Doesn't yet attempt to solve problems independently | Waits for an adult to fix problems; doesn't engage with obstacles as puzzles to solve |

**Assessment prompts:**
- "What have you already tried?"
- "What's your hypothesis about why it's not working?"
- "What's the next thing you'd try if this doesn't work?"

## Grade conversion (optional)

If your school requires letter grades or percentages, a simple conversion:

| Score range | Grade equivalent |
|---|---|
| 3.5 – 4.0 | A (90–100%) |
| 2.5 – 3.4 | B (80–89%) |
| 1.5 – 2.4 | C (70–79%) |
| Below 1.5 | Needs improvement (below 70%) |

Compute as an average across categories you assessed, weighted by your program's priorities (e.g., weight programming and building more heavily for a technical class; weight collaboration and project work more for a general STEM elective).

## End-of-season portfolio prompt

For programs that use a portfolio or reflection component, a useful end-of-season prompt for students:

> *"Choose one moment this season where something didn't go the way you expected. Describe what happened, what you did about it, and what you learned. Be specific — 'the robot broke' is not specific; 'the attachment motor stalled on the third mission every run because we were over-geared' is specific."*

This prompt surfaces real learning better than "what did you enjoy?" and produces more honest self-assessment.
