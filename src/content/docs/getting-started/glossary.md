FLL comes with a wall of acronyms and insider terms. Here's what they all mean.

---

## Programs and organizations

**FIRST**
The non-profit organization that runs FLL, FTC, FRC, and other robotics programs. Stands for *For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology*. The umbrella org — everything in the FLL world traces back to FIRST.

**FLL / FIRST LEGO League**
The family of robotics programs serving students aged 4–16, run by FIRST in partnership with LEGO Education. Has three divisions: Discover, Explore, and Challenge. When people say "FLL" without specifying, they almost always mean FLL Challenge.

**FLL Challenge**
The competitive division of FLL for ages 9–14 (US/Canada) or 9–15 (elsewhere). Teams build and program an autonomous robot and complete an Innovation Project. What most of this wiki is about.

**FLL Explore**
The non-competitive exhibition division for ages 6–10. Teams build a motorized model around the season theme and present at a local expo — no head-to-head competition.

**FLL Discover**
The introductory division for ages 4–6. Classroom or at-home play-based STEM activities using DUPLO bricks. No competition or exhibitions.

**FTC / FIRST Tech Challenge**
The next step after FLL for older kids (grades 7–12, ages 12–18). Uses custom-built robots with more complex engineering. Many kids move to FTC after aging out of FLL Challenge.

**FRC / FIRST Robotics Competition**
The high school program (grades 9–12, ages 14–18). Large, complex robots built during a 6-week build season. College-scholarship territory.

**LEGO Education**
The branch of LEGO Group that makes educational kits, including the SPIKE Prime used in FLL Challenge. Different from the LEGO you buy at a toy store — the hub (the programmable brick) and sensors are LEGO Education specific.

**PDO / Program Delivery Organization**
The regional partner that runs FLL in your state or country. They organize qualifying tournaments, handle local registration, and collect regional fees. Examples: FIRST Washington (WA), FIRST in Texas (TX), ORTOP (OR). Finding your PDO is one of the first things to do — use [FIRST's partner finder](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-local-support).

**FIRST HQ / Headquarters**
The national FIRST organization based in Manchester, NH. Manages the FIRST Dashboard, sets overall program rules, and runs World Championship.

## Registration and administration

**FIRST Dashboard**
The online portal at [my.firstinspires.org](https://my.firstinspires.org) where teams register nationally, manage team rosters, and pay the national registration fee. Every team needs an account here before doing anything else.

**YPP / Youth Protection Program**
FIRST's background check and training requirement for all adult members of a team (coaches, mentors, anyone who will be with kids). Required before you can be added to a team in the FIRST Dashboard. Costs nothing — just a background check through a third party and a short online training.

**Team Captain / Lead Coach**
The adult who creates and manages the team in the FIRST Dashboard. Usually the primary coach. Has admin access over team roster, registration, and event sign-ups.

**Second Coach**
FIRST requires at least two screened adults on every team. This is the second adult who completes YPP and is listed on the team. Often a parent who helps at practices.

## Competition structure

**Qualifier / Qualifying Tournament**
The local tournament where teams compete for the first time in a season. Usually in November–December. Teams that meet certain score and judging thresholds advance to the next level.

**Regional Championship / State Championship**
The next level above a qualifier. Brings together top teams from multiple qualifiers in a region or state. Winning teams or award recipients may advance to World Championship.

**World Championship / Worlds**
The FIRST LEGO League World Championship. Held in late April–early June. Top teams from each region worldwide compete. Invitation only — you qualify through regional awards.

**Advancement criteria**
How teams earn a spot at the next level. Usually a combination of Robot Game score, judging scores, and specific award nominations. Each PDO sets its own advancement criteria — check with yours.

## At a tournament

**Robot Game**
The portion of competition where the robot runs autonomously on the game mat and scores points by completing missions. Each team gets multiple 2.5-minute runs; best score counts.

**Innovation Project**
The research and presentation component. Teams identify a real-world problem related to the season's theme, design a solution, and present it to judges in a 5-minute session.

**Core Values judging**
A judged session where the team demonstrates how they work together, how they handle challenges, and how they embody the FLL Core Values. Sometimes includes a live teamwork activity.

**Robot Design judging**
A judged session where the team explains their robot's mechanical design, programming strategy, and engineering decisions to a panel of judges.

**GP / Gracious Professionalism**
FLL's foundational value — the idea that you can compete fiercely and still be kind, respectful, and collaborative. Teams are judged on GP throughout the day, not just in Core Values judging. See [Gracious Professionalism in Practice](/guides/gracious-professionalism/) for what it actually looks like.

**Coopertition**
A blend of "cooperation" and "competition" — helping another team even if it doesn't benefit you. Core to FLL culture. Sometimes teams help rivals debug a robot issue between rounds.

**Champion's Award**
The highest team award at most FLL tournaments — given to the team that best embodies all three components (Robot Game, Innovation Project, Core Values) together. Not just the highest-scoring robot.

**Pit**
The area where teams set up their robot, tools, and gear between rounds. Teams spend most of the day here making adjustments and meeting other teams.

## Hardware and software

**SPIKE Prime**
The LEGO Education robotics kit used for FLL Challenge in the current (Founders Edition) program. Includes the SPIKE Prime hub (the programmable "brain"), motors, sensors, and LEGO Technic building pieces.

**SPIKE App / LEGO Education SPIKE App**
The official programming environment for SPIKE Prime. Supports block-based coding and Python. Free download from LEGO Education.

**Hub**
The programmable brick at the center of a SPIKE Prime robot — the brain. Houses the processor, speaker, lights, and ports for connecting motors and sensors.

**EV3 / Mindstorms EV3**
An older LEGO Education robotics kit that preceded SPIKE Prime. Some teams still use it; some PDOs accept it for qualifying. Being phased out — new teams should use SPIKE Prime.

**Challenge Set**
The official FLL game materials: the challenge mat (the playing field) and the mission models (the LEGO structures on the mat that the robot interacts with). New each season; must be the official FIRST version for competition.

**Future Edition**
The upcoming major update to FLL Challenge, launching with the 2026–27 BIOGLOW season. New hardware platform (not SPIKE Prime compatible), semi-cooperative matches, wireless connectivity. Relevant if you're buying gear now — see [What is FLL?](/getting-started/what-is-fll/) for details.
