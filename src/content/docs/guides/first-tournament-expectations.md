The [Tournament Day Checklist](/guides/tournament-day-checklist/) covers what to pack and what the schedule looks like. This guide covers everything the checklist can't: what the experience *feels* like, and how to help your team navigate it well.

## It will feel chaotic — that's normal

First tournaments feel overwhelming to almost everyone. There are dozens of teams, strangers everywhere, schedules changing, robots breaking, kids getting anxious. This is not a sign something has gone wrong. It's just what tournaments are.

The teams that handle it best aren't the ones with the most prepared robots — they're the ones who've been told in advance that chaos is part of the experience, and that their job is to stay curious and calm, not to be perfect.

**Tell your team this before you go.** Explicitly. "Today will feel a little chaotic. Something will probably go wrong. That's okay. We're here to see what we built do its thing, meet other teams, and have a good time."

## The emotions you'll see (and how to respond)

### Pre-tournament nerves

Kids who care about their robot get nervous before tournaments. This is healthy — it means they're invested. Anxiety becomes a problem when it overwhelms performance.

Helpful framing before you arrive:
- "You already did the hard work. Today is just showing it."
- "Every team here was nervous at their first tournament."
- "We're not here to beat anyone. We're here to see how far we've come."

Avoid: "Don't be nervous!" (invalidates the feeling), "We need to win" (increases stakes), or "You'll be fine" (dismissive).

### The robot doesn't perform as expected

It will happen. Maybe the attachment falls off. Maybe the robot veers off course and misses missions. Maybe the program that worked in practice suddenly doesn't. This moment — watching something the kids spent months building not work — is one of the hardest parts of FLL for first-year families.

**Your reaction in this moment matters more than the score.** If you stay calm and curious ("interesting — what happened there?"), the kids will too. If you visibly deflate or express frustration, they will carry that.

After the run, head to the pit area. Ask one question: "What did we see?" Not "what went wrong" — "what did we see." Then: "What's one thing we want to try differently next run?"

### The other robots are better

First-year teams often encounter teams who have been competing for several seasons. Their robots are faster, more reliable, and complete more missions. This can be demoralizing if kids aren't prepared for it.

Before the tournament: "Some of the teams here have been doing this for 3, 4, 5 years. Their robots will look different from ours. That's okay — we're in our first season, and so were they once."

If a kid says "our robot is the worst one here": "I don't know if that's true, and it doesn't matter. What I know is you built something that works, and that's genuinely hard. Look what you made."

### Tears

Tears happen. Kids cry when the robot fails, when they don't win an award they expected, when they're exhausted at the end of a long day. This is normal and okay.

Don't rush to fix it. Sit with the feeling for a minute: "I know. That was disappointing. It's okay to feel that." Then, when they're ready: "What was something today that you're proud of?"

### Unexpected joy

Also happens: kids who were nervous become completely lit up watching robots compete. They cheer for teams they've never met. They make friends in the pit area. They come home wanting to start building again immediately. 

This is the whole point.

## Social dynamics to watch for

### Kids comparing robots

In the pit area, kids inevitably look at other teams' robots and compare. Some of this is healthy curiosity ("how did they do that?"). Some becomes discouraging ("theirs is so much better").

Redirect comparison toward learning: "What's one thing they did that we could learn from?" This is literally what FLL's gracious professionalism value is for.

### Helping other teams

One of the most meaningful FLL moments is when a team helps a competitor fix a robot problem. This happens — experienced teams routinely offer help to newer ones, including sharing parts, advice, or just moral support. 

If your team has the opportunity to help another team, take it. If another team offers help, accept it graciously. This is the culture FLL is trying to build.

### The team dynamic under pressure

You will see your team's dynamics clearly at a tournament in a way practice doesn't always reveal. Who steps up under pressure, who checks out, who gets frustrated with teammates, who becomes a calming presence. 

Notice it, but don't intervene in the moment unless something is genuinely harmful. After the tournament, your [Core Values Activities](/guides/core-values-activities/) and team debrief are the right place to process it.

## For coaches and parents: managing your own reactions

This is genuinely hard. You've invested time, money, and emotional energy in your team. Watching things go wrong publicly is difficult. 

A few things that help:
- **Decide in advance that you'll be proud no matter what.** Not as a performance — actually make peace with it before you walk in. Then it's not a coping mechanism, it's a choice.
- **Watch what the kids watch, not the scoreboard.** Follow their energy, not the rankings.
- **Don't have sideline conversations about the robot's performance within earshot of the kids.** They hear everything.
- **Find another coach to check in with.** Other coaches at the tournament have been through this. A five-minute conversation in the hallway can recalibrate your perspective.

## After the tournament: the debrief

The debrief is one of the most valuable things you can do — and most teams skip it. Within a day or two of the tournament, gather the team and ask:

1. **What are you most proud of?** (Go around the circle — everyone answers.)
2. **What surprised you?**
3. **What was the hardest moment?**
4. **What's one thing you want to do differently next time?**
5. **What was the best part of the day?**

Write down the answers. They're useful if you compete again, and they help kids process the experience rather than just carrying it.

## If your team doesn't advance

Most first-year teams don't advance past their first qualifying tournament. That's not failure — it's the design of the program. The goal of the qualifying round is to have a complete FLL experience, not to win.

What to say: "This was our first tournament. We did it. We competed, we presented to judges, we met other teams, our robot ran on a real competition mat. That's a big deal. If we come back next season, we'll start from a completely different place — because of today."

Many of the strongest FLL teams are built on the experience of a season that didn't go to championships. The first season is for learning what it actually feels like.

## If your team does advance

If you advance to regionals or beyond: celebrate genuinely, but recalibrate expectations. Every subsequent round gets more competitive. The emotional dynamics described above repeat at each level, often more intensely. The debrief, the Core Values practices, and the "it's okay to lose" framing don't stop being relevant — they become more important.
