Core Values isn't a judging checkbox — it's how your team works together all season. The teams that do well in Core Values judging are the ones who actually practiced it, not the ones who rehearsed talking points. These activities build the real thing.

Use them at the start of build season, when energy is low mid-season, or any time the team feels stuck or fractious.

:::tip[Timing]
Each activity includes a suggested time. Most work best with 4–10 participants. All are designed to need no special materials beyond what's already in your build space.
:::

## Why activities matter

FLL judges run a short team activity during the Core Values session specifically to watch how kids interact when they're *not* prepared for it. The behaviors they see in an unscripted 5-minute challenge are much more revealing than a rehearsed speech. The best preparation is making those behaviors normal — by practicing them repeatedly throughout the season.

---

## Activity 1 — Silent Sort (10 minutes)

**What it builds:** Inclusion, nonverbal communication, shared leadership

**How it works:**
1. Give each team member a sticky note with a number, word, or category written on it — without showing anyone else.
2. The team must arrange themselves in order (by number, alphabetically, by category) **without speaking** — only gestures and expressions.
3. Once sorted, reveal the notes and check if they got it right.

**Debrief questions:**
- Who took the lead? Did everyone feel included in the process?
- What was frustrating? What worked well?
- How is this like situations in our team's build sessions?

---

## Activity 2 — One-Word Story (5–10 minutes)

**What it builds:** Active listening, building on others' ideas, not dominating

**How it works:**
The team builds a story together — each person contributes exactly one word at a time, going around the circle. No planning, no skipping, no correcting. The story will be silly. That's the point.

Example: "Once ... a ... robot ... ate ... the ... entire ... competition ... mat ... and ..."

**Debrief questions:**
- Did anyone try to steer the story? How did that feel for others?
- What happened when someone added a word you didn't expect?
- How does this connect to how we handle each other's ideas during builds?

---

## Activity 3 — Tower Challenge (15–20 minutes)

**What it builds:** Teamwork under pressure, gracious failure, iteration

**Materials:** 20 index cards or sheets of paper, tape, and a coin or small object to balance on top.

**How it works:**
1. The team has 10 minutes to build the tallest freestanding tower using only the cards and tape, with a coin balanced on top.
2. No single person can hold the tower while others build — everyone must contribute to the design.
3. After 10 minutes, measure and record the height.
4. Optional: do a second round applying one lesson from the first.

**Debrief questions:**
- How did you make decisions when people disagreed?
- Did anyone's idea get dismissed? What happened?
- What did you change in round 2, and why?

---

## Activity 4 — Strength Spotlight (10 minutes)

**What it builds:** Inclusion, appreciating different contributions, team identity

**How it works:**
1. Each team member writes their name on a blank sheet of paper.
2. Pass the sheets around — everyone writes one specific strength they've observed in that person. Not "nice" or "smart" — specific: "remembers the gear ratio from three sessions ago" or "stays calm when the robot breaks."
3. Return sheets to their owners. Everyone reads their own silently, then shares one that surprised them.

**Debrief questions:**
- Did anything on your sheet surprise you?
- Are there contributions on your team that don't get noticed as much as they should?
- How can we make sure every team member knows their role matters?

---

## Activity 5 — Failure Museum (15 minutes)

**What it builds:** Discovery mindset, psychological safety, honest reflection

**How it works:**
1. Ask each team member to share one thing that failed during build season — a robot design that didn't work, a program that crashed, a presentation that flopped.
2. Each "exhibit" in the Failure Museum gets a title ("The Attachment That Fell Off Every Run"), a one-sentence description of what happened, and one thing learned from it.
3. Celebrate the failures together — the more dramatic, the better.

**Why this matters:** Teams that can laugh at failures and extract lessons from them iterate faster and handle tournament-day problems better than teams that treat failure as shameful.

**Debrief questions:**
- What's the most important thing we learned from a failure this season?
- Is it safe on our team to try something and have it not work?
- What failure are we most proud of, because of what it taught us?

---

## Activity 6 — Two Truths and a Build Goal (10 minutes)

**What it builds:** Getting to know each other, setting shared goals

**How it works:**
Similar to "Two Truths and a Lie" but adapted for teams:
1. Each person shares two true things about themselves (outside of robotics) and one real goal they have for the season.
2. The group guesses which goal belongs to which person if you mix them up — or just uses it as a check-in.

Best used at the start of a season or when new members join.

---

## Activity 7 — Decision Jam (15 minutes)

**What it builds:** Handling disagreement, collaborative decision-making

**How it works:**
Present the team with a fake but realistic dilemma — something with no obviously right answer. Examples:
- "We have 3 weeks left and our robot can reliably do 4 missions. Do we spend time making those 4 missions more consistent, or try to add a 5th mission that we've never completed in practice?"
- "Two team members want to change the attachment design the day before the tournament. The rest don't. What do you do?"

The team must reach a decision together — with every member's voice heard — in 8 minutes.

**Debrief questions:**
- How did you handle the disagreement?
- Did everyone feel heard?
- Would you make the same decision differently if it were a real situation?

## Building habits, not performances

These activities only matter if they change how your team actually works together. A few things that help:

- **Run them regularly** — at the start of the season, monthly, and when the team hits a rough patch. Don't save them for the week before the tournament.
- **Debrief every time** — the questions at the end of each activity are where the learning happens.
- **Name the values when you see them** — when a kid includes a quieter teammate or handles a broken robot calmly, say it out loud: "that's gracious professionalism right there."
- **Model it as a coach** — how you react when the robot breaks, when the team disagrees, when something goes wrong on tournament day is the most powerful Core Values lesson you'll give all season.
