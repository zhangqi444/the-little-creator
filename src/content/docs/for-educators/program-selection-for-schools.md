Families and coaches often ask "Which program is right for my student?" — but the earlier question, asked at school and district level, is: "Which program should we offer?" Those two questions look similar but have different decision criteria.

A parent is optimising for fit for one child. A school administrator is optimising for sustainability across many students, seasons, and staff changes. This guide is for the latter.

---

## Quick-reference comparison

| Program | Typical grades | Weekly time commitment | Startup kit cost | Annual registration | Minimum space | Full-time staff role? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **FLL Discover / Explore** | Pre-K–Gr 6 | 1–2 h/week | $150–$300 | ~$200/team | Classroom table | No |
| **FLL Challenge** | Gr 4–8 | 2–4 h/week | $600–$1,000 | ~$225/team | Classroom table | No |
| **VEX IQ (VIQRC)** | Gr 3–8 | 2–4 h/week | $500–$800 | ~$150/team | Classroom table | No |
| **FTC** | Gr 7–12 | 4–8 h/week | $1,500–$3,000 | ~$295/team | Small room / workshop | Part-time coach |
| **VEX V5/VRC** | Gr 8–12 | 4–8 h/week | $1,200–$2,500 | ~$150/team | Small room / workshop | Part-time coach |
| **FRC** | Gr 9–12 | 8–20 h/week (build season) | $7,000–$12,000 | ~$5,500/team | Full workshop with tools | Dedicated mentor/coach |

Cost ranges are estimates as of 2025–26; check official FIRST and RECF sites for current registration fees and kit pricing. Kit cost can be reduced by purchasing used hardware — see [Used vs. New Hardware](/guides/choosing-equipment-used-vs-new/).

## The five decisions

### 1. Grade band

Start with who you are serving.

- **Elementary (K–6):** FLL Discover, FLL Explore, or VEX IQ. FLL Discover/Explore are softer introductions with LEGO DUPLO/SPIKE Essential. FLL Challenge and VEX IQ are the competitive entry points.
- **Middle school (Gr 6–8):** FLL Challenge, VEX IQ, or beginning FTC. FLL and VEX IQ are the most common middle-school programs. FTC can work for strong 7th/8th graders, especially if high school FTC is available as a next step.
- **High school (Gr 9–12):** FTC, FRC, VEX V5/VRC. FTC and VEX V5 are accessible for most schools. FRC has the highest commitment and cost but the largest community and alumni network.
- **College:** VEX U (VURC) only. University-level, student-run, PROS C++ or Python, two-robot format.

If your school spans multiple grade bands (K–8 or 6–12), consider running two programs that form a **natural pipeline**: VEX IQ → VEX V5, or FLL → FTC → FRC. Continuity reduces re-learning and keeps students engaged as they advance.

### 2. Budget

Be honest about the total cost of ownership, not just the startup kit.

**Annual recurring costs per team:**
- Registration fees (see table above)
- Replacement parts (motors, sensors, fasteners)
- Travel to qualifier + regional (estimate $300–$2,000 depending on program and distance)
- Software licenses (usually free; check current terms)

**One-time or infrequent costs:**
- Startup kit
- Power tools, shop consumables (FRC and FTC)
- Safety equipment (safety glasses; FRC requires more)
- Storage shelving and bins

**Funding paths to know before you decide:**
- FLL and FTC: FIRST Scholarship Program (grants available for Title I/under-resourced schools; see firstinspires.org/team-grants)
- VEX IQ and VEX V5: RECF Inspire Awards and partner grants (recf.org/grants)
- FRC: Season-based FIRST grants for new teams; Boeing, Qualcomm, and other corporate sponsors actively fund FRC
- All programs: see [Grants and Sponsorship Guide](/guides/grants-and-sponsorship-guide/)

If grants are available for the program you are considering, factor them in before ruling out cost.

### 3. Space and infrastructure

**Classroom-compatible programs (FLL, VEX IQ):**
- Need one to two 6-foot folding tables, power strips, and storage bins
- A regular classroom during a free period works
- No special ventilation, tool storage, or electrical upgrades needed

**Workshop-light programs (FTC, VEX V5):**
- Benefit from a dedicated room or shared STEM space with locking storage
- FTC needs power tools (drill, driver, hand tools)
- VEX V5 needs charging stations and a 12x12 ft practice field (can fold down)
- A science prep room or tech-ed classroom often works

**Workshop-required programs (FRC):**
- Needs a proper workshop: 400–600 sq ft minimum, 3-phase power or 20-amp circuits, metal fabrication capable (drill press, bandsaw, grinders at minimum)
- Field perimeter sections for practice matches (~25x54 ft full field; at minimum a 10x10 ft practice zone)
- If your school has a woodshop, auto shop, or fab lab you may already have what you need

**Space audit before committing:** Walk the available space. Check power access, fire egress (larger robots need to be moved), ventilation for battery charging, and whether the custodial schedule allows after-hours access.

### 4. Staffing

The single most common reason school programs fail is not budget or equipment — it is coach turnover.

Each program's staffing demand:

| Program | Coach profile | Time per week | Technical depth needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| FLL Discover / Explore | Any enthusiastic adult | 1–2 h | None — facilitation only |
| FLL Challenge | Teacher or interested parent | 2–4 h | Basic LEGO building / Scratch-level coding |
| VEX IQ | Teacher or interested parent | 2–4 h | Basic robotics concepts |
| FTC | Coach with some engineering background | 4–8 h | Java / Kotlin or Blocks; mechanical intuition |
| VEX V5 | Coach with STEM background | 4–8 h | C++ or Python; mechanical intuition |
| FRC | Dedicated mentor (often industry volunteer) | 10–20 h in build season | Fabrication + WPILib Java + project management |

**Mitigating the single-coach risk:**
- Always aim for two coaches per team — even if one is a shadow the first year
- Build a mentor list early (local engineering firms, universities, parents with STEM backgrounds)
- FIRST and VEX maintain volunteer/mentor matching resources; FIRST offers Tools and Technology, Business, and Sustaining Grant mentors
- See [Finding Mentors](/guides/finding-mentors/) for a practical approach

**Succession planning:** When hiring for a coaching role (or recruiting a volunteer), ask what happens when they leave. Document everything. The school-based programs with the most longevity treat coach documentation as infrastructure — see [Team Documentation System](/for-educators/team-documentation-system/).

### 5. Existing STEM capacity

What your school already has shapes which program is the lowest-friction launch.

| You have... | Best fit |
|---|---|
| Active LEGO club or Mindstorms/SPIKE history | FLL Challenge |
| VEX equipment already in storage | VEX IQ or VEX V5 (match hardware to grade) |
| CS elective with Java or Python | FTC or FRC |
| Woodshop, fabrication, or auto tech program | FRC (leverage existing tools and space) |
| Drone or electronics club | FTC or VEX V5 |
| Strong math/physics department | FRC (students and teachers already speak the language) |
| Nothing yet | FLL Challenge or VEX IQ for K–8; FTC for high school |

Launching a program that builds on existing infrastructure reduces the coaching burden, increases student buy-in, and makes the first season more likely to succeed.

## Program-specific considerations

### FLL (FIRST LEGO League)

Three divisions (Discover, Explore, Challenge) cover ages 4–16, making it the only program with elementary school coverage built in. The LEGO SPIKE hardware reduces cost relative to VEX or FTC. Judging covers Core Values and Innovation Project as well as robot design — students who are not primarily builders still get meaningful competition experience.

Considerations:
- Season resets every August (new game, new challenge); requires annual coach reorientation
- FLL Challenge requires teams to attend a qualifier — factor in travel and chaperone logistics
- FLL Challenge has a 10-student maximum per team; plan roster management in advance
- Future Edition (BIOGLOW 2026-27) introduces updated divisions — see [What is FLL?](/getting-started/what-is-fll/)

### VEX IQ (VIQRC)

Natural companion to FLL for elementary/middle schools. VEX IQ's collaborative Teamwork format (two alliances of two teams each) is intentionally cooperative and low-pressure for new competitors. The snap-fit plastic parts reduce injury risk compared to metal construction kits.

Considerations:
- VEX IQ and VEX V5 hardware is incompatible — do not expect to share parts across programs
- Engineering Notebook is judged (good for STEM skills; adds coach overhead)
- RobotEvents (robotevents.com) is the registration platform for all VEX programs

### FTC

High school level but accessible for strong 8th graders. FTC uses commercially available hardware (REV, goBILDA) and Java or Blocks programming. The Engineering Portfolio (the FTC equivalent of an engineering notebook) is a structured document — a significant time investment, but it teaches real documentation practice. The FTC Game Manual 0 is an exceptional free resource for new coaches.

Considerations:
- FTC teams can be 2–15 students; most effective teams run 8–12
- FTC has League play in some regions — teams play multiple low-stakes matches across the semester before a qualifier, reducing the "one tournament and you're out" pressure
- FTC hardware repair costs can accumulate across a season; budget 15–20% of kit cost for replacement parts

### FRC

The most demanding program in cost, space, and time — but also the most visible, the most alumni-connected, and the most directly aligned with STEM career exploration. The 6-week build season (January–March) is intense. The rest of the year (September–December and post-season) is planning, fundraising, outreach, and Skills work.

Considerations:
- FRC is genuinely difficult to launch without an industry mentor; recruit one before registering
- FRC registration opens in late summer; the team kit ships after Kickoff (early January); plan 12–18 months ahead
- FRC regional vs. district model affects cost and qualification path significantly — confirm which system your region uses
- FRC is eligible for the largest FIRST grants; apply before season start

### VEX V5 / VRC

Often described as the FTC equivalent in the VEX ecosystem. Slightly lower registration cost than FTC; C++ or Python vs. Java; VEX-specific hardware ecosystem. VRC has Skills Challenge competitions in addition to standard matches, giving smaller or newer teams an additional advancement path.

Considerations:
- VEX V5 and VEX U are closely related; a VEX V5 team at a university/college can transition to VEX U
- PROS (the open-source VEX firmware) is used at higher levels — see [VEX V5 Programming Basics](/guides/vex-v5-programming-basics/) for the landscape
- VRC Skills-only Worlds qualification path means teams with strong autonomous code can reach Worlds without winning alliance matches

## Multi-program considerations

Running more than one program at the same school is possible — and common in K–12 schools. Some patterns that work:

**Feeder pipeline:** VEX IQ in middle school → VEX V5 in high school, or FLL in grades 4–8 → FTC in grades 9–12. Students arrive knowing how to build and compete; coaches build on existing culture. This is the single highest-leverage structural decision for long-term program health.

**Parallel tracks:** Some large high schools run both FTC and FRC simultaneously. This usually only works with a full-time STEM teacher or significant volunteer mentor capacity. Do not launch both in year one.

**Hardware separation:** FLL, VEX IQ, FTC, FRC, and VEX V5 all use different hardware. There is no shared-kit efficiency. Budget and store as independent programs.

**Shared coaching culture:** Even if hardware differs, practices like engineering notebook discipline, drive team training, and season retrospectives transfer across programs. A coach who has run FLL can apply the same coaching philosophy to FTC.

## Decision framework

Answer these five questions:

1. **What grade band is your primary audience?** Eliminates options outside age range.
2. **What is your realistic annual budget, including travel?** Eliminates options exceeding budget without grant paths.
3. **Do you have or can you recruit a coach with the right technical depth?** Eliminates options without a credible coach candidate.
4. **Does your space support the program?** Eliminates options requiring infrastructure you do not have.
5. **Does this program connect to existing STEM capacity?** Prefer the option with the least-friction launch.

If more than one program passes all five filters, choose the one with the strongest natural feeder pipeline from or to other programs at your school. If none pass, start with grants and staffing before committing.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it happens | What to do instead |
|---|---|---|
| Registering before securing a coach | Enthusiasm before logistics | Identify at least two coaches before paying registration |
| Choosing FRC for a middle school | Name recognition | FRC is high-school only; FLL or VEX IQ is the right tier |
| Underestimating travel cost | Focus on kit cost | Budget qualifier + regional travel before committing |
| Launching two programs in year one | Ambition | Pick one, do it well, then expand in year two or three |
| Assuming IT will approve the software | Skipping infrastructure check | Test device provisioning (Android Studio, WPILib, VEXcode) against school network policy before season starts |
| No succession plan | First-year coach is the program | Document everything from day one; see [Team Documentation System](/for-educators/team-documentation-system/) |

## Getting started

1. **Narrow to one or two candidate programs** using the decision framework above.
2. **Request sample budgets** — contact your regional Program Delivery Partner (PDO) for FLL or FIRST for FRC/FTC. For VEX, contact RECF (recf.org).
3. **Attend a competition as an observer** before committing. Most events welcome walk-in observers; it is the fastest way to understand what you are signing up for.
4. **Talk to a coach running the program nearby** — Regional program coordinators can connect you with local teams. An hour with a second-year coach is worth more than any document.
5. **Apply for grants before registration** — Many FIRST and RECF grants have rolling deadlines. Do not leave funding on the table because you registered first and asked later.
