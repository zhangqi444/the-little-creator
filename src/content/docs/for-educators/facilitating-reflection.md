Reflection is the mechanism that turns experience into learning. A team that builds, competes, and moves on will repeat the same mistakes season after season. A team that pauses regularly to ask "what are we noticing?" turns each session and competition into deliberate practice.

This guide covers how to run structured reflection sessions after regular practice and after competition. It complements [Questioning Techniques](/for-educators/questioning-techniques/) (guiding students through in-the-moment problem-solving), [Coaching Communication](/for-educators/coaching-communication/) (feedback frameworks for mistakes and setbacks), and the [Season Retrospective guide](/guides/season-retrospective/) (end-of-season structured review).

---

## Why structured reflection beats informal debrief

Most teams already debrief — coaches ask "how did that go?" on the way to the car. The problem with informal debrief is:

- **It gets captured by loudest voices.** Students with strong opinions dominate; quieter students never speak.
- **It defaults to outcomes, not processes.** "We scored 200 points" tells you nothing about why.
- **It's forgettable.** Impressions fade before the next practice.
- **It rarely produces action.** Without a written record, insights die in the car park.

Structured reflection is different in four ways: **equal participation**, **process focus**, **written output**, and **forward action**. It takes 10–15 minutes at the end of a practice or 20–30 minutes after a competition, and that investment pays dividends in team learning speed.

---

## Core reflection principles

**Separate reflection from evaluation.** Reflection is about understanding; evaluation is about scoring. Mix them and students perform for the coach rather than thinking clearly.

**Make it safe to say "I don't know" and "I got it wrong."** Psychological safety is a prerequisite. If students fear judgment, reflection becomes a performance. See [Building an Inclusive Team Culture](/for-educators/inclusive-team-culture/) for foundation work.

**Focus on process, not outcome.** "We lost" is an outcome. "We didn't finish our autonomous because we hadn't tested the gyro since recalibrating" is a process observation. Outcomes are often outside a team's control; process is always inside it.

**Write it down.** Verbal-only reflection evaporates. A running log of reflection insights (your session log or engineering notebook) creates a searchable knowledge base by season end.

## The core four-question format

Use this format for any reflection — after a regular practice, after a scrimmage, after a qualifier:

| # | Question | Purpose |
|---|----------|---------|
| 1 | **What did we set out to do today?** | Anchors to intention, not just what happened |
| 2 | **What actually happened?** | Factual observation, not evaluation |
| 3 | **What made the difference?** | Causal analysis — why the gap (or alignment) between intention and outcome |
| 4 | **What will we do differently next time?** | Forward action — must produce at least one concrete change |

Run this in 10 minutes. Question 4 must end with a written action item assigned to a person with a due date. If it doesn't, the reflection produced no change.

## Post-practice reflection (10–15 minutes)

**When:** Last 10–15 minutes of the session, before dismissal.

**Format:** Seated circle. Coach facilitates. One student takes notes (rotate the role).

**Structure:**

1. **Check-in round** (2 min): Each student rates the session 1–5 on a single word or phrase — one sentence each, no discussion yet. This surfaces outliers before the main conversation.

2. **Four-question format** (8 min): Work through the four questions as a group. Coach asks; students answer. Resist the urge to fill silence — give 5–10 seconds before redirecting.

3. **Action close** (2 min): Confirm what's different next session. Who owns it? Written in the session log before the team leaves.

**Coach facilitation tips:**

- Don't start with "how did you feel?" — it invites vague answers. Start with "what did we set out to do?"
- If the team is stuck on question 3 (what made the difference), try: "If we ran this session again tomorrow, what's the one thing we'd change?" or "What's the one thing that got in our way?"
- If one student dominates, use structured turn-taking: "I want to hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet."

## Post-competition reflection (20–30 minutes)

Competition days are emotionally charged. Run the full reflection before energy disperses — ideally within 2 hours of the last match, not on the drive home a week later.

**Timing by program:**

| Program | Best timing |
|---------|-------------|
| FLL | During lunch break or immediately after awards |
| FTC / VEX IQ / V5 | After alliance selection results are known; final reflection after awards |
| FRC | Drive home or first Monday practice after event |
| VEX U | Between Day 1 and Day 2 (if two-day event); after final alliance match |

**Competition reflection format:**

1. **Decompress round** (3 min): One word or phrase to describe the day — emotional acknowledgement before analysis. Don't skip this; suppressing emotion prevents clear thinking.

2. **Score and results factual summary** (3 min): What did we do? What were our scores? Did we advance? Facts only — no evaluation yet.

3. **Four-question format focused on three events** (15 min): Pick three specific moments from the day — one that went well, one that didn't, one that surprised you. Run the four questions for each. This is more generative than a global "how did the day go?"

4. **Three action items** (5 min): Exactly three changes for the next practice or event. More than three won't get done. Written, assigned, dated.

5. **One-word close** (2 min): Each student gives one word for how they want to feel at the next event. Ends on forward energy, not the day's outcome.

## Reflection formats by program

| Program | Cadence | Format notes |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| **FLL** | Every practice + every tournament | 10 min post-practice; align with Core Values rubric — judges may ask about team process |
| **FTC** | Every practice + every event (league + qualifier + state) | Post-event reflection feeds Engineering Portfolio process section directly |
| **FRC** | Every major build session + every event; also after scouting debrief | 15 min post-session; integrate with Impact Award story (team growth arc is award content) |
| **VEX IQ** | Every practice + every tournament | Short format (8 min) suits younger students; focus on collaboration and GP as well as robot |
| **VEX V5** | Every practice session + every Skills/tournament day | Include autonomous vs. TeleOp separate reflection at competitions |
| **VEX U** | Every robot session (Robot A and Robot B) + every event | Run reflection with both robot teams together after events — coordination gaps surface here |

## Adapting for different age groups

**Ages 8–11 (FLL / VEX IQ beginners):**
- Keep it to two questions: "What went well?" and "What would we change?"
- Use physical anchors (thumbs up/sideways/down) before verbal responses
- Coach models honest answers first to normalise it: "I noticed we ran out of time on attachment three — that was my fault for not watching the clock."
- 8 minutes maximum

**Ages 12–14 (FLL seniors / VEX IQ / FTC beginners):**
- Full four-question format works well
- Add student facilitation rotation — coach observes, doesn't lead
- Students take notes; review notes at next session start
- 12 minutes

**Ages 15–18 (FTC / VEX V5 / FRC):**
- Include causal analysis depth — "what made the difference" should reach system causes, not just immediate triggers
- Students can use a shared doc or whiteboard; coach optional
- Competition debriefs can include data review (match scores, autonomous success rate)
- 15–20 minutes

**Collegiate (VEX U):**
- Full structured format with written output expected
- Frame as engineering process: reflection is how mature engineers improve systems
- Include cross-team debrief (Robot A and Robot B teams)
- 25–30 minutes

## Using reflection output in documentation

Reflection produces content your team needs anyway:

**Engineering notebook:** Post-practice reflections map directly to engineering notebook entries. "What we tried → what happened → what we changed" is the engineering cycle. A reflection that stays verbal and isn't captured loses this directly.

**FTC Engineering Portfolio:** Process improvement sections require evidence of iteration. Your competition reflections are that evidence — if written.

**FRC Impact Award:** Team growth narrative requires a story arc. Your reflection log is the raw material — three-action-items-per-event, accumulated over a season, becomes the quantitative evidence of progress.

**VEX judging (notebook review):** VIQRC and V5RC judges look for reflection language in notebooks ("we discovered that… so we changed…"). If your team reflects verbally and records it, this is easy. If they don't, judges see no learning arc.

## Running reflections students don't want to do

Some teams resist reflection. Common patterns and responses:

| Resistance | What's actually happening | Response |
|------------|--------------------------|----------|
| "We're tired and just want to go home" | Legitimate — timing is wrong | Move reflection to start of next session ("let's start by reviewing last time") |
| Students give one-word answers | Psychological safety issue or unfamiliarity | Normalise it — share your own one-sentence genuine answer first; wait longer for silence |
| One or two students dominate | Default team dynamics | Structured turn-taking or written round (sticky notes before discussion) |
| "There's nothing to reflect on, it was fine" | Outcome thinking, not process | Reframe: "Even great practices have one thing we'd do differently. What's ours?" |
| Students are upset about results | Emotion blocks analysis | Decompress round first; delay analysis by 10 minutes if needed |

## Signs your reflection practice is working

Over time, a team with healthy reflection habits shows:

- Students bring improvement ideas to practice without being asked
- Problem diagnosis happens faster — students know how to work through "what made the difference"
- Engineering notebook entries shift from "we did X" to "we tried X, got Y, changed Z"
- Competition results are attributed to process ("our autonomous was inconsistent because we hadn't run 10 test runs before today") not luck or judges
- Students can describe their team's improvement arc across the season

## Quick-start checklist

**First session:**
- [ ] Introduce the four-question format with a 10-minute practice reflection
- [ ] Assign note-taking role to one student (rotate weekly)
- [ ] Write at least one action item before the team leaves

**First competition:**
- [ ] Schedule 25 minutes for post-event reflection (block on the day plan)
- [ ] Run decompress round before analysis
- [ ] End with exactly three action items, written and assigned

**By mid-season:**
- [ ] Students are facilitating reflection without coach prompting
- [ ] Reflection notes are stored in engineering notebook or session log
- [ ] At least one reflection insight has appeared in notebook or Portfolio
