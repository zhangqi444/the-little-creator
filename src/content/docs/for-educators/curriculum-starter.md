An 8-week starter curriculum for a brand-new FLL classroom or club. Designed for a once-a-week, 90-minute session. Adapt to your time and group — this is a starting point, not a prescription.

## Assumptions

- **Audience:** Kids ages 9–12, no prior robotics experience
- **Group size:** 6–10 kids with one or two adult coaches/teachers
- **Hardware:** One LEGO SPIKE Prime kit per 2–3 kids
- **Software:** LEGO SPIKE App installed on each laptop or iPad before week 1
- **Time:** 8 sessions of 90 minutes each, once per week
- **Outcome:** By week 8, every kid has built and programmed a robot to complete one mission on the season's mat

If your context is different (more time, fewer kits, older kids, after-school vs. classroom), use this as a skeleton and adjust.

## Week-by-week

### Week 1 — Welcome & hardware tour

**Objective:** Kids can identify the SPIKE Prime hub, motors, and sensors, and turn one on.

- Welcome, introductions, group norms
- Unbox kits together (the unboxing is part of the magic — don't pre-build)
- Hardware tour: hub, motors, color sensor, distance sensor, ultrasonic, force sensor
- First "win": connect a motor, run a 2-second program from the SPIKE App
- Send home: nothing — let week 1 be light

### Week 2 — Driving forward, turning

**Objective:** Kids can program a robot to drive a specified distance and turn.

- Build the basic SPIKE Prime driving base (official instructions in the app)
- Block-based programming: motor pair, drive forward, turn
- Mini-challenge: drive to a piece of tape on the floor, turn 90°, drive back
- Discussion: why do different kids' robots end up in different places?

### Week 3 — Sensors: distance & color

**Objective:** Kids can program a robot to react to a sensor reading.

- Demo: robot stops at a wall using the distance sensor
- Demo: robot reacts to a colored line
- Hands-on: each pair makes their robot stop before hitting an obstacle
- Discussion: what's the difference between time-based and sensor-based programs?

### Week 4 — Looking at the season's challenge

**Objective:** Kids understand the season's theme, missions, and core values.

- Watch the season reveal video together (from the official FLL channel)
- Open the season Challenge document and tour it together
- Pick one or two simple missions to attempt — don't try to do them all
- Group decision: which mission do we tackle first?

### Week 5 — Mission attempt #1: build

**Objective:** Kids can design a robot attachment for a chosen mission.

- Look at the mission model on the mat
- Sketch ideas on paper before building
- Build attachment iterations — most attempts will fail, that's the point
- End-of-session: each pair demos their current attachment idea

### Week 6 — Mission attempt #1: program

**Objective:** Kids can program their robot to complete (or attempt) the chosen mission.

- Pseudocode the mission steps on a whiteboard before coding
- Program in pairs, one driver one navigator
- Test on the mat repeatedly — expect 10+ runs to get one clean attempt
- Coaches: ask "what did you change?" not "did it work?"

### Week 7 — Core Values & teamwork

**Objective:** Kids can articulate the FLL Core Values and identify how their team has demonstrated them.

- Review the Core Values rubric (from the official Challenge document)
- Each kid shares a moment when their team demonstrated one Core Value
- Begin Innovation Project brainstorming (this is its own multi-week effort — week 7 is just the kickoff)
- Discussion: judges score Core Values too, not just the robot

### Week 8 — Practice run & reflection

**Objective:** Kids can run their robot through a full match-style attempt and reflect on the season so far.

- Set up the mat for a 2.5-minute timed match
- Each pair runs their robot, with one judge (coach) timing
- Group reflection: what went well, what to work on, what's next
- Decide together: tackle another mission next? Refine this one? Start the Innovation Project?

## What this curriculum doesn't cover

- **The Innovation Project** — needs its own multi-week sequence; this curriculum starts the kickoff in week 7 but doesn't finish it
- **Tournament prep** — assumes you'll continue past week 8 with mission iteration and Core Values practice
- **Engineering notebook discipline** — gets harder once you're competing; introduce earlier if your kids will tolerate it
- **Strategy across all missions** — a deeper season covers picking high-value missions and optimizing run order; that's a season-2 conversation

## Adapting

- **Shorter sessions (45 minutes):** Spread each week's content across two sessions. The hardware tour alone can be a full session.
- **Younger kids (8–9):** Spend two weeks each on weeks 2 and 3. Don't rush sensors.
- **Older kids (12+):** Compress weeks 1–3 into two sessions. Move to Python earlier if motivation supports it.
- **Less hardware:** With one kit per 4 kids, expect more downtime; have an off-robot activity (sketching, pseudocoding, watching past season reveals) for half the group at a time.
