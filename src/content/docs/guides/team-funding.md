Running an FLL team costs money — registration fees, the Challenge Set, travel to tournaments. Most families don't realize that funding is available and that teams regularly cover most or all of their costs through grants and local sponsors. This guide covers where to look and how to ask.

:::tip[Typical first-year costs]
- FIRST national registration: ~$225 (US, 2024–25 season — confirm at [my.firstinspires.org](https://my.firstinspires.org))
- Regional/PDO registration: $50–$200 depending on your state
- LEGO Education SPIKE Prime set: ~$350 new (often reused season to season)
- FLL Challenge Set (mat + mission models): ~$75–$125
- Tournament travel: varies widely — could be $0 (local) to $500+ (nationals)

**Total first year: roughly $700–$1,000 for a new team with no existing equipment.** Subsequent years drop significantly since SPIKE Prime is reused.
:::

## Step 1 — Check if your PDO has grants

Many regional Program Delivery Organizations (PDOs) offer team grants, especially for first-year teams or teams in underserved communities. These often cover registration fees and sometimes equipment.

- **FIRST Washington:** has a grant program — check [firstwa.org](https://firstwa.org) under "Grants" or "Scholarships"
- **FIRST in Texas:** posts grant info at [firstintx.com/team-grants/](https://firstintx.com/team-grants/)
- **Other PDOs:** check your PDO's website under "grants," "scholarships," or "financial aid"

Use [FIRST's partner finder](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-local-support) to find your PDO if you don't know it.

---

## Step 2 — FIRST national grant programs

FIRST headquarters runs grant and scholarship programs year-round. The most relevant for new teams:

- **FIRST Grants database:** [firstinspires.org/community/grants](https://www.firstinspires.org/community/grants) — searchable by program and region
- **Dean's List and other recognition programs** sometimes carry scholarship components for advancing teams
- Look for "Rookie Grant" or "New Team Grant" listings — these target teams in their first 1–2 seasons

Grant windows open and close seasonally. Check during late spring (April–June) for the following season's opportunities.

## Step 3 — Local sponsors

Local businesses are often the fastest path to funding — a single sponsor can cover your whole season. The key is asking the right businesses in the right way.

**Best targets:**
- Engineering and tech companies (even small ones) — they care about STEM pipelines
- Law firms and accounting firms — community involvement is part of their culture
- Local manufacturers — robotics resonates with their industry
- Credit unions and community banks — many have community grant budgets
- Your kids' parents' employers — ask parents if their company has a matching program or community grant

**What to ask for:**
- $200–$500 covers registration + Challenge Set for most teams
- Don't ask for a vague "donation" — give a specific dollar amount tied to a specific cost
- Offer something back: logo on team shirt, mention in tournament program, a visit to see the kids present

**Simple pitch email template:**

> Subject: Sponsoring a local kids' robotics team — [Team name], [City]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm coaching a FIRST LEGO League robotics team of [N] kids ages [X]–[X] in [City]. We compete in [month] at [venue/region].
>
> We're looking for a sponsor to help cover our registration and equipment costs (~$[amount]). In return, we'd be happy to put your logo on our team shirts and mention your support at our tournament.
>
> If you'd like to learn more or see what we do, I'd be glad to share a short video of the kids at work. Just reply here and I'll send it over.
>
> Thank you,
> [Your name]

Keep it short. Most sponsors decide in 60 seconds.

## Step 4 — Fundraising (if you need to fill gaps)

If grants and sponsors don't cover everything, small fundraisers can close the gap without a lot of effort.

**Low-effort options:**
- **Bake sale or concession stand at a local event** — $150–$300 for a few hours
- **Amazon Smile / AmazonSmile alternatives** — set up a wishlist for equipment; some families prefer buying a specific item over donating cash
- **GoFundMe or similar** — works best when the team has a specific goal ("we need $300 for our Challenge Set") and a short video of the kids. Vague campaigns don't convert.
- **Car wash** — classic for a reason; $5–$10 per car, a few hours = $200+
- **Restaurant fundraiser nights** — many chains (Chipotle, Panda Express, etc.) do community fundraiser nights where they donate 20–30% of sales to your group

**What to avoid:**
- Selling overpriced candy door-to-door — low margin, high effort, not worth it
- Raffles without checking local laws — some states require a permit

## Tracking and transparency

Keep a simple budget spreadsheet from day one. Know:
- What you've raised
- What you've spent
- What's left

If you receive a grant, most require a brief report after the season. Track purchases so you can do this in 15 minutes, not 3 hours.
