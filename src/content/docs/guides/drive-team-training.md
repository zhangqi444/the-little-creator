The drive team is your robot's operating system on competition day. A well-practised drive team can extract 20–30% more performance from an average robot. A poorly prepared one can cost an excellent robot half its scoring potential. This guide covers how to build and train a drive team across all competitive programs: FTC, FRC, VEX IQ, VEX V5/VRC, and VEX U.

FLL is intentionally excluded: FLL robot missions are pre-programmed and run autonomously — there is no live drive team operating the robot during a run.

---

## Drive team roles by program

Drive teams are not identical across programs. Know who does what before you start training.

### FTC — two operators, one coach (optional)

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| Driver | Controls robot movement and primary mechanism |
| Operator | Controls secondary mechanisms (intake, lift, claw) |
| Coach | Provides strategic guidance from alliance station; optional but recommended at higher levels |

FTC matches are 2.5 minutes: 30 seconds Autonomous + 2 minutes Driver-Controlled. The same two students typically execute both.

### FRC — driver, operator, human player, coach

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| Driver | Controls robot movement; typically joystick/gamepad |
| Operator | Controls mechanisms: shooter, intake, climber, etc. |
| Human Player | Interacts with game pieces at the human player station (varies by game) |
| Drive Coach | Communicates strategy; is the only driver-station member allowed to communicate with alliance partners during a match |

FRC drive teams are strictly regulated: only the four designated roles may be in the alliance station during a match. The drive coach is responsible for reading the field and relaying strategic adjustments.

### VEX IQ / VIQRC — driver and operator

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| Driver | Controls robot movement |
| Operator | Controls secondary mechanisms |

VIQRC Teamwork Challenge matches involve **two robots and two teams sharing a field cooperatively**. Each team controls its own robot. There is no opposing alliance — both drive teams need to coordinate with each other to maximise the shared score.

VIQRC Driving Skills matches are solo: one driver, one operator, one robot, 60 seconds.

For younger students (especially in the 8–10 age range), a coach may assist on strategy, but the students operate the robot.

### VEX V5 / VRC — driver and operator, optional coach

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| Driver | Controls drive base movement |
| Operator | Controls scoring mechanisms (intake, lift, claw, etc.) |
| Drive Coach | Optional third member; may not touch controls; provides strategic guidance |

VRC matches: 15-second Autonomous Period + 1 minute 45 seconds Driver Controlled Period.

### VEX U / VURC — two robots, two drivers, two operators, optional lead coach

VURC fields **two robots per alliance** operated simultaneously by the same alliance. A typical VEX U drive team is:

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| Robot 1 Driver | Controls 15-inch (or assigned) robot drive base |
| Robot 1 Operator | Controls Robot 1's mechanisms |
| Robot 2 Driver | Controls 24-inch (or assigned) robot drive base |
| Robot 2 Operator | Controls Robot 2's mechanisms |
| Lead Coach | Coordinates both sub-teams; watches the whole field; reads opposing alliance |

VURC team members **must cross-train** — if either driver or operator is unavailable on competition day, others must be able to step in.

## Why driver selection matters

The best build team member is not automatically the best driver. Driver selection should be deliberate.

**What makes a good driver:**
- Spatial awareness — can track multiple objects simultaneously on a moving field
- Composure under pressure — performance does not degrade with audience or time pressure
- Mechanical empathy — can sense robot strain and avoid mechanism damage
- Coachability — responds to real-time guidance without defensiveness
- Reliability — shows up to every practice, not just the fun ones

**What does NOT predict good driving:**
- Programming ability (unrelated skill set)
- Building seniority (the person who designed the robot is often attached to it, not objective about it)
- Loudness in team discussions

**Selection process:** Run time trials or standard scoring runs and pick on performance, not politics. The team member who consistently executes best drives.

## The practice regime

### Start with controls orientation (Week 1)

Before any competitive driving, every drive team member should:

1. Label and configure their gamepad/controller layout in code. Do not rely on memory.
2. Drive the robot in an empty space — turn left, right, full speed, slow speed, stop.
3. Activate each mechanism in isolation: intake on/off, lift to preset positions, claw open/close.
4. Understand what happens when a mechanism fails mid-match (fallback behaviour).

**For FRC:** Verify the robot passes inspection criteria before investing heavily in driver training. An inspection fail wastes practice time.

### Isolation drills (Weeks 2–3)

Break the match into components. Practise each in isolation before combining them.

**Example isolation drills:**

| Drill | What it trains |
|---|---|
| Game-piece pickup loop | Intake targeting and pickup consistency |
| Navigation around field zones | Spatial precision, avoiding penalties |
| Scoring cycle from pickup to deposit | Full cycle muscle memory |
| Endgame / parking sequence | Time pressure, late-match calm |
| Autonomous verification | Consistency check; run 10 times in a row |

For each drill: **record a baseline**, then track improvement over sessions. "We are hitting the pickup 7/10 times" is more actionable than "we have been practising a lot."

### Combined run practice (Weeks 3–6)

Once isolation drills are solid, run full simulated matches:

1. Set a match timer on the field.
2. Use actual game pieces if available.
3. Score the run: count points using official scoring rules.
4. Debrief immediately after (5 minutes max, while the run is fresh).

**Target:** By four weeks before competition, full runs should be at or above your expected match score.

### Pressure simulation (Week before competition)

In the final week, simulate competition conditions:

- Audience present (other team members, coaches, parents watching)
- Clock visible and counting down
- Someone calling out time: "1 minute left!", "30 seconds!"
- Robot starts from legal starting position only
- No retries within a run

Many drive teams perform well in empty gym practice and poorly under audience pressure. Exposure to simulated pressure in a safe environment is the cure.

## Match communication protocols

Drive team communication is a skill, not just a habit. Miscommunication during a match costs more points than most mechanical failures.

### The four communication principles

1. **Short calls only.** During a match, there is no time for explanation. "Drop it, go left" beats "I think the intake is stuck, maybe try going back to reset it."
2. **One voice at a time.** Only the coach talks strategy; drivers respond with action, not discussion.
3. **Acknowledge calls.** Drivers confirm they heard: a brief "got it" or a raised hand. Silence means unknown.
4. **No blame during the match.** Debriefs are for analysis. Mid-match criticism breaks focus.

### Call vocabulary

Establish a shared vocabulary before competition. Examples:

| Call | Meaning |
|---|---|
| "Reset" | Return to starting position or safe zone |
| "Cycle" | Begin a scoring cycle from current position |
| "Park!" | Abandon current action, move to endgame position |
| "Hold" | Stop all movement, assess |
| "Dump it" | Score whatever you have, do not wait for ideal position |
| "Your side" | The driver's own designated field zone |

Write your team's call vocabulary down. Practise using it in runs.

### Alliance communication (FTC, FRC, VRC, VURC)

Before each match, the drive coach or a designated team member should speak with the alliance partner's drive team for 2–3 minutes:

- Agree on who covers which field zones
- Agree on who does what in Autonomous (FRC: which starting positions; FTC: which paths)
- Agree on endgame priority order if time is tight
- Exchange signals: what to do if one robot is stuck

In FRC, the drive coach is the **only person** who may communicate with the alliance partner's station during a match. Ensure your coach knows this rule.

## Program-specific tips

### FTC

- The Autonomous to TeleOp transition matters. The robot state at the end of Autonomous (position, mechanism state) determines TeleOp starting efficiency. Practise the handoff deliberately.
- FTC scoring often rewards **consistent cycle completion** more than occasional peak scores. Train for consistency first, speed second.
- Endgame is frequently the highest-stakes 30 seconds. Practise endgame in isolation until it is reliable before integrating into full runs.

### FRC

- FRC drive coaches are on their feet for 2.5 minutes, reading a complex field. This is its own skill. Train the coach separately: they should be able to estimate real-time match score and communicate adjustments quickly.
- Autonomous consistency at FRC is worth drilling separately at high repetition. A reliable 15-second autonomous run is a free point contribution every match.
- For swerve drive robots: swerve has a steeper driver learning curve than tank. Budget at least two extra weeks for a rookie driver on swerve.

### VEX IQ / VIQRC

- VIQRC Teamwork Challenge requires coordinating with a partner team you may have never practised with. Train your drive team to **adapt on the fly**: if a partner robot is slower, adjust your path; if they cannot reach a scoring zone, cover it.
- Practise **at field element scale** — approximate if you do not have a full VIQRC field. Spatial misjudgement is the most common rookie mistake.
- For younger students: focus on one mechanism per session. Do not try to master everything at once.

### VEX V5 / VRC

- The 15-second Autonomous Period is high leverage. A consistent autonomous that scores the Autonomous Win Point (AWP) is worth more than its direct point value — it affects alliance selection perception and rankings.
- VRC drive team practice often suffers from **mechanism changes late in the season** resetting driver muscle memory. Freeze major mechanisms at least two weeks before your first qualifier.
- Driver and operator roles in VRC are distinct enough that they need separate drill regimes. Do not conflate them in practice.

### VEX U / VURC

- Two robots operating simultaneously means **collision risk is real**. Your first combined practice should focus on spatial separation: agreeing which robot covers which field zone and not crossing paths.
- Assign Robot 1 and Robot 2 consistent field roles (for example, Robot 1 handles the near zone; Robot 2 handles the far zone). Switching roles mid-season resets all muscle memory.
- Cross-training is mandatory. Every team member should be able to drive either robot. This avoids single-point-of-failure risks on competition day.

## Knowing when to improve vs. freeze

The temptation to "just add one more feature" to the robot late in the season is the most common drive team problem.

**Freeze rules:**
- No mechanism changes in the final two weeks before a qualifier. Only repairs and tuning.
- No drive code changes in the final week (bug fixes excepted).
- No new drive team members in the final week.

A robot the team **knows perfectly** outperforms a better robot the team is still learning. Stability beats novelty.

## Common drive team mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Better approach |
|---|---|---|
| Starting driver practice too late (last 2 weeks) | Insufficient muscle memory under pressure | Begin driver practice when first full robot is functional |
| Selecting the driver by seniority, not performance | Drive quality suffers; resentment if decision was unfair | Use time trials or scored practice runs to decide |
| Skipping endgame practice | Endgame failure is often the biggest scoring differential | Drill endgame in isolation until it is automatic |
| No coach role defined | Mid-match strategy guidance is absent | Assign a coach even if optional; brief them separately |
| Practising without a timer | Drives go over time; team does not feel clock pressure | Use a visible match timer from day one |
| No alliance communication before the match | Robots interfere with each other in autonomous or field zones | Make pre-match alliance briefing a non-negotiable habit |
| Changing the robot the day before competition | Resets driver muscle memory at worst possible time | Enforce a hard freeze two weeks out |
| All practice in quiet room only | Performance degrades under audience pressure | Simulate audience and noise in final week practice |

## Season-long training plan

A rough template across a 10–12 week build-to-competition cycle:

| Week | Focus |
|---|---|
| 1 | Controls orientation; learn button layout; basic navigation |
| 2 | Isolation drills: intake, scoring, navigation |
| 3 | Combined drills: two to three mechanism sequences |
| 4 | First full timed runs; baseline score recorded |
| 5–6 | Improve specific weak points from baseline runs |
| 7–8 | Alliance communication protocols; run with mock partner |
| 9 | Pressure simulation; mock match with audience |
| 10 | Robot freeze; refinement only; mental preparation |
| Competition day | Warm-up run only; stick with what you know |

Adjust the timeline for your program. FRC's 6-week build season compresses Weeks 1–4 significantly.
