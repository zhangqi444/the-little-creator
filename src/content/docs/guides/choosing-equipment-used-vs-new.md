One of the most common budget questions for new robotics families is: "Can we buy this used?" Often, yes — but some items are risky to buy secondhand, and a few are nearly worthless once a season ends.

This guide walks through each program and each category of hardware, so you can spend confidently and avoid the expensive mistakes that trip up first-year teams.

For full cost ranges and a budget spreadsheet template, see [Season Budget Template — All Six Programs](/guides/budget-template/). For funding strategies, see [Team Funding](/guides/team-funding/).

---

## The general rule

> **Structural parts and field elements: safe used. Electronics: verify carefully. Batteries: buy new.**

Most robotics gear holds up well mechanically. The failure modes for used hardware cluster around batteries (capacity degrades silently), motors (worn brushes or stripped gears), and seasonal field elements (the game mat or mission models for last year's season have no value for this year's event).

## FLL Challenge

### Buy new
- **Current-season Challenge Set** (game mat + mission models) — must match the active season; last year's set has no competition value. These sell out; order by September.
- **Replacement hub battery** — SPIKE Prime hub batteries degrade with charge cycles. A used battery with unknown history can leave you dead mid-practice.

### Safe to buy used
- **LEGO Education SPIKE Prime Set** — the hardware (hub, motors, sensors, LEGO elements) does not wear out quickly with typical FLL use. Buy from another team that has upgraded or disbanded. Check that the hub charges, all ports work, and the colour sensor is not cracked.
- **Extra LEGO Technic elements** — BrickLink and LEGO Pick-a-Brick are standard sources; condition rarely matters for structural pieces.
- **Carrying cases, tables, and team supplies** — no electronics involved; condition is visible.

### What to check before buying a used SPIKE Prime set
1. Plug the hub in — does it charge and show the correct battery indicator?
2. Download the SPIKE App and connect — does the hub pair via Bluetooth and USB?
3. Test each motor port with a motor attached — do all six ports respond in the app?
4. Check the colour sensor lens for cracks — a scratched lens reads colours inconsistently.
5. Ask how old the battery is and how many seasons it has been through. Two or more seasons of heavy use is a yellow flag.

:::tip[Where to find used FLL sets]
Ask in your regional FLL community Facebook group or your school district's STEM coordinator. Teams often sell complete sets after their final season. FIRST also sometimes offers refurbished sets through regional affiliates.
:::

## FTC

### Buy new
- **Control Hub** (REV Robotics) — the single most important electronic in your robot. A failed Control Hub mid-season can sideline your team. Buy new if budget allows.
- **Batteries (REV 12V Slim)** — degrade significantly with use and are not worth the risk secondhand.
- **Motors (REV HD Hex, Core Hex, goBILDA Yellow Jacket)** — motors with worn brushes or encoder damage are common in used FTC hardware and hard to diagnose without running them under load.

### Safe to buy used
- **REV Robotics structural components** (extrusion, brackets, hubs, motion components) — metal and plastic parts hold up well. Inspect for obvious cracks or stripped threads.
- **goBILDA channel, plates, and motion hardware** — same story. Visual inspection is sufficient for structural parts.
- **Previous-season game pieces (for practice)** — used game elements from prior seasons can be excellent for early-season driver practice. You cannot compete with them, but you can train with them.
- **Servos (with caution)** — CR servos used for continuous rotation wear out faster than position servos. Ask for a demo video. A $10 saving is not worth a broken servo at your first qualifier.

### What to check before buying used FTC electronics
- Control Hub or Expansion Hub: verify firmware loads, all motor and servo ports respond, the wireless LED activates, and the DS app pairs cleanly.
- Motors: run each motor under light load. Listen for grinding or irregular speed. Check encoder counts — connect the motor and rotate by hand while watching encoder output in the FTC Dashboard.
- Servos: command through full range. Any jitter or deadband is a sign of wear.

:::tip[Where to find used FTC hardware]
The FTC Discord has a buy-sell-trade channel that is the most active marketplace. Chief Delphi also has periodic FTC equipment listings. Your regional FTC coordinator may know of disbanding teams.
:::

## FRC

FRC teams typically run $6,000-$21,000 for a first season. Used hardware can cut early costs significantly — but FRC electronics carry higher risk due to age and vendor discontinuations.

### Buy new
- **roboRIO 2** — the FRC control system. Buy new unless you can verify firmware loads and the device was in service last season. The original roboRIO 1 is being phased out; avoid it for new builds.
- **Radio (OpenMesh OM5P-AC)** — FRC radios are flashed with FRC-specific firmware. A used radio that has not been reflashed can cause connection failures at an event. They are inexpensive new; do not risk it.
- **Main battery (MK Battery ES17-12)** — FRC batteries degrade with deep discharge cycles. Many teams run with weakened batteries and do not realise it until robot brownouts under load. New batteries are under $30.
- **CAN bus wiring harnesses** — not expensive; wiring with hidden damage is a debugging nightmare.

### Safe to buy used
- **REV Robotics MAXTube, VEXPRO, Andymark structural components** — structural aluminium and steel fabricated parts are excellent secondhand. Inspect welds and drill patterns visually.
- **Gearboxes (WCP SS, AM Sport, REV MAXPlanetary)** — inspect for gear wear, stripped shafts, and damaged mounting holes. A used gearbox in good shape is fine.
- **Swerve modules (with caution)** — swerve is mechanically complex. Bearings and bevel gears wear. Ask for the number of competition seasons and test rotation smoothness before buying.
- **Practice field elements (AM, VRC, vendor-built)** — great for drive practice; condition matters only cosmetically.
- **CTRE Falcon 500 / Kraken X60 / NEO motors** — these brushless motors are durable if not overloaded. Verify the motor spins freely, no burning smell, and the encoder wire connector is undamaged.

:::tip[Where to find used FRC hardware]
[Chief Delphi Buy/Sell/Trade](https://www.chiefdelphi.com/c/chit-chat/buy-sell-trade) is the standard FRC marketplace. Local FRC teams nearing retirement or upgrading to swerve often list aluminium and gearboxes. FIRST also organises equipment exchanges at some regionals.
:::

## VEX IQ

### Buy new
- **VEX IQ batteries** — like FLL, battery capacity degrades silently. Teams that wonder why their robot slows down in the second half of a match often have old batteries.
- **IQ Smart Motors** — IQ Smart Motors have built-in encoders and a plastic gearbox that can strip with repeated collisions. New motors are inexpensive enough that buying used rarely makes sense.

### Safe to buy used
- **VEX IQ plastic structural parts** (beams, plates, standoffs, connectors) — plastic snap-together parts are very robust. Visual inspection for cracks is sufficient.
- **Previous-season field elements (for practice)** — useful for drive practice even if competition-season-ineligible.
- **VEX IQ Brain (with caution)** — verify firmware loads, all Smart Ports respond to a connected motor, and the brain pairs to the controller and VEXcode. A brain with a dead port can strand a critical mechanism mid-match.

### Where to find used VEX IQ hardware
The [VEX Forum](https://www.vexforum.com/) has a buy/sell/trade section. VEX Education kits are also sold through school district equipment auctions.

## VEX V5

### Buy new
- **V5 Brain** — the central nerve of your robot, and the most expensive repair. Buying used saves roughly $100 at risk of a hidden failure; most teams opt for new.
- **V5 Smart Batteries** — V5 batteries have a fixed charge cycle life and degrade noticeably after two to three seasons of heavy use. Buy new.
- **V5 Smart Motors (11W)** — these brushless motors are durable, but they are often the first thing a team crashes. A motor with a stripped cartridge or bent shaft is common in used VRC hardware. Inspect carefully — buy the cartridge set separately and replace it.

### Safe to buy used
- **VEX metal structural components** (channel, angle, gussets, shafts) — metal does not wear out with typical use. Inspect for bends from collisions.
- **V5 Controller** — controllers degrade mainly through joystick drift and button wear. Test all axes and buttons before buying.
- **High Strength gears, sprockets, and chain** — robust; visual inspection suffices.
- **Motion components (bearings, pillow blocks, linear slides)** — inspect for cracks and smooth motion.

:::tip[Where to find used VEX V5 hardware]
[VEX Forum Buy/Sell/Trade](https://www.vexforum.com/c/vex-community/trading-post) is the standard marketplace. Many teams list complete robot tear-down sets at the end of a season.
:::

## VEX U

VEX U teams build two robots — a compact 15-inch robot and a full-size 24-inch robot — making hardware costs roughly double a V5RC team. The used-vs-new logic follows V5RC exactly, scaled up.

### Special VEX U considerations
- **Custom fabricated parts** — VEX U allows machined aluminium, 3D-printed parts, and custom electronics. These vary enormously in quality. Only buy used custom parts from a team you know or can inspect in person.
- **Third-party motor controllers** — verify firmware, check burn marks on MOSFETs, and test under light load.
- **PROS installations and robot code** — you will not inherit software value from a used hardware purchase; code is repo-owned and not transferable in any meaningful sense.

## Seasonal items that lose most of their value

A few categories are worth almost nothing after their competition season ends:

- **FLL Challenge Set** (game mat + mission models) — each year's set is specific to that season's game. It has some use for practice nostalgia, but no competition value.
- **FTC and FRC game elements** — same principle; useful only for drive practice after the season.
- **Seasonal rulebooks and ref guides** — download the current PDF; do not pay for a printed copy of last year's.

## Protecting yourself when buying used

1. **Ask for a video of the item working** before paying.
2. **Buy locally if possible** — lets you test in person before money changes hands.
3. **Understand the return policy** — most forum and Discord sales are final. Ask explicitly before buying electronics.
4. **Factor in shipping damage risk** — electronics shipped without anti-static packaging or adequate padding can arrive damaged. Ask how the seller plans to pack it.
5. **Cross-check against the current vendor page** — confirm the part you are buying is still competition-legal for your program this season. Rules occasionally delist older hardware.
