FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC) is the largest and most complex program in the FIRST family — for **grades 9–12 (ages 14–18)**. Each season starts with a Kickoff event in January, after which teams have roughly six weeks to design, program, and build an industrial-sized robot from a Kit of Parts plus custom fabrication. Teams compete in three-team alliances on themed fields.

FRC is what FTC alumni typically progress to, and what many top FLL Challenge alumni aim for years in advance. It's the most demanding youth robotics program in mainstream education — closer to a college-level engineering project than a school activity.

Our wiki's FRC coverage is just getting started. Use this page as a starting point and follow the links into the [FRC Resource Map](/resources/frc-resource-map/) for canonical sources.

## How FRC compares to FLL and FTC

| Program | Ages / Grades | Hardware | Team size | What they do |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FLL Challenge | 9–16 / grades 4–8 | LEGO Education SPIKE Prime | 2–10 | Autonomous LEGO robot + Innovation Project |
| FTC | 12–18 / grades 7–12 | REV Control Hub + goBILDA | 8–12 typical | Hybrid autonomous + driver-controlled robot |
| **FRC** | **14–18 / grades 9–12** | **Open Kit of Parts + custom fabrication** | **10–20 typical, often 25+** | **Industrial-sized robots in three-team alliances** |

FRC teams often have multiple sub-teams (mechanical, electrical, programming, CAD, business, outreach), formal mentorship from professional engineers, and significant fundraising to support travel and parts (a typical FRC season costs ~$5,000–$30,000 per team).

## A FRC season at a glance

- **January (Kickoff Saturday)** — New game released, build season starts (**BIOCORE releases January 9, 2027** for the 2026/27 season)
- **January–March** — Six-week build season: design, fabricate, program, iterate, ship the robot
- **March–April** — District events and regional competitions
- **April** — FIRST Championship (Houston) for advancing teams

FRC's signature value is **Coopertition** — cooperation and competition coexisting. Teams help each other in the pits, share parts, and lift each other to do their best. Alliances are randomized then captain-picked.

## What you need to get started

- **Team:** 2 lead adult coaches (required) + typically 10–20+ students grades 9–12. Often supplemented by professional engineering mentors.
- **Hardware:** The Kickoff Kit (provided with registration) — includes the KitBot starting point, motors, wheels, controllers. Most teams add custom fabricated parts (gears, sheet metal, 3D-printed components).
- **Software:** Programming environment supports Java, C++, and LabVIEW. Most teams use WPILib (the FRC robotics library).
- **Registration:** Through FIRST — same Dashboard / Storefront flow as FLL and FTC. New Storefront opens July 2026 for the 2026/27 cycle. New teams should also check [rookie team criteria](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/frc/new-team-criteria).

## Programming in FRC

FRC robots run on the **roboRIO** controller. Programming environments:

- **WPILib** — the canonical FRC robotics library, supports Java and C++
- **LabVIEW** — visual programming (less common in newer teams)
- **PathPlanner**, **PhotonVision** — community libraries for autonomous routines and vision processing

Most teams use Java with WPILib. The official docs and tooling are at [wpilib.org](https://wpilib.org/).
