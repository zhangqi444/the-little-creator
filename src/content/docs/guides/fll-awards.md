FLL tournaments hand out a lot of awards. First-year families often don't know what they mean or which ones lead to advancement. This guide explains the award system and how teams move up through the competition levels.

---

## Competition levels

Most U.S. teams compete through these levels:

1. **Qualifying tournament** — local event, first time competing. Most teams start here.
2. **Regional or State championship** — top teams from qualifiers advance here.
3. **World Championship** — top teams from regional championships. Held in late spring.

Some regions have only two levels (qualifier → championship); others have three or four. Your PDO sets the structure for your region — check their site for specifics.

---

## The main awards

### Champion's Award

The highest award at most FLL tournaments. Given to the team that best exemplifies all three components of FLL together: Robot Game performance, Innovation Project quality, and Core Values strength.

This is not just the highest robot score. A team that scores 300 points but has a weak Innovation Project and poor Core Values presentation will not win Champion's Award over a team that scores 200 but excels across all three.

**Typically advances to the next level.**

---

### Robot Performance Award

Given for the highest Robot Game score(s). Some tournaments give this to the top 1–3 Robot Game finishers.

Note: high robot scores alone rarely advance a team. Advancement usually requires strong performance across all judged areas, not just the robot.

---

### Innovation Project Award

Given to the team with the strongest Innovation Project — real-world problem identification, innovative solution, and effective presentation.

Judges look for: a specific problem, a genuinely innovative solution (not just "raise awareness"), evidence the team connected with real stakeholders, and a clear sharing plan.

---

### Robot Design Award

Given for outstanding robot design — mechanical engineering, programming strategy, and the ability to explain design decisions clearly.

Judges want to hear *why* the team made decisions, not just *what* they built. "We tried three different attachments before choosing this one because..." scores higher than a perfect robot with no explanation.

---

### Core Values Award

Given to the team that best demonstrates the FLL Core Values throughout the day — not just in the Core Values judging session, but in how they interact with other teams, handle setbacks, and support each other.

Judges watch teams throughout the day. How a team reacts when the robot fails in front of a crowd is Core Values in action.

---

### Motivate Award

Given to the team that best spreads enthusiasm for FLL and STEM in their community. Teams that have done meaningful outreach — school demos, community presentations, recruiting new teams — are strong candidates.

---

### Engineering Excellence Award (or similar)

Some tournaments give this for outstanding engineering thinking demonstrated across robot design and the Innovation Project together. The exact name varies by region.

---

### Rising Star / Rookie Award (some regions)

Given to first-year teams that show exceptional promise. Not universal — check if your regional tournament offers it.

---

### Advancement — how it actually works

Each PDO sets its own advancement criteria. Common patterns:

- **Award-based:** Win Champion's Award or one of the top judged awards → receive an advancement bid
- **Score threshold:** Meet a minimum Robot Game score AND have strong judging scores → advance
- **Combined ranking:** Each judging panel submits scores; teams are ranked across all three; top N advance

What this means practically: **you can advance without winning any single award.** A team that finishes 3rd in every category often advances over a team that dominates one category and is poor in the others.

**Always confirm advancement criteria with your PDO** — they vary significantly by region and sometimes change year to year.

## What judges see vs. what teams show

Judges spend about 5 minutes with each team per session. In that time they're trying to understand:

| Session | What judges want to see |
|---|---|
| Robot Design | Design decisions with reasoning — why you chose what you chose |
| Innovation Project | Real problem, innovative solution, evidence of stakeholder connection |
| Core Values | Team culture — how members support each other and handle difficulty |

The biggest mistakes:

- **Robot Design:** Reciting what the robot does without explaining why design choices were made
- **Innovation Project:** Vague solutions ("we would raise awareness") without specifics
- **Core Values:** Only the loudest kid answers; others look unengaged

See [Preparing for FLL Judging](/guides/judging-prep/) for detailed prep guidance.

## The Champion's Award vs. just winning Robot Game

This is the most common first-year misunderstanding. Families watch the robot run, see a high score, and assume their team is winning.

The Robot Game is one third of the picture. Teams that focus only on robot score while neglecting their Innovation Project or Core Values presentation often do not advance — and are surprised when they don't.

The teams that consistently advance are the ones that are genuinely good at all three. A useful rule: if your team spends 80% of practice time on robot and 20% on everything else, you're out of balance.

---

## After the ceremony

Whether you win an advancement bid or not:

- Talk to judges after the ceremony if possible — most are happy to give brief feedback
- Write down what went well and what to improve (goes in the engineering notebook)
- Celebrate something specific — not just "we tried hard" but one concrete thing the team actually did well

Losing at a qualifier is normal for first-year teams. Almost every experienced team has a story about their worst tournament becoming their most valuable learning experience.
