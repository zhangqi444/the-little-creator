Every coach misses a session eventually. Life happens — illness, travel, work conflicts, family emergencies. The question is whether your team can function without you, and whether the session is productive or wasted.

Teams that handle missed sessions well don't do so by accident. They've thought through it before it happens, built habits that make the team partly self-sufficient, and have a plan that a substitute — parent, helper, or older student — can follow without knowing everything you know.

This guide walks through what to build before you need it, how to hand off a session, and what students can do on their own.

---

## Why this matters more than it seems

A poorly planned missed session doesn't just waste 90 minutes. It can:

- Stall momentum when the team is in a critical build or judging prep period
- Create a behaviour or safety problem if no responsible adult is present
- Leave students with incomplete information that produces rework at the next session
- Signal to the team — and their families — that the program is fragile

A well-planned missed session can actually build team independence. Students who solve their own problems, run their own session, and check in asynchronously are more ready for competition than students who wait for a coach to tell them what to do.

---

## Three scenarios and what each requires

### Scenario 1: Planned absence (you know in advance)

The easiest case. You have time to prepare a session plan, brief a substitute, and pre-answer the questions students will ask.

What you need:
- A written session plan the substitute can follow
- A pre-brief with the substitute (15–20 minutes is usually enough)
- Any materials, tools, or access the substitute needs to run the session

### Scenario 2: Last-minute absence (you find out the night before or morning of)

You don't have time for a proper handoff. You have time for a quick message.

What you need:
- A standing "session self-start" document students already know about
- A short message to the substitute or student lead with the day's specific focus
- A way for students to flag a real problem to you asynchronously (group chat, shared doc)

### Scenario 3: No adult available

This shouldn't happen for sessions involving minors — every session needs a responsible adult present, regardless of coaching absence. If you genuinely cannot find an adult to supervise, cancel the session and reschedule.

Do not leave students unsupervised. This is a non-negotiable.

## Before the season: what to prepare

The best substitute coverage happens before you ever miss a session. Three things to build early:

### 1. A session self-start document

A standing one-page doc (or shared note) that tells students:

- Where materials and tools are stored
- The safety rules that apply regardless of who's present
- How to start a session if the coach isn't there yet
- Who to contact if something goes wrong

You don't need to rebuild this every session. It's a standing document that covers the invariants.

### 2. A student lead who can run drills

Every team should have at least one student who can run a standard practice — robot runs, programming tests, review of documentation — without prompting. This isn't about giving a student coach authority; it's about having someone who can keep things moving.

See [Student Leadership Guide](/guides/student-leadership/) for how to develop this.

### 3. A parent helper rotation or regular volunteer

Identify one or two parents or adults who are familiar with the team, know where things are, and can step in as a responsible adult presence when needed. They don't need to know robotics. They need to know the safety rules, where the first aid kit is, and who to call if something goes wrong.

Keep this person's contact information somewhere both you and the students have access to.

## Writing a good session plan for a substitute

A session plan for a substitute is different from notes you'd write for yourself. The substitute doesn't have your context. Write it for someone who knows the students but doesn't know robotics.

### What to include

**Goal for the session (one sentence)**
"By the end of tonight, the drive base should be fully assembled and driving in a straight line."

Not: "Make progress on the robot."

**Step-by-step agenda with time estimates**

```
0–5 min     Students arrive, check in, pull out robot and tools
5–25 min    Continue drivetrain assembly: students know what to do, check in with [student name] for current state
25–55 min   Drive test and iteration: [student name] knows the programming steps
55–70 min   Documentation: each student writes one notebook entry
70–80 min   Clean up, put everything away, lock tools case
```

**What the substitute needs to watch for**
- Students should not use power tools without an adult present (specify which tools)
- [Student name] sometimes rushes and skips documentation — remind them at the 55-min mark
- If the drive base isn't working by 45 min, they should stop and troubleshoot — not push through to testing

**What to do if something goes wrong**
- Robot won't connect to controller: try these steps [or link to troubleshooting doc]
- Injury: first aid kit is [location]; contact [emergency contact]
- Conflict between students: pause the session, reset, message me

**How to close the session**
- Confirm all tools are put away and counted
- Make sure students have logged what they did (even briefly)
- Let me know by message what got done and what's left for next session

### What not to include

Don't write a session plan that requires the substitute to teach anything. If completing a step requires explaining a new concept, save it for when you're back. Design the session around things the students already know how to do.

## Briefing a substitute

A written plan is not enough on its own. A 15-minute conversation before the session significantly improves coverage quality.

Cover:
- The one thing that needs to happen today (the session goal)
- The one thing to watch for (the friction point or risk)
- Any individual student situations to be aware of (a student having a hard week, a known conflict)
- The authority boundary: what decisions the substitute can make vs. what to hold for you
- How to reach you if something urgent comes up

Don't brief the substitute with everything you know about the team. Give them what they need for this session.

## What students can do independently

Some work doesn't need a coach to direct it. These are reliable self-directed session types:

### Build/hardware work (low-supervision)
- Continuing assembly work from a clear instruction sheet or previous session
- Rebuilding a mechanism they've already built once
- Maintenance tasks (replacing worn parts, tightening connections, cleaning sensors)
- Sorting and inventorying spare parts

### Programming (low-supervision)
- Running and logging repeated test drives
- Iterating on code they've already written (improving, not starting from scratch)
- Code review: reading through existing code as a team and writing questions for next session
- Working through official curriculum exercises or documentation challenges

### Documentation (no supervision required, any adult is fine)
- Engineering notebook entries for sessions that have already happened
- Updating a mission log, decision log, or test record
- Team member biography or "why we do robotics" entries
- Preparing questions for an expert interview

### Strategy/analysis (discussion-based, student-led)
- Reviewing game rules together and discussing strategy choices
- Watching recorded match footage and taking notes
- Building or updating a scoring matrix or mission priority list

### What NOT to assign for an unsupervised or lightly supervised session
- New concepts requiring coaching explanation
- Complex mechanism changes where a mistake could damage hardware
- Judging practice or rehearsal (this needs feedback; prep without feedback teaches the wrong version)
- Anything involving power tools if the substitute isn't familiar with the equipment

## Program-specific notes

| Program | Independent session strengths | Things that need a coach present |
|---|---|---|
| **FLL / VEX IQ** | Documentation, repeat robot runs, spare-parts sorting, core values activities | New SPIKE Prime / VEX IQ build steps, new programming concepts, judging rehearsal |
| **FTC** | Engineering Portfolio writing, TeleOp drive practice on known code, parts inventory | OnBot Java debugging, new mechanism design, alliance strategy discussion |
| **FRC** | Driver practice (if field space is clear and safety rules are established), Impact writing sessions, match footage review | CAN bus or electrical work, new subsystem design, Autonomous code changes |
| **VEX V5 / VRC** | Autonomous run logging, TeleOp iteration on known code, notebook entries | New V5 motor/sensor configuration, Skills run calibration, new mechanism build |
| **VEX U** | PROS code review, match footage scouting, notebook and documentation work | Two-robot coordination practice (needs both operators), any new autonomous coordination |

## Asynchronous check-in during a session you're missing

You don't have to be completely out of the loop. A brief message exchange mid-session can catch problems early.

Practical approach:
- Ask the substitute or student lead to send a quick status at the session midpoint ("drive base is together, testing now" / "stuck on X, what should we do?")
- Set a clear threshold: only message for blockers, not updates
- Respond with a short directive if needed; don't try to run the session remotely

Trying to direct a session from a distance in real time usually makes things worse. Brief, targeted check-ins are better.

---

## When to cancel instead of covering

Some sessions are better cancelled and rescheduled than covered by a substitute. Cancel if:

- The work for the session genuinely requires coaching expertise to do safely or correctly
- No suitable adult is available to be physically present (safety non-negotiable)
- The team is at a critical juncture where a poorly directed session could cause meaningful setbacks (e.g., major mechanism change during competition week)
- The timing can't be recovered — but cancelling won't cause a significant problem

Cancelled sessions don't need to be dramatic. "I can't make Tuesday — let's shift to Thursday" is enough.

---

## After the session: debrief

Get a summary from the substitute or student lead as soon as possible after the session. Even a short message.

What you need to know:
- What got done (specifically — what state is the robot in?)
- What didn't get done, and why
- Any student situations that came up
- Anything the substitute noticed that you should know

Update your session notes or documentation log accordingly. The team's continuity depends on you knowing where things actually are, not where you expected them to be.

---

## Quick-start checklist

Build these before the season so you're ready when a missed session happens:

- [ ] Session self-start document written and shared with team
- [ ] Student lead identified who can keep a session moving
- [ ] Parent helper or substitute adult identified and briefed on safety rules
- [ ] Emergency contact and first aid kit location documented and accessible
- [ ] At least one "reliable independent session" format the team knows how to run
- [ ] Shared communication channel (group chat or doc) so the team can reach you asynchronously
- [ ] Policy decided: when is it better to cancel than cover?
