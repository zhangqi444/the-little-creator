VEX's award structure is consistent across VEX IQ (VIQRC), VEX V5 (V5RC), and VEX U (VURC) — same award names, same criteria, with each award assessed per-program at every event. The top award is **Excellence**, and it requires strength across multiple dimensions — not just a winning robot.

This guide explains what each award rewards, how teams advance via awards, and what realistic targets look like for new teams.

:::note[Award lists by program]
The full per-program award lists are in the current season's [game manual](/resources/vex-resource-map/#vex-iq-competition--program-page) (link is to VIQRC; V5RC and VURC manuals are linked from their respective pages on [RobotEvents.com](https://www.robotevents.com)). Specific awards available at any event are listed in the event's information packet.
:::

## The headline award: Excellence

**The Excellence Award** is VEX's top award — analogous to FLL's Champion's Award, FTC's Inspire, and FRC's Impact. It recognises the team that exemplifies overall excellence across the program — strong robot performance, strong engineering documentation, strong Skills Challenge, strong Gracious Professionalism, and strong judging interview.

Excellence is **not** a single criterion. Judges look for:

- A high ranking in qualification matches (typically top 1/3 of the field)
- A competitive Skills Challenge score
- A well-maintained engineering notebook
- A coherent, well-presented design process in the judging interview
- Strong Gracious Professionalism observed throughout the event
- A high-quality robot design and build

Excellence is the highest qualification path — winning Excellence at a regional or state event typically qualifies the team to advance to the next level (state championship, signature event, or VEX Worlds depending on the event's advancement structure).

## Judged awards (driven by interview + notebook)

These are awarded by the judging panel based on the pit interview, the engineering notebook, and behaviour observed during the event.

### Design Award

Recognises the team with the **best documented engineering design process**. Judges focus on the notebook and the pit interview — they want to see *how* the team designed the robot, what tradeoffs they considered, what didn't work and why. Often goes to a team with strong documentation but not necessarily the highest-performing robot. Closest counterpart to FTC's Think Award.

### Build Award

Recognises the **best-built robot** — mechanical execution quality. Judges look at wiring quality, structural integrity, manufacturing precision, ease of maintenance, robustness. Often goes to a team whose robot looks intentional and refined.

### Amaze Award

Recognises a team whose **robot does something genuinely impressive** — an unusual mechanism, a creative scoring strategy, an elegant solution to a difficult problem. Judges want to see something they haven't seen elsewhere on the field that day. The "wow factor" award.

### Think Award

Recognises strong **programming or autonomous achievement**. Often goes to a team with a particularly clever or robust autonomous routine, or who has done notable work with sensors / computer vision / advanced control. Available at most events; specific criteria vary by program.

### Innovate Award

Recognises a team with an **innovative approach to the season's game** — either a novel mechanism, an unusual strategy, or a creative interpretation of the rules. Different from Amaze in that Innovate focuses specifically on game-strategic innovation.

### Inspire Award (VEX IQ only at some events)

Available at some VEX IQ events. Recognises a team that exemplifies the program's mission — strong outreach, mentorship of other teams, community engagement.

### Judges Award

A discretionary award the judging panel can give to recognise a team they want to highlight for reasons not covered by other awards — often for an exceptional story, recovery from adversity, or particularly strong Gracious Professionalism. No fixed criteria.

### Sportsmanship Award

Recognises a team that consistently demonstrates **Gracious Professionalism** — helping other teams, gracious behaviour after wins and losses, generous pit dynamics. Often determined by referee and judge observation throughout the event.

## Match-based awards

Determined by on-field performance.

### Tournament Champion

The two-team alliance (V5/V5RC, VEX IQ) that wins the elimination bracket. Both teams on the alliance receive the award. For VEX U, the winning alliance is also two teams (each operating two robots, so four robots total).

### Tournament Finalist

The losing alliance from the elimination finals.

### Robot Skills Champion

The team with the **highest combined Driver Skills + Programming Skills score** at the event. Combined score, not the best single attempt. Often qualifies independently of match performance — a team that's mid-pack in alliance matches can still advance via Skills.

## Other awards

### Energy Award (sponsored)

Recognises a team with strong team spirit — visible enthusiasm, cheering for other teams, energy in the pit. Often a fun recognition that goes to a smaller or younger team with great morale.

### Service Award (VEX IQ-leaning)

Recognises a team that has gone above and beyond to help other teams — typically by sharing parts, expertise, or pit space during the event.

## How teams advance

The exact advancement criteria vary by region and event level, but the typical pattern at a regional event:

1. **Excellence Award** — first advancement slot
2. **Tournament Champions** — both teams on the winning alliance
3. **Design Award** — next slot
4. **Robot Skills Champion** — next slot (sometimes combined with another award winner)
5. **Additional slots** based on the event's advancement matrix

Most advancement paths flow: **local qualifier → state championship → VEX Worlds.** For VEX U specifically: regional / national events → VEX Worlds (VURC division).

## What rookies should target

A first-year VEX team realistically aims for:

- **Completing all qualification matches** with the robot scoring
- **Posting a Skills Challenge run** (any successful Skills attempt counts as success in year one)
- **Being picked at alliance selection** — likely if you're operational
- **Being in the conversation for Sportsmanship or Energy Award** — these reward team culture, which rookies often have in abundance
- **Service Award** — if your team has actively helped others, this is approachable

A second-year team can realistically target **Build** (good mechanical execution is independent of strategy depth), **Amaze** (one impressive mechanism can win this), or **Think** (one strong autonomous routine can win this).

A team should *not* set "we will win Excellence" as a first-year goal. Excellence requires consistent execution across multiple dimensions, which takes years of practice.

## The single most useful piece of advice

Maintain the engineering notebook **continuously throughout the season**, not just before tournaments. Judges can tell the difference between a notebook that captures real-time decisions (with bad handwriting, dead-end sketches, real dates) and one that was assembled the week before the event (clean, polished, suspiciously coherent). Authentic notebooks win Design Awards. Polished-but-assembled notebooks usually don't.
