Not every family wants to start a team from scratch. If you'd rather join an existing team — and there are plenty of them — this guide walks you through how to find one, what to ask, and how to make a good first impression.

---

## Step 1 — Find teams in your area

Each program has its own official event-registration platform. Both list active teams and upcoming events; searching for events near you almost always reveals the teams competing at them.

### FIRST programs (FLL, FTC, FRC)

**[FIRST Team Search](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-a-team)**
- Lists every registered FLL, FTC, and FRC team globally.
- Search by zip/postal code, program, and radius.
- Shows team name, school or org affiliation, and contact info (when teams have made it public).

**[FIRST Events](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-an-event)**
- Browse upcoming tournaments near you.
- Teams competing at a local event are almost always local — this is an easy way to identify active, engaged teams.

### VEX programs (VEX IQ, VEX V5 / VRC, VEX U)

**[RobotEvents.com — Team Search](https://www.robotevents.com/teams)**
- Covers VEX IQ, V5RC, and VEX U programs.
- Filter by program, season, country, and state/region.
- Each team listing shows the school/org name and, often, a coach contact or team email.

**[RobotEvents.com — Events Near Me](https://www.robotevents.com/events)**
- Find upcoming VEX tournaments in your area.
- Click through to an event to see the registered team list.

## Step 2 — Contact a team

Once you have a short list of teams, reach out. Most teams genuinely welcome interest from new members — recruiting is a perennial challenge for coaches.

**What to say:**
> Hi, I'm [name], the parent of [child's name/age]. We're interested in joining an FLL/VEX IQ/FTC/FRC team. Do you have open spots for next season, and if so, what's your tryout or sign-up process?

**Where to reach them:**
- **Email or phone** shown on the FIRST Team Search or RobotEvents listing (some teams hide contact info until they have open spots).
- **School front office** — if the team is school-based, call the school and ask for the robotics coach.
- **Social media** — many teams have a public Instagram, Facebook, or X (Twitter) account.
- **In person at an event** — if a local tournament is coming up, attending as a spectator is the single best way to meet teams. Walk up to the pit area during pit access times, introduce yourself, and ask questions. Coaches at tournaments are busy, but most will tell you how to follow up.

**If you get no reply:**
Teams are often run by volunteer coaches with day jobs. Wait a week and try again through a different channel. If a team is unresponsive through multiple channels, move on to your next option.

## Step 3 — Understand what you're joining

Different teams have very different cultures, time commitments, and expectations. Before you commit, ask:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| How many hours per week during the season? | Range is wide — 2 h/week to 15+ h/week for competitive FRC teams |
| Is this school-based or community-based? | School teams often require enrollment; community teams usually do not |
| Do you have a tryout or is it open enrollment? | Some competitive teams select members; others take everyone who applies |
| What does it cost to participate? | Kit fees, registration fees, travel — varies enormously by team |
| How many students are on the team? | Smaller teams mean more hands-on time per student; larger teams have more resources |
| What is the coaching style? | Coach-led vs. student-led; check if it matches what your child wants |
| Is there space for your child's interest? | Programming, building, design, outreach, documentation — most programs need all of these |

---

## Step 4 — Attend a practice before committing

Most coaches will happily let prospective members observe (or even participate in) a practice before signing on. This is strongly recommended — it lets your child see whether the team's vibe, pace, and style are a fit.

Things to notice at a practice:
- Are kids doing the work, or is the coach doing most of it?
- Do team members seem to enjoy being there?
- Is there a clear structure to the session, or is it chaotic?
- How does the team handle disagreement or frustration?

## Step 5 — What if there is no team near you?

If your search comes up empty, a few options:

**Start a new team.** It is more work, but entirely doable. See [Forming a Team](/guides/forming-a-team/) for where to begin, and [First Four Weeks](/guides/first-four-weeks/) for a week-by-week plan. Many programs offer financial assistance for first-year teams — see [Team Funding](/guides/team-funding/).

**Ask your school.** If your child's school does not have a team, a teacher or administrator may be willing to start one. The [Curriculum Starter guides](/for-educators/) are designed exactly for this scenario.

**Check a nearby school or community org.** Team search tools often surface teams at schools a town over. Most community-based teams welcome members regardless of which school they attend.

**Join a regional waitlist.** Some large teams with more applicants than spots maintain a waitlist. Ask to be added — spots sometimes open up when a student graduates, changes schools, or drops out.

## Program-specific tips

### FLL Challenge
- Most FLL teams are 2–10 kids; many have open enrollment and welcome newcomers at the start of a season.
- The new season starts in August when the game challenge is released — reach out between May and August to get ahead of sign-ups.
- Your **regional PDO (Program Delivery Partner)** keeps a list of local teams. Find yours via the [FLL Resource Map](/resources/fll-resource-map/).

### FTC
- Teams typically have 8–12 members; some are selective, especially for the driver and portfolio roles.
- The FTC season starts at Kickoff in September. Contact teams between June and August.
- Game Manual 0 ([gm0.org](https://gm0.org/)) has a community section with FTC team directories.

### FRC
- FRC teams of 25+ students often have more roles available than you would expect — programming, electrical, mechanical, outreach, media/documentation.
- Many FRC teams have a formal application or interview process. Prepare your child to explain what they want to contribute, not just what they want to learn.
- Rookie year (starting fresh) teams are often announced in the fall. If you find a first-year team, you may be able to be a founding member.

### VEX IQ
- VEX IQ teams are very small (2–5 kids typically). Openings are rare mid-season but common at the start of a new season (May–September).
- Many VEX IQ teams are run through elementary or middle schools. Contact the school's STEM coordinator.

### VEX V5 / VRC
- Like FTC, VRC teams are moderately sized (6–15 students). Competitive teams may be selective, especially for driver roles.
- RobotEvents shows both school-based and community club teams. Community clubs are often more open to new members.

### VEX U
- VEX U is collegiate. Search RobotEvents for teams at your university or nearby colleges.
- If your school has a makerspace or engineering society, someone there likely knows whether a VEX U team exists.
- No university team? VEX U rules allow independent teams not affiliated with a school. See [Starting a VEX U Team](/guides/vex-u-first-team/) for details.
