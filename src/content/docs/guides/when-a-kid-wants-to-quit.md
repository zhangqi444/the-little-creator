It happens on almost every team at some point. A kid who was excited in September is checked out in October. Or they're quietly miserable every practice. Or they come to you and say they want to stop.

This is not a failure. It's a coaching moment. Here's how to handle it well.

---

## First: listen before you fix

When a kid signals they want to quit, your first job is to understand why. Don't jump to solutions. Ask questions.

**Useful questions:**
- "What's the part you're enjoying least right now?"
- "Is there something that's been hard or frustrating that you haven't told me about?"
- "Is it FLL specifically, or is something else going on?"

Sometimes the answer is "I'm just tired" (normal, especially mid-season). Sometimes it's "the other kids don't listen to me." Sometimes it's something at school or home that has nothing to do with robotics.

**What you're listening for:** Is this a fixable FLL problem, or is this about something bigger? The response is very different.

---

## Common causes and what to do about each

### "It's not fun anymore"

**What's usually happening:** Practice has become all work, no play. The joy drained out around week 5 when the pressure started.

**What to try:**
- Do one session that's purely exploratory — no mission pressure, no judging prep. Let them build whatever.
- Reduce session length. Tired kids aren't learning.
- Ask them what they'd like to do more of. Give them that.

---

### "I'm not good at any of this"

**What's usually happening:** Skill gap has become visible. Other kids are faster/better and this kid has started to feel irrelevant.

**What to try:**
- Find the thing they *are* good at and make it theirs. Every team has roles: presenter, photographer, note-taker, parts manager, encourager. Not every kid needs to program.
- Give them a task at just above their current level — hard enough to feel real, achievable enough to succeed.
- Name something specific they did well, even if small. Kids track this.

---

### "Nobody listens to me / one kid runs everything"

**What's usually happening:** Team dynamics have calcified. One or two dominant personalities have crowded out quieter kids.

**What to try:**
- Enforce turn-taking explicitly: "Before we decide, I need to hear from everyone."
- Restructure who owns what. If the dominant kid owns all the robot decisions, give this kid ownership of one specific thing the dominant kid can't touch.
- Have a private conversation with the dominant kid — not accusatory, just clarifying expectations about team dynamics.

See [Coaching Without Doing It For Them](/guides/coaching-without-doing/) for the broader team dynamics toolkit.

---

### "I'm just overwhelmed / too much going on"

**What's usually happening:** School, sports, family — life got full. FLL is optional, and something has to give.

**What to try:**
- Reduce their commitment temporarily. "Can you come to practices but skip the homework-style prep stuff for two weeks?"
- Let them take a lesser role without leaving entirely. They stay part of the team but with lighter load.
- Accept that sometimes the timing is genuinely wrong. A kid who leaves mid-season due to life circumstances is not a failure — for you or for them.

---

### "I don't like the competition pressure"

**What's usually happening:** The tournament is coming up and the stakes feel too high. Kids who are perfectionists or have performance anxiety often hit a wall here.

**What to try:**
- Reframe what "winning" means explicitly. "Our goal is to do our best work and learn something. That's it."
- Do a low-stakes mock run in front of a small audience to desensitize.
- Let them opt out of one specific part (e.g., "you don't have to present to judges — you can stay with the robot") if it's causing genuine distress. Adjust rather than force.

---

## When to push and when to let go

**Push gently when:**
- The issue is temporary (overwhelm, frustration, a bad week)
- Quitting would mean letting down teammates who are counting on them
- They have a history of quitting things at the hard part and you sense that's what's happening
- There are just 2–4 weeks left in the season

**Let go when:**
- The kid is genuinely miserable, not just frustrated
- There's something bigger going on (mental health, family crisis, school struggle)
- Staying is causing harm — to them or to the team dynamic
- They've communicated clearly and consistently, not just once after a hard day

**The rule of thumb:** One bad practice doesn't mean quit. Three weeks of dread before every practice means listen more carefully.

---

## If they do leave

If a kid decides to leave mid-season, the way you handle it matters.

- **Don't guilt-trip.** "Think about what this does to the team" rarely helps and sometimes scars.
- **Do leave the door open.** "If you want to come back, we'd love to have you — no questions."
- **Tell the rest of the team matter-of-factly.** "[Name] has too much going on right now and won't be able to continue. We're going to adjust and keep going."
- **Protect their dignity.** Don't let it become team gossip.

Some kids who leave come back next season with more motivation than they would have had if they'd been forced through a miserable experience.

---

## A note for parents whose child wants to quit

Your instinct may be to make them honor the commitment, especially if you've spent money on registration and equipment. That instinct is understandable but worth examining.

The goal of FLL is not to produce kids who finish things. It's to build kids who love learning and can work as a team. A kid who grinds through a miserable season to honor a commitment may come out with a dislike of robotics that lasts years. A kid who leaves gracefully when something wasn't right, with their curiosity intact, may come back on their own next year.

Commitments matter. Wellbeing matters more. You'll know your kid — trust that judgment.
