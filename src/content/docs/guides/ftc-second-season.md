Season one is about learning what FTC actually is. Season two is about competing intentionally. Here's what experienced FTC coaches and students consistently change after their first year.

---

## 1. Start the engineering portfolio on day one

Year one teams scramble to build their Engineering Portfolio in the week before the meet. Year two teams start it on the first build session and never stop.

**What that looks like:**
- Open the Portfolio document at the first team meeting and add the date
- After every session, one team member (rotating) spends 10 minutes documenting what was decided and why
- Photos of failed prototypes are more valuable than photos of finished assemblies — judges want to see your *process*, not your product
- The Portfolio is your primary judging evidence for Inspire, Think, and Design awards

The hardest habit to break: only documenting when things work. Judges actively look for evidence of iteration — problems encountered, solutions tried, which ones failed and why you pivoted.

See [FTC Engineering Portfolio — Practical Guide](/guides/ftc-engineering-portfolio/) for full structure and scoring guidance.

## 2. Narrow your robot scope earlier

Year one teams build a robot that *tries* to do everything. Year two teams pick 3–4 game elements to own reliably and don't apologize for it.

**The math:** A robot that scores 80 points per match reliably will outrank a robot that tries for 140 and scores 60. Consistency wins more matches than ambition.

**How to scope your season:**
1. Read the game manual immediately when released and list all point-scoring opportunities
2. Estimate the probability of your team completing each one cleanly by week 10
3. Eliminate the low-confidence, high-complexity elements
4. Build your robot design, code, and TeleOp choreography around the elements you kept
5. Revisit scope at week 6 — add back one element only if your core elements are already solid

---

## 3. Plan your autonomous from the start

Year one FTC teams often ship a basic 10-point autonomous (park or push one object) and plan to "improve it later." Later never comes.

**Year two approach:**
- Set an autonomous target score at the start of the season — not later
- Design the robot and sensor setup with autonomous capability in mind from day one (encoder wheels, AprilTag camera mount, REV Through Bore encoders)
- Treat autonomous code as a first-class project, not an afterthought
- Run autonomous at the start of every practice, before driver practice, so it gets real iteration time

A strong autonomous in FTC changes alliance partner conversations at alliance selection and can swing a match when TeleOp is close.

See [FTC Programming Basics](/guides/ftc-programming-basics/) for autonomous implementation patterns.

## 4. Build driver practice into every session

Year one teams spend 90% of practice building and coding and 10% driving. Year two teams flip closer to 50/50 by October.

**Why it matters:** FTC is a 2.5-minute driver-controlled match. Raw driver skill — smooth control, efficient picking/placing, knowing when to play defense vs. score — is a major differentiator at higher-level meets. Most rookie teams show up to a qualifier having practiced autonomous extensively but having run only a handful of full driver practice sessions.

**What a useful driver practice session looks like:**
- Set a timer and simulate a full match (0:30 autonomous + 2:00 teleop + 0:30 endgame)
- One team member watches and records score, notes where time was wasted
- Rotate drivers — every team member who might drive should be comfortable with the controls
- Practice endgame (climbing, parking) separately and in full-match context

## 5. Prepare differently for judging

Year one teams walk into the judging interview and describe their robot. Year two teams tell a story.

**The single biggest FTC judging mistake:** treating the interview as a show-and-tell of features. Judges are evaluating your *engineering process* — how you identified problems, tried solutions, measured results, and iterated.

**The story structure judges want to hear:**
- "We designed X to solve Y problem. We built prototype A, tested it, and found issue B."
- "We tried three different intake geometries before landing on this one. Here's why we switched."
- "Our autonomous worked in the shop but failed at the scrimmage. We diagnosed the problem as Z and fixed it by W."

Every student should know the team's story. Not just the robot lead. Not just the captain. Judges talk to whoever is near the robot.

**Prepare for hard questions:**
- "What would you change if you had another month?"
- "How did you divide the work on this team?"
- "Walk me through a decision that didn't go the way you expected."

See [Preparing for FTC Judging](/guides/ftc-judging-prep/) for the full session structure and award-matching strategies.

## 6. Actually use the Connect Award strategy

Most FTC teams leave Connect Award points on the table because they don't plan outreach with awards in mind.

Connect Award recognizes teams that connect with their communities — schools, STEM organizations, FLL or FRC teams, corporate partners. The key word judges watch for is *sustainable*: one-off school visits are nice; recurring partnerships are what wins Connect.

**Year two playbook:**
- Identify 1–2 partners (a local FLL team, a library STEM club, a company) at the start of the season
- Plan 3–4 touchpoints over the season, not one big event
- Document each touchpoint in the Engineering Portfolio with photos, dates, and what both sides got from it
- Have one student prepare a 2-minute verbal summary of your outreach impact for judges

## 7. Use alliance selection strategically

Year one teams often don't understand alliance selection or treat it as something that happens to them. Year two teams treat it as a game they can influence.

**How alliance selection works in FTC:**
- After qualification matches, the top 4 ranked teams each select 2 alliance partners
- Higher-ranked teams have more power — but a team ranked 8th can still be a sought-after pick
- Teams get picked based on autonomous consistency, TeleOp reliability, and reputation from pit scouting

**How to be picked even when ranked mid-field:**
- Be consistent: mid-field ranked teams with reliable autonomous and low error rates often beat high-ranked inconsistent teams in picks
- Scout yourself: know your match stats and be ready to discuss them honestly with potential alliance captains
- Pit relationships matter: walk around, introduce yourselves, watch other robots, ask good questions — teams pick partners they know

**If you're an alliance captain:**
- Scout during matches and note teams that executed reliably under pressure
- Check autonomous consistency (does it complete every match, or only sometimes?)
- Look for complementary strengths — if you score top elements well, find a partner who parks + scores endgame well

## 8. Fix your team structure

Year one FTC teams often run on enthusiasm and everyone doing a bit of everything. By season two, structured roles are worth the awkward conversation to implement.

**Roles that matter on a typical FTC team:**
- **Robot Lead (builder):** owns mechanical design decisions, calls build priorities
- **Software Lead:** owns code architecture, autonomous, and driver controls
- **Portfolio Lead:** owns documentation, coordinates what gets recorded after each session
- **Drive Team (2–3 students):** driver, operator, and human player (if applicable); these may or may not be the build/code leads

Roles don't mean others can't contribute to another area — it means one person has final authority and accountability for each area. This prevents design-by-committee stalls and the "I thought you were handling that" tournament-day disasters.

---

## 9. Use the scrimmage

Most regions offer practice meets or scrimmages before official qualifier season begins. Year one teams skip them because "the robot isn't ready." Year two teams go regardless.

**Why to go even with an unfinished robot:**
- You learn tournament logistics: check-in, inspection, field setup, judging room flow
- You get match data on your autonomous under real field conditions (your shop field and the tournament field are never quite the same)
- You meet teams from your region — pit relationships start forming
- The experience of a chaotic robot under match pressure before it really counts is invaluable

Go to the scrimmage with whatever you have.

---

## 10. Keep it student-led

The biggest risk in year two is a coach who now knows how FTC works and subtly starts solving problems that students should solve.

Year one: you were learning alongside them. Year two: you have a mental model of what good looks like, and the temptation to steer toward it is real.

**Year two mindset:**
- Let the team make design decisions you disagree with — and let them discover the consequences
- Ask questions instead of giving answers: "What happens if the intake motor stalls?" not "You need a current limiter"
- Reserve direct advice for safety, rules compliance, and genuine time-runs-out situations
- The judging interview goes significantly better when students have genuine ownership of their decisions

See [Coaching Without Doing It For Them](/guides/coaching-without-doing/) for the principles behind this.
