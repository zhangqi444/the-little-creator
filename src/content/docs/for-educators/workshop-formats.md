Curriculum starters and lesson plans assume a full season. These workshop formats assume you have a room full of students for two to four hours and you need to leave them with something real — a working robot, a first program, or a genuine taste of what the program feels like.

Use these for:

- **One-day school events** (STEM day, career day, maker fair)
- **Summer camps** (single-day or as one module of a multi-day camp)
- **Intro sessions** for prospective teams before registration
- **Open houses** and recruitment events for existing clubs

---

## How to read these formats

Each format specifies:

- **Duration** — total session length with recommended pacing
- **Kit / hardware** — what needs to be in the room before kids arrive
- **Group size** — how many students one facilitator can manage
- **Energy level** — physical activity involved (relevant for mixed classrooms)
- **The "win condition"** — what participants should take away; if you cannot answer "what did they build / run / do?" at the end, the session failed

All formats assume one lead facilitator. Add a co-facilitator for groups over 16 or where hardware troubleshooting is likely to bottleneck the room.

## FLL — SPIKE Prime Discovery (2 hours)

**Duration:** 2 hours | **Ages:** 8–12 | **Group size:** up to 20 (pairs) | **Energy:** medium

**Kit required:**

- One SPIKE Prime starter set per pair
- Laptops with SPIKE App 3.x installed — verify Bluetooth pairing works before the room opens
- A printed or projected reference photo of a simple two-motor drive base

**Win condition:** Every pair drives a self-built robot across the table under controller input before leaving.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Open** | Introduce the SPIKE Prime hub — name the ports, motors, and wheel parts. 5 min max. |
| 0:05 | **Build** | Pairs build a drive base from the reference photo. Let them struggle on axle alignment — it teaches more than any demo. Circulate; resist fixing things prematurely. |
| 0:35 | **Connect** | Connect hub to SPIKE App. Show: New Project, Blocks, Drive base template, Run. Every pair confirms "Connected" status before moving on. |
| 0:50 | **Program** | Add a second move block; change speed; change direction. One concept at a time. No sensor work in a 2-hour session. |
| 1:10 | **Drive** | Free driving. Set a simple obstacle or tape line on the table. Pairs drive around it. |
| 1:30 | **Challenge** | "Can you make the robot turn in a circle?" — open-ended; no solution given. Pairs experiment, share what worked. |
| 1:50 | **Reflect** | What is hard about making a robot do exactly what you want? How might a team train for a competition? 5-min whole-group discussion. |

**Facilitator notes:**

- Charge hubs to 100% the night before. Low battery during a demo is a session-killer.
- Download the SPIKE App to every laptop before the event — school Wi-Fi often blocks the installer during the session.
- If pairs finish the drive challenge early, give them the colour sensor and ask "can you make the robot stop when it hits the tape?" — open-ended, and it occupies fast groups without creating a two-speed room.

## FLL — Innovation Project Sprint (3 hours)

**Duration:** 3 hours | **Ages:** 10–16 | **Group size:** up to 24 (teams of 3–4) | **Energy:** low-medium

**Kit required:**

- No robot hardware required
- Poster paper, markers, sticky notes, timer
- Optional: printed FLL Core Values card for each team

**Win condition:** Each team presents a 3-minute pitch on a real-world problem and a proposed solution, with a named "expert" they would interview.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Frame** | What is the Innovation Project? (5 min). Key rule: you must interview a real person who lives this problem. |
| 0:10 | **Theme reveal** | Give teams a theme (any FLL season theme or a made-up one). 2 min to read. |
| 0:12 | **Brainstorm** | Silent sticky-note brainstorm: "Who has this problem? What is hard about it right now?" 8 min. |
| 0:20 | **Sort** | Teams cluster stickies into problem areas. Pick ONE. |
| 0:30 | **Research plan** | Who would you interview? What would you ask them? Write 3 interview questions. |
| 0:50 | **Solution design** | Generate 3 solution ideas; pick the most realistic. Simple sketch on poster paper. |
| 1:20 | **Prototype** | Build a physical or paper prototype of one component. No glue — tape and paper only. |
| 2:00 | **Pitch prep** | 3 min per team: who has the problem, what is your solution, what would you build or test, who would you interview. |
| 2:30 | **Presentations** | 3 min per team plus 1 min Q&A from peers. |
| 3:00 | **Debrief** | How is this different from a school science project? What would make a judge trust your solution? |

**Facilitator notes:**

- The "interview a real person" rule is the hardest concept for students to internalise. Name it explicitly in the framing, again when they write interview questions, and again during the debrief.
- Fast teams will want to make their prototype elaborate. Redirect: "the pitch matters more than the model at this stage."

## FTC — First Drive Workshop (3 hours)

**Duration:** 3 hours | **Grades:** 6–10 | **Group size:** up to 16 (teams of 4) | **Energy:** medium

**Kit required:**

- One mecanum-drive robot (pre-built or brought by a mentor team) per 8 participants — borrowing from a local FTC team is the easiest path
- Android phone or FTC Driver Hub running FTC Driver Station app
- At least one Control Hub with a working TeleOp OpMode loaded
- Optional: one extra REV Smart Robot Servo or motor to demo wiring

**Win condition:** Every participant drives the robot and changes one number in TeleOp code.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Overview** | What is FTC? Season format, team size, hardware kit. 5 min. Show the actual robot while talking. |
| 0:05 | **Hardware tour** | Walk through the Control Hub, motor cables, servo ports. 10 min. Let participants follow the wire from motor to port to code. |
| 0:15 | **Driver Station demo** | Pair the Driver Station; show the TeleOp OpMode selector; one mentor drives the robot. |
| 0:30 | **Driving rotation** | Rotate participants through driving — 2–3 min each. Simple obstacle course on the floor (tape boxes). |
| 1:00 | **Code tour** | Open the TeleOp file in OnBot Java or FTC Blocks. Show: where gamepad1.left_stick_y lives; where the motor power assignment lives. |
| 1:20 | **Tweak and test** | Each sub-group changes one value (speed cap, direction inversion, deadband). Push, test, observe. |
| 2:00 | **Robot game demo** | If available, run a mock match with a current-season game element: two teams, 2 min, keep score manually. |
| 2:30 | **What does joining a team look like?** | Walk through FIRST Team Search, registration timeline, cost range. Open Q&A. |
| 3:00 | **Close** |  |

**Facilitator notes:**

- The code-tweak block is where most sessions over-run. Narrow it: one file, one variable per group. If OnBot Java is slow to compile, pre-load Blocks to save time.
- An actual game element (a ring, a cone, a sample) makes the game-demo block significantly more engaging. Ask a local FTC team to lend one.

## FRC — Kickoff Experience (4 hours)

**Duration:** 4 hours | **Grades:** 9–12 or adults | **Group size:** up to 30 | **Energy:** medium-high

**Kit required:**

- One FRC robot (old or current-season) — borrow from a local team
- Laptop running Driver Station software
- A projector to display game animation or field layout
- A mock field element (even a cardboard box representing a game piece target)
- Optional: FIRST Robotics Competition game manual excerpt (public PDF)

**Win condition:** Participants can name all four sub-teams, have driven the robot, and can explain what Gracious Professionalism means in their own words.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Game reveal simulation** | Show a short segment of a past game animation. Give participants 5 min to read a one-page "Game summary" you have pre-prepared. |
| 0:10 | **Strategy session** | Small groups: "If you were building this robot, what would you prioritise?" Share back. |
| 0:30 | **Robot tour** | Walk through every subsystem: chassis, drivetrain, mechanisms, electrical panel, pneumatics if present. Participants follow the power flow from battery to motor. |
| 1:00 | **Driving rotation** | Rotate through driver and operator roles. 5 min per pair. Safety circle — no one stands within 1 metre of the robot while it moves. |
| 2:00 | **Build challenge** | Teams of 5 build a cardboard "intake mechanism" in 30 min using only tape, cardboard, and rubber bands. Must pick up a tennis ball or game piece. |
| 2:30 | **Test and demo** | Each team tests their intake. One-minute demo; peer feedback. |
| 3:00 | **Sub-team breakdown** | Explain mechanical, electrical and software, design and CAD, and Impact Award sub-teams. Each participant votes for their natural fit; discuss. |
| 3:20 | **GP panel** | 2–3 mentors or alumni answer: "Tell me about a time a team helped a competitor instead of beating them." What is Gracious Professionalism and why does it matter in FRC? |
| 3:45 | **Q&A and next steps** | Registration process, team search, time commitment, cost. |
| 4:00 | **Close** |  |

**Facilitator notes:**

- The build challenge is the most memorable block. Keep it short and low-stakes; the point is collaboration under time pressure, not engineering quality.
- Gracious Professionalism needs concrete examples, not a definition. The GP panel format works better than a slide.
- Safety: establish the safety circle rule before the robot moves and re-state it every rotation.

## VEX IQ — Build and Drive (2 hours)

**Duration:** 2 hours | **Ages:** 8–13 | **Group size:** up to 20 (pairs) | **Energy:** medium

**Kit required:**

- One VEX IQ Super Kit or Starter Kit per pair
- Controller per pair
- Chromebook or laptop with VEXcode IQ installed

**Win condition:** Every pair drives a self-built drive base under controller input.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Parts tour** | Name the Brain, Smart Motors, beams, and pins. Show how pins connect — the snap-click sound is distinctive. 5 min. |
| 0:05 | **Build** | Pairs build the reference drive base using VEX IQ Clawbot IQ Lite build instructions. No motors yet; assemble chassis first. |
| 0:30 | **Add motors** | Show how to connect Smart Motors to the chassis and plug into Ports 1 and 6 (left and right). |
| 0:45 | **First drive** | Open VEXcode IQ, New Blocks Project, use the built-in Drive Base template, connect Controller, Download, drive. Each pair drives for 2–3 min. |
| 1:10 | **Autonomous block** | Add a `drive forward for 500 mm` block. Run it. Does it go straight? Adjust if not. |
| 1:30 | **Challenge** | Tape a small course on the floor. "Can you navigate the course using only autonomous blocks — no driver?" Pairs experiment. |
| 1:50 | **Reflect** | What is different about autonomous vs driver control? Where might autonomous be useful in a real competition? |

**Facilitator notes:**

- VEX IQ pins are forgiving but kids often skip the click — check connections before the drive phase, not after.
- Port assignment matters: if motors are in the wrong ports, the robot drives sideways. Build a port cheatsheet for the wall.
- The VEX IQ Smart Motor includes a built-in gyro. You can hint at "what if the robot knew which way it was pointed?" without getting into IMU programming in a 2-hour intro session.

## VEX V5 — Strategy and Drive Workshop (3 hours)

**Duration:** 3 hours | **Grades:** 7–12 | **Group size:** up to 20 | **Energy:** medium

**Kit required:**

- One VEX V5 competition robot (borrow from a local VRC team if possible)
- V5 Controller and battery
- Game elements from the current or a recent season (rings, cubes, or generic substitutes)
- Laptop with VEXcode Pro V5 installed (optional — driving segment does not require it)

**Win condition:** Participants have driven the robot, discussed scoring trade-offs, and can name the three VRC event types.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **Season reveal** | Show the current or most recent VRC game animation. Give participants the one-page scoring summary. |
| 0:10 | **Strategy session** | "What scores the most points? What is hardest to execute? What would you design for?" Small groups, 10 min. Share back. |
| 0:30 | **Robot tour** | Walk through the V5 Brain, Smart Motors, pneumatics if present, power switch, controller pairing. |
| 0:45 | **Driving rotation** | Each participant drives the robot on a practice field or open floor. 2–3 min each, with a scorer counting game pieces. |
| 1:30 | **Code peek** | Open the TeleOp file in VEXcode Pro V5. Show the gamepad axis assignments. Change the deadband value; observe the effect. |
| 1:50 | **Autonomous intro** | Show a pre-recorded or live autonomous run. Discuss: how is the robot knowing where to go? (Encoders and IMU at a conceptual level.) |
| 2:10 | **Event types** | Explain Teamwork Challenge, Autonomous Coding Skills, Driver Skills. Why does a strong autonomous score matter across all three? |
| 2:30 | **Alliance selection mock** | Split into two alliances of four. Each alliance has 2 min to agree on a strategy. Run a 1-min match. Debrief: what would you change? |
| 2:50 | **Team formation Q&A** | RobotEvents search, registration timeline, kit cost, scrimmage opportunities. |
| 3:00 | **Close** |  |

**Facilitator notes:**

- The driving rotation with a scoring element is the highest-engagement block in this format. Allocate time generously; it also surfaces natural drivers for follow-up conversations.
- For the "code peek" block, you do not need the robot wired to a laptop — showing the code on a projector and explaining the logic is enough for a 20-minute intro.

## VEX U — College Team Intro Session (4 hours)

**Duration:** 4 hours | **Level:** College | **Group size:** up to 24 | **Energy:** medium

**Kit required:**

- At least one V5 robot (15-inch or 24-inch class)
- V5 Controller; laptop with PROS C++ installed
- Whiteboard for architecture diagrams
- Game manual for the current VURC season (public PDF — vexrobotics.com)

**Win condition:** Participants can explain the two-robot VURC format, have written one PROS function, and can name the four sub-team roles typical in a VEX U team.

### Agenda

| Time | Block | Activity |
|------|-------|----------|
| 0:00 | **What is VEX U?** | VURC format overview: two robots per alliance, 15-inch and 24-inch size classes, 15-sec Autonomous, Teamwork and Skills events. Compare briefly to V5RC. 15 min. |
| 0:15 | **Game manual tour** | Walk participants through the game manual structure: field layout, scoring table, autonomous bonus, Skills flow. Each pair reads one section; 5-min jigsaw share-out. |
| 0:45 | **Robot tour** | 15-inch vs 24-inch design tradeoffs: speed and agility vs capacity and reach. What decisions would you make differently? |
| 1:00 | **Driving session** | Each participant drives one robot for 2–3 min. Discuss: what feels different about controlling a 24-inch robot vs a 15-inch? |
| 1:45 | **PROS code walkthrough** | Open `src/main.cpp` in PROS. Walk through `initialize()`, `autonomous()`, `opcontrol()`. Show how to add a second motor group. |
| 2:15 | **Coding challenge** | Small groups: add a `driveStraight(500)` function to the provided skeleton. Test on the actual robot if time allows. |
| 3:00 | **Team architecture workshop** | Whiteboard exercise: map out all the work a VEX U team needs to do across a season. Assign to four buckets: Mechanical, Software, Documentation, Strategy. Who does what? |
| 3:30 | **Registration and competition calendar** | VURC registration, Skills-only Worlds path, Signature Events, off-season events. |
| 3:45 | **Open Q&A** | What would your first season look like? What is one thing you would want to tackle first? |
| 4:00 | **Close** |  |

**Facilitator notes:**

- VEX U often attracts engineering students expecting to hit the ground running. The coding challenge block is where you will see who is comfortable with C++ and who needs to pair-program. Steer toward pair-based work over solo contributions.
- The two-robot coordination concept is genuinely hard to implement in four hours. A conceptual whiteboard discussion is the right depth — do not try to write the coordination logic in code.
- PROS on Linux (common on engineering laptops) may need udev rules for the V5 USB port. Check this with participants before the session, not during.

## Adapting these formats

### Shorter than 2 hours?

Drop the challenge or open-drive block; keep the build and first run. Even 45 minutes can leave participants with a robot they drove themselves — that is the hook.

### Mixed age groups?

Pair an older student with a younger one during build and drive. Older students explain; they retain more. Younger students often build faster — use that energy.

### No kit available?

Run the FLL Innovation Project Sprint (3 hours) or the FRC Kickoff Experience without the drive time. Strategy, game analysis, and Gracious Professionalism conversations do not require hardware.

### Multi-day camp?

Stack formats across days: Day 1 is Build and Drive, Day 2 is Autonomous intro, Day 3 is Mini competition. Any format's "Challenge" block scales into a full day with more iteration time.
