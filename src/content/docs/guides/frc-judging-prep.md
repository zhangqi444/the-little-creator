FRC award judging is distributed across the event in ways that can surprise first-year teams. Unlike FLL's scheduled room-by-room sessions or FTC's single sit-down interview, FRC has **multiple simultaneous judging tracks** — pit interviews, scheduled Impact sessions, spontaneous roving judges, and direct observation of team behaviour on and off the field. Missing any one of these can cost you an award you otherwise earned.

This guide explains each track, what judges evaluate, and how to prepare students to present confidently under real event conditions.

:::note[Official criteria]
This guide explains the process and offers practical prep advice. For the exact award criteria and rubric, download the current season's [Awards Manual](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/frc/season-materials) from FIRST Resources. Criteria and award names change year to year — always verify against the current manual.
:::

## The FRC judging landscape

FRC runs several types of judged evaluation simultaneously across the event:

| Track | Who it's for | Format |
|---|---|---|
| **Pit interviews** | All teams | Roving judges visit the pit during quals; 5–10 min conversation |
| **Impact interview** | Teams applying for Impact | Scheduled 20–25 min formal session; student panel presentation + Q&A |
| **Engineering Inspiration interview** | Teams identified by judges | Similar format to Impact; may be by invitation |
| **Rookie All Star interview** | Rookie teams selected by judges | Informal to semi-formal; typically pit-based |
| **Spontaneous GP observations** | All teams | Judges observe team behaviour in pit, stands, field |
| **Submission-based awards** | Teams who applied (Innovation in Control, etc.) | Judges review submissions; may do pit follow-up |

Most teams encounter **pit interviews and GP observation**. Teams with strong outreach programs should additionally prepare a full **Impact presentation**. Every team should know which awards it's in contention for before the event so nothing catches them flat-footed.

## Pit interviews

Roving judges visit every pit — usually during the qualification rounds when matches are running and other students are in the stands. The window is short (5–10 minutes at most) and often unannounced.

### What judges ask in the pit

Judges don't follow a fixed script, but the topics are consistent across events:

- "Tell us about your team — how did you get started this season?"
- "Walk me through your robot's main subsystems and what each one does."
- "What's the most significant design change you made this season, and why did you make it?"
- "What's the toughest engineering problem you ran into, and how did you solve it?"
- "What are you still working on improving?"
- "Who on your team did what?" (engineering, programming, build, outreach — judges check for broad participation)

For submission-based awards (Innovation in Control, Excellence in Engineering, Industrial Design), judges who've read your submission will probe those areas specifically.

### How to staff your pit for judges

**Never leave the pit empty.** A judge who comes and finds no one loses interest and may not return.

**Have students present, not mentors.** If only mentors are available, they should flag the judge and say "our students are in the stands — give me 30 seconds to grab them." Mentors should not pitch the team.

**Identify your pit lead.** One student (not necessarily the captain) should own the pit interview: introduce the team, give the two-minute overview, and route specific questions to the right students.

**Have the robot visible and accessible.** Judges will look at mechanisms. Panels should be off or easily removable. If you have a mechanism that's hard to see in the pit, have a photo or diagram ready.

### The 2-minute robot overview

Practice this until any student on the team can give it smoothly:

1. **Team identity** — "We're team [number], from [city/school]. This is our third season." (10 seconds)
2. **Season context** — brief framing of the game and what the robot needs to do (15 seconds)
3. **Main subsystems** — drivetrain, intake, mechanism 1, mechanism 2; one sentence each on what it does and why you made that choice (60 seconds)
4. **Key iteration** — one specific design decision you changed mid-season and why (30 seconds)
5. **Hook** — one distinctive thing about your team that a judge will remember (15 seconds)

Two minutes is usually all you get before the judge asks a follow-up question. Don't spend it all on the drivetrain.

## The Impact interview

The **Impact Award** is FRC's most prestigious recognition. Applying requires submitting an Impact essay and a video before the event. Teams invited to the Impact judging session have a 20–25 minute formal interview with a panel of 4–6 senior judges.

This is the most demanding judging experience in FRC. Preparation matters enormously.

:::note[Not for everyone]
Impact is only realistic for teams with multi-year sustained outreach programs. If your team is in year 1–2, focus your preparation energy on pit interviews and Rookie All Star. See [FRC Awards](/guides/frc-awards/) for realistic year-one targets.
:::

### Structure of the Impact session

| Phase | Duration | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| Opening presentation | 8–10 min | Students present to judges using allowed materials (verify season restrictions) |
| Judge Q&A | 10–15 min | Judges probe essay, video, presentation; may redirect to specific students |
| Closing | 1–2 min | Judges thank the team; do not reveal outcomes |

### What Impact judges are looking for

Impact judges evaluate the *depth, breadth, and sustainability* of your community impact — not just what you did, but whether it changed anything and whether it will keep happening after your seniors graduate.

High-impact signals:
- **Measurable outcomes.** Not "we ran an FLL camp" but "we started 12 new FLL teams in three years; 8 are still active; 140 students who otherwise had no access to robotics have now competed."
- **Systems, not events.** Repeatable programs that exist independent of any single student: a curriculum you wrote, a partner organisation that continues the relationship, a team that keeps running without you.
- **Years of progression.** The story arcs. What did you do in year 1, how did you realise it was not enough, what did you build in year 2, what does year 3 look like?
- **Authentic student ownership.** Students ran the programs, students talk to the judges. Mentors provide infrastructure; the mission is the students'.

Common weaknesses judges probe:
- "You mention starting 5 FLL teams — how many are still active? What happened to the others?"
- "Who actually ran the summer camp — mentors or students? Walk us through a day."
- "What happens to this program when your current seniors leave?"
- "Your essay mentions a school partnership — has the school embedded anything into their curriculum, or do you run it as an add-on?"

### Preparing students for the Impact presentation

**Four to six students** typically present (verify the season-specific maximum). Each should have genuine ownership of a section of the story — not equal speaking time, but authentic expertise.

**Structure the presentation around your narrative arc.** Judges have read the essay. Don't recite it. Instead, bring it to life: tell the story of how you discovered a gap, what you tried first, what failed, what you built, and what the evidence shows.

**Rehearse the Q&A more than the presentation.** The 10–15 minute Q&A is where awards are won or lost. Run mock sessions with adults playing difficult judges. Use probing questions ("but is that actually sustainable?"). The team that gives the same polished presentation every time but folds under hard questions loses to the team with a rougher presentation and honest, deep answers.

**Assign the hard questions.** Identify the three weakest areas of your Impact story. Assign one student to own each. That student should be able to give a two-minute honest answer to the toughest version of that question.

### Day-of logistics for Impact

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| Arrive 10 minutes early | Session rooms often have strict start times |
| Know your materials rules | Some seasons allow display boards; others restrict them. Check the Awards Manual. |
| Bring backup copies of key materials | Essay, video link, any visual aids |
| Coach role | Silent unless judges address them directly |
| Post-session | Thank judges and leave promptly. No outcome will be shared. |

## Engineering Inspiration interview

Engineering Inspiration is for teams with a strong focus on *engineering education* outreach — curriculum programs, school partnerships, student mentorship for younger engineers. The format mirrors Impact (scheduled interview, student panel) but the scope is narrower.

If your judges invite you to an Engineering Inspiration session, apply the same preparation discipline as Impact: genuine student ownership, specific measurable outcomes, rehearsed Q&A. The same questions that probe Impact sustainability apply here.

## Rookie All Star

Rookie All Star is the year-one equivalent of Impact. A well-prepared rookie team can realistically win this.

Judges evaluate:

- **Early outreach**: Did you already start giving back, even in year one?
- **Team structure**: Is there a plan for year two? Do you have underclassmen who will carry it forward?
- **Engineering process**: Did your students actually engineer the robot, or did mentors do it for them?
- **Documentation**: Is there evidence of your season's engineering decisions?

Rookie All Star is typically awarded based on pit observations and a brief pit interview — there's no separate session room for most events. Your pit lead's two-minute overview and the team's authentic enthusiasm matter more than polish.

## GP observation: the continuous interview

Gracious Professionalism is observed all day, not just in the judging room. Judges — and volunteers who feed observations to judges — watch for:

- **In the pit**: Do you help neighbouring teams? If an alliance partner's mechanism breaks, does your team offer parts or tools?
- **In the stands**: How does your team react to a bad call or a match loss?
- **On the field**: Does your drive team behave with dignity toward refs, field staff, and opponents?
- **Around the venue**: Are team members welcoming when strangers approach? Do they share information about their robot?

GP is not a box to check. Judges can tell the difference between a team that rehearsed nice words and a team that acts with integrity under pressure.

Common GP mistakes that hurt teams at judging:

- Cheering loudly against a specific opposing team rather than for your alliance
- Pit neighbours who report that your team was dismissive or territorial about shared resources
- Drive team members who visibly argue with referees
- Students in the stands loudly explaining why a specific ref call was wrong

## Preparing the whole team

Most teams focus prep on the Impact presentation team and ignore the 90% of students in the stands or the pit. Every student should know:

1. **The team's 30-second pitch**: who you are, what you built, one distinctive thing
2. **Two or three things your robot does**: non-technical enough to explain to a sponsor, specific enough to satisfy a judge
3. **The GP standard**: what it looks like in practice at this specific event
4. **Which judging track you're competing in**: Rookie All Star? Impact? Just pit interviews?

Judges roving the stands sometimes ask students casual questions. A student who says "I don't really work on the robot" is a missed opportunity. A student who says "Honestly our intake was really hard — we went through four versions before it stopped jamming" is evidence of a team that actually does FIRST right.

## Common judging mistakes

| Mistake | Better approach |
|---|---|
| Pit is unstaffed when judges arrive | Always have at least two students in the pit during qualification rounds |
| Mentor answers judge's questions to "help" | Mentors stay silent; judges want to hear from students |
| Students describe robot features, not design decisions | Practice the *why* behind each choice, not just the *what* |
| Only one student can explain the whole robot | Each student owns a subsystem and practices explaining it |
| Impact presentation sounds like a brochure | Tell the real story: gaps found, things that failed, what changed |
| Team has not rehearsed Q&A | Run mock sessions with adults asking probing questions at least two weeks out |
| GP is treated as a judged session, not a daily standard | Build the culture during practices, not the week before the event |
| Team does not know which awards apply to them | Assign an awards captain; map your team's work to award criteria before the event |

## Checklist: two weeks out

- [ ] Decide which awards you're applying for; assign a student "awards captain"
- [ ] Identify the students who will present at the Impact or Engineering Inspiration interview (if applicable)
- [ ] Run the 2-minute robot overview drill with every student who will be in the pit
- [ ] Run at least two full mock Impact Q&A sessions with adults asking hard questions
- [ ] Review any award submissions (Innovation in Control, Excellence in Engineering) against judging criteria
- [ ] Brief every team member on GP expectations for this event — specific behaviours, not generic reminders
- [ ] Confirm pit staffing plan: who is in the pit during each qualification round?
