A working rubric for assessing the FLL Core Values in your classroom or club during the season. Designed to give you actionable observation targets, not a tournament-style scoring document.

> **Authoritative source disclaimer:** The official judging rubric used at FLL tournaments is published by FIRST in the season's [Challenge guide](/resources/fll-resource-map/). This page is a *practice* tool for use throughout the season — not a substitute for the official document. When in doubt about competition scoring, the official rubric wins.

## How to use this rubric

Pick **one Core Value per session** rather than trying to assess all six every meeting. Observe during normal team activity — not in a separate "values test." Score each kid (or the team as a whole) on a 1–4 scale at the end of the session, then share back with kids what you observed.

The goal is **growth visible across the season**, not a single high score. A kid moving from 2 → 3 over six weeks is more valuable than a kid sitting at 4 from day one.

## The six Core Values

FIRST defines six Core Values for FLL: **Discovery, Innovation, Impact, Inclusion, Teamwork, and Fun.** Below, each has a one-line definition, observable behaviors per level, and one or two coaching prompts.

### Discovery

*"We explore new skills and ideas."*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | Kid asks a "what if?" question that the team hadn't considered, then tries it. Documents the result in the notebook regardless of outcome. |
| 3 | Kid tries new approaches when the obvious one fails. Asks questions about how things work beyond what's strictly needed. |
| 2 | Kid follows the plan but doesn't volunteer new ideas. Will try suggestions when prompted. |
| 1 | Kid avoids unfamiliar tasks. Sticks to one role even when invited to try another. |

**Coaching prompts:** "What's something you don't understand yet?" • "What would happen if we tried ___?"

### Innovation

*"We use creativity and persistence to solve problems."*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | When something fails, kid proposes a meaningfully different approach, not just an incremental tweak. Tries multiple iterations without giving up. |
| 3 | Generates one alternative when blocked. Persists through 2–3 failed attempts before asking for help. |
| 2 | Asks for help quickly when something doesn't work. Tries the same approach twice before moving on. |
| 1 | Stops working when blocked. Waits to be told what to try next. |

**Coaching prompts:** "What's a different way to think about this?" • "What did you change between attempt one and attempt two?"

### Impact

*"We apply what we learn to improve our world."*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | Kid connects the Innovation Project to a real person they know and can describe how the solution would help that person specifically. |
| 3 | Kid can explain who benefits from the team's Innovation Project and why. |
| 2 | Kid knows the team's Innovation Project topic but can't articulate the impact clearly. |
| 1 | Kid is unsure what the Innovation Project is or what problem it addresses. |

**Coaching prompts:** "Who would use this and how?" • "How is this different from what already exists?"

### Inclusion

*"We respect each other and embrace our differences."*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | Kid actively invites quieter teammates into discussions. Notices when someone is being talked over and creates space for them. |
| 3 | Kid works well with everyone on the team, including kids they're not close friends with. |
| 2 | Kid works with their preferred subset of the team but not others. |
| 1 | Kid excludes specific teammates or undermines their contributions. |

**Coaching prompts:** "Whose idea haven't we heard yet?" • "What did Sam suggest? Let's try that."

### Teamwork

*"We are stronger when we work together."*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | Kid takes on tasks no one else wants. Shares credit when team succeeds. Picks up slack without being asked. |
| 3 | Kid does their assigned role reliably. Asks for or offers help when needed. |
| 2 | Kid does their role but won't help with tasks "not theirs." Needs reminders to coordinate. |
| 1 | Kid works independently and resists collaboration. Hoards or refuses tools/parts. |

**Coaching prompts:** "Who could you team up with on this?" • "What does your teammate need from you right now?"

### Fun

*"We celebrate what we do!"*

| Score | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| 4 | Kid is visibly enjoying the work and helps others enjoy it too. Celebrates teammates' wins. |
| 3 | Kid is engaged and positive. Smiles when something works. |
| 2 | Kid is going through the motions. Engagement varies by task. |
| 1 | Kid seems unhappy or bored. May be a signal that the role/team/format isn't a fit. |

**Coaching prompts:** Don't try to coach Fun directly. Coach the *conditions* — workload balance, role rotation, social dynamics. If Fun scores are low across the team, restructure meetings.

## Suggested observation cadence

| Week | Focus Core Value |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1–2 | Inclusion (sets group norms early) |
| Weeks 3–4 | Discovery + Innovation (peak build-iteration phase) |
| Week 5 | Teamwork (when interpersonal tension typically appears) |
| Weeks 6–7 | Impact (Innovation Project intensifies) |
| Week 8+ | Fun (check the energy before tournament) |

This is a suggestion, not a rule. Adapt to what your team needs most.

## Sharing observations with kids

After observing, give specific feedback. Not *"You did great on Teamwork!"* but *"When Aiden was stuck on the gear ratio, you stopped your own work and showed him how you'd solved it last week. That's exactly what Teamwork looks like."*

Specificity is what makes the rubric useful. Generic praise teaches nothing.

## What this rubric is not

- Not the official tournament rubric. That's published in the season's Challenge guide and changes annually.
- Not an evaluation tool for grading kids. Use it for growth tracking and conversation, not assessment.
- Not a substitute for actually doing FLL. Kids learn the Core Values by living them in the team, not by being scored on them.
