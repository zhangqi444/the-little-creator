You know how to fix the robot. You can see the problem. Your fingers are itching. The clock is ticking.

Don't touch it.

This is the central challenge of FLL coaching. Your job is not to solve problems — it's to create conditions where kids can solve problems. That's harder than it sounds, especially if you have engineering or technical background.

This guide gives you practical techniques for staying in the coach role.

---

## Why it matters (beyond just rules)

FIRST has rules about adult involvement at competition, but that's not the main reason to hold back. The real reason: kids who solve their own problems grow. Kids who watch adults solve problems do not.

When you fix the robot, the kid learns that adults fix things. When the kid fixes the robot, they learn that they can fix things. The second lesson is the one that matters 10 years from now.

Also: judges can tell. Robot Design judges have interviewed thousands of teams. They know immediately when kids are reciting what adults told them versus actually understanding their own robot.

---

## The question technique

The most powerful tool in the coaching toolkit: ask questions instead of giving answers.

**Instead of:** "You need to reset the gyro before each run."
**Ask:** "Why do you think the robot is drifting now but wasn't earlier? What changed?"

**Instead of:** "That attachment won't work because the gear ratio is wrong."
**Ask:** "What's happening when you run it? What do you think is causing that?"

**Instead of:** "You should present in this order: problem, solution, stakeholder, sharing plan."
**Ask:** "When you explained your project to me just now, what felt unclear? How would you want to rearrange it?"

The goal is to get the kid to the answer, not to give the answer. It takes longer. Do it anyway.

### When questions feel like torture

Sometimes kids are stuck and questions just frustrate them. Signs you've pushed questioning too far:
- A kid is visibly distressed (not just mildly frustrated — genuinely upset)
- The same question loop has repeated three or more times
- Time pressure is real and you're less than two days from a competition

In those moments, it's okay to give a hint: "What if you checked whether the problem is mechanical or in the code?" (not "the problem is the gear slip in your attachment"). One level of indirection is still better than a direct answer.

## Handling the dominant kid

Almost every team has one: the kid who moves fastest, talks most, and gets to solutions before others can think. Left unchecked, they'll build the whole robot and the others will check out.

**Techniques:**

- **Rotating roles:** No one does the same job two sessions in a row. The kid who programmed last week must hand off and observe this week.
- **Parallel work:** Split into two groups working on different problems at the same time. The dominant kid can't be everywhere.
- **Explicit turn-taking:** "Before we decide, I want to hear from everyone. [Name], you go first." Start with quieter kids.
- **Ownership assignments:** "This attachment belongs to [Name]. [Dominant kid], your job is to explain it without touching it." Transfers ownership explicitly.
- **Let natural consequences work:** If the dominant kid makes a decision that doesn't work out, that's a learning moment for everyone — including them.

---

## Handling the disengaged kid

The opposite problem: a kid who hangs back, seems checked out, doesn't contribute.

Usually this happens because:
1. They don't know how to contribute (skill gap)
2. They feel overshadowed (social gap)
3. They're not interested in the current task (engagement gap)

**Approach by cause:**
- **Skill gap:** Pair them with someone who explains patiently. Give them a specific, bounded task: "Your job this session is to figure out how this sensor works and teach us."
- **Social gap:** One-on-one check-in away from the group. "I've noticed you're not as involved lately. Is there a part of this you'd like to own?" They may surprise you.
- **Engagement gap:** Let them own the part they care about. The kid who hates programming but loves presenting should own the Innovation Project presentation. Don't force everyone into every workstream.

## Letting things fail

The robot is going to fail. The presentation is going to be imperfect. Some missions will not get completed on time.

Your instinct will be to prevent this. Resist it.

Failure in practice is cheap. Failure at competition is just a bit more expensive. Both are learning. The team that has experienced failure and recovered from it is more resilient than the team that the coach protected from ever failing.

**Practical rule:** If it's more than one week before competition, let it fail and debrief. If it's the night before competition, maybe intervene — but even then, involve the kids in the fix.

---

## The debrief habit

After every practice, take 5 minutes for a group debrief:

- "What went well today?"
- "What's one thing we want to do differently next time?"
- "Who did something that helped the team?" (call out specific teammates)

This builds the habit of reflection. It's also your best window into how kids are feeling — who's frustrated, who's quietly proud, who needs more support.

Write down what comes up. The best coaches keep a simple coach's log — one paragraph per session. Three months later when you're writing the engineering notebook, you'll be glad you did.

---

## Coaching yourself

Most FLL coaches feel like imposters at some point. "I don't know enough to be doing this." "What am I doing here?"

Here's the thing: you don't need to know the answers. You need to know how to ask good questions and create a safe space for kids to figure things out. That's a people skill, not a technical skill.

The best FLL coaches aren't always the most technical. They're the ones who ask good questions, stay calm when things break, and genuinely enjoy watching kids figure stuff out.

That's the job. You can do that.
