Alliance selection is one of the highest-leverage moments in a competition. The right partner amplifies your strengths. The wrong one limits you. The difference between choosing blindly and choosing well is **scouting** — systematic match observation that turns live data into decision-making power.

This guide covers practical scouting for FTC, FRC, and VEX V5/VRC, where alliance selection determines playoff brackets. FLL and VEX IQ don't use competitive alliance selection (partners are assigned or randomised), so this guide doesn't apply to those programs.

---

## Why scouting matters

Most teams lose alliance selection fights for one of three reasons:

1. They didn't know enough about other teams' robots to make a confident pick.
2. They waited too long to approach potential partners.
3. They picked for peak performance instead of consistent performance.

A scouted team you've watched across five matches is worth far more than a team you've heard about. Scouting converts anecdote into evidence.

## Program-specific context

### FTC — two-team alliances

FTC playoff alliances have **two robots**. The top 4 teams after qualifying rounds select first. At most regional events there are 20–30 teams; at state/world championships significantly more.

**What you're looking for:** A robot that reliably scores in the areas you don't, doesn't break down, and communicates clearly. FTC matches are 2.5 minutes — reliability across five-plus matches matters more than one exceptional performance.

### FRC — three-team alliances

FRC playoff alliances have **three robots**. The top 8 teams select, each choosing two partners. At a regional event (40–60 teams) you'll watch 12 qualifying matches per team; at district events the math is similar.

**What you're looking for:** Complementary roles. High-level alliances in FRC typically have a strong autonomous contributor, a consistent teleop scorer, and a capable endgame/defence robot. Know which role you fill before you start scouting for partners.

### VEX V5 / VRC — two-team alliances

VRC playoff alliances have **two robots**, same as FTC. The top teams (usually top 8–16 depending on event size) are ranked by Qualifying Points after Swiss-style rounds.

**What you're looking for:** A driver-controlled consistency record and Skills Challenge scores as a proxy for robot reliability. VRC scouting also includes notebook quality as an indirect signal of team process maturity — strong notebook teams tend to iterate more predictably.

## Step 1 — Set up your scouting system

A scouting system is any process that lets you record data during matches and retrieve it during alliance selection. It doesn't need to be fancy.

### Minimum viable scouting (solo or small team)

If you're a small team (3–5 people), one or two people can scout while others compete.

**What to record per match:**
- Team number
- Match number
- Autonomous: did it work? What did it score?
- TeleOp: main scoring action, approximate cycle count or score contribution
- Endgame/parking: achieved or not?
- Reliability: any disconnects, tipping, mechanism failures?
- Notes: unusual behaviour, interesting capability, communication style on field

A simple spreadsheet (one row per team per match) is enough. Google Sheets shared on a phone works fine.

### Expanded scouting (FRC-scale teams)

FRC teams typically field dedicated scouting squads of 4–8 people, each watching a specific drive station position or alliance colour across every match.

**Standard FRC scouting data points:**
- Autonomous: did it run? Starting position? Points scored? Leave/note?
- TeleOp: cycles per match (note pickups + scores separately), missed pickups, cooperative play
- Endgame: climb level achieved, time to climb
- Fouls: received yellow/red cards? Regular penalties?
- Defended: how did the robot perform under defence?
- Defended others: did it play defence? Effective?

FRC teams often use purpose-built scouting apps (Blue Alliance, ScoutingPASS, or custom tools) that export to a spreadsheet for sorting. The format matters less than the discipline of actually filling it in for every match.

## Step 2 — Watch matches with purpose

There is a difference between watching a match and scouting a match. Scouting means you already know what you're looking for before the match starts.

### Before the match starts

- Write down the team numbers for both alliances.
- Know which team you're assigned to watch (if you're in a group).
- Watch the pit or field setup: is the robot at the field, is the drive team calm, any last-minute repairs?

### During autonomous

- Did it complete its routine?
- What did it score and where?
- Did it leave the starting zone? (FRC)
- Did it interfere with alliance partners?

### During driver-controlled period

- Count cycles — or at minimum record whether the team scored frequently, occasionally, or rarely.
- Watch what they **attempt vs. complete**. A team that attempts 10 cycles and completes 6 is different from one that attempts 6 and completes 6.
- Does the drive team communicate with their alliance partner? Or ignore them?
- Watch for robot defensive behaviour: pushy, evasive, or neither?

### Endgame / last 30 seconds

- Did they achieve the endgame goal?
- How long did it take? Did they rush or plan?
- Did they help or block their partner?

### After the match

- Mark any reliability issues: tip-overs, brownouts, mechanism jams, disconnects.
- Write a one-sentence summary while the match is fresh: "Fast cycles, weak auto, reliable but slow climber."

## Step 3 — Organise your data

Raw observations are only useful if you can sort and filter them. After every few matches (or at lunch), consolidate.

**Useful aggregates to track:**

| Column | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Average TeleOp score | Overall offensive capability |
| Autonomous reliability (%) | Matches where auto ran successfully |
| Endgame completion (%) | Consistency in the final scoring period |
| Breakdown count | Number of matches with mechanical/connection issues |
| Matches played | Low match count = smaller sample, use with caution |
| Notes summary | Qualitative patterns |

Sort by average score descending — that's your starting list. Then filter for reliability: a team with a high average but two breakdowns is lower-priority than one with a slightly lower average and zero breakdowns.

## Step 4 — Pit scouting

Scouting isn't only watching matches. A 3-minute pit visit before or between rounds yields information that match video can't show.

**Questions to ask (politely, briefly):**

- "What's your robot designed to do?" — Lets them describe their own strengths.
- "How's it running today?" — A team that says "we had a motor issue we fixed" is honest about reliability.
- "Have you had any disconnects?" — Robots on the cusp of alliance selection that have had connection issues are riskier picks.
- "Are you hoping to be picked, or are you planning to pick?" — Not everyone will answer, but knowing their expectations helps.

Avoid questions that feel interrogative. You're having a brief friendly conversation, not an interview. Teams that feel respected are more likely to be good alliance partners if you end up paired together.

## Step 5 — Alliance selection strategy

By the time rankings are finalised, you should have reviewed your data and have a shortlist of 3–6 target partners ranked by priority.

### Know your role first

Before you pick, know what you bring to an alliance:
- Are you a reliable high-scorer in TeleOp?
- Do you have a strong autonomous?
- Are you primarily a support/endgame robot?

Your ideal partner **complements** your role, not duplicates it. Two robots that both do the same thing may underperform a pair where each handles something different.

### Build a tiered pick list

| Tier | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 1st pick target | Best reliable scorer you'd want as first partner |
| 2nd pick target | Backup if 1st pick is taken; also consider who fills a role gap |
| Alternate picks | 3–4 additional options if your preferred picks are already chosen |
| Avoid list | Teams with consistent breakdowns, fouls, or communication issues |

In FRC, the 3rd pick (2nd partner) often matters as much as the first. Consider it as part of your strategy, not an afterthought.

### The selection conversation

When you approach a team to invite them:

1. **Be direct.** "We'd like to pick you as our first alliance partner if you're available." They'll say yes, no, or that they're expecting to pick someone themselves.
2. **Be brief.** They're busy. Give them 30 seconds to decide or to say they'll think about it.
3. **If they say no**, move on immediately. Don't show frustration.
4. **Follow up** — after rankings are posted, confirm with your targets before selection begins.

Some events allow teams to pre-commit to alliances informally before the official selection period starts. This is common and acceptable. Just be honest about your intentions.

### Being picked vs. picking

If you're in the mid-ranks (not in the top-4 or top-8 selecting), your job is to make yourself visible and desirable.

- **Post a summary of your robot's strengths** on your team page or pit sign.
- **Approach teams you'd like to be picked by** before rankings finalise. A brief "We'd be happy to be considered as your second pick" conversation is normal.
- **Be easy to reach** — make sure selecting teams know where your pit is and how to find you.

## Common scouting mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Better approach |
|---|---|---|
| Only scouting the top teams | You may need picks 2 and 3; mid-tier consistency matters | Scout every team across all matches |
| Prioritising peak score over average | One spectacular match can mislead you | Weight average and reliability equally |
| Ignoring autonomous | Auto scores are free points every match | Track autonomous separately; even a partial auto adds up |
| Not tracking breakdowns | A team may look strong but break every third match | Record any mechanical/connection failure, even minor |
| Waiting until rankings post to talk to partners | The best partners get locked up during qualifying | Start conversations before the final round |
| Only scouting your "side" | In FRC, alliance partners are both equally important | Cross-train so multiple people can cover any position |
| Relying on reputation alone | A team's result from a previous event may not reflect today | Prioritise data from this event over past reputation |
