Competition day is exciting. It's also stressful — for students, for coaches, and honestly for parents too. Some nerves are good; they sharpen focus. But too much anxiety can shut kids down, make matches worse, and turn a great day into a miserable one.

This guide is about helping the kids on your team manage that anxiety well — before the event, during it, and after.

---

## Why competition nerves happen

Nerves are a normal response to genuinely uncertain, high-stakes situations. Your kid has practiced, built, programmed, and prepared — and now it all happens in public, in front of judges, with other teams watching, and the result is unknown.

That's the whole point. If you already knew you were going to win, it wouldn't feel like much.

Understanding why nerves happen helps you respond constructively rather than dismissively:

- **Fear of failure in public** — especially for students who've never competed before.
- **Attachment to outcomes** — wanting to win (or qualify, or advance) strongly enough that the possibility of not winning feels catastrophic.
- **Uncertainty about the unfamiliar** — the event venue, the format, the schedule, unfamiliar teams, unfamiliar faces.
- **Accumulated pressure** — from parents' expectations, coaches' investment, or internal perfectionism.
- **Technical anxiety** — the robot worked yesterday; will it work today?

The goal isn't to eliminate nerves. It's to keep them in the useful range.

## Before the event: preparation reduces anxiety

Most competition-day anxiety can be reduced by reducing uncertainty beforehand. Here's what actually helps:

### Simulate the event environment

Run at least one full practice that looks like competition day:
- Timed rounds, no replays.
- Limited pit time between "matches."
- Team members presenting to each other as if to judges.

Familiarity with the format dramatically reduces anxiety on the day.

### Walk through logistics out loud

The unknown logistics create disproportionate anxiety. Go through:
- What time do we leave? Who is our transport?
- What does the venue look like? (Show photos if you can find them.)
- What happens when we arrive — where do we check in, where is the pit, when is the first match?
- Who is responsible for what on the day?

For younger teams (FLL, VEX IQ), parents and coaches can do this together. For older teams (FRC, VEX V5), student leaders can run the logistics walkthrough themselves.

### Set expectations, not predictions

Before any competition, tell your team what success looks like — and define it in terms they can control:
- "We're going to give our best performance of this robot, whatever that means today."
- "We're going to do everything we've prepared for in judging. We can control that."
- "We're going to make at least one new connection with another team."

Avoid outcomes-based framing: "We could definitely make it to State if we focus." This puts kids in the position of fearing they've let everyone down if they don't advance.

### Reduce the logistics burden on the kids

Arriving late, forgetting equipment, or scrambling for food saps mental energy that kids need for competing. Coaches and parent volunteers can own:
- Packing checklists (see [Tournament Day Checklist](/guides/tournament-day-checklist/))
- Transport timing
- Food and snacks throughout the day

When kids don't have to worry about logistics, they can channel their energy into competing.

## Morning of the event: the arriving hours

### Give extra travel buffer

Tournament venues are often loud, crowded, and confusing on arrival. Schedule an extra 30 minutes beyond what you think you need. Rushing into a noisy venue immediately spikes anxiety.

### Normalise the nerves early

When kids arrive, nervous energy is normal and visible. Name it:
- "Everyone feels nervous before their first event. That's completely normal."
- "This is what it feels like when something matters to you. That's a good thing."

Don't try to argue kids out of feeling nervous or tell them "you'll be fine." Acknowledge it, name it as normal, and move forward.

### Establish a team routine

A short pre-event team ritual (5–10 minutes) can ground anxious kids:
- Team huddle with a shared phrase or cheer.
- A few minutes of quiet focus if the team is introverted.
- A physical warm-up — stretches, a short walk around the venue.
- For older teams, a brief technical walkthrough: "Here's the match plan. Here's what we do if X happens."

The routine itself matters less than its consistency — it signals "we've done this before, we know what to do."

### Let them explore the venue

If time allows before the first match, let kids walk around the venue, watch other teams pit, and watch a match or two. Familiarity with the physical space significantly reduces anxiety.

## During the event: match by match

### Regulate your own anxiety first

Kids are exquisitely sensitive to adult anxiety. If the coach is visibly tense, the team will absorb it.

Before any interaction with a nervous kid, take a breath. You don't have to be calm — but you need to be the most regulated adult in the vicinity.

This applies especially to parents watching from the stands. Audible parental anxiety — sighing loudly, making worried faces, calling out instructions — communicates to the child that the situation is dangerous. Save your emotions for the drive home.

### Short check-ins, not long debriefs

Between matches, keep team interactions brief and focused:
- "What worked?" (30 seconds)
- "What are we doing differently next match?" (30 seconds)
- "Any mechanical issues?" (30 seconds)

Long emotional debriefs after a bad match are well-intentioned but often make things worse. Kids need to stabilise, not rehash. Save the full review for after the event.

### Separate the robot from the kid

When something goes wrong — the robot fails a mission, a match score is low, the program crashes — resist the instinct to jump to analysis. First:
- Acknowledge what happened: "That was frustrating."
- Separate the event from the person: "That's what happened in the match. That's not who you are."
- Then, and only then: "What do we want to do differently?"

FLL and VEX IQ teams especially need this because kids this age often fuse their self-worth with performance outcomes.

### Let kids process differently

Some kids regulate by talking. Some need five minutes alone. Some want to immediately go fix something. Some want to eat.

Coaches: read your team. Don't enforce a single emotional processing style on everyone. Give kids what they need to reset before the next match.

### Celebrate small wins visibly

Especially in multi-round formats (FLL, VEX IQ Teamwork, FTC league), a single good moment can reset the emotional state of a team. Make it visible when something goes right:
- A mission that scored when it usually doesn't.
- A clean drive with no penalties.
- A judging session where a kid answered a hard question well.

Don't wait for the final scoreboard.

## If a kid is very distressed

Sometimes anxiety crosses from normal nerves into genuine distress — crying, withdrawal, physical symptoms (headache, stomach ache), refusal to participate.

When this happens:

1. **Move them out of the noise.** A quiet corner, outside the venue for a moment, or the car if necessary. Stimulating environments make distress worse.
2. **Don't try to problem-solve immediately.** Sit with them. "This is hard. I'm here." Physical presence is more useful than words right now.
3. **Ask what they need.** "Do you need a minute? Do you want to talk? Do you want some water?" Let them choose.
4. **Don't push them back in immediately.** Give them time. A forced return to the match table while still distressed will go worse than waiting five minutes.
5. **Keep the door open without pressuring.** "We have [X] time before the next match. No rush — I'll let you know when we're getting close." This gives them a timeline without forcing an immediate decision.

If a kid is consistently distressed across multiple events, that's information worth bringing to parents (or listening to as a parent). It may indicate the pressure level around competition is too high, or that the format isn't a good fit for this child right now.

## After the event: the debrief that helps

### Wait until the car ride home

The most effective debrief happens 30–60 minutes after the event ends, not immediately in the venue. Emotions are still high in the venue. Let them settle.

### Ask about experience before outcome

Start with:
- "What was the best moment of the day?"
- "What's something you're proud of?"
- "What was the most fun part?"

Then:
- "Is there anything you'd want to do differently next time?"

Only after this: "How do you feel about how the overall event went?"

Leading with outcome ("We didn't advance — what happened?") triggers defensiveness and shame. Leading with experience signals that the whole day mattered, not just the result.

### Validate the full emotional range

Kids who are disappointed, frustrated, or sad after a competition that didn't go as hoped are having completely valid responses. Don't rush them past these emotions:
- "Of course you're disappointed. You worked really hard and you wanted to advance. That makes total sense."

And for students who are unexpectedly happy despite a poor score:
- "You had fun and that's real. That matters."

Competitive outcomes and emotional wellbeing are separate. Don't conflate them.

### Separate this event from the program

Especially for first-year teams: one disappointing competition is data, not destiny. Make sure kids (and parents) hear:
- "One event is one data point. The season is longer than this."
- "Teams that keep going after a hard event learn the most."
- "Judges and robot issues can vary a lot between events. Your work doesn't change based on today's result."

## Program-specific notes

### FLL (ages 8–16, three divisions)

FLL is the youngest competitive audience. Kids in FLL Explore (ages 6–10) and FLL Challenge (ages 9–16 lower end) are particularly likely to fuse outcome with self-worth.

- Core Values judging in FLL explicitly assesses how teams handle challenges — you can reframe a hard match as "we just demonstrated Core Values under pressure."
- FLL events are deliberately celebratory in tone. Use the event culture as a resource: the GP spirit is visible everywhere, and it normalises struggle.
- Parents of FLL kids have the most direct influence. A quiet word from a parent in the stands can make or break a kid's experience.

### VEX IQ (ages 8–14)

VIQRC's collaborative Teamwork format means kids are playing with — not against — alliance partners they meet on the day. This often reduces anxiety (it's not zero-sum) but introduces new pressure: not wanting to let down a team you just met.

- Before Teamwork rounds, give kids a quick framing: "Your job is to play your part well and communicate. You can't control what your partner does."
- Autonomous Coding Skills runs are done alone — closer to FLL's individual pressure pattern. Treat them similarly.

### FTC (grades 7–12)

FTC is often a first major solo-team competition for many students. The Engineering Portfolio interview and judging add a layer of anxiety that pure robot performance doesn't: public speaking, adult judges, being evaluated as a person not just as a robot.

- Normalize judging nerves separately from robot nerves. "It's okay to feel nervous in the judging room. The judges are there to learn from you, not to catch you out."
- FTC allows coaches in the pit but not at the match field. Kids drive and control their robot alone at the match field. Make sure this is well-rehearsed before competition day.

### FRC (grades 9–12)

FRC is a large-event environment with hundreds of people, loud music, light shows, and significant school and mentor investment. First-year students at FRC events often describe feeling overwhelmed.

- Pit time management matters more at FRC than almost anywhere else. A chaotic pit creates ambient anxiety. Designate one pit manager whose job is process, not robot work.
- FRC alliance selection is publicly announced and can be emotionally difficult for teams not picked early. Prepare students for the possibility before it happens.
- The Impact Award interview is high-stakes for the student presenters specifically. Give the Impact sub-team dedicated emotional support separate from the rest of the team.

### VEX V5 / VRC (grades 8–12)

V5RC is structured around skills-based rankings and alliance selection, which rewards consistency over a single-match high. Students who understand this are less likely to catastrophize a single bad match.

- Emphasize the Skills Challenge as a measure of robot consistency that the team controls entirely — a reliable anxiety antidote when alliance match results feel chaotic.
- Alliance selection at VRC events can be tense. See [VEX V5 Tournament Guide](/guides/vex-v5-tournament/) for how to prepare student representatives for the experience.

### VEX U (college)

College students experience competition anxiety differently from K–12 kids — usually with more cognitive control but also more at stake (team identity, mentorship reputation, academic year effort). VURC anxiety is more likely to show as tension between team members than as visible distress.

- Watch for interpersonal conflict as an anxiety proxy. Pre-competition tension between team members often reflects anxiety, not genuine disagreement.
- VEX U teams are adults — treat them as such. Coaching intervention should be invited, not imposed. Offer resources and let the team self-manage.

## A note for parents

You're not just supporting your kid at a competition. You're modeling how to handle high-pressure, uncertain situations.

Your kid is watching how you respond when the robot fails, when a judge gives unexpected feedback, when another team scores higher. Whatever you do in those moments, your kid internalises as "this is how adults handle hard things."

The most useful thing you can do on competition day is let it matter — and let it be okay when it doesn't go perfectly. Show them that you're proud of their effort, that you trust them to handle the outcome, and that you'll be there either way.

That's worth more than any strategy tip in this guide.

## Quick reference: what helps vs. what doesn't

| Situation | Helps | Doesn't help |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-event nerves | Name them as normal; simulate the event beforehand | Dismissing ("you'll be fine") or amplifying ("this is so important") |
| Robot failure mid-event | Brief acknowledge + reset + next match focus | Long debrief, analysis, blame |
| Very distressed kid | Move to quiet space; sit with them; ask what they need | Logical argument; forcing return; minimizing |
| Parent watching from stands | Quiet support; visible calm | Audible anxiety; shouting instructions |
| Poor results | Separate result from identity; ask about experience before outcome | Leading with "what went wrong" |
| First-time competitor | Extra logistics prep; low-stakes expectations | Outcome-based framing ("you could win!") |
| Alliance selection anxiety (FRC/VEX) | Prepare students for the process in advance | Telling them "it doesn't matter" (it does) |
