Youth robotics programs have a natural progression — each level adds complexity and prepares students for the next. This guide helps families think about when to move on, what they're moving toward, and how to make the transition without losing the things that made the previous program valuable.

## The progression at a glance

| Program | Ages | Hardware | Competition format | What's added vs. previous level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **FLL Discover** | 4–6 | LEGO DUPLO | Exhibitions (no competition) | First exposure to STEM; no robot |
| **FLL Explore** | 6–10 | LEGO + simple motors | Exhibitions | Simple motorized models; team project |
| **FLL Challenge** | 9–16 | LEGO Education SPIKE Prime | Tournaments (head-to-head) | Autonomous programming; research project; judging |
| **VEX IQ** | 8–14 | Snap plastic + smart motors | Tournaments (head-to-head + Skills) | More mechanical complexity; head-to-head alliance matches |
| **VEX V5** | 11–18 | Metal + pneumatics | Tournaments (alliance matches) | Full engineering kit; C++ option; much higher complexity |
| **VEX U / FRC** | University / 14–18 | Custom (FRC); VEX U build | Regional + championship | Professional-level competition; significant time investment |

Most families follow one of two paths:
- **FLL path:** Discover → Explore → Challenge → (optional) FTC or FRC
- **VEX path:** VEX IQ → VEX V5 → VEX U

FLL Challenge and VEX IQ overlap in age range (9–14) and are often run at the same schools or clubs. Some teams do both; most pick one. For the university-level continuation of the VEX path, see [What is VEX U?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-u/) — it's a real continuing-engagement option, not just a footnote.

## FLL Discover and Explore → FLL Challenge

**When to move up:** When your child is in the 9–16 age range, wants more challenge, and is comfortable with basic building and following instructions. FLL Challenge is a significant step up in complexity and commitment.

**What changes:**
- Robots are programmed to run autonomously — no remote control during matches
- There's a research component (Innovation Project) with judging presentations
- Tournaments are competitive, with scores and advancement
- The season runs roughly 4–6 months (August–December for most teams)

**What stays the same:**
- LEGO bricks and the LEGO building system
- Team-based (2–10 members)
- Newcomer-friendly culture; Core Values emphasis
- No prior programming experience required to start

**Good signs a team is ready for Challenge:**
- Kids finish Explore activities quickly and want harder problems
- Interest in "how does this work?" engineering thinking
- Comfortable sitting with frustration when something doesn't work
- At least one adult willing to coach through a full season

## FLL Challenge → VEX IQ

**When to consider it:** After 1–2 FLL seasons, when kids want more mechanical engineering complexity, want to try head-to-head match play (not just robot game scores), or want a higher skill ceiling.

**What changes:**
- **Hardware:** VEX IQ uses a different building system (snap-together plastic, not LEGO). Components are more engineering-focused — more gear types, chain/sprocket, lifts, pneumatic-like mechanisms.
- **Programming:** VEXcode (Blocks or Python) instead of LEGO SPIKE App. The concepts are nearly identical; the syntax differs.
- **Competition format:** VEX IQ has alliance matches (two robots competing together as a team) in addition to individual Skills runs. This adds a strategic and cooperative dimension FLL doesn't have.
- **No research project:** VEX IQ is purely robot competition — no Innovation Project or judging panels for non-robot content.
- **Season length:** VEX IQ tournaments run most of the school year (fall through spring), with more events available than FLL.

**What stays the same:**
- Team-based, newcomer-friendly culture
- Autonomous programming as the core skill
- Similar age range (8–14 for IQ)
- Similar time commitment per week

**Good signs a team is ready for VEX IQ:**
- Strong interest in mechanical engineering (how the robot is built, not just that it works)
- Wants more competitive match play
- Comfortable with the programming concepts from FLL and ready to learn a new environment
- Interested in trying alliance strategy (working with another team in a match)

**Note on equipment:** FLL SPIKE Prime sets are not compatible with VEX IQ. VEX IQ requires its own hardware (~$250–$350 for a starter kit). Some schools and clubs have VEX IQ sets available to borrow — check before purchasing.

## VEX IQ → VEX V5

**When to consider it:** High school age (grades 7–12); after 1+ VEX IQ seasons; when kids want significantly more engineering challenge and are committed to a higher time investment.

**What changes significantly:**
- **Hardware:** VEX V5 uses metal components, real gearing systems, pneumatics, and a more powerful brain. Robots are larger, heavier, and more complex to build.
- **Programming:** VEXcode supports Blocks, Python, and C++. Competitive V5 teams often move to C++ or the open-source PROS framework for performance. This is a real software engineering challenge.
- **Match format:** VRC (VEX Robotics Competition) has two 15-second autonomous periods + 1:45 driver-controlled periods per match. Alliance strategy is critical — teams must communicate and coordinate with their alliance partner.
- **Time commitment:** VRC is a much larger time investment than VEX IQ. Many competitive teams practice several days per week. It's comparable to a serious sports commitment.
- **Community:** The VEX Forum, team reveal videos, and open-source code sharing are all active. The culture is more like a competitive STEM sport.

**What stays the same:**
- Same VEXcode programming environment (just adds C++ as an option)
- Same robot competition format at its core
- RobotEvents for finding tournaments
- Team-based; coaches still needed

**Good signs a team is ready for VEX V5:**
- Strong mechanical reasoning — can look at a mechanism and understand why it's designed that way
- Interest in programming beyond basic movements (sensor feedback, PID, autonomous paths)
- Willing to commit 5–10+ hours/week during build season
- Has a mentor with engineering or programming background (not required, but helps significantly at the V5 level)

## FLL → FTC and FRC (FIRST pathway)

If your family is committed to the FIRST ecosystem rather than VEX, there's a parallel progression:

| Program | Ages | After FLL Challenge |
|---|---|---|
| **FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC)** | 12–18 | First step up; Android-based control system; more complex robots than FLL |
| **FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC)** | 14–18 | Large-scale robots; 6-week build season; substantial adult mentor involvement |

FTC is the most natural next step from FLL Challenge within the FIRST ecosystem. It uses Java for programming, introduces a more complex control system, and runs head-to-head alliance matches. Many FLL coaches find FTC more challenging to support without an engineering mentor.

FRC is the top level — 120-lb robots, major time commitment, typically supported by industry sponsors and engineer mentors. Not for every family, but transformative for students who go deep.

## Doing multiple programs at once

Some clubs and teams run FLL Challenge and VEX IQ in parallel — different kids or overlapping membership. This can work if:
- The seasons don't conflict (FLL peaks Nov–Feb; VEX IQ runs through spring)
- You have enough coaches/mentors for each team
- Kids genuinely want both, not because they're being pushed

It's more common for students to do one program per year and switch after a season or two when they're ready for more.

## Key questions to help you decide

1. **What does your kid love most about their current program?** If it's the building → VEX IQ. If it's the research and presentation → FLL/FTC. If it's the programming challenge → either path works.
2. **How much time can your family commit?** FLL Challenge ≈ 3–5 hrs/week. VEX IQ ≈ 3–6 hrs/week. VEX V5/FRC ≈ 8–15+ hrs/week.
3. **What does your school/club already support?** The easiest move is into a program that already has equipment, coaches, and local tournaments nearby.
4. **Is there a team to join?** Starting fresh on a new platform is harder than joining an existing team. Check RobotEvents or FIRST's team finder before committing to a new platform.
