Robotics teams that earn the **FTC Connect Award**, the **FRC Impact Award**, or a **VEX Excellence Award** all have one thing in common: their outreach work is *real*. Judges can tell within five minutes whether a team's community program is genuine ongoing work or a three-event checklist assembled for award season. This guide is about doing the real thing.

It also matters beyond the trophy. Outreach is how FIRST and RECF grow. When your FTC team runs a LEGO robotics demo at a local school, you're adding kids to the pipeline who become your alliance partners five years later. The ecosystem benefits when you contribute to it.

:::note[FLL note]
FLL doesn't have a dedicated outreach award, but **Core Values judging** rewards teams who give back — running demos, mentoring younger students, and practicing Gracious Professionalism in the community. If you're an FLL coach, the outreach mindset here applies even without a dedicated award track.
:::

## Know your award target

### FTC: Connect Award

The Connect Award recognises the team that best connects their community to FIRST. Judges look for:

- Recruiting new people *into FIRST* (starting teams, running demos that lead to sign-ups)
- Building long-term partnerships (sponsoring organisations, school districts, youth clubs)
- Communicating what FIRST does to people who've never heard of it

Key distinction: Connect is about **community expansion**, not just team visibility. Running a booth at a local festival counts if it leads to new FIRST participation. Running the same booth to show off your robot, without follow-through, counts for less.

### FRC: Impact Award

The Impact Award is the most prestigious FRC award. It is a **multi-year project** — teams rarely win before year three. Judges evaluate:

- Sustained outreach over multiple seasons (not a one-time burst)
- Measurable outcomes (teams started, students mentored, events hosted, measurable STEM engagement)
- Student ownership of the outreach program (not mentor-run with student names attached)
- Mission propagation: are you helping other teams run outreach, not just running your own?

For a full treatment of the submission process (essay, video, interview), see [FRC Impact Award Submission](/guides/frc-impact-submission/).

### VEX: Excellence Award

The Excellence Award is the most prestigious VEX award (VIQRC, V5RC, VURC). It is holistic — judges consider robot performance, engineering notebook quality, team skills, and community involvement. Outreach is one input, not the whole picture.

What outreach evidence resonates with Excellence judges:
- Consistent community presence (demos, workshops, mentoring) documented in the notebook
- GP in the community, not just on the field
- Genuine engagement with younger students (VIQRC teams mentoring elementary kids; V5RC teams mentoring IQ teams)

See [VEX Judging Prep](/guides/vex-judging-prep/) for the full Excellence judging breakdown.

## Planning a meaningful outreach program

### Start with a problem, not a program

The weakest outreach programs start with "we need three outreach events." The strongest start with a question: *Who in our community isn't participating in STEM, and why?*

Common gaps worth addressing:
- Schools with no FIRST/VEX teams (especially Title I / underfunded schools)
- Kids who aged out of FLL but don't know FTC exists
- Parents who think robotics is only for "tech kids"
- Community organisations (libraries, scouts, youth centres) that want STEM activities but don't know where to start

Once you identify a real gap, you build a program around solving it. That's what judges respond to — and what actually works.

### The four outreach models

**1. Demo and recruit**
Show up, run a robot demo, talk to attendees, collect contact info, follow through. Works at: science fairs, school career days, library events, local festivals. Low barrier to entry — any team can start here. The failure mode is stopping at the demo and never following through on leads.

**2. Workshop or camp**
Run a hands-on session: build a small robot, complete a challenge, learn a skill. Can be a single 90-minute workshop or a multi-day camp. Requires more preparation and materials but creates deeper engagement than a demo. Great gateway to starting new teams.

**3. Mentoring another team**
Commit to supporting a specific FLL, FTC, or FRC rookie team through their season. Show up at practices, answer questions, attend their event as a cheering section. This is the highest-impact outreach model for FIRST awards — it directly grows the ecosystem — and judges know it. It's also the most work.

**4. Systemic partnership**
Create a relationship with an institution: a school district that commits to starting FIRST teams, a community centre that hosts your workshops annually, a library that runs a FIRST Club using your team's donated materials. Systemic partnerships outlast any single season event and signal genuine commitment.

## Building the FTC Connect Award program

### Year-one realistic goals

You won't win Connect in year one unless you've done exceptional work. The realistic year-one goal is to:

1. Complete at least two substantive outreach activities (not just robot demos)
2. Build at least one ongoing relationship (not a one-time appearance)
3. Start documenting everything in your Engineering Portfolio from day one

### What the Engineering Portfolio needs

Connect judges read the portfolio looking for narrative evidence — not just a bullet list of events. For each outreach activity, document:

- **What you did** (one sentence)
- **Who you reached** (numbers + demographics if you have them)
- **What happened next** (did anyone join FIRST? Start a team? Come to a later event?)
- **What you learned and changed** (did you adjust the format after the first time?)

The "what happened next" evidence is what separates Connect contenders from teams that just had a busy events calendar.

### The FIRST Partner Network

The FIRST Partner Network lists organisations that have agreed to support FIRST. If your sponsor is already in the network, leverage that relationship — their partnership is already part of the FIRST ecosystem story. If they're not, help them join.

## Building the FRC Impact Award program

### Think in three-year arcs

Impact is not won by having the most events in one season. It's won by demonstrating that your team's presence has changed your community over multiple years. The judges' mental model is: *if this team disappeared, what would the community lose?*

A realistic multi-year arc:
- **Year 1–2:** Build the program. Run workshops, identify the schools or youth organisations you want to partner with long-term, start mentoring an FLL team. Document everything obsessively.
- **Year 3–4:** The program is running on its own momentum. Students you demoed to in Year 1 are now FLL competitors. You have measurable multi-year data. This is when submission becomes realistic.
- **Year 5+:** You're propagating — other teams are learning from your model. You're contributing to regional FIRST community, not just your own team's footprint.

### Measurable outcomes matter

Judges discount vague claims ("we inspired many students") and credit specific evidence. The most powerful Impact metrics:

| Metric | Why judges weight it |
|--------|---------------------|
| FLL/FTC/FRC teams started or mentored | Direct ecosystem growth |
| Number of students in your programs | Scale + sustainability |
| Programs sustained over 2+ years | Systemic, not one-off |
| Partnerships with under-resourced schools | Equity evidence |
| Mission propagation (other teams using your model) | You're improving the ecosystem, not just participating in it |

### Student ownership is non-negotiable

Impact judges have seen many submissions where mentors did the outreach work and students signed the forms. They will probe for it in the interview. The questions they ask test whether students can speak in detail about the *process* — how you recruited, what went wrong, what you changed. If students can't answer these questions, the submission suffers.

Structure the outreach sub-team so:
- A student lead owns the outreach program (not "helps with")
- Students contact partner organisations, not just their parents
- The Impact essay is written *by the students*, reviewed by adults — not the reverse

## Building the VEX Excellence outreach component

### Less is more for Excellence

Excellence judges evaluate many dimensions; outreach is one of them. A team that runs many outreach events but has a weak notebook or poor robot performance is unlikely to win. The goal is to demonstrate consistent, genuine community engagement as part of a holistic program — not to out-outreach everyone.

What "consistent and genuine" looks like:
- Outreach is documented in the notebook alongside robot work (not in a separate PR binder)
- Team members can speak to specific interactions during the pit interview
- Outreach is connected to the team's identity (a team from a rural area mentoring other rural youth; a college VEX U team running workshops at the local high school)

### The notebook connection

VEX judges start with the notebook. If your outreach work is not in the notebook — dates, activities, photos, reflections — they won't have much to ask about in the pit interview. A simple pattern that works:

After each outreach activity:
1. Write one notebook entry: date, event, what the team did, how many people participated, one lesson learned
2. Photograph the event (you'll have it for judging materials)
3. If you're V5RC/VURC, include a brief reflection on how the team's engineering identity shaped how you presented your work

## Common outreach traps

| Trap | What it looks like | Fix |
|------|-------------------|-----|
| Event inflation | 15 events in March before regionals, none in September–January | Spread outreach across the full season from day one |
| No follow-through | Great demo day, no contact with leads afterward | Assign one team member to own follow-through; send emails the next day |
| Mentor-run outreach | Coach organises everything; students show up | Students own outreach: they contact partners, run sessions, debrief |
| Generic messaging | "We teach STEM" with no specificity | Describe exactly who you're reaching and what they learn |
| One-program focus | Everything is about your team's program only | If you're FTC, talk about FLL and FRC too — you're representing FIRST |
| Forgetting documentation | Activities happened but aren't recorded | Portfolio/notebook entry required within one week of every activity |

## Cross-program outreach ideas that scale

These activities work across all competition programs. Scale complexity to your team's capacity.

**Demo at a community event**
Time: 2–4 hours. Materials: your robot, a table, a printed one-pager. Works for any program. The floor for any outreach program.

**Elementary school visit**
Time: 45–90 minutes. Contact the school two months out. Run a demo plus a 15-minute age-appropriate build activity (paper/cardboard for younger kids). Ask if the school has a FIRST team; if not, leave contact info for the teacher who was most interested.

**Library FIRST information night**
Partner with a public library to run an information session for parents. Many families don't know FIRST or VEX exist. Bring a robot, printed materials, and a current team member who can answer "how do I get my kid into this?" from the parent's perspective.

**FLL rookie team mentorship**
Commit to one FLL team for their full season. Show up twice before their first tournament. This is two practice-day visits, a few answered text questions, and cheering them on at the qualifier. Low time investment, high impact.

**Scrimmage or mini-tournament**
Run a skills scrimmage or practice tournament for rookie teams in your region. Invite teams that don't usually get scrimmage opportunities. This is a logistical lift but it's the kind of systemic contribution that judges notice.

**Workshop at a school STEM night**
Many schools run STEM fairs or parent nights. Offer to run a 20-minute interactive robotics station. These reach parents and students in one event.

## Getting started: a six-week ramp

If your team has done no outreach and wants to build a sustainable program:

**Weeks 1–2:** Map your community. List schools within 15 miles, community centres, libraries, youth groups. Identify two or three where your team could add real value. Assign one student to own outreach.

**Week 3:** Make contact. Send two emails (or calls) to the organisations you identified. Propose something specific — a demo on a specific date, or a conversation about running a workshop. Don't ask "what would you like?" — offer something concrete.

**Week 4:** First activity. Run a small demo or visit. Document it the same day: who came, what you did, what people asked, what you'd do differently.

**Week 5:** Follow up. Send a thank-you, share a photo from the event, and ask about a next time. This is the step most teams skip. It's what turns a one-off event into a relationship.

**Week 6:** Reflect and plan. Debrief as a team: what worked, what didn't, is this the right partner? Update your portfolio/notebook. Plan the next activity.

Six weeks from starting you'll have one real activity and one ongoing relationship. That's a foundation.
