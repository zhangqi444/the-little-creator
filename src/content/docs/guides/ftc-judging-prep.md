FTC judging is where the season's awards are decided — and it operates very differently from FLL. Robot performance at the competition table matters, but most advancement awards hinge on judging. Understanding the process and preparing your team deliberately is one of the highest-leverage things a coach can do.

:::note[Official criteria]
This guide explains the process and offers practical prep advice. For the exact award criteria and rubric, download the current season's [Awards Manual](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/ftc/team-management-resources) from FIRST. Criteria and award names change year to year — always verify.
:::

## How FTC judging works

FTC judging is **interview-based**. Instead of separate judging rooms for different topics (like FLL), FTC teams have a **single judging session** — typically 5–7 minutes — in which a panel of 2–3 judges talks to students about their season, their engineering process, their outreach, and their team culture.

The judges have your **Engineering Portfolio** in hand. They've usually skimmed it before you walk in. The interview lets them go deeper on what they read.

| What judges evaluate | Evidence they use |
|---|---|
| Engineering process | Engineering Portfolio + interview |
| Robot design decisions | Portfolio + live Q&A |
| Programming approach | Portfolio + live Q&A |
| Outreach and community impact | Portfolio + interview |
| Gracious Professionalism and team culture | How the team presents and interacts |
| Sustainability and team operations | Portfolio + interview |

There is no separate Core Values session. There is no separate Innovation Project session. Everything flows through the single interview and your portfolio.

## The Engineering Portfolio is your foundation

**Almost every FTC award references the Engineering Portfolio.** If your portfolio is weak, your judging session will be an uphill battle. If your portfolio is strong, the interview is your chance to bring it to life.

Key things to understand before walking into the judging room:

- **You are being judged on your engineering *process*, not just your final robot.** Judges want to see iteration, failure, learning, and reasoning — not a brochure for a perfect robot.
- **The portfolio is capped at ~15 pages.** Every page should earn its place. Padding with photos or filler hurts you.
- **Students should have written and understand everything in the portfolio.** Judges will ask about specific items. "My mentor made that section" is not a good answer.

For a full guide on building the portfolio itself, see [FTC Engineering Portfolio — Practical Guide](/guides/ftc-engineering-portfolio/).

## The judging session: what to expect

### Before you enter

- Know your scheduled time and arrive 5 minutes early.
- Bring a **printed copy of your Engineering Portfolio** — most events expect a hard copy in the judging room. Check the tournament info sheet.
- Bring the robot (or expect that a table display may be available — check event logistics).
- The coach stays quiet. Judges are evaluating students. If a coach jumps in to answer a question, it signals the students aren't ready.

### In the room

The session typically starts with judges asking the team to **introduce themselves and give a brief overview of their season**. From there it becomes a conversation, not a formal presentation.

Common opening questions:

- "Tell us about your team — who are you and what have you been up to this season?"
- "Walk us through your robot's main mechanisms."
- "What's the most significant change you made to your robot this season, and why?"

Judges will then probe areas of interest based on what they read in the portfolio or what the team raises. The conversation can go in many directions.

### After the session

- Judges submit scores immediately after the session.
- There's no formal debrief from judges. Don't chase them for feedback.
- Team debrief is valuable: what went well, what would you say differently? This improves future tournaments.

## What judges are really looking for

### Engineering depth

Judges want evidence of **real engineering** — not a team that built a robot, but a team that *designed* a robot: identified requirements, brainstormed options, prototyped, tested, failed, iterated, and ended up somewhere different from where they started.

Red flags:
- "We just built what worked."
- "Our mentor helped us figure out the design."
- "We didn't really change much — the first version was pretty good."

What impresses judges:
- "We started with a holonomic drive, tested it for two weeks, realised we were losing too much power in turns for this game's field elements, and switched to a standard mecanum with dedicated strafing — here's why that trade-off made sense for us."
- "We tried three different intake designs. The first one jammed constantly. Here's what we learned from each version."
- Specific numbers: cycle times, motor current measurements, test results.

### Programming maturity

Judges for FTC aren't expecting World Championship-caliber code. They want to see that students **understand what the code does** and made intentional choices.

Be ready to explain:
- What programming tool you used (FTC Blocks, OnBot Java, Android Studio) and why.
- How your TeleOp is structured — what happens when a driver presses a button?
- How your Autonomous works — encoder-based? Pose estimation? What sensors?
- How you handled inconsistency in Autonomous — what happens if the robot is slightly mis-positioned at the start?

### Outreach and sustainability

Many FTC awards — especially Inspire — weight outreach and community impact heavily. Judges want to see that your team does more than build a robot.

Relevant outreach examples:
- Mentoring younger FLL or FTC teams
- Running robotics workshops for schools or libraries
- Recruiting new members and documenting how to keep the team going
- Sponsorship relationships and how you sustain them

Sustainability means judges believe this team will exist next year. Evidence: documented processes, multi-year mentoring relationships, a roster that includes underclassmen, written team handbook.

### Gracious Professionalism

GP in FTC is observed throughout the day, not just in the judging room. Judges talk to pit neighbours, volunteers, and referees. They notice:

- Whether your team helps other teams in the pit
- How your students handle a bad match or a ref call they disagree with
- Whether students are welcoming when other teams approach your pit

In the interview itself: do students speak well of other teams? Do they give credit to others? Are they honest about their own weaknesses?

## How to prepare your team

### Practice the pitch, not a script

Rehearsing word-for-word answers backfires. Judges ask follow-up questions; scripted teams fall apart under probing. Instead:

- **Practice explaining topics conversationally.** Put a student in front of the team and say "explain the intake to me like I've never seen it." Other students ask follow-up questions. Rotate.
- **Use your portfolio as a practice tool.** Go through each section: "Who can explain what we were trying to show here?" Students should be able to speak to every page.

### Distribute speaking roles genuinely

Every student in the room should be able to speak to *something*. Judges notice if one or two students carry everything.

Don't force artificial equality ("everyone says one sentence") — that reads as rehearsed. Instead, assign genuine ownership: "You built the intake, so you explain the intake design decisions." "You wrote the Autonomous, so you field the programming questions."

A student who says "I focused on outreach this season, so I can tell you about our STEM night at the library, but Sam is better placed to explain the drivetrain" is authentic and impressive.

### Prepare for hard questions

Judges will probe weaknesses. Prepare teams to answer honestly:

- "What's the biggest problem you still have on your robot?"
- "What would you change about your Autonomous if you had two more weeks?"
- "Is there anything in the portfolio you wish you'd documented better?"

Honest, thoughtful answers ("Our endgame mechanism is only about 60% reliable and here's why we ran out of time to fix it") are far more impressive than defensive deflection. Engineers acknowledge limitations; they don't pretend they don't exist.

### Day-of logistics

| Item | Notes |
|---|---|
| Printed Engineering Portfolio | One copy minimum; some teams bring two |
| Robot (if required by event) | Check event logistics — some events have a display area |
| Team placement | Spread students so judges can make eye contact with everyone |
| Coach position | Near the door or against the wall — visible but silent |

## Matching awards to your team's strengths

FTC awards recognise different strengths. Before the tournament, be realistic about what your team has invested in:

| Award | What drives it |
|---|---|
| **Inspire** | Strongest overall team — engineering + outreach + GP across the board |
| **Think** | Exceptional software, programming depth, autonomous performance |
| **Innovate** | Unique mechanical design or novel problem-solving |
| **Design** | Elegant, efficient, well-documented mechanism design |
| **Connect** | Community outreach, sponsorships, STEM advocacy |
| **Motivate** | Team spirit, recruitment, enthusiasm — harder to engineer |
| **Control** | Programming: sensors, state machines, automation |
| **Promote** | Marketing materials, team branding, community communication |

For a full breakdown of what each award actually measures and how they interact, see [FTC Awards — What Each One Rewards](/guides/ftc-awards/).

## Common judging mistakes

| Mistake | Better approach |
|---|---|
| Students recite rehearsed answers that don't match follow-up questions | Practice conversational explanations, not scripts |
| Coach speaks up to "help" a student who paused | Let students think. Silence is fine. |
| Portfolio describes a perfect robot with no failures | Judges distrust perfection. Show the iteration. |
| Only one student can answer programming questions | All students should have a passing understanding of what the code does |
| Team is vague about outreach ("we did a community thing") | Specific: who, how many students, what impact, follow-up |
| Students bad-mouth other teams or complain about ref calls | GP means competing with integrity. Always. |
| Team runs out of time and never covers their strongest material | Structure the intro tightly; get to differentiated strengths fast |
