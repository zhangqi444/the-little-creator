Alliance selection gets you a partner. Alliance **strategy** decides what you do together. The difference between winning alliances and losing ones often isn't robot capability — it's coordination.

This guide covers how to build and execute match strategy with your alliance partners across FTC, FRC, and VEX V5/VRC. FLL and VEX IQ use randomly assigned or co-operative partner formats, so the competitive alliance dynamics here don't apply to those programs.

---

## Why strategy matters as much as capability

Consider two alliances:

- **Alliance A**: both robots are individually strong but they don't talk before matches. They both chase the same high-value game piece in autonomous. They block each other in TeleOp. One goes for the endgame bonus early and ends up in the other's way.
- **Alliance B**: the robots are slightly weaker on paper. But they spend 3 minutes pre-match deciding who owns which zone. In autonomous, Robot 1 handles the near side; Robot 2 handles the far side. In TeleOp, Robot 1 scores; Robot 2 feeds and clears defence. The endgame is sequenced so both robots finish without collision.

Alliance B wins more often.

Coordination is a force multiplier. It costs nothing to build. It is almost always under-invested.

## Before the match — the pre-match alliance meeting

Every competitive event gives alliances a few minutes between the match queue and the field setup. Use all of it.

### Who should be in the meeting

- Lead driver from each team (the person making real-time decisions)
- Head coach (optional at smaller events; essential at championships)
- Keep observers out — limit distraction

### What to cover in 3 minutes

**1. Autonomous strategy (most important)**
- Who starts where?
- Who scores what in auto? Divide the field clearly.
- What is the safe fallback if one auto fails? (Don't assume both autos will work perfectly.)
- If autos might interact or collide — who yields?

**2. TeleOp zone ownership**
- Which side of the field or which scoring locations does each robot own?
- Who cycles? Who defends or feeds? (If roles are split)
- When/how does zone assignment shift if the other robot breaks down?

**3. Endgame sequencing**
- Who goes for endgame first?
- How do you avoid blocking each other's path to the endgame structure?
- If there is a shared endgame element (e.g., a single climb bar, a shared parking zone) — who has priority?

**4. Communication signals**
- What does each team use to signal "I'm going for endgame now"? (Verbal call, hand signal, agreed match-clock trigger)
- What do you say if your robot breaks down mid-match? (Don't leave your partner guessing.)

**5. Opponent awareness**
- What are the opposing robots likely to do?
- Is there a high-value autonomous you should try to disrupt or avoid being disrupted by?
- Is one of them a strong defender? Adjust your field plan accordingly.

## Autonomous strategy in depth

Autonomous is the highest-leverage part of most modern robotics games. Every point scored autonomously is a point scored before any human input — and those points often come with bonuses.

### Divide the field before the match

The biggest autonomous mistake is **both robots going for the same scoring element**. On most fields, the high-value autonomous targets cluster near the centre or near the shared starting zones. If you haven't agreed in advance, you risk a collision, an interference penalty, or simply leaving points uncollected.

Rule: **whoever has the stronger/more reliable autonomous gets first choice of starting position and target**. The partner takes the complementary role.

### The fallback conversation

Always ask: "What happens if your auto doesn't run — or ends early?"

A good alliance plans for this:

- If Robot 1's auto fails and it sits idle, does Robot 2 cover its zone?
- If both autos fail, where do the robots park to avoid fouling?

This isn't pessimism — it's professionalism. Judges and experienced coaches notice teams that plan for failure modes.

### FTC-specific autonomous notes

FTC autonomous lasts 30 seconds. With two robots sharing a field, collision risk is real. **Agree on spatial separation**: one robot near, one robot far, or one robot left, one robot right. Common FTC auto collisions happen when both robots path through the centre tile stack — avoid this.

Also note the auto bonus: in many FTC games, scoring a specific element or reaching a specific zone in auto gives a multiplied bonus. Align on who goes for the bonus vs. who stacks raw points.

### FRC-specific autonomous notes

FRC autonomous lasts 15 seconds and is heavily strategic. Alliances of three robots have enough combined field coverage to attempt full field clearing. At the regional level, agreeing on which robot starts at which station and which scoring element each robot targets is usually sufficient. At the championship level, coordinated auto sequences (who delays, who runs first) become meaningful.

**Leave bonus**: in many FRC games, leaving the starting zone in auto gives a point bonus. Make sure both partners who can leave do leave — don't let a good leave bonus get wasted because a robot sat still.

### VEX V5 / VRC-specific autonomous notes

VRC autonomous lasts 15 seconds (Autonomous Period) with an Autonomous Win Point (AWP) as a bonus for meeting a specific condition. **The AWP is usually worth more than the raw autonomous score** in terms of long-term ranking implications — discuss whether chasing the AWP is achievable for your alliance.

Also: in VRC, Skills scores are visible before the match. A high Skills score signals a team can run autonomous reliably. Factor this into your agreement about who owns which autonomous zone.

## TeleOp zone strategy

TeleOp is where most match points are scored. Uncoordinated TeleOp produces traffic jams, missed cycles, and frustration. Coordinated TeleOp produces clean parallel scoring.

### Zone division patterns

**Left-right split**: Each robot owns a half of the field. Works well when the scoring is distributed horizontally and robots have similar capabilities.

**Near-far split**: One robot works close to the human player station; the other works the far side of the field. Works well when there is a feed-and-score mechanic.

**Scorer-feeder split**: One robot scores; the other retrieves and passes game pieces. Works when the game has distinct picking and scoring locations, and one robot is better at each role.

**Scorer-defender split**: One robot scores; the other applies defensive pressure on the opposing alliance. Used when the opposing alliance's scorer is a bigger threat than adding more offensive robots.

Don't decide the split mid-match. Pick before the match. Communicate the plan. Stick to it unless something breaks.

### When the plan breaks down

Plans break down. A robot gets disabled. A mechanism jams. The field is different from what you expected.

Agree on a simple fallback during the pre-match meeting:

> "If either of us breaks down, the other robot takes the whole field. Call it out verbally on the field."

Drive coaches should be listening and ready to adapt. The worst outcome is both robots freezing mid-match because neither knows who should take over.

### Avoiding traffic jams

The most common TeleOp coordination failure is **both robots trying to collect or score at the same location simultaneously**. This creates:

- Physical collisions (fouls or robot damage)
- Lost time while robots sort themselves out
- Frustration that affects driver focus

Prevention: during the pre-match meeting, assign entry and exit paths if the game has narrow chokepoints. Agree on "if we're both heading for the same thing, Robot 2 yields."

## Endgame sequencing

Many robotics games reward an endgame bonus: a climb, a park, a specific position. Endgame is often the most physically demanding part of the match — and the most collision-prone.

### Sequence who goes first

If both robots are going for the same endgame structure (a single climb bar, a shared platform, a narrow parking zone), **one robot must start before the other**. Decide this in the pre-match meeting.

Typical sequencing logic:
- The robot that takes longer to set up goes first.
- The robot that needs a specific entry angle goes first if entry is blocked by the other.
- The robot with the less reliable endgame mechanism goes first, so it has recovery time if it misses.

### The time trigger

Agree on a clock time when Robot 1 breaks off scoring and goes for endgame. A common pattern:

> "I start my endgame sequence at 0:30 remaining. If I'm not already in position by 0:25, I abort and park."

This prevents the "one more cycle" trap where a driver delays endgame, runs out of time, and scores zero endgame points.

### Communicate the start

Use a verbal signal or hand signal when you initiate endgame. Your partner needs to know to clear the path, not cross into your trajectory, and possibly sequence their own endgame around your movement.

**FTC endgame notes**: FTC endgame (typically 30 seconds) involves climbing, tilting, or parking. If one robot climbs first and blocks the platform, the second robot may lose its endgame entirely. Sequence clearly.

**FRC endgame notes**: FRC endgame is often 20 seconds. Three robots and limited climbing structures means sequencing is critical. Know the field layout: some structures fit one robot at a time; some fit two. If your alliance has three climbers but only two spots, plan who parks vs. climbs.

**VRC endgame notes**: VRC endgame usually involves a parking zone, elevation platform, or climb. Two robots in a small field zone can collide easily. Agree who approaches from which direction.

## In-match communication

Match communication is a skill. Most rookie teams under-invest in it.

### Who talks and who drives

In most programs, the **head coach talks** and the **driver drives**. The driver should be focused on the robot, not on shouting across the field. The head coach watches the whole field, tracks time, and calls cues.

Rule: one person speaks at a time. Short sentences. Cues only — not explanations.

> Correct: "Go endgame. 30 seconds."
> Avoid: "I think you should probably consider starting the endgame thing now because there's only about 30 seconds left and the other robot is coming over there."

### Standard cue vocabulary

Build a short call list before the event. Use it consistently. Examples:

| Cue | Meaning |
|---|---|
| "Endgame go" | Start endgame sequence now |
| "Their house" | Opposing robot is near our scoring zone — be aware |
| "Clear" | Zone is empty, safe to enter |
| "Park" | Abort current action, get to parking zone |
| "Their side" | Move to the opposite field half |
| "Hold" | Stay still, wait for a beat |

Agree on the calls with your alliance partner's coach before the match. If they use the same word to mean something different, fix that before you are on the field.

### Coordinating with the other drive team

In FTC and VRC, the alliance drive teams are adjacent on the same drive team alliance station. In FRC, alliance stations are shared.

Use this proximity. A quick "I'm going endgame in 30" before the clock hits that mark lets your partner's driver adjust. These short mid-match exchanges are legal and effective.

### What not to call out

Some things are distracting, not helpful:

- Emotion: "Oh no!" / "What happened?!" — your driver can't fix it in real time
- Uncertainty: "I think maybe you should go…" — be decisive or stay quiet
- Detailed technical instructions: no time, wrong moment

Save the debrief for after the match.

## Opponent awareness and defensive play

Most alliances win by executing their own strategy reliably. But in close matches, awareness of opponents matters.

### Reading the opposing autonomous

If you've scouted, you know what the opposing robots typically do in auto. Discuss whether their auto sequence crosses your planned path. Adjust starting position or path timing accordingly.

### When to deploy a defensive robot

Sometimes your alliance's highest-leverage move is to stop the opposing alliance from scoring rather than add more of your own offense.

Consider defence when:
- One opposing robot is scoring dramatically faster than your alliance can match
- The game mechanic can be disrupted (blocking a collection zone, shadowing a high-value scorer)
- You have a robot that is reliable at driving interference and the rules permit it

**Caution**: defensive play attracts fouls if not executed cleanly. Know the rules around contact, entanglement, and field zones before deciding to play defence.

### Responding to defence against you

If the opposing alliance deploys a defender against one of your robots:

- Communicate to your partner: "I'm being defended — can you take my zone?"
- The non-defended robot should absorb more of the offensive load
- The defended robot can attempt to draw the defender away from the key scoring area
- Avoid retaliating with illegal contact — fouls in defence situations are one of the most common ways alliances lose matches they should have won

## After the match — alliance debrief

A 5-minute debrief with your alliance partner after each qualifying match improves your strategy for the next time you are together in playoffs.

Ask:
1. Did we execute the plan? Where did we deviate?
2. What worked? What do we keep?
3. What didn't work? What do we change?
4. Were there any near-fouls we need to avoid?

Keep it short. The goal is incremental improvement, not a full post-mortem.

In playoff rounds specifically, you may have minimal time between matches. Pre-decide any strategy adjustments quickly and re-commit to the plan.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | What happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No pre-match meeting | Both robots make independent decisions; zone collisions; missed bonuses | Always meet, even for 2 minutes |
| Both autos go for the same element | Collision or interference; one auto wasted | Divide the field before the match |
| Endgame timing not agreed | One robot blocks the other; late start; missed endgame points | Set a clock-based trigger and agree in advance |
| Unclear zone ownership in TeleOp | Both robots converge on same spot; lost cycles | Assign zones explicitly before the match |
| Coach explains instead of cuing | Driver loses focus; too much cognitive load | Short cues only; debrief after the match |
| Reacting to defence emotionally | Fouls; lost tempo; distracted driver | Pre-plan how to respond to defence before the match |
| No fallback plan | Alliance freezes when one robot breaks down | Agree: "If one of us is out, the other takes the field" |
| Replanning mid-match without communicating | Partner doesn't know the plan has changed | Signal any plan change explicitly with a call |

## Quick reference: pre-match checklist

Before every alliance match, cover these five questions:

1. **Autonomous**: Who starts where? Who scores what? What is the fallback if one auto fails?
2. **TeleOp**: Who owns which zone? Who is scoring vs. feeding vs. defending?
3. **Endgame**: Who goes first? What is the clock trigger? Who signals the start?
4. **Signals**: What cues will we use during the match?
5. **Opponent**: What do we know about them? Do we need to adjust?

Five questions. Three minutes. Repeat before every match.
