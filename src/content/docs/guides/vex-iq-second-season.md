Season one is about figuring out what VEX IQ Robotics Competition actually is. Season two is about competing with intention. Here's what experienced VIQRC coaches and students consistently change after their first year.

---

## 1. Start the engineering notebook on day one

Year one teams often realise in October that they have no documentation. Year two teams open a notebook at the first build session.

**What that looks like:**
- Date every entry — judges notice retroactive notebooks immediately
- Document *why* you made each design decision, not just *what* you built
- Photograph failed prototypes alongside finished ones — iteration evidence is exactly what judges want to see
- Keep a single notebook across Teamwork Challenge prep and Autonomous Coding Skills design; they tell one story

The hardest habit to break: only writing entries when things go well. A page that says "drivetrain kept skipping — tried adjusting motor torque setting, still slipping — switched to direct-drive axle mounts, fixed" is stronger evidence than a photo of a working robot.

See [VEX Judging Prep](/guides/vex-judging-prep/) for what judges look for in VIQRC notebooks.

---

## 2. Narrow your Teamwork Challenge strategy

Year one teams try to score every object on the field. Year two teams pick the highest-value, most-reliable scoring actions and drill them.

**The math:** a pair of robots that reliably scores 60 points beats a pair that *attempts* 90 and averages 45. Consistency wins more matches than ambition.

**How to narrow:**
1. Score every field element by expected value: (points × probability of completing it cleanly in a real match)
2. Cut low-value or high-complexity actions — anything requiring a precise multi-step sequence under time pressure
3. Agree on a role split with your alliance partner *before* the match: who clears which zone, who scores which structure
4. Run the same strategy until it is automatic, then add one more element

Year one coaches often add new mission attempts the week before the tournament. Year two coaches freeze the strategy 3 weeks out and spend the remaining time drilling what they have.

---

## 3. Take Autonomous Coding Skills seriously from the start

Year one teams treat Autonomous Coding Skills as a secondary event — something to attempt after the Teamwork robot is done. Year two teams plan both in parallel from day one.

**Why it matters:** the Autonomous Coding Skills ranking is separate from Teamwork. A strong ACS run can lift a team's combined placement significantly — and the skills required (gyro correction, encoder-based movement) make the robot *better* in Teamwork matches too.

**Changes to make:**
- Write your first ACS routine in the first two weeks of the season, even if it just drives forward and scores one element
- Add `gyroStraight` and `turnToHeading` helper functions early so autonomous routes stay reliable as the field changes
- Practice ACS separately from Teamwork practice — it has its own three-attempt format and its own mental preparation
- Log every ACS run with score + notes so you can compare sessions

See [VEX IQ Programming Guide](/guides/vex-iq-programming/) and [VEX IQ Robot Design Guide](/guides/vex-iq-robot-design/) for technical foundations.

---

## 4. Plan the robot for both events at once

Year one teams build for Teamwork Challenge and retrofit for ACS. Year two teams design with both in mind from the start.

**What that means in practice:**
- If your Teamwork strategy requires a claw on a specific side, check whether that creates problems for your ACS approach before you commit
- Build a drive base that moves predictably: calibrated motors, proper gear ratios, inertial sensor wired up from session 1
- Design intake/scoring mechanisms that work in both the collaborative and solo context — if an attachment only works with a partner's robot setting something up, you may score zero in ACS
- Keep the robot simple enough to rebuild quickly after a breakdown. VEX IQ snap-together plastic is forgiving — use that.

---

## 5. Practice with your alliance partner earlier

Year one teams meet their alliance partner for the first time in the queue. Year two teams practice with an alliance partner weeks in advance.

**Why this is hard:** VIQRC teams often don't connect with other local teams until the competition. Break that pattern.

**What to do:**
- Contact another local team through RobotEvents or a regional Discord/Facebook group before the season gets busy
- Run 3–4 joint practice sessions, even over video call with robots on both sides visible
- Agree on a role split in writing: who owns which field zones, who scores which high-value elements, what happens when one robot gets stuck
- Practice the hand-off: some Teamwork strategies only work if both robots start in coordinated positions

Even one joint practice session changes the dynamic on competition day. Teams that have never run together struggle to coordinate in real time; teams that have practiced together can adapt quietly.

## 6. Let students lead the pit

Year one coaches often run the pit themselves — setting up the robot, making repair decisions, talking to judges. Year two coaches step back.

**Practical shifts:**
- Assign a Pit Captain (one student role): they manage the robot between matches, call out what needs repair, direct teammates
- Assign a Driver and an Operator with clear responsibilities — no "we both kind of drive" ambiguity
- Coaches in the pit should ask questions, not give answers: "What do you think broke?" not "The axle collar shifted — move it back."
- During judging, let students speak entirely. Coaches who interrupt hurt the team's judging score.

The judges are watching whether this is genuinely student-driven. They have been doing this a long time.

---

## 7. Prepare for judging — genuinely, not by script

Year one teams either ignore judging entirely or over-rehearse scripted presentations that sound robotic to judges.

Year two teams prepare authentically:
- Run mock judging sessions with a parent or different coach as the "judge" — 5-minute unscripted Q&A, not a rehearsed pitch
- Every student should be able to explain the team's design decisions in their own words (not word-for-word identical to a teammate)
- Prepare for the follow-up question: judges always ask "why did you choose that?" — the team should have a real answer, not a shrug
- For younger students (ages 8–11 typical VIQRC range): genuineness beats polish. Judges do not expect TED-talk delivery. They want to see real kids who made real choices.

See [VEX Judging Prep](/guides/vex-judging-prep/) for the full VIQRC judging structure, award-to-strength matching, and a prep timeline.

## 8. Cross-train every team member

Year one: one student drives, one student operates, everyone else watches.

Year two: every student can run every role in a practice match.

**Why it matters:** VIQRC teams are small (typically 2–6 students). One absent team member at a tournament — sick, school conflict, family emergency — should not break the team's strategy. Cross-training also reveals hidden skills; the quietest student on the team is sometimes the most precise operator.

**How to do it:**
- Rotate Drive and Operator roles every two or three practice sessions
- Rotate who does the ACS runs so multiple students know the autonomous routine and can restart it after a reset
- Rotate who does notebook entries so multiple students understand the documentation well enough to answer judge questions about it

---

## 9. Register and schedule early

Year one teams scramble for spots. Year two teams have the calendar sorted before build season starts.

- Check RobotEvents in August/September for local qualifier dates — events fill up quickly in competitive regions
- If your season overlaps with school standardised testing or major holidays, flag those dates on the team calendar on day one
- Book travel (if needed for state/national championships) the moment you know dates
- If your region uses a separate team registration system beyond RobotEvents, confirm both registrations are active

---

## 10. Enjoy what's different about VEX IQ

VEX IQ is collaborative in a way most competitions are not — you share the field with an alliance partner you may not know. That is unusual and worth leaning into.

Year one teams often focus so hard on their own robot that they forget the other team on the field is a partner, not an obstacle. Year two teams treat every alliance pairing as a collaboration: introduce yourselves early in the queue, agree on a quick strategy, cheer for each other's successes.

The Excellence Award — the top VIQRC award — goes to teams that demonstrate outstanding performance *across everything*: robot, autonomous skills, notebook, *and* gracious professionalism in how they treat other teams. Year two is when teams start winning Excellence, because year two is when they understand that everything is connected.
