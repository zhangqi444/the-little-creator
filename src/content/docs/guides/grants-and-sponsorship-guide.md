Funding a robotics team is one of the most common first-year surprises. Registration fees, kits, and travel add up faster than expected — and most coaches don't realize how much help is already out there. This guide covers the full funding landscape for all six programs: grants from FIRST and RECF, regional PDO grants, corporate sponsorship, and how to build a funding strategy that compounds over multiple seasons.

For a quick first-year FLL budget, also see [Team Funding](/guides/team-funding/) and [Season Budget Template](/guides/budget-template/).

---

## Budget reality by program

Before fundraising, know your target. These are rough first-year estimates; confirm current fees on the official registration platforms.

| Program | Typical first-year cost | Main cost drivers |
|---|---|---|
| FLL Challenge | $700–$1,200 | Registration, SPIKE Prime set, Challenge Set, travel |
| FTC | $1,500–$3,500 | Registration, REV/goBILDA parts, Control Hub, tools |
| FRC | $8,000–$25,000+ | Registration (~$5,000–6,000), robot parts, travel, safety gear |
| VEX IQ | $600–$1,100 | Registration (RobotEvents), Super Kit (~$400), field elements |
| VEX V5 | $1,500–$3,000 | Registration, V5 Starter Kit (~$900), additional motors/sensors |
| VEX U | $1,000–$4,000+ | Two robots, college travel, PROS tools (open-source) |

FRC is in a league of its own. Most FRC teams treat fundraising as a year-round programme, not a one-time task.

## Tier 1 — Official grant programmes

### FIRST family (FLL, FTC, FRC)

**FIRST Grants Database**
- [firstinspires.org/community/grants](https://www.firstinspires.org/community/grants)
- Searchable by program (FLL/FTC/FRC) and region. Updated seasonally — check April–June for upcoming year grants.
- Look specifically for "Rookie Grant," "New Team Grant," and "Underserved Community" filters.

**FRC-specific: Dean's List scholarship component**
Advancing FRC teams may unlock scholarship funds tied to team recognition. Ask your regional director.

**FIRST in Michigan / Districts**
If you're in an FRC district model (Michigan, Indiana, Israel, etc.), your district may have a separate grant pool for new or rural teams. Check your district's website.

### VEX / RECF family (VEX IQ, VEX V5, VEX U)

**RECF Grants page**
- [roboticseducation.org/resources/grants/](https://www.roboticseducation.org/resources/grants/)
- RECF runs grant programs for underserved communities and first-year teams. Updated annually — check late spring for the next season's cycle.

**VEX U scholarship programme**
VEX U teams at universities may qualify for RECF scholarships designed for college programs. Check [roboticseducation.org](https://www.roboticseducation.org) under VEX U.

## Tier 2 — Regional and PDO grants

Program Delivery Organizations (PDOs) for FIRST, and regional VEX event partners, often run their own grant programmes — sometimes more accessible than national ones because competition is lower.

**How to find your PDO or regional partner:**
- FIRST: use [firstinspires.org/find-local-support](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-local-support)
- VEX: check your state/region on [robotevents.com](https://www.robotevents.com)

**Examples of PDO grants:**
- FIRST Washington — team grants, especially for Title I schools ([firstwa.org](https://firstwa.org))
- FIRST in Texas — posts grant info at [firstintx.com](https://firstintx.com)
- IET UK (FLL UK partner) — grants for school clubs in underserved areas
- Many regional VEX event partners offer equipment loans or registration fee waivers for first-year teams — ask your regional EP (Event Partner) directly

**Tip:** PDO staff want teams to succeed. Email or call them directly and ask: "Do you have any grants or equipment loan programmes for new teams?" You'll often get a faster answer than searching the website.

## Tier 3 — Corporate and foundation sponsors

Corporate sponsorship is usually the largest single funding lever, especially for FRC teams that need $10K+.

### Who to target

| Sponsor type | Why they give | Sweet spot ask |
|---|---|---|
| Engineering/tech companies | STEM pipeline, brand alignment | $500–$5,000 |
| Law firms / accounting firms | Community involvement is cultural | $200–$1,000 |
| Local manufacturers | Robotics resonates with their work | $500–$2,000 |
| Credit unions / community banks | Community grant budgets are common | $250–$1,000 |
| Parents' employers | Corporate matching programmes | Varies — often 1:1 match |
| STEM-aligned foundations | Mission alignment; multi-year possible | $1,000–$10,000+ |

### Employer matching programmes

Many large corporations (Google, Microsoft, Boeing, Caterpillar, 3M, Lockheed, etc.) run employee giving match programmes. A parent who works at such a company can donate $500 and their employer matches it — effectively doubling your take for no extra ask. Ask every family: "Does your employer have a community giving match programme?"

### Sponsorship tiers (for teams seeking multiple sponsors)

Rather than a single all-or-nothing ask, create tiered levels. Example for an FRC team:

| Tier | Amount | Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| Gold | $2,500+ | Logo on robot, banner, shirt; social mention; team visit |
| Silver | $1,000 | Logo on shirt and banner; social mention |
| Bronze | $500 | Name on banner; social mention |
| Friend | $100–$499 | Name on team website / programme |

Adjust amounts for your program's budget scale. FLL and VEX IQ teams should compress these (Bronze at $100, Gold at $500) — asking a small business for $2,500 is not appropriate.

### Outreach email template

> **Subject:** Sponsoring [Team name] — local robotics team, [City/School]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm coaching/mentoring [Team name], a [program name] robotics team of [N] students ages [X]–[X] based in [City]. We compete [month/season] at [venue or region].
>
> We're looking for sponsors to help cover our [equipment / registration / travel] costs. Our [Silver / Bronze] sponsorship level is $[amount] and includes [specific benefit — e.g., your logo on our team shirts]. For larger contributions, we can offer [Gold benefit].
>
> We'd love to represent your company at our events and give your team a chance to see what the kids are building. Happy to share a short video or set up a brief call.
>
> Thank you,
> [Your name], [Role]
> [Contact / website / social if you have one]

Keep it under 200 words. Sponsors decide in 60 seconds.

### Follow-up and stewardship

- Follow up once, 10–14 days after the initial email, if no response.
- After the season: send a one-page recap — photos, what the team accomplished, a thank-you note from a student. This is what converts one-time sponsors into multi-year partners.
- If a sponsor renews for year two, their tier should remember them — don't start the ask from scratch.

## Tier 4 — Grants from foundations and government programmes

For teams in schools or nonprofits, additional funding paths exist:

**STEM foundation grants**
- [STEMfunders.org](https://www.stemfunders.org) — aggregates active STEM grants by state and program
- Toshiba America Foundation — grants for STEM classroom teachers
- Siemens Foundation — STEM education grants
- Verizon Innovative Learning — Title I school technology grants

**Federal and state programmes (US)**
- Title I discretionary funds (schools only) — check with your district's STEM coordinator
- USDA Community Facilities Programme — rural areas may qualify
- 21st Century Community Learning Centers grants — after-school STEM programmes

**Charitable gaming / raffle fundraisers**
In some states, regulated charitable gaming (raffles, bingo) can be a legal fundraiser for registered nonprofits. Check your state's regulations before proceeding — permits are often required.

## Building a multi-year funding strategy

Year-one funding is mostly survival mode. Years two and three is when you can build something durable.

### Year one: cover costs

- Apply to every grant you find. Rejection rate is high — this is normal.
- Land 1–2 local sponsors. Small amounts are fine.
- Keep costs minimal: borrow equipment where possible.

### Year two: stabilize

- Re-approach year-one sponsors with a recap and a renewal ask. Personal renewal conversations convert at roughly 60%.
- Add one new sponsor category you didn't try in year one.
- Apply to regional PDO grants — now you have a track record.

### Year three and beyond: build a programme

- Create a sponsor tier system and a one-page prospectus (PDF). This looks professional and makes it easy for sponsors to choose.
- Set a fundraising goal before the season starts, not after.
- Assign a parent or student to be the "sponsorship lead" — dedicated ownership matters.
- Target one foundation grant per year in addition to corporate sponsors.
- For FRC teams: aim for enough to cover the full year budget plus a small reserve for travel surprises.

## Programme-specific notes

### FLL Challenge
Focus on local sponsors, PDO grants, and parent employer matching. Budget is manageable. Avoid over-fundraising — you don't need a $5K war chest for an FLL team.

### FTC
The hardware cost step-up from FLL is significant. FIRST FTC grants and PDO grants are worth pursuing. Corporate sponsors at the $500–$1,000 level are realistic. Engineering companies respond well to FTC's "build a real robot" pitch.

### FRC
Treat fundraising as a year-round sub-team task. Target $10K–$20K+ depending on event travel. A dedicated "Business" sub-team that manages sponsors and the Impact Award outreach is standard practice for competitive FRC teams. The Impact Award submission itself documents your outreach — build them together.

### VEX IQ
Similar to FLL in budget scale. RECF grants are the most relevant official source. Regional VEX event partners sometimes offer first-year registration waivers — ask early.

### VEX V5
One or two local sponsors can cover most of the budget. The "engineering challenge" framing resonates with tech employers. V5 teams that advance to Worlds need travel funding — build that into year-two planning.

### VEX U
College teams often have access to department or student org budgets in addition to RECF grants. The university's engineering school or corporate partnerships office may have discretionary STEM funding. Don't overlook your institution's internal channels before seeking outside sponsors.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Asking for vague "donations" | Sponsors don't know what they're buying | Tie every ask to a specific cost ("$300 covers our registration fee") |
| Not following up | Most sponsors don't reply to the first email | Follow up once at 10–14 days |
| No recap after the season | Kills year-two renewal | Send a one-page photo recap to every sponsor |
| Applying to grants at the last minute | Grant windows close months before the season | Check grant calendars in spring for the following fall season |
| Missing employer match opportunity | Free money left on the table | Survey every family about employer match programmes |
| Over-fundraising small teams | Creates accounting complexity, donor fatigue | Match fundraising target to actual budget need |
| FRC teams under-budgeting travel | Travel costs surprise teams that qualify to regionals/state | Build $2,000–$5,000 travel reserve into year-one FRC budget |
