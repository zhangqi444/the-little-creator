Your pit is your team's home base during a competition. A well-organised pit helps you find tools in seconds, fix problems calmly, and present your team professionally to judges and visiting teams. A poorly organised pit leads to wasted minutes hunting for a missing wrench while the queue manager is calling your name.

This guide covers pit setup for all six programs — from a folding table at an FLL event to a full pit bay at an FRC Regional.

## What every pit needs

Regardless of program, every well-run pit has the same core elements:

| Category | What to bring |
|---|---|
| **Tools** | Screwdrivers (flathead + Phillips), Allen key set, needle-nose pliers, wire strippers/cutters |
| **Fasteners** | Spare bolts, nuts, and standoffs matching your robot's hardware; a small container sorted by size |
| **Charging station** | All batteries labelled; charger(s) rated for the battery type; rotation system so a charged battery is always ready |
| **Laptop/tablet** | Programming environment loaded and tested before you leave home |
| **Cable management** | USB cables, spare sensor/motor cables, zip ties, electrical tape |
| **Signage** | Team number prominently displayed; helps judges and queue managers find you fast |
| **Notebook** | Engineering notebook, portfolio, or lab notebook as required — accessible, not buried |
| **Water and snacks** | Pit crew stays sharp; hunger causes mistakes |

## General layout principles

**Zone your table.** Divide the pit table into three zones:
1. **Active zone** — robot + tools in current use
2. **Staging zone** — charged batteries, replacement parts queued up
3. **Documentation zone** — notebook, laptop, team portfolio

**Keep the floor clear.** Power strips on the floor get kicked. Battery bags on the floor get stepped on. Tape down cables; use hooks or velcro to keep gear off the ground.

**Assign roles.** At minimum: one person "owns" the robot and tracks what's been changed; one person owns the battery rotation; one person is the first point of contact for judges and visitors. Without clear ownership, things go missing and changes don't get logged.

**Label everything.** Team number on every battery, every tool bag, every charger. Gear wanders.

## FLL

FLL pits are typically one 6-foot folding table shared between teams or provided per team depending on the venue. Space is tight.

### What to bring

- SPIKE Prime hub + robot
- All batteries (hub battery + AAA for remote if using it), charger
- Laptop with SPIKE app installed and last working program saved
- Mat section or low-friction surface for test runs (if space allows)
- Engineering notebook (required for judging)
- Innovation Project materials (poster or display, model if applicable)

### Layout tips

- Put the robot in the centre of the table — it is the focus
- Keep the notebook open and visible, not face-down under gear
- If you bring a foldable practice mat section, confirm it fits before the event; most FLL venues don't allow large mat setups in the pit

### What to skip

FLL pits are not the place for elaborate banner stands or custom wooden displays — you'll crowd your neighbours and spend setup time you don't have. Clean and purposeful beats flashy.

## FTC

FTC pits are typically a 6x6 or 8x8 ft bay with a table, power strip, and overhead frame. Most events allow banners.

### What to bring

- Robot + extra set of drive motors and gearboxes
- REV Hub(s) and Control Hub — with firmware already updated
- Android phone (with FTC Robot Controller app) or standalone Control Hub
- Driver Hub (or second phone) with Driver Station app
- Laptop with Android Studio / OnBot Java and all programs saved
- REV Hardware Client on laptop (for Control Hub firmware)
- All batteries (REV Slim, 12V, etc.), charger
- Full set of spare servos (stripping a servo during load-in is common)
- Wire crimping tool + Anderson PowerPole connectors if applicable
- Engineering Portfolio (printed or tablet) accessible for judges

### Layout tips

- Set the robot on a stand or low box so judges and visitors see it at eye level — easier to demonstrate
- Put the Control Hub/REV Hub cable bundle on one side; keep the laptop on the other
- Print a one-page "robot spec sheet" (drive type, motor count, autonomous programs) and tape it to the front of your table — judges appreciate it, and it saves you from giving the same intro many times

### Judging visits

FTC judges roam the pit throughout the event. Have a 2-minute robot overview ready for anyone who stops by. Make sure every team member can deliver it — not just the captain.

## FRC

FRC pits are 10x10 ft bays. You will have a full table, power outlet, and overhead frame. Some events provide an overhead shelf. You will share the pit area with 40-80 other teams.

### What to bring

- Robot (bagged or in cart), battery cart
- At least 4 charged batteries (FRC matches drain fast; you need rapid rotation)
- CAN bus terminator plugs (forget one, and a match debug turns into a CAN hunt)
- Laptop with WPILib, Driver Station, and all match programs
- Field Management System (FMS) compatible radio — updated before the event
- Spare pneumatics fittings and tubing if your robot has a pneumatics system
- Spare motor controllers (Victor, Spark MAX, Talon) — failure at regionals is real
- Hand drill and spares for field repairs
- Label maker — for cables, battery terminals, spare parts bins
- Engineering Notebook / Chairman's documentation (Impact binder)

### Pit layout priorities

- **Battery charging station first**: Set it up before anything else. Four batteries cycling means you never miss a match for a dead battery.
- **Toolbox on the right side**: Most teams have right-handed technicians; keep the bench clear on the left for the robot.
- **Pit board**: A whiteboard or large sticky note listing: next match number, battery currently in the robot, batteries currently charging, and known issues. Reduces miscommunication during the hectic middle rounds.
- **Cable management**: FRC robots have a lot of wires. When making field repairs, use numbered zip tie tags on every cable so you know what you've disconnected.

### Signage

FRC pit signage matters. A team number banner that is visible from the aisle helps alliance partners and judges find you during busy qualifying rounds. Keep it above head height so it's readable across a crowded pit area.

### Inspection

All FRC robots pass through official inspection before competing. Know your robot's weight (without battery), bumper dimensions, and which items need to come off for inspection. Pack a tape measure.

## VEX IQ (VIQRC)

VEX IQ pits are typically one folding table shared among 2-4 teams, or a dedicated small space depending on venue size.

### What to bring

- VEX IQ robot (can be disassembled for transport; use your build instructions)
- IQ smart batteries + charger (bring 2+ batteries; charge overnight before the event)
- Laptop or tablet with VEXcode IQ installed
- USB-A to micro-USB cable for programming
- Spare smart motors and sensor cables
- Engineering notebook (required for Teamwork/Autonomous Skills judging)
- A small container of spare VEX IQ beams, pins, and connectors

### Layout tips

- Keep spares in a small labelled container — loose VEX IQ parts vanish instantly on a crowded table
- Have the notebook open before judges walk by; don't hunt for it when they arrive
- VEX IQ teams often share tables with another team — introduce yourselves early and agree on which half of the table is yours

### Build speed

VEX IQ robots can be rebuilt from scratch in under an hour if you have your build instructions. If a structural failure happens, don't panic — triage: what's the minimum fix to keep the robot running?

## VEX V5 / VRC

VEX V5 pits at most events are 8x8 or 10x10 ft bays with a table, outlet, and frame.

### What to bring

- V5 robot + all motors and sensors
- V5 Brain + controller, charging cables
- At least 2 V5 Smart Batteries + dual charger
- Laptop with VEXcode Pro V5 (or PROS) loaded
- USB-A to USB-C cable (for Brain)
- Spare V5 Smart Motors (one motor failure can end a match)
- Spare pneumatics (tank, solenoid, tubing) if your robot uses pneumatics
- Spare V5 steel structural components (channels, standoffs) for field repairs
- Engineering Notebook — open and accessible for pit judges

### Layout tips

- Print a "robot at a glance" card: drive type, autonomous programs (names and what they do), tuning notes. Tape it to the front of your pit table.
- Set the robot on a lift stand if you have one — makes under-robot inspection and cable access much faster during repairs
- Keep the V5 Brain firmware up to date before the event; firmware mismatch is a common source of mysterious connection issues

### Judging

VRC judging is pit-based. Judges walk the pit area and approach teams without warning. Every team member needs to be ready to talk about the robot and the notebook — not just the captain. Practice the 2-minute overview until everyone can deliver it.

## VEX U / VURC

VEX U pits accommodate two robots and typically a college-team-sized group of students. Events vary in pit bay size; confirm with the event organiser.

### What to bring

- Both robots with all motors and sensors
- V5 Brains x2, controllers x4 (driver + operator per robot), all cables
- Multiple V5 Smart Batteries + charger set for both robots
- Full PROS development environment on laptop (PROS CLI + VS Code extension)
- Spare motors, pneumatics, and structural components for both robots
- Two separate tool sets — one per robot — to avoid pit confusion when both robots need work simultaneously
- Engineering Notebook covering both robots' design history

### Two-robot pit management

Running two robots in one pit creates the obvious problem: both robots may need attention at the same time. Designate:

- **Robot A lead** — the person who owns all changes to the 15-inch robot
- **Robot B lead** — same for the 24-inch (or whichever size B is)
- **Notebook keeper** — logs every change made to either robot, with timestamps

Changes to one robot that affect match strategy (e.g., a new autonomous routine) must be communicated to the drive team for the other robot before the next match.

## Common pit mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Only one person knows where things are | That person gets called to the field; pit crew freezes | Label everything; brief all pit crew on the layout |
| Batteries not labelled or rotated | Robot goes to a match with 30% charge | Assign a battery manager; label batteries 1-4 |
| Notebook buried under tools | Judge arrives, you can't find it | Documentation zone on the table, always accessible |
| Repair made in queue without logging it | You forget what changed after a bad match | Use a pit whiteboard; note every change with match number |
| Too much equipment on the table | No room to actually work on the robot | Pack only what you'll use; leave spares in a bag under the table |
| No spare of the most failure-prone part | Servo strip, motor fails; match forfeit | Identify your robot's weakest link in practice and carry a spare |
| Standing around waiting between matches | Wasted time; team gets bored and unfocused | Fill queuing time with planned tasks: charge batteries, review next auto program, walk the pit, debrief last match |

## Quick-reference pit checklist

Use this at load-in before your first match:

- [ ] Team number displayed and visible from the aisle
- [ ] All batteries on chargers; rotation system agreed
- [ ] Laptop open, last working program loaded and verifiable
- [ ] Tools in known locations; everyone has been shown where they are
- [ ] Engineering notebook / portfolio on the table, accessible
- [ ] Pit roles assigned: robot lead, battery manager, judge point-of-contact
- [ ] Pit board/whiteboard set up (FRC/FTC/VRC) with match schedule visible
- [ ] Floor clear; cables managed; nothing that trips people
