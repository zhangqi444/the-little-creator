Season one is about survival. Season two is about actually doing FLL. Here's what experienced coaches consistently change after their first year.

---

## 1. Start earlier — actually earlier

You probably started "early" last year and still felt rushed. Second-year coaches typically start 4–6 weeks before they think they need to.

**What to move up:**
- **Robot Game strategy:** Start discussing missions before the Challenge Set arrives. Watch reveal videos, read the game description, sketch strategies as a team. When the mat arrives, you're already thinking, not starting from zero.
- **Innovation Project:** Year one teams often treat IP as an afterthought after robot. It's half your judging score. Start IP research in September, not October.
- **Notebook:** Start it on day one of the season. Retroactive notebooks show in judging. Real-time notebooks don't.

---

## 2. Narrow the robot strategy

Year one teams try to complete every mission. Year two teams pick 8–10 missions and get really good at them.

**The math:** A robot that reliably scores 200 points beats a robot that *tries* for 350 and scores 150. Reliability wins tournaments more than ambition.

**How to narrow:**
1. Score every mission on the mat by expected value: (points × probability of completing it cleanly)
2. Cut missions with low expected value or that require complex multi-step movement
3. Build your attachment strategy around the missions you kept
4. Spend practice time drilling those missions until they're automatic, not exploring new ones

See [FLL Robot Strategy Guide](/guides/robot-strategy/) for the full framework.

---

## 3. Let the kids lead more

Year one: you probably guided more than you realized. Year two: watch how much the kids can do when you step back.

**Practical shifts:**
- Let kids run the practice agenda (with a loose structure you set in advance)
- When the robot fails, ask "what do you think happened?" before explaining
- Assign specific roles: one kid owns the attachment for each mission, one kid owns the code for each run
- Let them present to judges in their own words — over-coached presentations are obvious and score lower

The hardest thing for coaches: not fixing the robot during a bad practice run. Let them troubleshoot first.

---

## 4. Take Core Values judging seriously

Year one teams often lose the Champion's Award in Core Values judging — not because they have bad values, but because they don't know how to demonstrate them to judges.

**What judges actually look for:**
- Specific examples of how the team helped each other (names, situations)
- Evidence the team handled conflict constructively (not "we never fight")
- Kids who can describe the team's process, not just the outcome
- The *whole team* contributing to the conversation — not just the loud ones

**What to practice:**
- Run 5-minute mock Core Values sessions at the end of regular practice
- Rotate who speaks — every kid should be comfortable answering any question
- Review the Core Values rubric as a team; decide together what your best examples are
- See [Core Values Team Activities](/guides/core-values-activities/) for activities that build this naturally

---

## 5. Prepare specifically for each judging panel

Three separate 5-minute sessions. Three different panels. Each one matters.

**Robot Design:** Judges want to understand *why* you made decisions, not just *what* you built. Have kids explain trade-offs: "We tried X but switched to Y because..."

**Innovation Project:** Judges want a real problem, a real solution, and real connection to the season theme. The solution needs to be *innovative* — not just "raise awareness." Have a specific, testable idea.

**Core Values:** See above. Don't overprepare scripts — judges can tell. Do help kids find their own genuine examples.

Run at least two full mock judging sessions with a parent or friend playing judge before tournament day. See [Preparing for FLL Judging](/guides/judging-prep/) for what each panel looks for in detail.

---

## 6. Build a second-drive team

Year one: two or three kids do most of the driving. Year two: every kid on the team knows how to run every attachment.

Why it matters: teams that rely on one driver are one sick kid away from disaster at a tournament. Depth is resilience.

**How to build it:**
- Rotate who runs each mission in practice — even missions "owned" by one person
- Deliberate cross-training: spend one practice where everyone switches roles
- Make it a team goal: "by regionals, every person can run missions 3, 5, and 7"

---

## 7. Fix your engineering notebook

Year one notebooks: usually started late, missing dates, heavy on photos, light on reasoning.

Year two notebooks judges want to see:
- **Dated entries** for every session (even short ones)
- **Decision rationale:** why you chose approach A over B, not just what you chose
- **Failed attempts documented:** judges respect teams that tried things and learned from failure
- **Clear sections** for Robot Game, Innovation Project, and Core Values — one notebook covers all three

See [FLL Engineering Notebook Guide](/guides/notebook-guide/) for the full structure.

---

## 8. Manage the adults better

By season two you know which parents help and which create chaos. Structure it.

**What works:**
- One lead coach who makes final calls — multiple coaches with equal authority creates confusion
- Clearly defined roles for parent helpers (logistics, snacks, transport — not coaching during practice)
- A simple communication channel (group text or email) with a predictable cadence
- Tournament day roles assigned in advance: who drives, who handles pit setup, who talks to judges if needed

See [Parent Communication Guide](/for-educators/parent-communication/) for templates and a communication calendar.

---

## 9. Register early

Year two teams know that qualifier spots fill up. Don't assume you'll get your preferred date and venue.

- Register nationally as soon as the FIRST Dashboard opens for the season (typically May–June)
- Register with your PDO as soon as their registration opens — some regions sell out in days
- Book travel (if needed for regionals or championship) the moment you know dates

---

## 10. Enjoy it more

Year one was about learning the rules. Year two, you know enough to enjoy what's actually happening: kids solving problems, building something real, growing in ways that show up years later.

The teams that burn out are usually the ones who made it too serious too fast. Keep it fun. Let kids fail and recover. Celebrate the small wins as loudly as the big ones.
