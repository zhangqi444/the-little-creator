The FRC Impact Award (formerly Chairman's Award) is the most prestigious recognition in FRC. Winning it requires a three-part submission: a **written essay**, a **video presentation**, and a **judged interview** with multiple senior students. Each piece is independently weighted; together they tell a single story about your team's impact on STEM and the FIRST community.

This guide is the FRC counterpart to the [FLL Engineering Notebook Guide](/guides/notebook-guide/) — but Impact is a different beast. It's not about documenting the season; it's about documenting *years* of community-facing work and making the case that your team's impact justifies recognition.

:::note[This is a multi-year project]
Impact is rarely won by teams in their first three years. The award rewards a portfolio of sustained outreach — multiple mentored teams, ongoing community programs, measurable change. If you're in year one or two, your realistic target is to *start building the work that future Impact submissions will document*. The submission itself comes later.
:::

## What the three pieces actually do

### The essay (10,000 character limit)

A focused written narrative about your team's identity, vision, and impact. The character cap forces tightness — most teams overshoot the first draft by 50% and have to cut. The essay is read by judges *before* the interview; it shapes the questions they ask.

A good Impact essay is **specific**. Generic phrases ("we inspire students through STEM") are filler. Specific claims with evidence ("we started 4 FLL teams in Pierce County, totalling 23 students, all of whom have since competed at state") are the substance.

### The video (3 minutes, hard cap)

A produced video that conveys the team's spirit, members, and impact. Tight 3-minute videos win — long ones get cut off. Should feature multiple students speaking, evidence of outreach (footage of events, not just photos), and a coherent emotional arc.

The video does what the essay can't: convey the team's *culture* and *spirit*. Judges score the video on production quality, but also on whether it makes them want to know more.

### The interview (about 7 minutes)

Multiple senior students (typically 2–4) interview with the Impact judging panel. Judges ask follow-up questions based on the essay and video. The interview tests whether the students *know* the work — whether the essay is real or someone wrote it for them.

The students who do the interview should be the students who *did* the work. If the captain wrote the essay alone, the captain shouldn't be the only interviewer — depth across students reads better.

## What gets evaluated

Impact judges look for evidence across these areas:

**Team identity and sustainability**
- Multi-year continuity
- Recruiting and retention practices
- Leadership transitions across student generations
- Strong adult mentor pipeline

**Outreach and the FIRST community**
- Teams started or mentored (FLL, FTC, FRC) — this is often the most-cited evidence
- Camps, workshops, demo days hosted by your team
- Partnerships with schools, libraries, museums, professional organisations
- Recognition by your local / state government, professional groups

**Engineering and innovation**
- Robot achievements (relevant but not the primary criterion)
- Engineering culture, design process documentation
- Innovation in robot design or strategy

**Gracious Professionalism**
- Treatment of other teams at events
- Behaviour observed during the event itself
- Any cited examples of going above and beyond for other teams

## How to structure the essay

A widely-used Impact essay structure:

1. **Hook + identity (1–2 paragraphs)** — Who are you? Why does your team exist? What's your "thesis"?
2. **Vision and mission (1 paragraph)** — Where are you trying to take FIRST in your community?
3. **Specific outreach evidence (3–5 paragraphs)** — Each major program / partnership gets a paragraph with concrete numbers, ongoing relationships, measurable outcomes
4. **Team sustainability (1–2 paragraphs)** — How do you sustain across years? Leadership pipeline, mentor pipeline, finances
5. **Robot / engineering culture (1–2 paragraphs)** — Briefly cover engineering accomplishments
6. **Gracious Professionalism examples (1 paragraph)** — Specific stories of GP in action
7. **Future vision (1 paragraph)** — What's next? Where will you be in 5 years?

The essay is read on screen by judges, not printed. Format matters — paragraph breaks, white space, scannable structure. Don't write 10,000 characters of solid prose.

## How to structure the video

A widely-used 3-minute Impact video structure:

- **0:00–0:20** — Cold open / team identity, hook
- **0:20–1:30** — Outreach evidence (footage of events, multiple students speaking)
- **1:30–2:00** — Engineering culture, robot achievements (briefly)
- **2:00–2:30** — Sustainability, leadership transition
- **2:30–3:00** — Future vision, close

Production quality matters less than authenticity. A well-shot iPhone video of real students at real events beats a polished but generic production. Captions for clarity (accessibility); name + role lower-third when each student first appears.

## Common Impact submission mistakes

**Generic claims without numbers.** "We inspire students through STEM" means nothing. "We've mentored 4 FLL teams (49 students) over the past 3 years, of which 12 have continued to FTC" is specific.

**Outreach without ongoing relationships.** One-off events are weak evidence. Ongoing partnerships are strong.

**The essay sounds like one person.** A team essay should reflect a team voice. If one student wrote the whole thing alone, judges can usually tell.

**Video too long.** 3:00 is a hard cap. 2:55 is safe; 3:05 gets you disqualified.

**Robot achievements over-emphasised.** Impact is not about how well your robot plays. Brief mention is fine; spending half the essay on robot specs is a miss.

**Interview students who didn't do the work.** If your interview students can't speak to the outreach in real detail, judges will notice.

## How to actually pursue Impact over multiple years

This is where Impact differs from every other FRC award — you're not preparing for one tournament; you're building a *body of work* over years.

**Year 1 (rookie):**

- Focus on building the team. Recruit, find mentors, establish meeting cadence.
- Start one outreach program — typically mentoring a single FLL or FTC team.
- Document everything. Photos at every event, notes, names, contacts. Start an "Impact archive" folder you'll thank yourself for in year 3.

**Year 2:**

- Continue the year-1 outreach; add one more thing (a workshop, a school partnership, a demo day).
- Begin drafting an Impact essay as an internal exercise — it'll be terrible, that's fine. The exercise reveals what evidence you're missing.

**Year 3:**

- You should have at least 2 major outreach programs running.
- Begin submitting Impact at one event. Don't expect to win. Use the judges' feedback to identify weak areas.

**Years 4+:**

- Submit at every regional / district event.
- Make annual updates to the essay and video as new evidence accumulates.
- Compete for Engineering Inspiration in parallel (similar structure, narrower focus — easier to win first).
