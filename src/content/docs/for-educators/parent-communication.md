Good parent communication saves you hours of individual questions, prevents last-minute surprises at tournaments, and builds the kind of trust that makes volunteers show up when you need them. This guide covers what to communicate, when, and includes copy-paste-ready message templates.

---

## The core principle: predictable cadence beats frequent updates

Parents don't need daily updates. They need to know:
1. What's expected of their kid (time commitment, materials, behaviour)
2. What's coming up in the next two weeks
3. How to reach you if something goes wrong

A predictable weekly or biweekly message — even a short one — is more effective than sporadic bursts followed by silence.

---

## Communication timeline

### Before the season starts

Send an **onboarding message** that covers:
- What FLL (or VEX) actually is — don't assume they know
- The time commitment: practice days/times, expected tournament date(s)
- What the team needs from parents (volunteer help, snacks rotation, transport)
- How you'll communicate (email list, group chat, etc.)
- Your contact info

### Start of season (first 2 weeks)

Send a **season kickoff** message covering the season theme, initial schedule, and what kids will be working on.

### Monthly during the season

A brief **monthly update**: what the team has accomplished, what's coming up, any needs or asks.

### 2–3 weeks before tournament

A **tournament preparation message**: date, location, schedule, what to bring, volunteering needs.

### Day before tournament

A **final reminder**: logistics only — time, location, parking, what to wear.

### After tournament

A **debrief message**: results (briefly), what went well, thanks to volunteers, what's next.

## Message templates

### Template 1 — Season onboarding

> **Subject: Welcome to [Team Name] — [Season Year] FLL Season**
>
> Hi everyone,
>
> Welcome to the [Team Name] robotics team! I'm [your name], and I'll be coaching your kids through the [Season Name] FIRST LEGO League season.
>
> **What is FLL?**
> FIRST LEGO League Challenge is a robotics competition covering ages 9–16. Teams build and program a LEGO robot to complete missions on a themed game board, research a real-world problem for an Innovation Project, and present their work to judges. It's part engineering, part science fair, and a lot of teamwork.
>
> **Time commitment**
> Practices: [Day], [Time], [Location] — starting [date]
> Tournament: [Approximate date/month, confirm when registered]
> We expect kids to attend at least [X]% of practices to be ready for the tournament.
>
> **What we need from parents**
> - Punctual pickup (practices end at [time]; lingering kids make it hard to close up)
> - One volunteer shift at the tournament (I'll send a sign-up closer to the date)
> - Optional: snacks rotation, transport coordination for away tournaments
>
> **How I'll communicate**
> I'll send updates via [email/WhatsApp/group chat] — roughly weekly during active season. For urgent issues, [phone/text/email] is best.
>
> Questions? Reply here or [contact method].
>
> Looking forward to a great season,
> [Your name]

### Template 3 — Tournament preparation (2–3 weeks out)

> **Subject: Tournament Day — What You Need to Know**
>
> Hi everyone,
>
> Our tournament is coming up! Here are the details:
>
> **Date:** [Date]
> **Location:** [Venue name and address]
> **Arrival time:** [Time — typically 30–60 min before matches start]
> **Expected finish:** [Approximate end time]
>
> **What to bring**
> - Wear the team shirt (or [colour] if we don't have shirts)
> - Comfortable shoes — you'll be on your feet all day
> - Water bottle and snacks for the morning; [lunch plan — provided/bring your own/food available]
> - Notebook and pencil (useful for students tracking scores)
>
> **Robot transport**
> I'll bring the robot and equipment. If you're driving separately and can take [specific items], let me know.
>
> **Volunteering**
> I still need [X] parent volunteers for [specific roles — table judging assistant, pit monitor, etc.]. Reply if you can help. Volunteers usually get a better view of the whole event.
>
> **What to expect**
> The day is long and exciting. Teams have 3 robot match runs, judging sessions (robot design, innovation project, core values), and downtime in the pit area. Kids should expect nerves — that's normal. Your job as a parent is encouragement, not coaching from the sidelines.
>
> Questions? [Contact method].
>
> See you there,
> [Your name]

## Tips for managing parent group chats

If you use WhatsApp, Signal, or a similar group chat:

- **Set expectations up front:** "I'll use this for logistics and updates. For individual concerns, please message me directly."
- **Mute reply-alls:** Ask parents to reply directly to you, not to the group, for non-group-relevant messages.
- **Post sparingly:** A high-volume group chat gets muted. Fewer, more useful messages stay read.
- **Don't use group chat to handle problems:** If a parent has a concern about their child, handle it in private.

## Handling difficult parent conversations

Occasionally you'll need to have a harder conversation: a kid isn't pulling their weight, attendance is a problem, or a parent is overstepping.

**Guidelines:**
- Have these conversations privately, not in group channels
- Lead with observation, not judgment: "I've noticed Jamie has missed 4 of the last 6 practices" rather than "Jamie isn't committed"
- Be clear about the impact: "If attendance doesn't improve, it's not fair to the rest of the team for Jamie to compete"
- Offer a path forward: "Can we agree on an attendance plan for the next month?"
- Document the conversation briefly (notes for yourself) in case it needs to be revisited

**When parents overstep at events:**
It happens — a parent coaches from the sideline, argues with a judge, or makes comments about another team. Address it calmly and privately at the first opportunity: "I know you want the best for [kid] — can we agree that during matches, the coaching stays with me and the team?" Most parents respond well when approached with good intent.
