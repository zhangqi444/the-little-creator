How you respond in the 90 seconds after something goes wrong at practice defines what your team believes about mistakes. This guide is about those 90 seconds — and the longer conversations that follow.

It complements the family-facing [Parent Communication Guide](/for-educators/parent-communication/) and the [Mental Models for Competition](/guides/mental-models-for-competition/) guide for student-facing frameworks. This guide is for the coach's side of the conversation.

---

## Why feedback framing matters

Students who compete in robotics are repeatedly exposed to failure in public: the robot misses a mission, the presentation stumbles, the code crashes at inspection. Left without a feedback model, coaches default to whatever they experienced growing up — which is often either harsh evaluation ("that was sloppy") or false reassurance ("you all did great!"). Both patterns erode trust and learning over time.

Research on feedback (particularly Carol Dweck's work on growth mindset and John Hattie's meta-analysis on effective feedback) points consistently to the same finding: **feedback about process outperforms feedback about outcome or person**. "Your sensor calibration was off — here's what we can check" lands better than "you didn't try hard enough" or "don't worry, next time will be better."

In a robotics context, this matters doubly because:
1. Most failures have a **diagnosable cause** — the team can often find it if you give them the space to look.
2. The engineering cycle **requires iteration** — if failure feels like judgment, kids skip it in favour of "good enough."

## The four feedback modes (and when to use each)

### 1. Descriptive feedback
State what you observed without evaluation.

> "The robot made it through the first two missions and then stopped at the third."

Use when: the team is in diagnostic mode and needs a neutral starting point.

Avoid: adding "because you didn't test enough" — that collapses descriptive feedback into evaluative feedback before the team has a chance to investigate.

### 2. Process-focused feedback
Connect the outcome to a concrete step or decision.

> "The grip motor stalled out. That usually means the current limit is set too low or the gear ratio is working against you. Let's check which."

Use when: you can identify a mechanical, programming, or design cause.

Why it works: it treats the failure as information, not judgment. The student hears "here's what we know about what happened" rather than "here's what you did wrong."

### 3. Questioning feedback
Turn the analysis back to the student.

> "What do you think happened there?"
> "If we ran that again tomorrow, what would you change first?"

Use when: the student is capable of diagnosing with a nudge, or you want to build ownership. Best for intermediate-to-advanced teams.

Avoid: using questions rhetorically to imply blame ("Well, did you *test* it?"). The tone makes the difference.

### 4. Encouragement feedback
Acknowledge genuine effort, growth, or courage.

> "You stuck with the debugging loop for 45 minutes. That's exactly the discipline this needs."

Use when: the student did something genuinely well — effort, persistence, collaboration, a good question — even if the outcome wasn't what anyone wanted.

Avoid: using this to paper over a real problem. Students are perceptive; hollow praise in the face of obvious failure teaches them your encouragement doesn't mean anything.

## After something goes wrong: a 3-step response pattern

This is the 90-second framework for in-the-moment coaching after a visible failure (robot crash, bad match, presentation stumble):

**Step 1 — Acknowledge without spinning.**
Don't immediately pivot to "but here's what you did right." Name what happened first.

> "That run didn't go the way we wanted."

A beat of honesty lands much better than instant cheerleading.

**Step 2 — Separate the robot from the kid.**
Make explicit that what failed was a system, not a person.

> "The robot had a problem. That's not the same thing as *you* failing."

This is especially important for younger students (FLL, VEX IQ) and for students who tend to over-identify with their work.

**Step 3 — Move to next action, not next emotion.**
Redirect to something concrete.

> "Let's go back to the pit and find out what happened before the next match."

Staying in the emotional moment too long — reassuring, explaining, consoling — often makes it worse. A concrete next step gives students somewhere to put their energy.

## The post-event debrief

After a tournament or a significant competition, a structured debrief beats an unstructured "how do you feel?" The goal is to extract learning without making the conversation feel like punishment.

A simple three-part format:

1. **What worked?** Start here. Even in a rough event, there are genuine positives. Get specific — "the autonomous ran clean in qualifying match 3" beats "you were really great."

2. **What surprised us?** This is a safer entry point than "what went wrong." It invites curiosity rather than blame: "We didn't expect the field reset to be different from what we practiced on."

3. **What would we do differently next time?** Forward-facing. Keeps the conversation about choices and process rather than past mistakes.

Take notes during this conversation if you can. Students feel heard when they see the coach writing things down — and it gives you a concrete list for pre-season planning.

## Program-specific notes

### FLL / VEX IQ (younger students, ages 8–14)

- Keep post-failure conversations short. A 5-minute debrief is usually more than enough.
- At this age, failure can feel catastrophic. The coach's body language matters as much as the words. If you're calm, they can be calm.
- Avoid comparisons to other teams: "Team A's robot did that part fine" is almost never useful and often harmful.
- Core Values language is your friend here: "gracious professionalism" and "doing your best" are shared frameworks the kids already know.

### FTC / VEX V5 (middle and high school, ages 12–18)

- Students at this level can handle more direct process feedback. They often appreciate straight talk if it's delivered without contempt.
- Avoid unsolicited technical opinions during a student's explanation of what went wrong. Let them finish their diagnosis before you offer yours.
- Watch for perfectionism spirals — some high-achieving students treat any mistake as evidence of systemic failure. Normalise iteration explicitly: "Every good robot we've seen at competition has been through at least three rounds of this exact kind of debugging."

### FRC / VEX U (high school and college, ages 14–22)

- Students at this level may have strong opinions — sometimes correct, sometimes not. A useful approach: ask them to propose a fix before you offer one. If their proposal is workable, let them run it.
- Multi-mentor teams sometimes give students conflicting feedback in the moment. Agree on a "one voice at a time" rule for during-competition coaching.
- After major failures (robot breakdown at a critical match, impact presentation doesn't advance), give the team a short physical break before debriefing. Trying to debrief in the first 10 minutes after a high-stakes loss often produces more heat than insight.

## Handling specific hard situations

### The student who shuts down after failure

Signs: goes quiet, physically withdraws, refuses to engage with the robot.

Approach: don't force a verbal response. A non-verbal task — "can you hold this while I check the connector?" — re-engages without requiring them to perform emotional recovery they're not ready for. Check in one-on-one later, away from the group.

### The student who blames teammates

This is often a symptom of their own discomfort with failure. Redirect to the system:

> "The question we're trying to answer right now isn't whose fault it was — it's what the robot needs. Can you help with that?"

If blame-shifting is a recurring pattern, address it in a separate conversation (not in front of the team).

### The student who deflects with humor

Some students use jokes to avoid processing. This isn't always a problem — humor as a coping mechanism is legitimate. Let it pass unless it's blocking genuine diagnosis. If the team is laughing instead of learning, try a gentle redirect:

> "Alright, alright. Seriously though — what did we see? What do we need to fix?"

### When the coach made the call that led to the failure

Own it directly.

> "I told you to run the full routine even though we hadn't tested the last mission in competition conditions. That was my call and it didn't work out. Here's what I'd do differently."

Students notice when coaches model accountability. This is one of the highest-value things you can do.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it backfires | Better approach |
|---|---|---|
| Praising outcomes, not process | Teaches that results are what matter | Praise specific effort, choices, or persistence |
| Diagnosing publicly and negatively in front of the team | Shuts down the student who made the error | Pull aside for individual feedback; keep group debrief on systems |
| Comparing to other teams | Creates envy and damages team cohesion | Compare to the team's own prior performance |
| Over-consoling after genuine errors | Students perceive it as dismissive; doesn't lead to learning | Acknowledge, name what went wrong, redirect to next step |
| Asking "why didn't you test this?" | Rhetorical blame question, not genuine inquiry | "What can we do before the next match to confirm it's fixed?" |
| Staying in emotional debrief too long | Keeps the team in a low-efficacy state | Move to concrete action after 3-5 minutes |
| Skipping debrief entirely | Misses the learning opportunity | Even a 3-minute debrief is worth it |

## Quick reference

| Situation | What to do |
|---|---|
| Robot fails mid-run | Calm, factual observation; move to diagnosis |
| Bad qualification match | 3-step: acknowledge then separate kid from robot then next action |
| Student shuts down | Non-verbal re-engagement; one-on-one check-in later |
| Student blames teammates | Redirect to system; address pattern separately |
| Post-tournament debrief | What worked, what surprised us, what we'd change |
| Coach made the bad call | Own it directly |
