Congratulations — your team advanced. That's a real accomplishment. Now you have 3–4 weeks (sometimes less) before the next event, and the gap between qualifier competition and regional/state championship is real.

This guide covers what actually changes at the next level, what to focus on in the short window you have, and how to prepare your team emotionally for a harder field.

---

## What changes at regionals / state championship

### The competition is significantly harder

Every team at regionals earned their spot. The average robot score, Innovation Project quality, and Core Values polish are all noticeably higher than at qualifiers. Teams that coasted on robot score alone will notice this.

**What this means practically:**
- A score that placed you 2nd at your qualifier might place you 8th at regionals
- Innovation Projects are better researched, more confidently presented
- Judges are more experienced and ask harder follow-up questions
- Core Values is not an afterthought for the teams you're now competing against

This isn't discouraging — it's just a calibration. Your team earned this spot. Now you push harder.

### The day is longer and more intense

Regional and state championships run longer than qualifiers, often 8–10 hours. Kids get tired, hungry, and emotionally raw in ways they didn't at a half-day qualifier. Build that into your planning.

### Advancement paths are narrower

Only a small fraction of teams advance from regionals to championship / World Festival. Most teams' season ends at regionals. That's not failure — reaching regionals already puts you in the top tier of your region.

## The 3–4 week prep plan

### Week 1: honest assessment

Run every part of the competition as if it were real. Then answer these questions honestly:

**Robot Game:**
- What's your current reliable score? (not your best run — your consistent score)
- Which missions have a <70% success rate? Cut them or fix them.
- Is there one more mission you could reliably add? Only add it if the answer is yes and you have two full weeks to practice it.

**Innovation Project:**
- Did judges ask questions your team couldn't answer confidently? Fix those gaps.
- Was the solution specific enough? "Raise awareness" won't cut it at regionals.
- Did all team members contribute to the presentation? Judges notice if one kid carries it.

**Core Values:**
- Did the team show genuine teamwork or just say the right words?
- Did you do anything visibly Gracious Professionalism at the qualifier (helping another team, etc.)?
- What's your Core Values team activity for regionals? Practice it until it flows naturally.

### Week 2: targeted improvement

One or two specific improvements based on your assessment above. Not a full rebuild — targeted fixes.

**For the robot:** fix the specific failure modes you identified. Run the 5x drill (5 consecutive attempts per mission) to validate.

**For Innovation Project:** one more practice presentation, focused on the weak spots. Invite a parent or stranger to play judge and ask hard questions.

**For Core Values:** one focused team activity that practices something you identified as weak.

### Week 3: stabilize

Same as qualifier prep week 1 (from [Tournament Week Prep](/guides/tournament-week-prep/)):
- Freeze the robot again after any changes
- Full mock tournament day in a different space
- Confirm logistics with families

### Days before: same checklist, higher stakes

- Everything charged the night before
- Programs saved locally on the device going to the event
- Notebook complete
- IP materials packed
- Spare parts kit reviewed

## Managing expectations

### With the kids

Be honest: "The teams at regionals are really good. We might not advance again. That's okay — we've already done something most teams never do. Let's focus on doing our best work and see what happens."

Kids who are over-confident going into regionals tend to fall apart when things get hard. Kids who are genuinely prepared and have realistic expectations are resilient.

### With the parents

Some parents will shift into "we're going to Worlds" mode after one advancement. Gently recalibrate early:

> "Regionals is a much harder field — every team there earned their spot. We'll do our best work and see what happens. Even if we don't advance, getting here is a real achievement."

Set this expectation before regionals, not during or after.

### With yourself

Coaching a team through regionals means managing your own nerves too. The best thing you can do is be calm — kids mirror coach energy. If you're visibly anxious, they'll be anxious.

Your job at the event is logistics and calm. The kids do the competing.

## If you advance again (to championship / Worlds)

The cycle repeats, compressed. Typically 6–10 weeks from regionals to championship; Worlds is in spring.

Key differences at championship level:
- Travel logistics become real (hotel, flights for some teams)
- Competition is international or national — another significant step up
- Book travel the moment you know dates — it fills fast
- Some PDOs provide travel stipends; ask yours

See the [FLL Resource Map](/resources/fll-resource-map/) for championship-specific resources when you get there.

---

## If your season ends here

Most seasons end at regionals. That's the norm, not the exception.

The teams at Worlds are extraordinary. Reaching regionals already puts your team in the top fraction of FLL teams globally. That's genuinely impressive.

What matters most at the end: What did the kids learn? What are they excited about next season? What would you do differently as a coach?

A debrief session within a week of the event, while memories are fresh, is worth more than any trophy.
