This guide extends the [Classroom Assessment Rubric](/for-educators/assessment-rubric/) to cover all six programs — FLL, FTC, FRC, VEX IQ, VEX V5/VRC, and VEX U — and adds formative assessment tools, structured self-assessment templates, and program-specific guidance for mixed-skill teams.

:::note[Official competition rubrics]
This is a classroom and club teaching tool. For official judging, download the current season's rubrics from [FIRST](https://www.firstinspires.org) or [VEX Robotics](https://www.vexrobotics.com/competition). Tournament rubrics and this guide serve different purposes and should not be conflated.
:::

## Why assessment looks different in robotics

In a standard classroom, assessment usually means individual performance on a discrete task. In a robotics team, most of the meaningful work is collaborative, iterative, and hard to attribute to a single student. This creates three problems:

1. **Attribution problem** — Who actually built that autonomous routine? All three of them? Just one?
2. **Role imbalance problem** — Some students dominate; others disengage. High team scores can mask low individual growth.
3. **Skill-diversity problem** — A team with a strong programmer and a strong builder may have very different individual growth curves. One standard rubric doesn't capture both.

The tools in this guide are designed to work despite these problems, not pretend they don't exist.

## Program quick-reference

Different programs have different primary skill emphases. Adjust your assessment focus accordingly.

| Program | Ages | Primary skills to assess | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **FLL** | 9–14 | Programming (SPIKE), Building, Core Values, Innovation Project, Problem Solving | Only program with a mandatory research project component |
| **FTC** | 12–18 | Programming (Java/Blocks/OnBot), Building (REV/goBILDA), Engineering Notebook, Strategy | Notebook is scored at competition; weight it in classroom too |
| **FRC** | 14–18 | Programming (Java/WPILib), Mechanical/Electrical, Engineering Notebook, Strategy, Outreach | Most technically complex; outreach and Impact Award matter |
| **VEX IQ** | 8–14 | Programming (VEXcode Blocks/Python), Building (snap), Teamwork, Skills routines | Collaborative matches; both drive and programming skills count |
| **VEX V5/VRC** | 14–18 | Programming (C++/Python), Mechanical design, Notebook (Engineering), Alliance strategy | Alliance selection and scouting add strategic layer |
| **VEX U** | 18+ | Full-stack engineering, Programming (any language), CAD/fabrication, Research | University teams; self-directed; peer-review model fits |

## Core assessment dimensions (all programs)

These five dimensions apply across all six programs. Scoring uses the same 1–4 scale as the [Classroom Assessment Rubric](/for-educators/assessment-rubric/).

### 1. Technical skill

| Score | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **4** | Executes program-appropriate technical tasks independently; can explain and teach what they did |
| **3** | Completes most technical tasks with occasional support; understands what they're doing |
| **2** | Completes tasks with significant guidance; understanding is partial |
| **1** | Not yet attempting technical tasks independently |

**Program-specific anchors:**
- *FLL/VEX IQ* — programming in Blocks/Python; attaching and tuning sensors; autonomous missions
- *FTC/VEX V5* — Java or C++ programming; hardware mapping; PID tuning; encoder-based driving
- *FRC* — WPILib Java; subsystems and commands; pneumatics or electronics integration; vision processing
- *VEX U* — custom electronics, CAD-driven design, full-stack software architecture

### 2. Engineering process

| Score | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **4** | Identifies a problem, generates multiple options, builds/tests, uses data to decide, iterates deliberately |
| **3** | Works through most of the engineering cycle with prompting; records results |
| **2** | Builds and tests but without systematic reasoning; changes things randomly |
| **1** | Follows instructions without engaging in design decisions |

**Notes:** This is the hardest dimension to assess without a notebook. For FTC, FRC, and VEX V5+, engineering notebooks give you direct evidence. For FLL and VEX IQ, rely on observation and conversation.

### 3. Collaboration and role fluency

| Score | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **4** | Takes different roles across sessions; actively distributes work; includes quieter members |
| **3** | Works well in a preferred role; can shift when needed; rarely excludes others |
| **2** | Defaults to one role; sometimes dominates or withdraws; responds to redirection |
| **1** | Stuck in one role (or no role); contribution is limited without direct facilitation |

**Notes:** Role fluency matters more than role specialization. A student who *only* programs is less developed than one who can program *and* explain a design decision to a teammate. Watch for healthy specialization vs. unhealthy siloing.

### 4. Communication and documentation

| Score | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **4** | Explains technical decisions clearly to teammates and adults; documentation is detailed and current |
| **3** | Can explain most decisions when asked; documentation covers main work |
| **2** | Struggles to explain work in the moment; documentation is sparse or retrospective |
| **1** | Cannot explain what they did; no meaningful documentation |

**Program-specific anchors:**
- *FLL* — Innovation Project presentation; can talk judges through their robot decisions
- *FTC/FRC/VEX V5* — Engineering notebook quality; can explain notebook entries to a judge
- *VEX U* — Research documentation; code comments; design review presentations

### 5. Mindset and growth orientation

| Score | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **4** | Treats setbacks as information; seeks feedback; improves specific things between sessions |
| **3** | Recovers from failure; accepts feedback; makes some targeted improvements |
| **2** | Gets discouraged after repeated failure; defensively resists feedback |
| **1** | Avoids challenging tasks; shuts down after failure; has not yet developed self-regulation strategies |

**Notes:** This is a developmental dimension, not a judgment. A student who scores 1 in October and 2 in January has grown significantly. Document the trajectory, not just the snapshot.

## Formative assessment tools

### 1. Exit ticket (3-2-1 format)

Use at the end of any session. Takes 3 minutes. Captures real-time learning.

> **3** things you worked on today  
> **2** things you figured out or improved  
> **1** thing you're still stuck on or want to try next session

Collect these. Patterns across a team tell you who needs support and what concepts need re-teaching. A student who answers "1 thing I'm still stuck on" the same way three sessions running needs a direct conversation.

**Program note:** For FTC/FRC/VEX V5, add: *"What did you add to the notebook today?"* — it creates accountability without requiring you to check notebooks every session.

### 2. Role log (weekly)

One sheet per student per week. Ask them to fill it in, not you.

| Session | My role | What I did | What I didn't understand | What I want to try |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | Programmer | Wrote the gyro-straight function | Why PID gain works | Tune the I term |
| Wednesday | Builder | Rebuilt the claw attachment | How to get better grip | Try rubber band wrap |

Patterns to watch: consistent role → consider reassignment. "Didn't understand" fields that are never filled in → student not reflecting honestly, or not being challenged.

### 3. Observation checklist (coach-side)

Keep this on a clipboard or phone. Spend 5–10 minutes deliberately watching one student per session.

```
Student: _______________  Date: _______________  Program: _______________

Technical engagement:
  [ ] Working on assigned task
  [ ] Working on self-directed task
  [ ] Watching (learning? disengaged?)
  [ ] Off task

Communication:
  [ ] Explained idea to teammate
  [ ] Asked a question
  [ ] Asked for the answer
  [ ] Resolved a conflict independently

Engineering process:
  [ ] Made change based on test result
  [ ] Made change without clear reason
  [ ] Discussed options before deciding

Mindset:
  [ ] Persisted after failure
  [ ] Shut down after failure
  [ ] Sought feedback

Notes:
```

Over a season, this gives you qualitative evidence to accompany rubric scores.

### 4. Peer feedback card (mid-season)

A structured peer review mid-season helps students build metacognitive awareness and surfaces team dynamics issues that coaches often miss.

Give each student a card to fill out anonymously about one teammate (rotate so everyone receives feedback).

> **One thing [name] does that helps the team:**  
> **One thing I wish [name] would do differently:**  
> **One thing I think [name] is getting better at:**

Debrief by synthesizing patterns, not individual comments. "Several people mentioned that the team needs to rotate roles more — not just one person." Avoid attribution.

## Self-assessment templates

### Template A — Individual session reflection (all programs)

*5 minutes at end of session. Student-facing language.*

---

**Date:** __________ **Session focus:** __________

**What I worked on today:**

**What went well:**

**What didn't work, and what I think caused it:**

**What I'll try differently next time:**

**My role today:**  ☐ Programmer  ☐ Builder  ☐ Researcher  ☐ Driver  ☐ Documenter  ☐ Other: ___

**Collaboration check:**
- Did I share my ideas?  ☐ Yes  ☐ Sort of  ☐ No
- Did I listen to others' ideas?  ☐ Yes  ☐ Sort of  ☐ No
- Was everyone involved?  ☐ Yes  ☐ Mostly  ☐ No

---

### Template B — Mid-season self-assessment (all programs)

*~15 minutes. Useful at 4-week mark or after first scrimmage/qualifier.*

---

**Rate yourself 1–4 on each dimension:**

| Dimension | My score | Evidence (be specific) |
|---|---|---|
| Technical skill | | |
| Engineering process | | |
| Collaboration & role fluency | | |
| Communication & documentation | | |
| Mindset & growth orientation | | |

**One thing I'm proud of this season so far:**

**One thing I want to improve in the second half of the season:**

**One thing my team does well together:**

**One thing my team needs to work on:**

**A goal for the next four weeks:** (make it specific and checkable — "get better at programming" is not specific; "write the autonomous that completes missions 3 and 4" is)

---

### Template C — End-of-season portfolio reflection (all programs)

*~20 minutes. Use as a final portfolio prompt or for grading conversation.*

---

**Choose one moment this season where something genuinely surprised you — a failure, a breakthrough, or an unexpected result. Describe it in detail:**

*("The robot broke" is not specific. "The gyro sensor started drifting during our third qualifier run because we hadn't zeroed it after the field reset" is specific.)*

**What you did about it:**

**What you learned from it:**

**Choose one skill you didn't have at the start of the season that you have now. Describe what you can do now that you couldn't before:**

**Choose one thing you would do differently if you were starting this season over:**

**For FTC / FRC / VEX V5 / VEX U only — notebook/documentation reflection:**  
*Open your notebook/docs. Find your weakest entry. Why is it weak? What would a better version look like?*

## Program-specific assessment notes

### FLL

- **Innovation Project** adds a distinct research and presentation dimension not present in other programs. Assess each student's *understanding of and contribution to* the project separately from the project's quality.
- **Core Values** are competition-judged but season-long. Use the [FLL Core Values Rubric](/for-educators/core-values-rubric/) alongside this guide.
- **Robot design maturity** at FLL level is age-appropriate. Don't expect engineering-notebook-level documentation from 9-year-olds; do expect them to test and iterate.

### FTC

- **Engineering notebook** is formally judged. Treat documentation as a real assessment category from week one, not an afterthought.
- **Programming complexity** spans a wide range (Blocks → OnBot Java → Android Studio). Calibrate Technical Skill scores to the student's actual coding environment.
- **Driver practice** is often underweighted as "not really learning." It develops spatial reasoning, pressure management, and motor learning. Track it.

### FRC

- **Scale and specialization** — FRC teams have 15–40 members. Assess subteam contribution and cross-subteam communication separately.
- **Outreach and Impact Award** are genuine learning opportunities. Students who own the team's community outreach are doing meaningful project management and communication work — score it.
- **Electrical and pneumatics** are FRC-specific safety-critical disciplines. If you have students working in these areas, assessment should include safety protocols as a non-negotiable floor.

### VEX IQ

- **Driving Skills and Autonomous Coding Skills** both count in competition. Programming a reliable autonomous should be weighted alongside teleop driving — they're equally valued in the game.
- **Teamwork Challenge** — Collaborative matches mean students on *different* teams work together. This is an unusual and valuable collaboration context. Debrief it.
- **Ages 8–14** — Be developmentally calibrated. An 8-year-old scoring 2 on "Engineering process" is often doing very well for their age.

### VEX V5/VRC

- **Alliance selection** is a real strategic skill. Students who research other teams, maintain a scouting spreadsheet, or manage alliance negotiations are doing work that deserves credit.
- **Engineering notebook** is formally judged. Same note as FTC applies.
- **C++ / Python** at this level requires real computer science knowledge. Technical Skill 4 at V5/VRC level is substantially more demanding than at VEX IQ or FLL level.

### VEX U

- Teams are self-directed university students. Structured assessment from a coach is often absent. Use peer-review and self-assessment templates most — they fit the autonomy level.
- **Research documentation** and **design rationale** carry more weight than at younger-student levels. Engineering process quality is the primary distinguishing factor between good and great VEX U teams.
- **CAD and fabrication** add a manufacturing dimension not present in snap-together systems. Assess design intent vs. manufactured outcome.

## Grading in mixed-skill teams

If you need to assign individual grades:

1. **Weight individual process over team outcome.** The team winning States does not mean every team member deserves an A. The student who wrote 80% of the code deserves credit for that work; so does the student who organized every practice session.

2. **Use evidence, not impressions.** Exit tickets, role logs, observation checklists, and self-assessments give you a paper trail. Without evidence, you're guessing.

3. **Separate growth from baseline.** A student who came in knowing nothing and is now at 3/4 on technical skill has grown more than a student who arrived at 3 and stayed there. Document growth, not just final state.

4. **Discuss grades with students before finalizing.** A five-minute conversation — "I gave you a 3 on collaboration; does that feel right to you?" — surfaces blind spots and builds trust.

**Simple grade conversion (if required):**

| Average score (across weighted dimensions) | Grade |
|---|---|
| 3.5 – 4.0 | A |
| 2.5 – 3.4 | B |
| 1.5 – 2.4 | C |
| Below 1.5 | Needs improvement |

Weight dimensions to match your program's goals. For a general STEM elective, weight collaboration and growth equally with technical skill. For a dedicated engineering class, technical skill and engineering process can carry more weight.
