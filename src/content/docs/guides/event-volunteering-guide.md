Robotics events only happen because volunteers make them happen. Every qualifier, regional, and championship runs on an army of adult helpers: referees on the field, judges in the interview rooms, scorers at laptops, inspectors at the pit entrance, field resetters between matches. These roles are almost always open to any adult willing to show up, learn the rules, and help.

This guide explains how to find volunteering opportunities, what the main roles involve, and how to make the experience work well for you and the teams you serve.

---

## Why volunteer?

**You learn the game.** Nothing helps a coach understand judging criteria and referee calls better than seeing them from the other side of the table. Volunteer once as a judge or referee and your coaching instincts sharpen immediately.

**You give back.** Your team benefits from every volunteer at every event they attend. Contributing your time closes that loop for someone else's team.

**You meet the community.** Event volunteering puts you in a room with coaches, educators, and FIRST/VEX organisers you would not otherwise meet. These connections matter for mentors, sponsors, and future collaborators.

**You can do it even when your team is competing.** Some roles (queuing, field reset) require you to be at the field, which is awkward on your team's match days. Other roles (judging, inspection, scoring) keep you away from the competition floor — actually easier when you have a competing team, because you are not tempted to hover.

## Finding volunteer opportunities

### FIRST programs (FLL, FTC, FRC)

FIRST manages volunteer sign-up through its volunteer portal at **firstinspires.org**. Create a free account, then search for events in your area. Each event listing has a "Volunteer" tab with open roles.

- Background checks are required for all FIRST volunteers. FIRST provides these at no cost to you; allow 1–2 weeks for processing.
- Roles are listed with training requirements. Many can be done with event-day training only; a few (head referee, lead judge) require prior certification.
- Contact the Event Coordinator if you want to volunteer but don't see a specific role that fits. Events frequently need general help that doesn't appear in the formal role list.

### VEX programs (VEX IQ, VEX V5/VRC, VEX U)

VEX events are managed through **RobotEvents.com**. Each event page has a volunteer registration link. Event Partners (the local organisations running the event) manage their own volunteer lists, so the process varies by event.

- Check the RECF website (recf.org) for volunteer guidelines.
- VEX events generally do not require background checks at the local level (though signature events may have additional requirements).
- Contact the Event Partner directly if you want a specific role or have questions.

## Major roles in detail

### Field Referee

Referees enforce the game rules during matches. At most events there is one **head referee** (who makes final calls) and several **assistant referees** (who observe different parts of the field).

**What you do:** Stand at or near the competition field, watch the match, call rule violations, signal scores, discuss edge cases with the head ref. After the match ends, refs score the field together before it is reset.

**What makes a good referee:**
- Know the game manual. Rules are complex and change each season. Volunteer referees are expected to read the manual before the event.
- Stay calm under pressure. Teams and coaches will sometimes push back on calls. The head ref has final authority; support that and don't relitigate calls publicly.
- Be consistent across matches. The same situation in match 1 and match 5 should get the same call.

**Time commitment:** Full day, from field setup through alliance selection. Expect to stand the entire day.

**FLL note:** FLL Challenge referees score robot runs against the game mission model, not traditional match fouls. This requires familiarity with that season's specific mission scoring.

---

### Field Resetter

Field resetters restore the playing field to its starting configuration between matches. This role requires physical stamina and attention to detail.

**What you do:** Enter the field after the referees clear it, place game elements precisely in their starting positions, signal ready, exit before the next match begins.

**What makes a good resetter:**
- Speed and precision. Matches run on a tight schedule. Slow resets back up the queue and frustrate everyone.
- Memorise the field setup diagram. Knowing it by heart lets you reset faster and catch errors before the ref approves.
- Work quietly. Teams are on deck nearby; you don't need to narrate.

**Time commitment:** Full competition day, every match. This is a physically demanding role.

---

### Judge

Judges evaluate teams on the non-robot aspects of the competition: Innovation Project (FLL), Engineering Portfolio (FTC), Impact Award (FRC), Engineering Notebook (VEX). They also assess Core Values (FLL) and Gracious Professionalism across all programs.

**What you do:** Review submitted materials before the event (for FRC Impact; sometimes FTC Portfolio), conduct team interviews in a judging room, score teams on defined rubrics, deliberate with your judging panel to select award recipients.

**What makes a good judge:**
- Read the judging rubric for that season. Judging criteria are published; teams are scored against them, not against each other.
- Ask probing questions, not leading ones. "Tell me more about that" reveals more than "So you used a PID controller, right?"
- Protect confidentiality. What teams share in judging rooms stays with the judging panel.
- Manage your panel well. Good deliberations produce accurate results; rushed or dominated deliberations produce noise.

**Time commitment:** Typically half a day (interviews and deliberation). Judges often do not need to stay for the full competition day.

**FLL note:** FLL uses three separate judging sessions (Innovation Project, Robot Design, Core Values). FLL judge training takes about 30–60 minutes at the event.

---

### Robot Inspector

Inspectors verify that robots comply with game-legal size, weight, and component rules before teams compete. This role suits technically minded volunteers.

**What you do:** Use provided tools (sizing box, scale, component checklist) to inspect each team's robot. Pass compliant robots; flag non-compliant ones and explain what needs to change.

**What makes a good inspector:**
- Be thorough and consistent. The rules exist to ensure a level playing field.
- Be kind. Many teams are nervous at inspection. A calm, educational explanation of a violation is more useful than a brusque pass/fail.
- Know the legal components list. FTC, FRC, and VEX each maintain lists of approved parts; know how to look up edge cases quickly.

**Time commitment:** Mostly the morning before matches begin, though re-inspections happen throughout the day.

---

### Scorekeeper / AV

Scorekeepers enter match results into the event management software; AV operators run the scoring display and announcement system.

**What you do:** Receive score sheets from referees, enter results accurately and quickly, publish scores to the display.

**What makes a good scorekeeper:**
- Speed and accuracy. Entry errors cascade into ranking problems.
- Stay focused. The scoring table is in the middle of a loud event; distractions are constant.

**Time commitment:** Full competition day.

## Program-specific notes

### FLL (FIRST LEGO League)

FLL events are often the most family-friendly. Typical event size is 10–24 teams; some regional invitationals are larger. Volunteer roles at FLL include referee, judge (three separate panels), scorekeeper, field resetter, and general helpers (check-in, lunch, photos).

**Key tip:** FLL judging is built around how kids communicate, not just what they built. Good FLL judges use short, encouraging questions and leave the kids feeling heard.

### FTC (FIRST Tech Challenge)

FTC events run on a tighter schedule than FLL and involve more technical complexity: programming, mecanum drivetrains, and detailed engineering portfolios. Referees deal with penalty calls from a dense game manual. Judges evaluate portfolios and conduct a single sit-down interview.

**Key tip:** FTC teams are mostly 13–18 year olds. Expect technically confident students who know their robots well. Judges should probe the process, not just the capability.

### FRC (FIRST Robotics Competition)

FRC events are large (30–60+ teams), loud, and fast. Volunteer roles are more specialised. Head Referees and Lead Robot Inspectors require prior certification. FRC also has a dedicated **Pit Admin** function for managing team queuing and pit rules.

**Key tip:** Read the FRC Volunteer Manual before the event. FRC has detailed procedures for a reason — knowing them keeps the event safe and fair.

### VEX IQ (VIQRC)

VIQRC events are similar in feel to FLL — younger students (8–14), collaborative format, friendly atmosphere. Judging focuses on the engineering notebook and pit interview. Referees score Teamwork Challenge matches and Autonomous Coding Skills runs.

**Key tip:** VIQRC scoring has specific rules about collaborative vs. independent elements. Referees need to understand the difference between Teamwork and Skills runs before taking the field.

### VEX V5/VRC

V5RC events are more competitive and technically intense than VIQRC. Alliance selection and Robot Skills Challenges both run throughout the day. Pit interviews are roving — judges walk through the pits rather than scheduling formal sessions.

**Key tip:** VRC Engineering Notebooks are graded against a published rubric. Judges should read the rubric and score based on evidence in the notebook, not on impression.

### VEX U (VURC)

VURC events are collegiate and expect a more technical level of conversation from judges and inspectors. Two-robot team dynamics make inspection slightly more complex. Signature Events have larger volunteer requirements.

## What NOT to do

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| Coach your team from the volunteer area | Conflict of interest; undermines the team's independence |
| Discuss your team's performance with other judges | Taints the judging panel's objectivity |
| Argue referee calls publicly | Erodes team trust in the officiating process |
| Skip the volunteer briefing | You'll miss event-specific rule variations and logistics |
| Over-explain to teams during inspection | Brief and clear beats long and thorough; you have other teams waiting |
| Leave your station without telling your coordinator | Gaps in coverage affect the whole event |

## Volunteering when your team is competing

Volunteering at the same event where your team is competing is allowed but requires care.

- As a **judge**, you will be recused from evaluating your own team. Disclose your affiliation to the head judge before judging begins.
- As a **referee**, you may referee matches involving your team. Most programs allow this; head referees monitor for consistency.
- As an **inspector**, you should not inspect your own team's robot. Disclose and another inspector covers that inspection.

When in doubt, ask the head volunteer coordinator how the event handles this.

## Getting started

1. **Create your account**: firstinspires.org (FIRST) or RobotEvents.com (VEX)
2. **Complete the background check**: required for FIRST; submit early (allow 1–2 weeks)
3. **Browse event listings**: filter by date and distance; local qualifiers are the best place to start
4. **Sign up for a role you're curious about**: field reset is a great first role (fast-paced, clear task); judging is a great second role (reveals what teams experience in the room)
5. **Arrive early**: volunteer check-in is typically 30–60 minutes before the event opens to teams
6. **Attend the volunteer briefing**: usually 15–30 minutes; covers that season's specific rules and event logistics

You don't need prior experience. You just need to show up.
