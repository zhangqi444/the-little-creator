This guide is the FRC counterpart to [Your First FLL Tournament](/guides/first-tournament-expectations/). The emotional advice in the FLL guide transfers — first tournaments feel overwhelming, telling your team that ahead of time helps — but FRC tournaments are a different animal in scale and rhythm. A typical FRC event runs **three or four days**, fields **40–60 teams**, involves multi-day pit life with full meals, and operates more like a professional engineering event than a school club outing.

## Tournament formats — regional vs. district

FRC has two parallel competition structures:

- **Regional events** run in most of the US and internationally. A single 3-day event (Thursday practice + Friday/Saturday competition). Winning advances directly to FIRST Championship.
- **District events** (used in some US regions: Michigan, FIRST in Texas, Mid-Atlantic, New England, FIRST in Indiana, Israel, etc.) — teams play multiple smaller district events through the spring, accumulating points; top point-earners advance to a district championship, which then advances to FIRST Championship.

Both formats use the same match structure and most of the same awards. The big difference is pacing — district teams play 2–3 events per season; regional teams typically play 1 regional plus possibly a 2nd off-season.

This guide describes a **regional** event; district events are similar but compressed and lower-stakes per event (since you'll play more).

## The 3-day regional schedule

| Day | What happens |
|---|---|
| **Wednesday evening** (optional) | Some events allow load-in and partial pit setup |
| **Thursday** | Full load-in, robot inspection, practice matches all day, opening ceremony in afternoon |
| **Friday** | Qualification matches (most teams play 8–12 over Friday + Saturday morning) |
| **Friday evening** | Judging interviews continue; pit is open late |
| **Saturday morning** | Final qualification matches; alliance selection at lunch |
| **Saturday afternoon** | Elimination matches (quarterfinals through finals) |
| **Saturday evening** | Awards ceremony |

A team typically arrives Wednesday or Thursday morning and doesn't leave the venue much until Saturday night. Meals are often catered or delivered to the pit.

## The four things that will surprise a new FRC team

1. **The pit is your home for three days.** You'll work, eat, rest, debug, and host visiting scouts in your pit. Decorate it (banners, team colours, a whiteboard); make it functional (work surface, electrical, parts bins, snacks). Inspect other teams' pits for ideas.
2. **Scouting is its own role — and it's important.** A team typically dedicates 4–8 students to scouting: watching every match, recording what each other team scores, charts, what mechanisms work, defensive tendencies. That data drives alliance selection picks. If your team isn't scouting, you'll show up to alliance selection blind.
3. **Alliance selection is high-stakes and televised.** The top 8 ranked teams become alliance captains and pick partners over multiple rounds. The ceremony is broadcast on field-side video. Being picked feels like getting drafted; not being picked is a real disappointment. Coach your team through both outcomes ahead of time.
4. **The robot will break.** Wheel falls off mid-match, a NEO burns out, a CAN bus disconnect kills autonomous. You'll have 6 minutes between matches to fix it. Have a triage plan — who diagnoses, who fixes, who tests — practiced before the event.

## Inspection in FRC

FRC robot inspection is more rigorous than FLL or FTC. Inspectors check:

- Frame perimeter (within season's allowed dimensions, often 28"×30" or 30"×30")
- Weight (typically 125 lb limit, varies by season)
- Bumpers (correct colour, height, attached at full perimeter, fabric-rated)
- Electrical — power distribution, breakers, wiring gauge, main breaker placement, secured battery
- Pneumatics if used — proper relief valve, pressure rating
- Robot ID lights, team number visible
- Software safety — emergency stop works, robot disables when commanded

You'll typically have **all of Thursday** to pass inspection. Don't leave it until late Thursday — if you find a problem, you want time to fix it. Bring a printout of the season's inspection checklist; many veteran teams do a self-inspection before the official one.

## Match cadence and "pit rotation"

A typical FRC team plays a match every 90 minutes during qualifying. Between matches:

- 0–10 min: robot back to pit, drivers debrief
- 10–30 min: any repairs, reset wheels, charge batteries
- 30–60 min: scout next match opponents, plan strategy
- 60–80 min: queue up for next match
- 80–90 min: at the field, match starts

Establish "the queue is the queue" — when the queueing volunteer calls your team, you go *now*. Late teams forfeit matches. Have a person whose job is to listen for the queue call.

## Awards: what gets handed out

FRC awards include the **Impact Award** (formerly Chairman's Award — the top award, recognising teams with the strongest community impact), **Engineering Inspiration**, **Rookie All Star** (for rookie teams), **Industrial Design**, **Innovation in Control**, and the **Winning Alliance**. See the [FRC Awards guide](/guides/frc-awards/) for the full structure and how teams qualify.

## How to help your team navigate the day(s)

**Logistics:**

- Pack for three days — pit clothes, presentation clothes, comfortable shoes (you'll walk miles per day), enough snacks to share with neighbouring teams.
- Plan meals — many regionals have on-site food; some don't. Know before you arrive.
- Hotels fill up early — book months ahead.

**During matches:**

- Drivers shouldn't talk strategy; coaches should. Brief the drivers on what to do in 30 seconds before the match, then let them drive.
- After each match, do a 90-second debrief: what worked, what broke, what changed.
- Have one student writing notes on every match in a shared doc. Future-you will want them.

**During judging:**

- Multiple judges visit the pit throughout the day. Some are with the Engineering team, some with Impact / outreach, some Dean's List. Train multiple students to give a 2-minute pit interview to a stranger walking up.
- Bring the team's award submission materials to the pit — Impact essay, business plan, Engineering Inspiration documentation. Judges may ask.

**At alliance selection:**

- Scouting team should produce a *prioritised pick list* before alliance selection — top 8 teams ranked by who you want to play with. Lock it in before the ceremony so you're not deciding under pressure.
- If you're the captain (top 8 rank), you pick. If you're getting picked, you accept or decline (declining is rare and risky — you usually accept the first offer).
- If you're not picked at all: this happens. Have a script. "We competed hard, we learned, we'll be back" said out loud helps the team move past it.

**Awards ceremony:**

- Sit together. Cheer for other teams winning. Gracious Professionalism on display.
- Whatever happens, debrief Sunday morning. What surprised us? What do we change before the next event?

## What rookies should expect

A first-year FRC team realistically aims for:

- **Getting the robot through inspection** before Friday morning
- **Playing all scheduled qualification matches** with the robot operational for most
- **Being a candidate for Rookie All Star** (the rookie-specific award)
- **Being picked at alliance selection** — happens often if you're operational, since 8 captains pick 16 teams total
- **Learning enough to do significantly better at the next event**

A second-year team can realistically target a top-half finish, multiple judged award nominations, and being on the radar for Impact in year three.

## Where this differs from FLL Challenge

| Aspect | FLL Challenge | FRC |
|---|---|---|
| Tournament length | Half-day | 3 days (4 with practice day) |
| Team size on-floor | 2–10 | 3 drivers + coach on field; 15+ supporting in pit |
| Match format | Solo team runs | 3-team alliances vs. 3-team alliances |
| Robot size | 11"×11"×11" | ~30"×30" base, ~125 lb |
| Scouting | Not really a thing | Dedicated team role with structured data |
| Alliance selection | None | High-stakes draft at lunch on Saturday |
| Cost per event | $0–$50 | $5,000+ per regional (registration, travel, hotel) |
