The engineering notebook is one of the least-understood parts of FLL — and one of the most valuable. Judges use it to verify that your team actually did the work you're describing. A good notebook tells the story of your season: the dead ends, the decisions, the improvements. A bad notebook is a collection of clean summaries written the week before the tournament.

This guide covers what to put in, when to write it, and what makes judges take notice.

:::note[Check your season rules]
The specific requirements for notebooks vary by season and region. Some FLL programs require a physical notebook; others accept digital formats. Check the current season's [Challenge guide](https://www.firstlegoleague.org/season) and your local tournament rules for requirements. This guide covers general best practices, not season-specific rules.
:::

---

## Why the notebook matters

Judges have 20–30 minutes with your team. They can't verify months of work in that time — but they can read your notebook before or during the session and use it to ask better questions.

A notebook that shows *process* (attempts, failures, iterations) is more credible than one that only shows *results*. Any team can write "we tried three designs and chose the best one." A notebook that shows three sketched designs with notes on why each was rejected — and test data for the chosen one — tells judges this actually happened.

## What belongs in a notebook

### 1. Meeting logs

For every practice session, a brief entry:

- **Date and attendees**
- **What we planned to do**
- **What we actually did**
- **What we'll do next time**

This doesn't have to be long. Four bullet points per session is fine. The goal is a running record that judges can flip through to see activity throughout the season — not a detailed essay.

**Example entry:**
> *Oct 14 — Present: Alex, Jamie, Morgan, Sam*
> *Plan: Finish mission 3 program and test mission 4 attachment.*
> *Did: Mission 3 runs consistently now (8/10 in testing). Mission 4 attachment fell off twice — think the connector pin is wrong size. Will check parts inventory next session.*
> *Next: Fix mission 4 attachment connector; start mission 5.*

### 2. Robot design decisions

When you make a design choice — drivetrain configuration, gear ratio, attachment mechanism — write down:

- What you decided
- What alternatives you considered
- Why you chose this option
- What you'll do if it doesn't work

Judges often ask "why did you design it this way?" A kid who can say "we tried a 3:1 gear ratio first but the torque wasn't enough for the lift, so we switched to 5:1 and it worked" is far more credible than one who says "it just seemed like the right choice."

### 3. Test data

When you test the robot, record the results — not just "it worked" but how it worked:

| Run | Result | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Miss — 8cm left | Starting position was off |
| 2 | Miss — 3cm left | Adjusted start; still drifting |
| 3 | Success | Added gyro correction at turn |
| 4 | Success | |
| 5 | Success | |

This kind of data shows iterative improvement. It also helps you diagnose problems weeks later ("it used to work, what changed?").

### 4. Innovation Project research trail

For each research session, record:

- What question you were trying to answer
- Where you looked (sources, not just "the internet")
- What you found
- What questions that raised

When you contact an expert or do an interview, note:
- Who you contacted and why
- What you asked
- What they said (summarise — don't need to transcribe)
- How it changed your thinking

Judges specifically ask about this trail. "We emailed a researcher at [university]" is much stronger when your notebook shows the email you sent and the key points of their reply.

### 5. Innovation Project design iterations

Your solution will change as you learn more. Capture each iteration:

- Sketch or description of the design
- What problem it was trying to solve
- What you tested or simulated
- What didn't work and why
- What you changed for the next iteration

A solution that went through three visible iterations is more impressive than one that appeared fully-formed — because the former shows genuine problem-solving.

### 6. Team roles and contributions

A brief note on who did what each session. Not to assign credit or blame — but so judges can ask each team member about their specific contributions and get honest, accurate answers.

## When to write in the notebook

**During or immediately after each session** — not the week before the tournament. Memory fades fast. The detail that makes entries useful ("the connector was a pin-2 not pin-3") disappears within days.

**Assign a note-taker** for each session. Rotate the role so everyone has practice. The note-taker's job is to capture the session, not participate less — keep entries brief enough that this works.

**Don't wait for things to be "done"** before writing. Incomplete work, failed attempts, and unresolved questions are exactly what belongs in a notebook. Judges are looking for process, not polish.

## Format: physical vs. digital

**Physical notebooks** (paper, bound journal):
- Pros: harder to retroactively edit (judges trust them more); easy to include quick sketches; no tech required
- Cons: harder to search; photos need to be printed or taped in; shared access is harder

**Digital notebooks** (Google Docs, Notion, dedicated apps):
- Pros: easy to include photos; searchable; multiple team members can contribute
- Cons: judges may be less familiar with the format; version history matters (shows entries were made in real time, not all at once)

Either works. What matters is:
1. Entries are dated
2. Entries were clearly made *at the time*, not retrospectively cleaned up
3. It's organized enough for a judge to navigate in 5 minutes

If using digital, keep a clear chronological log. Avoid reorganizing content by topic after the fact — it obscures the timeline that judges are looking for.

## Common notebook mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Only recording successes | Judges see a suspiciously clean process; no evidence of real problem-solving | Record failures explicitly — they're the evidence that you learned |
| Writing it all at the end of the season | Entries lack detail; judges can often tell | Brief entries in real time throughout the season |
| Vague entries ("we worked on the robot") | Judges can't ask follow-up questions from it | Specific: what mission, what change, what result |
| No design alternatives recorded | Team can't explain why they chose their design | Write down rejected options and why |
| Only one person writes in it | Other team members can't speak to the work | Rotate note-taker; all members should recognise their own entries |
| Over-polishing entries | Looks like retrospective cleanup | Acceptable messiness is fine; consistent dating and specificity matter more |

## What judges actually look for

When judges review a notebook, they're typically asking:

- **Continuity:** Does this cover the whole season, or just the last few weeks?
- **Process:** Can I see the iterations, the dead ends, the decisions?
- **Ownership:** Can each team member speak to what's in here, or did one person write everything?
- **Authenticity:** Does this feel like it was written as it happened, or summarised afterwards?
- **IP connection:** Does the notebook show how their Innovation Project research informed their solution?

You don't need beautiful handwriting, perfect grammar, or colour-coded tabs. You need *evidence of real, iterative work done throughout the season*.

## Quick-start for new teams

If your team hasn't been keeping a notebook and the tournament is coming up, don't panic — and don't try to fake it. A partially complete, honest notebook is better than a retroactively constructed one.

Do this:
1. Start today. Date every entry from now on.
2. For past work, write one summary entry per major milestone ("Before Oct 14 — built initial drivetrain, decided on tank drive, struggled with gyro calibration. Key decisions made: [list]"). Date it honestly as a summary.
3. Be transparent if judges ask — "we didn't start the notebook until week 6, but here's what we captured going forward." Judges appreciate honesty.
