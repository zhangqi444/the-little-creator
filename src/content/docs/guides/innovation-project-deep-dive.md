## The core mistake: jumping to solutions

The most common reason teams receive mediocre Innovation Project scores has nothing to do with their solution. It's that they chose a problem that was actually a solution.

**Examples:**
- ❌ "The problem is that there aren't enough recycling bins in our school." *(That's a solution: add bins.)*
- ✅ "The problem is that 62% of recyclable materials in our school end up in landfill." *(Now you can research root causes and propose a targeted solution.)*

---

- ❌ "The problem is there's no app that tracks how much water you use." *(That's a product idea.)*
- ✅ "The problem is that households underestimate their daily water use by an average of 40%, leading them to ignore conservation behaviours." *(Now you can research why this perception gap exists.)*

---

**How to test whether your team has a problem or a solution:**

Ask: *"Is this a description of what's wrong in the world, or a description of something that should be built/done?"*

If you can immediately name the fix (an app, a bin, a poster, a policy), you probably have a solution, not a problem. Go one level deeper: *why* is that fix needed? What's the underlying gap?

## The problem statement

A strong problem statement has four components:

| Component | What it captures | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Who** | The people experiencing the problem | "Elderly residents in care facilities…" |
| **What** | The specific gap or harm | "…have difficulty signalling that they need help…" |
| **Why it matters** | Scale or consequence | "…leading to delayed care responses in 1 in 5 incidents…" |
| **Why it's unsolved** | What makes it hard | "…because current call buttons require physical reach and manual operation." |

Writing all four components before starting research helps teams avoid accidentally pivoting mid-season.

**Practice exercise:** Give each team member 5 minutes to write their version of the problem statement. Compare them. If they're describing different things, your team hasn't agreed on the problem yet — and that's worth knowing in week 2 rather than week 7.

## Running a real research phase

### What "research" means in FLL

FLL judges distinguish between:

1. **Background research** — learning the landscape (internet searches, articles, books)
2. **Primary research** — gathering new information directly (expert interviews, surveys, observations)

Both matter. Background research tells you what's known. Primary research tells you what's specific to *your* context and earns you the "expert consultation" credit that FLL judges weight heavily.

A strong Innovation Project usually has both: "We learned from published research that X, and then when we interviewed Dr. Y, she told us that in *our region specifically*, the problem looks like Z — which is different."

---

### Finding and reaching experts

"Expert" in FLL means anyone with direct knowledge your team doesn't have. This includes:

| Expert type | Example | How to find them |
|---|---|---|
| Domain professional | Veterinarian, civil engineer, nutritionist | Local clinics, city departments, LinkedIn |
| Affected community member | Patient, farmer, student | Community centres, local advocacy groups |
| Academic researcher | University faculty in relevant field | University department websites |
| Practitioner at a company/non-profit | Staff at local environmental org | Organisation websites |
| Government official | City planner, public health officer | City/county government directory |

**Tips for getting a response:**

- **Local is better than famous.** A professor at a regional university is far more likely to reply to a kids' FLL team than a professor at a top-10 institution.
- **Email is fine; phone calls work too.** Keep the ask short: who you are, what you're researching, *one specific question* you'd like to discuss. Don't ask for their whole expertise — ask for 15–20 minutes.
- **Be specific about your question.** "We're researching food waste in school cafeterias and we want to understand why kids over-serve themselves even when portion guidance is visible. You study school nutrition — could we ask you about that specific behaviour?"

**What to do in the interview:**

Prepare 4–6 questions in advance. During the conversation, the most important follow-up question is usually: *"Is there anything about this problem that we're probably missing as outsiders?"* Experts will tell you things you didn't know to ask about.

Document: name, title, organisation, date, 3–5 key things you learned, and any sources they pointed you toward.

---

### Running a team survey

If your problem involves a local population (students in your school, families in your community), a simple survey is legitimate primary research. Rules:

- **5–10 questions maximum.** More is not better — response rate drops fast.
- **Mix quantitative and qualitative.** "On a scale of 1–5, how often do you X?" + "In your own words, what makes X difficult?"
- **Random or representative sample matters.** 30 responses from your team's friends is weaker than 30 responses from randomly selected students across grade levels.
- **Report your methodology.** Judges will ask "how many people did you survey?" and "how did you choose them?" Know the answers.

---

### Organising what you find

Create a simple **research log** with columns for:

- Date
- Source type (expert interview / article / survey / observation)
- Source name (person or URL)
- Key finding (1–2 sentences)
- How it changes our understanding

This log has two uses: it feeds your presentation narrative ("our research led us through three phases of understanding"), and it becomes evidence if a judge asks "where did you get that?"

## Diagnosing real vs. surface causes

The **5 Whys** technique is the most useful tool for Innovation Project problem analysis:

1. State the observable problem
2. Ask "Why does this happen?" → write the answer
3. Ask "Why does *that* happen?" → write the answer
4. Repeat until you reach a cause that feels structural rather than symptomatic
5. Your solution should target *that* level

**Worked example — school food waste:**

| Level | Statement |
|---|---|
| Observable | Kids throw away a lot of food at lunch |
| Why? | They take more than they eat |
| Why? | Portion sizes are pre-set and kids don't adjust down |
| Why? | The serving station doesn't offer smaller portions |
| Why? | The default is adult-size portions; child-size isn't offered |
| Root cause | No child-size portion option exists at the default serve line |

A solution at level 1 ("put up posters") doesn't address the root. A solution at level 5 ("introduce a visible small/medium/large self-serve option at the start of the line") does.

## Evaluating your solution

Before the team commits to their solution, run it through a quick evaluation grid:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Does it address the root cause (not a symptom)? | Surface solutions score poorly regardless of creativity |
| Is it genuinely new, or does it already exist at scale? | Check: does a quick search find this product/service already deployed? |
| Is it physically plausible (even if not built)? | "Magic" solutions — ones with no explanation of how they work — don't convince judges |
| Can your team explain it in detail? | If team members can't describe the mechanism, materials, or implementation path, the solution isn't developed enough |
| Who would implement it and why would they? | Judges often ask "who would actually do this?" Have a real answer |
| What would need to be true for it to succeed? | Identifying dependencies and constraints shows mature thinking |

## The research plan document

Some regions and competition formats ask teams to submit a **research plan** in advance of the judging session. Even when it's not required, writing one is good practice — it forces the team to clarify their thinking and gives coaches a diagnostic tool for identifying gaps.

**Suggested one-page structure:**

```
INNOVATION PROJECT RESEARCH PLAN
Team name / Team number / Season

PROBLEM STATEMENT
[One paragraph: who is affected, what the specific gap is, why it matters, why it hasn't been solved]

RESEARCH QUESTIONS
1. [Specific factual question your research must answer]
2. [Specific factual question]
3. [Specific factual question]
(3-5 questions. These should not be answerable by a 5-second Google search.)

RESEARCH METHODS
- Expert interviews: [names/titles of people you plan to contact]
- Background sources: [types of sources — government reports, academic papers, industry data]
- Primary data collection: [survey / observation / site visit, if applicable]

WHAT WE THINK WE'LL FIND (hypotheses)
[One sentence per research question — your best current guess. Writing this down lets you notice when research contradicts your assumptions.]

TIMELINE
Week 1-2: [background research]
Week 2-4: [expert outreach and interviews]
Week 4-5: [primary data if applicable]
Week 5-6: [solution design]
Week 6-8: [prototype and presentation prep]
```

A team that starts week 1 with this document is in a meaningfully different position than a team that starts week 5 asking "so, what's our solution?"

## Structuring the presentation for judges

The [Innovation Project Guide](/guides/innovation-project-guide/) covers the 5+5 minute structure. Here's what actually differentiates a strong presentation from an average one:

### The narrative arc

Judges see dozens of presentations in one day. The teams that stand out tell a story with a clear arc:

> "We started thinking the problem was X. Then we talked to [expert], and we realised the real problem was Y. That changed our solution completely — we had originally planned Z, but when we understood Y, we built W instead."

This arc demonstrates genuine research. It shows that the team's thinking *changed* because of what they learned. That's exactly what FLL wants to see.

### What judges probe in Q&A

Prepare your team for these real questions:

| Judge question | What they're really asking |
|---|---|
| "Why is this problem important?" | Can you articulate stakes beyond "it's bad"? |
| "Who did you talk to?" | Did you actually do primary research? |
| "What did that person tell you that surprised you?" | Was the interview substantive or just a quick call? |
| "How is your solution different from [obvious existing thing]?" | Do you know your competitive landscape? |
| "Who would pay for this / who would implement it?" | Is the solution grounded in reality? |
| "What would you change if you could start over?" | Can you reflect honestly on your process? |

Practise these specifically. A team that can answer "what surprised you in your research?" has clearly done real work.

### Every team member has substance, not just a line

A common coaching pattern is to give quieter team members a sentence or two at the beginning or end ("I'm Maya, I worked on the poster"). Judges notice this.

Instead, give every member a *question they own* — a part of the research or solution they personally understand well enough to answer follow-up questions about. Judges sometimes direct Q&A questions to specific students. Make sure no one is caught without real knowledge.

## After the presentation: the debrief

Win or lose at the tournament, the Innovation Project is most valuable as a learning experience. A 20-minute debrief the week after the event is worth doing:

1. What did we discover during our research that we didn't know at the start?
2. What question do we still wish we'd answered?
3. What part of our presentation felt strongest? What felt weakest?
4. If we were implementing our solution for real, what's the first step?

For many teams, the Innovation Project expert contact becomes a real relationship — someone willing to mentor the team in future seasons or serve as a reference contact. Follow up with a thank-you email and a brief summary of how the project went.
