FRC's award structure rewards teams for two largely independent things: **community impact** (Impact Award) and **engineering achievement** (everything else). The top award — Impact — explicitly does not require on-field success. Conversely, a team can dominate the field and never be considered for Impact. This bifurcation shapes which awards your team should target.

This guide covers the major award categories, what each rewards, and what a first- or second-year team can realistically aim for.

:::note[Award lists change]
Specific awards available at any event are listed in the current season's [Awards Manual](https://www.firstinspires.org/resources/library/frc/season-materials) under FRC Season Materials. This page describes the longstanding award structure; always verify against the current Awards Manual for season-specific changes.
:::

## The headline award: Impact

**The Impact Award** (renamed from Chairman's Award in 2023) is FRC's most prestigious recognition. It honours teams that demonstrate the broadest, deepest, and longest-lasting impact on their community through STEM outreach and the spread of the FIRST mission.

Winning Impact requires:

- A multi-year sustained outreach program (one-off events don't qualify)
- Evidence of *measurable* impact — how many students reached, how many new teams started, how the community changed
- A submitted **Impact essay** (written), a **video presentation**, and a **judged interview** with multiple senior students
- Strong Gracious Professionalism in everything the team does at the event

Impact-winning teams typically have:

- Started or mentored multiple other FRC, FTC, or FLL teams
- Hosted significant community events (camps, workshops, demo days)
- Built ongoing relationships with schools, libraries, museums, professional organisations
- A team identity that goes beyond robotics — a brand, a story, a presence

Impact is awarded at every regional / district event; the winners advance to championship-level Impact judging.

**Impact is not realistic for a team in its first 3 years.** It rewards a portfolio of work built over a long period.

## Engineering Inspiration

**Engineering Inspiration** is often described as "Impact's engineering sibling." It recognises teams that specifically inspire engineering and STEM in their schools and communities — through curriculum, partnerships with schools, mentorship of younger students.

The qualification path is similar to Impact (essay, video, judged interview), but the *focus* is narrower — specifically the impact your team has had on engineering education and inspiration. A team that runs an after-school robotics elective for middle schoolers, partners with a local engineering firm for student internships, or runs an annual summer engineering camp is the target audience.

Engineering Inspiration is sometimes a realistic stretch for a team in years 2–3 if their outreach has a strong engineering-education flavour.

## Rookie All Star

**Rookie All Star** is essentially "Impact for rookie teams." Available only to teams in their first season, it recognises rookies who are already exhibiting the qualities that lead to Impact — strong team structure, early outreach, engineering process, sustainable foundation.

Realistic target for a strong, well-organised rookie team. Often the right *aspirational* award for year one.

## Judged engineering awards

These reward specific facets of the team's engineering work.

### Industrial Design Award (sponsored)

Recognises form, function, and aesthetics of the robot — how well the design intent is realised. Often goes to teams whose robots look intentional, well-machined, and refined. Designed by professionals; judged in the pit.

### Excellence in Engineering Award (sponsored)

Recognises an elegant, well-documented engineering process. Judged in the pit + via documentation. Closest FRC analog to FTC's Think Award.

### Innovation in Control Award (sponsored)

Recognises advanced software achievement — autonomous routines, sensor fusion, computer vision, advanced control. Submitted via a separate Innovation in Control submission form (similar to FTC's Control Award).

### Creativity Award (sponsored)

Recognises creative thinking in robot design, strategy, or team organisation. A creative mechanism, an unusual game approach, or a clever solution to a constraint. Less common; specific events may not award it.

### Quality Award (sponsored)

Recognises robustness, reliability, and quality of construction — robots that work the same every time. A team that has a perfectly functional, never-broken robot all weekend often wins this.

### Engineering Inspiration (covered above)

### Safety Award (sponsored)

Recognises strong safety culture — proper pit safety, PPE usage, hazard mitigation. Safety captains are visible and active. The Safety Advisor inspects pits and watches teams throughout the event.

## Match-based awards

Determined by on-field performance, not judging.

### Winning Alliance

The three-team alliance that wins the elimination tournament. All three teams receive the award. Qualifies the winning teams (and often the finalist alliance) to advance to championship.

### Finalist Alliance

The three-team alliance that loses in the finals. Recognised; depending on the event and advancement structure, often also qualifies to advance.

## Individual awards

### FIRST Dean's List Finalist

Selected from student nominations submitted by mentors before the event. Two finalists per event. Recognises individual leadership and contribution. Dean's List Finalists advance to championship for the Dean's List Award.

### Volunteer of the Year, Woodie Flowers Finalist (mentor)

The **Woodie Flowers Finalist Award** recognises a *mentor* nominated by their students for outstanding communication and effect on the team. One per event. Often handed out emotionally during the awards ceremony.

## How awards combine to advance to championship

Regional advancement (simplified — exact rules vary by season):

1. **Impact Award** winner
2. **Winning Alliance** (often all three)
3. **Engineering Inspiration** winner
4. **Rookie All Star** winner
5. **Wild card** based on Impact and Engineering Inspiration finalists, plus point-based criteria

District system advancement is point-based across all your district events. Each event you play earns district points based on awards, qualification rank, alliance selection, and elimination performance. Top point-earners advance to the district championship.

## What rookies should target

Realistic year-one FRC goals:

- **Pass inspection by Thursday end-of-day** — surprisingly hard
- **Complete all scheduled qualification matches** with the robot scoring at least some points in most
- **Get picked at alliance selection** — likely if you're operational, since 16 teams are picked
- **Be on the radar for Rookie All Star** — invest in early outreach documentation
- **Win Safety Award** if your team has a real safety culture — highly winnable for rookies
- **Have a Woodie Flowers Finalist nomination written** for your lead mentor

A team should *not* set "we will win Impact" as a year-one goal. Impact requires years of sustained work.

## How to actually pursue judged awards

For every judged award you want to be in the running for, you need to do three things:

1. **Have the substance.** Outreach for Impact / Engineering Inspiration. Documented engineering process for Excellence. Innovative software for Innovation in Control. The substance is the work itself — you can't fake it.
2. **Document the substance.** The Impact essay, the Innovation in Control submission, the Excellence pit documentation. Judges judge from documentation supplemented by interviews, not from observation alone.
3. **Train students to talk about it.** Multiple students should be able to walk a judge through your work in 2 minutes. Don't rely on a single presenter.
