Robotics programs are for every kid. But the default team setup — fast-paced meetings, complex fine-motor tasks, loud tournament venues, dense game manuals — can create barriers for students with disabilities or learning differences.

With a few deliberate adaptations, most students can participate fully and meaningfully. This guide covers practical accommodations, inclusive team structures, and the mindset shifts that make the biggest difference.

---

## Who this guide is for

Coaches and educators who want to include students with:

- **Physical disabilities** — mobility, fine motor, limb differences, chronic fatigue, pain
- **Sensory differences** — visual impairment, hearing impairment, light/sound sensitivity
- **Cognitive and learning differences** — dyslexia, dyscalculia, processing differences, memory challenges
- **Neurodevelopmental differences** — autism spectrum, ADHD, anxiety, sensory processing differences
- **Communication differences** — speech and language differences, AAC (augmentative and alternative communication) users

Most of the strategies below apply across programs. Program-specific notes appear at the end.

---

## The core principle: design for the widest range

Accessibility is most sustainable when you design the team environment for the widest range of participants from the start — not when you scramble to accommodate one specific student after the fact.

This is sometimes called **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)**. It produces a better experience for everyone, not just for students with disabilities:

- Clear visual agendas help kids with ADHD *and* kids who learn better from structure.
- Written role summaries help students with processing differences *and* new members who missed a session.
- Noise management helps students with sensory differences *and* students who think better in calmer environments.

Think of accessibility as good coaching practice, not a special exception.

## Physical accessibility

### Adapting the build table

Standard robotics build sessions require a lot of fine motor work: pressing LEGO connectors, plugging in cables, tightening VEX screws. Students with motor differences can still contribute if you:

- **Raise or lower the work surface** so students who use a wheelchair or a standing position can access the work area comfortably.
- **Stabilise parts** using non-slip matting, clips, or a second pair of hands while the student positions components.
- **Pre-stage parts** — sort, label, and organise connectors so less searching is required.
- **Pair tasks strategically** — students with limited fine-motor ability can direct the build verbally while a teammate does the physical assembly. The directing student is doing the engineering; the assembler is the hands.

### Programming stations

Most robotics programming tools already work well for students with physical disabilities:

- SPIKE App, VEXcode, FTC Blocks, and WPILib all run in a standard browser or desktop environment.
- Students who can operate a computer with an alternative input device (mouth stick, eye tracker, switch access, sip-and-puff) can program just as effectively as any other student.
- If the default keyboard/mouse layout does not work, explore OS accessibility settings (sticky keys, mouse keys, on-screen keyboard) before assuming programming is inaccessible.

### Fatigue and pain management

Students with chronic illness, muscular dystrophy, or similar conditions may have limited energy budgets. Practical adaptations:

- **Shorter, more focused sessions** — two 45-minute meetings can be more accessible than a single 90-minute session.
- **Designated rest spots** — a chair near the build table that is not "stepping out"; staying in the room but stepping back is still participating.
- **Written role continuity** — when a student misses a session due to health, brief written notes let them catch up and remain part of the design process.

## Sensory accessibility

### Auditory and hearing differences

Students who are deaf or hard of hearing can participate fully in robotics:

- **Face the room** when giving instructions; avoid turning away to write on a board while talking.
- **Repeat what teammates say** during group discussion — do not assume everyone heard it.
- **Provide written versions** of verbal instructions and announcements (whiteboard summary, shared document, or printed agenda).
- **Caption video resources** — FLL game reveal videos and FIRST/VEX tutorial videos usually have captions; enable them.
- At tournaments, seat the student where they have a direct sightline to the field referee and the match-count display.

### Visual differences

Students with low vision or blindness can still program, design, and lead teams with accommodations:

- **Screen readers work** with most text-based programming environments (OnBot Java, PROS, Python, WPILib Java).
- **Increase display contrast and font size** on programming stations.
- **Tactile models** — printed 3D game field elements, raised-line field maps, or physical models made from LEGO or craft materials let students who are blind explore the season's challenge spatially.
- **Describe what you're showing** — "I'm showing the slide with the four scoring zones; the middle two zones score 3 points each" rather than just pointing at a screen.

### Sensory processing and noise sensitivity

Robotics meetings and tournaments can be overwhelming for students with sensory processing differences or anxiety:

- **Signal transitions** verbally before they happen — "In five minutes we're switching from building to programming."
- **Offer a break space** — a quieter corner, a set of ear defenders, or permission to step into a hallway for a few minutes. This is a safety valve, not an opt-out.
- **Reduce ambient noise during focus tasks** — background music, loud side conversations, and power tools all raise the cognitive load for students with sensory sensitivities.
- **Tournament preparation** — visit the venue or find photos/videos beforehand. Knowing what the noise level, lighting, and layout will look like substantially reduces anxiety on the day. See also [Managing First-Competition Nerves](/guides/first-competition-nerves/).

## Cognitive and learning differences

### ADHD

Students with ADHD often do very well in robotics because the program provides intrinsic motivation, hands-on work, and immediate feedback. Challenges tend to appear around organisational tasks, long sessions, and waiting for turns.

- **Break work into short chunks** with clear checkpoints — "Build the drive base chassis (20 min), then test forward/backward (10 min)" rather than "work on the robot for 60 minutes."
- **Rotate roles frequently** so no student is waiting for long stretches.
- **Use written checklists** for multi-step tasks (build sequences, pre-match robot checks, notebook entries).
- **Seat near the action** during programming sessions — not isolated at the back of the room.
- **Allow movement** — a student who paces or fidgets while thinking is still participating. Kinesthetic activity and thinking are not mutually exclusive.

### Dyslexia and reading differences

Robot game manuals are long and dense documents. Many students with dyslexia find them inaccessible when read cover-to-cover. Alternatives:

- **Read the manual aloud** together, in sections, over multiple sessions.
- **Summarise in bullet points** — write the relevant scoring rules on a whiteboard in plain language.
- **Use video resources** — FIRST and VEX produce season reveal videos and game walk-throughs that convey the same information non-textually.
- **Colour-code physical field diagrams** — different-coloured stickers on the field model to mark scoring zones rather than relying on the text description.
- For notebook entries, **allow audio/video recordings** as a draft medium — a student who struggles to write can narrate their design decisions, and the notebook entry can be typed or transcribed later.

### Processing and working memory differences

Some students need more time to process instructions and more repetition before information sticks. Strategies that help:

- **Give instructions in writing as well as verbally** — post the session plan on the whiteboard before the meeting starts.
- **One instruction at a time** — give a step, let the student start it, then give the next step.
- **Consistent meeting structure** — the same opening routine, same role assignments, same closing ritual reduce the working memory load of navigating a new environment each session.
- **Check-ins, not check-ups** — a quiet "how's that going?" after 5 minutes is supportive; standing over the student and watching is pressure.

### Autism spectrum

Autistic students bring genuine strengths to robotics: sustained focus, precision, systematic thinking, and deep subject-matter expertise. The challenges tend to be social and environmental rather than technical.

- **Explicit social rules** work better than implied ones — "In our team, we take turns speaking by raising a hand" is more accessible than expecting students to read social cues.
- **Predictable structure** — announce the agenda at the start of every session and warn before transitions.
- **Written role cards** — a card that says "Today your role is: Robot Inspector. Before each test run, check that all cables are connected and the battery shows green" removes ambiguity about what the student is supposed to be doing.
- **Honesty over social smoothing** — many autistic students prefer direct feedback over vague positive framing. "The chassis needs to be 2 cm shorter for the attachment to clear the field wall" is more useful than "Great start, but maybe the attachment could be a little different?"
- **Sensory environment** — see the sensory section above. Ear defenders, predictable lighting, and advance venue preparation are particularly valuable.

## Communication differences

### AAC users

Students who use augmentative and alternative communication devices (speech-generating devices, letter boards, eye-gaze systems) can participate in all aspects of robotics including programming, design, and judging presentations.

- **Allow time** — AAC communication is typically slower than speech. Ensure the team culture does not rush past an AAC user.
- **Programme robotics vocabulary** into the student's device at the start of the season: sensor names, part names, scoring zone names, team role names.
- **Judging presentations** — FLL and VEX judging panels are generally accommodating if you notify the event organiser in advance that a student uses AAC. A short written note with the student's name, their device, and a request for extra response time is usually sufficient.

### Speech and language differences

Stuttering, articulation differences, or expressive language differences should not limit a student's role on a competitive team:

- **Roles are not tied to fluency** — a student with a stutter can lead the robot programming sub-team, contribute to the engineering notebook, and participate in judging presentations.
- **Preparation reduces disfluency** — knowing exactly what they are going to say (from rehearsed notes or memorised talking points) typically helps students who stutter more than improvised speaking.
- **Team support during judging** — if a student prefers to have a teammate available to fill in during a difficult passage, that is fine. Judges are trained to evaluate the team's understanding and process, not individual speaking fluency.

## Inclusive team structure

### Role design

The standard robotics team roles (builder, programmer, researcher/innovation project, notebook keeper, drive team) are a starting point, not a constraint. Custom roles that play to individual strengths work fine:

- **Systems integrator** — the student who tests that the robot's subsystems work together. Great for students who are systematic and detail-oriented.
- **Documentation lead** — owns the engineering notebook and progress tracking. Good for students with strong written or organisational skills who may struggle with the physical build.
- **Strategy analyst** — studies the game rules and scoring matrix to recommend which missions or tasks to prioritise. Works well for students who prefer thinking over building.
- **Communications coordinator** — manages the team's outreach, social media (where programs require community engagement), and parent communication.

No student should be "the student we're including." Every student should have a genuine, valued contribution.

### Buddy and mentorship models

Pairing a student with a disability with a teammate (not as a carer but as a collaborative partner on a specific shared task) can work well when:

- Both students have complementary roles — one designs, one builds; one codes, one debugs.
- The pairing rotates so it does not become the default accommodation arrangement.
- The student with a disability is not always positioned as the junior partner.

### Preparing the team

If a student with a disability joins a pre-existing team, brief the existing members — not with medical detail, but with enough context to set expectations:

- "Kaito uses a speech-generating device. When he's speaking, we wait for him to finish before responding — same as we do for anyone else."
- "Maya uses ear defenders during loud sessions. That's just how she works best."

Normalise difference before problems arise rather than addressing it only when friction surfaces.

## Tournaments and events

### Pre-event preparation

- Contact the event organiser in advance about specific accessibility needs: wheelchair access, quiet room availability, dietary needs for all-day events.
- FIRST and RECF events are held in a range of venues — school gymnasiums, convention centres, university fieldhouses. Accessibility varies significantly. Request floor plan information if available.
- For students with high sensory sensitivity, an earlier arrival (before the main crowd) to walk the venue, locate the pit, and find the bathrooms reduces anxiety substantially.

### Judging accommodations

For formal judging rooms (FLL's three-room format, FTC's single portfolio session, VEX's pit interview):

- Notify the event organiser, head referee, or lead judge before the event that your team includes a student with a communication difference or AAC user.
- Request additional time if needed — most events allow this with advance notice.
- Written supplementary materials (engineering notebook, printed talking points) are already standard; they also serve as accessibility scaffolding.

### On the day

- Designate one adult (coach or parent volunteer) whose role includes accessibility support on the day — keeping track of the student's energy level, managing sensory environment, handling unexpected situations.
- Build in scheduled rest time around the match schedule rather than treating rest as an emergency response.
- Have a low-stimulation retreat plan in place before you need it.

## Program-specific notes

| Program | Key notes |
|---|---|
| **FLL Challenge** | Judging rooms are relatively quiet; match area can be very loud. Advance venue visit useful. Core Values rubric rewards genuine teamwork — inclusion is directly on-theme. |
| **FLL Explore** | Lower competitive pressure; no elimination rounds. More natural pacing for students who need additional time. Good introductory program. |
| **FLL Discover** | Play-based, no competitive events. Very low-barrier; ideal first program for students with significant support needs. |
| **VEX IQ** | Collaborative scoring format means alliance partner coordination is required; consider this when preparing communication support. Younger audience norm (ages 8–14). |
| **FTC** | Longer, louder events (often 8–10 hours). Significant fine-motor demands in FTC-scale hardware. Engineering Portfolio submission can be completed with accessibility accommodations. |
| **VEX V5 / VRC** | Scouting, alliance selection, and match strategy components benefit from students who can process data; good roles for students who prefer non-physical contributions. |
| **FRC** | Large events (100+ teams), multiple days, very loud. Safety gear (glasses, closed-toe shoes) required in pit — check fit for students with sensory sensitivities. Impact Award sub-team has many roles that do not require physical robot work. |
| **VEX U** | College-age participants; self-directed team structure. More flexibility to design completely custom roles and workflows around individual team members. |

## Finding additional support

You do not have to figure this out alone:

- **FIRST's Inclusion, Diversity, and Equity initiative** — firstinspires.org publishes resources on inclusive team practices. Search for "Inclusion" on the FIRST website.
- **The RECF community forums and Discord** — VEX coaches frequently share accessibility adaptations in community spaces.
- **Your local school's SPED or resource teacher** — the student's existing support team has documentation, strategies, and legal knowledge you do not need to reinvent. Ask to meet before the season starts.
- **OT (occupational therapy) resources** — OTs who work with the student often have specific ideas about workstation adaptations for fine-motor tasks. A 15-minute conversation can save hours of trial and error.
- **Parent knowledge** — the student's parents have already spent years learning what works. Ask them.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Better approach |
|---|---|
| Treating accessibility as a burden on the team | Frame it as team design — every member has a role that fits their strengths |
| Assigning only non-technical roles to students with disabilities | All roles are available; match the role to the student's actual strengths and interests |
| Waiting for a problem to arise before addressing sensory/environment needs | Set up the environment for the widest range from day one |
| Assuming a student needs help without asking | Ask the student directly: "What would make this easier for you?" |
| Over-disclosing a student's diagnosis to teammates | Share what the team needs to know behaviourally, not medical detail |
| Skipping advance event coordination | Contact the organiser weeks before the event, not the morning of |
| Making the student feel like a project | Treat them as a teammate with a specific working style, same as everyone else |
