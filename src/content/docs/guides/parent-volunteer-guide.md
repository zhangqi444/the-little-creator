Your kid is on a robotics team. You want to help. You have time, skills, and energy. This guide is for you.

Parent volunteers are often the difference between a team that thrives and a team that barely holds together. But there's a line between being genuinely helpful and accidentally taking over. This guide helps you find it — and stay on the right side of it.

---

## The parent's role in one sentence

Your job is to create the conditions for students to do their own work — not to do the work yourself.

That sounds simple. It's not. Especially when you can see the solution, the robot is frustrating your kid, and the qualifier is two weeks away. But the reason your kid signed up for robotics is to learn how to build, problem-solve, and compete. Every time a parent solves a problem for them, that learning disappears.

This principle applies across all six programs (FLL, FTC, FRC, VEX IQ, VEX V5, VEX U), though the details vary. Younger FLL/VEX IQ teams need more coaching scaffolding; older FRC/VEX U teams are often capable of running nearly everything independently — your role shifts accordingly.

## High-value parent contributions

### Logistics and coordination
This is where parents can provide the most leverage with the least risk of overstepping:

- **Transportation.** Getting kids to practices, scrimmages, and events reliably. Don't underestimate this — it's mission-critical and invisible when it works.
- **Snacks and meals.** Long tournament days drain energy and morale. A parent who manages food and water is genuinely appreciated.
- **Registration and paperwork.** FIRST and VEX registrations, team consent forms, travel waiver signing — the administrative overhead is real and coaches often drown in it.
- **Venue logistics.** At events: check-in, pit setup, knowing where everything is, managing the team schedule.
- **Communication.** Running the team messaging group, keeping parents informed, coordinating pickup times. The head coach shouldn't be texting 12 parents simultaneously.

### Fundraising and budget
- Researching and applying for grants (FIRST grants, local education foundations, corporate matching programs).
- Coordinating fundraisers (bake sales, car washes, crowdfunding, community sponsorships).
- Managing the team budget spreadsheet if you have finance skills.
- See [Team Funding](/guides/team-funding/) for a full guide.

### Event volunteering
Every FIRST and VEX event depends on volunteers to run. There are roles specifically designed for parents:
- **Field reset crew:** Reset the game field between matches. Physical, quick-moving, satisfying.
- **Queue manager / scorekeeper:** Help teams move from queue to field on time.
- **Pit admin / team check-in:** Welcome arriving teams, confirm paperwork.
- **Photography / media:** Document the event (with permission) — teams love having good photos afterward.
- **Judging assistant:** Help judges navigate between teams during judging rounds.

Volunteering at events is also one of the best ways to understand how competitions work — which makes you a much better practice-day resource.

### Skill contributions (used carefully)
Do you have relevant expertise? It can be genuinely useful — with guardrails:

| Skill | How to help without overstepping |
|---|---|
| Engineering / mechanical | Ask questions. "What do you think would happen if…?" Not "Here's what you should do." |
| Software / programming | Review code they've written. Explain concepts. Pair-program only when they're stuck. |
| Project management | Help them build their own timeline. Don't manage it for them. |
| Public speaking / presentation | Give feedback on their presentations. Let them lead. |
| Design (CAD, graphic) | Introduce tools and let them practice. Don't take over the CAD seat. |
| Photography / video | Document their work throughout the season — hugely valuable for notebooks, portfolios, and award submissions. |

The test: after your contribution, who could explain what was done and why — you, or the kid?

## Volunteering at events: what to expect

### Before the event
- Find out what your team's coach actually needs. Don't assume.
- Register as a volunteer with the event organiser (FIRST and VEX both have volunteer registration systems).
- FRC events especially rely heavily on adult volunteers — the process is well-organised.
- Know the schedule: when does the team arrive, when do gates open, when is lunch, when does the team pack up.

### During the event
- **Your primary job is support, not observation.** If you're at the venue, expect to be active.
- **Stay out of the pit during robot work.** Pit space is tight and is for students (and the head coach/mentor). Hovering in the pit creates distraction and pressure.
- **Manage the sideline.** Cheer loudly and enthusiastically during matches. Avoid shouting instructions — your kid can't hear you, and it creates anxiety.
- **Let the coach handle issues.** If something goes wrong (a robot breaks, a kid is upset), the coach deals with it. Your job is to keep the atmosphere calm and supportive.
- **Don't approach judges.** Judging interactions must be student-led. Parent lobbying — even well-intentioned — can create problems.

### After the event
- Celebrate wins *and* losses. Your reaction sets the emotional tone.
- Help with teardown — it's often forgotten but really appreciated.
- If your kid is disappointed, listen before reassuring. "That was hard" lands better than "You'll do better next time" when emotions are still fresh.

## The sideline: managing your own instincts

This is the hardest part.

You've invested time, money, and emotional energy. You care about the outcome. You know things that could help. When the robot fails in the middle of a match, every instinct says: do something.

What actually helps:

**Stay visibly calm.** Kids read parents' body language during competition. If you're tense and anxious on the sideline, your kid feels it on the field. The most useful thing you can do for their performance is look relaxed — even when you're not.

**Cheer the effort, not the result.** "You were so focused out there!" is more useful than "Why did the robot stop?" The debrief happens later, not immediately after a disappointing match.

**Redirect competitive instincts.** It's normal to feel competitive. Redirect that into cheering every team genuinely, helping with smooth event logistics, and giving your team the best support conditions. Gracious Professionalism applies to parents too.

**Trust the coach.** If you have concerns about how things are going, raise them privately with the coach, not in front of the team, and not during competition. The coach is managing a complex system in real time. Wait until after the event.

**Ask before acting.** Before doing anything that affects the team experience (talking to a judge, approaching another team, posting on social media), ask the head coach first.

## What NOT to do

Some well-meaning parent actions create problems:

| What | Why it backfires |
|---|---|
| Fixing the robot before a match | Removes ownership from students; may create rule violations (only students may work on the robot in some programs) |
| Talking to judges on behalf of the team | Undermines student presentation; may affect judging scores |
| Live-coaching during matches | Distracts kids; they can't process it in the moment anyway |
| Posting match videos/photos without checking | Some events restrict filming; always ask organiser and get blanket consent if involving other kids |
| Criticising the coach in front of kids | Undermines team cohesion and puts kids in the middle |
| Lobbying for awards | Judges notice; it reflects poorly on the team |

## Adapting by program

**FLL / VEX IQ** (younger kids, ages 8–14): Parent presence at practice is often normal and welcomed. Kids may need more scaffolding. The key rule: let kids lead the robot and project work. Your job is emotional support, logistics, and time management.

**FTC / VEX V5** (teens, grades 7–12): Teams typically prefer less parent involvement at practice. Your role shifts toward logistics, fundraising, and event support. Trust the student leads — they're developing real technical leadership skills.

**FRC / VEX U** (older students, grades 9–12 / college): Many FRC and VEX U teams run almost entirely independently. Mentors with specific technical skills (electrical, software, machining) are welcomed, but they contribute expertise, not control. Parents in a non-mentor role focus almost entirely on logistics and event support.

## When the coach needs more help

Sometimes teams are under-resourced and parents genuinely need to step up beyond a support role. If you're asked to take on more:

- Be explicit about what role you're playing (mentor vs. parent) and when.
- If you're mentoring, learn the rules about adult involvement — they differ by program.
- Keep yourself in the role of guide and questioner, not builder and decision-maker, even as a mentor.
- Document what you do. Other parents (and future coaches) will be grateful.

---

## A note on competition season pressure

Competition seasons are intense. Families feel it. The temptation to micromanage spikes as qualifiers approach.

The teams that do best under pressure are the ones that practised staying calm under pressure. Your calm, confident presence during the weeks before a qualifier matters more than any last-minute technical fix.

If you're feeling anxious about the outcome: that's normal, and it means you care. Channel it into making the team's support environment excellent — reliable logistics, good food, calm sideline energy. That's what actually helps.
