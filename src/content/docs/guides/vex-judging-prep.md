Judging is a decisive part of VEX advancement — for most awards, your performance in the pit interview and the quality of your engineering notebook matter as much as your robot's match record. Understanding how VEX judging works and preparing your team deliberately is one of the highest-leverage things a coach can do.

:::note[Official criteria]
This guide explains the process and offers practical prep advice. For the exact award criteria and judging rubrics, download the current season's [game manual and judges guide](https://www.vexrobotics.com/competition-game-manuals) from VEX Robotics. Criteria change year to year — always verify against the current manual.
:::

## How VEX judging is structured

Unlike FLL (three separate judging rooms) or FTC (a single sit-down portfolio interview), VEX judging is primarily **pit-based**. Judges walk through the pit area during the event and stop to talk to teams. There may also be a scheduled interview slot — check your event's information packet or agenda.

| Program | Age range | Judging format | Notebook depth expected |
|---|---|---|---|
| **VEX IQ (VIQRC)** | 8–14 | Roving pit visits; some events have a scheduled slot | Process over polish; iteration stories; clear student voice |
| **VEX V5 (V5RC)** | 11–18 | Roving pit visits; formal scheduled interviews at larger events | Engineering rigour; data; trade-off reasoning; student authorship |
| **VEX U (VURC)** | College | Scheduled pit interview common; university-level engineering depth | Full engineering depth; CAD, analysis, two-robot strategy rationale |

All three programs use the same judged awards — Excellence, Design Award, Build Award, Think Award, Sportsmanship Award (among others). See [VEX Awards — What Each One Rewards](/guides/vex-awards/) for the full award breakdown.

## The key principle: judges evaluate process, not just outcome

The most common mistake teams make is treating the pit interview as a chance to show off their robot. Judges already see the robot. What they can't see without asking is **how your team designed it**.

The questions judges really want to answer:

1. Did the team go through a real **design process** — brainstorm, prototype, test, iterate?
2. Can **multiple students** explain the robot (not just one builder or one programmer)?
3. Does the **engineering notebook** reflect decisions made in real time — or was it filled in the night before?
4. Does the team demonstrate **Gracious Professionalism** throughout the day?

A team with an 80% match win rate and a shallow notebook will often lose Excellence to a team with a 60% win rate and a rich, well-kept notebook.

## Engineering notebook — the foundation of every judged award

Your notebook is the primary evidence judges use to assess Design Award and Excellence Award. A strong notebook does the following:

### What makes a strong notebook entry

- **Date-stamped entries throughout the season** — not a batch written the week before tournament.
- **Real decisions documented in real time** — "We considered Option A (roller intake) and Option B (claw). We prototyped both; the roller was faster for this game object but jammed on the angled surface. We chose the claw with a foam pad to add grip."
- **Test data** — match results, autonomous scores, repeatability tests, measurements. Numbers are more convincing than prose.
- **Iteration records** — what changed between meetings and why. Photos help.
- **Clear student authorship** — entries written in student voices, with different handwriting or writing styles visible. Judges penalise notebooks that look adult-authored.
- **Diagrams and sketches** — even rough sketches of considered mechanisms are better than none.

### What hurts a notebook

- Blank pages or date gaps (implies work happened without documentation).
- Post-hoc writeups — clean summaries with no scratch-thinking visible.
- Only one student's handwriting throughout.
- No data, no trade-off analysis, no evidence of things that didn't work.
- Typing a whole season's "journal" in a Google Doc the night before doesn't fool judges.

:::tip[Notebook cadence]
Build notebook entry time into every practice session — even 10 minutes at the end of each meeting keeps documentation current and prevents the end-of-season panic.
:::

For general notebook guidance, see [FLL Engineering Notebook Guide](/guides/notebook-guide/) — most principles transfer directly to VEX.

## The pit interview: what to expect

### What judges will ask

Judges don't follow a fixed script, but the core questions are consistent:

**Robot and design**
- "Walk me through your robot — what does each subsystem do?"
- "What's the most significant design change you made this season? Why?"
- "What problem gave you the most trouble, and how did you solve it?"
- "What would you change if you had two more weeks?"

**Engineering process (notebook)**
- "Can you show me in your notebook where you decided between two options?"
- "How do you handle it when something doesn't work?"
- "Who works on the robot versus the notebook? Does that overlap?"

**Programming and autonomous**
- "What does your autonomous routine do, and how reliable is it?"
- "How do you tune your code between matches?"
- "What sensors are you using and why?"

**Team dynamics and Gracious Professionalism**
- "How does your team split up the work?"
- "Have you helped any other teams at this event?"
- "What would you do if a team you were competing against came to you for parts?"

### How to staff your pit for judges

**Have students in the pit, not parents or mentors.** When a judge walks up, coaches and parents should step back. If students are on the field for a match, it's okay to say so — judges will usually wait or return.

**Every student should know the basics.** Even students who focus mainly on outreach or documentation should be able to answer: what does the robot do, what was the hardest part to build, what does the autonomous routine score?

**Designate a "pit interview lead."** One student (not necessarily the captain) should own the opener: introduce the team, give a 90-second robot overview, and then route specific questions — "For the programming details, ask her, she wrote the autonomous."

**Keep the notebook accessible.** Have it open in the pit at all times. When judges ask about a design decision, you want to be able to show the entry on the spot, not say "I think we wrote that down somewhere."

**Put the robot at the front of the pit table.** Judges who are walking by and deciding whether to stop are partly making that decision based on visual interest. An accessible, well-organized pit with a robot visible at the front invites conversation.

## VEX IQ (VIQRC): judging tips for younger teams

VIQRC students are often 8–12 years old. Preparing younger students for judging is different from preparing teens:

### Keep it genuine, not rehearsed

Judges respond much better to "we tried three designs and this one drove straightest" than a polished, obviously-rehearsed speech. Kids at this age are believable when they speak naturally about things they actually did — and judges know the difference.

### Teach them to say "I don't know"

It's fine. Judges understand these are kids. Trying to bluff a technical answer at age 10 is transparent and hurts more than an honest "I'm not sure, but I can show you what we tried."

### Encourage all team members to speak

Assign "topics" in advance so every student has something to explain. The kid who focused on building the claw explains the claw. The kid who wrote the autonomous explains the code. No one should be silent in the pit; no one should speak for everyone else.

### Parent coaching: step back

The most common VIQRC judging mistake is parents (or coaches) talking to judges on behalf of the team. When a judge walks up to the pit, parents take a visible step back. The kids do all the talking. If the kids get stuck, resist the urge to rescue — judges want to see the kids handle it.

## VEX V5 (V5RC): judging tips for high-school teams

V5RC judges expect **technical depth**. Teens sometimes underprepare on the assumption they'll just "talk naturally" — a mock interview or two before the event is worth more than any other prep step.

### Be ready to discuss trade-offs with data

"We chose the X-drive because it gave us better field coverage" is okay. "We chose the X-drive after testing both tank and X — our cycle time improved by 3 seconds because lateral movement let us skip the full rotation at the goal zone" is what a Design Award contender sounds like.

### Notebook entries should reflect the age of the team

V5RC notebooks are compared against other high-school teams. Judges expect design decisions documented with data, trade-off tables, testing protocols, and dated entries. A VIQRC-level notebook (good for IQ) won't be competitive at V5RC.

### Know your autonomous in detail

Think Award and Excellence Award both consider programming depth. Students who wrote the autonomous should be able to explain: the control loop or dead-reckoning strategy, what sensor inputs are used, how the routine was tuned, what failure modes were observed and how they were handled.

### Gracious Professionalism is observed all day

Judges don't just evaluate teams in the formal pit interview — they watch how teams behave in the stands, at the skills field, and in the pit during the day. Helping a struggling team find a spare part, cheering for a match you're not in, gracious behaviour after a loss — these are all noticed. See [Gracious Professionalism in Practice](/guides/gracious-professionalism/) for specifics.

## VEX U (VURC): judging tips for college teams

VEX U judges expect university-level engineering rigour. College students are expected to have deeper analysis, more formal documentation, and a clearer engineering rationale for every significant design decision.

### Two-robot strategy belongs in the notebook

VURC teams run two robots simultaneously. Your notebook should explain how the two robots were designed to complement each other — role specialisation, autonomous coordination strategy, how conflicts (e.g., two robots pursuing the same game object) were resolved by design.

### CAD and analysis are expected

VURC notebooks from competitive teams include CAD screenshots, structural analysis, tolerance reasoning, and motor load calculations. Judges evaluate whether the team used engineering tools, not just built what seemed to work.

### PROS and software architecture depth

VEX U programming judges expect multi-task awareness, PID tuning documentation, and autonomous path planning rationale. If you use LemLib or OkapiLib, be prepared to explain what the library provides and what custom code sits on top — "we just used LemLib" without understanding of it won't satisfy a VURC judge.

### Both robots should have documented design histories

A common VURC notebook mistake is thorough documentation of Robot 1 and thin documentation of Robot 2 (often because Robot 2 is simpler or was built later). Judges notice. Both robots should have full design-process entries.

## Matching awards to team strengths

| Award | What it rewards | Best fit for |
|---|---|---|
| **Excellence Award** | Overall excellence: robot performance + Skills + notebook + GP + judging | Teams strong across everything |
| **Design Award** | Engineering design process via notebook and pit interview | Teams with exceptional documentation and design rationale |
| **Build Award** | Mechanical execution quality: wiring, structure, precision | Teams with clean, intentional builds |
| **Think Award** | Programming or autonomous achievement | Teams with notable autonomous routines or sensor work |
| **Amaze Award** | Something genuinely impressive or unusual on the robot | Teams with creative mechanisms or strategies |
| **Sportsmanship Award** | Gracious Professionalism observed throughout the day | Teams known for helping others and gracious behaviour |
| **Judges Award** | Discretionary — exceptional story or attribute not captured elsewhere | Varies |

For the full award descriptions and what qualifies teams for advancement, see [VEX Awards — What Each One Rewards](/guides/vex-awards/).

## Common judging mistakes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Parent or coach answers judge's questions | Judges are evaluating students, not adults | Visibly step back when judge approaches |
| Notebook filled in the week before tournament | Judges ask about specific entries; the timeline is obvious | Document at each practice session throughout the season |
| Only one student can explain the robot | Signals unequal participation | Every student owns at least one subsystem narrative |
| Treating the pit interview as a rehearsed presentation | Judges want a conversation, not a performance | Practice with real Q&A, not scripted delivery |
| Not knowing the autonomous routine in detail | Think Award and Excellence both probe programming depth | The programmer briefs the whole team on what the auto does |
| Notebook looks adult-authored | Judges know adult writing and student writing; it costs you Design Award | Students write entries; mentors may check grammar, not content |
| Not keeping notebook open in the pit | Judge asks about a decision; you can't find it | Notebook visible and open at front of pit table |
| Ignoring Gracious Professionalism outside the pit | GP is observed all day, not just in interviews | Coach brief: how we behave at the alliance station, in the stands, everywhere |
| Underpreparing for VEX U expectations | College judges expect engineering depth; "it worked" isn't an answer | VURC teams: treat judging prep as seriously as robot prep |

## Judging prep timeline

| When | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Throughout the season** | Notebook entries at every practice — dated, iteration-focused, student-authored |
| **2 weeks before tournament** | Review notebook for gaps; fill any missing test data or decision records |
| **1 week before** | Run one mock pit interview — 90 seconds to intro the robot, then Q&A; rotate so every student speaks |
| **Night before** | Review: who's answering which questions? Who owns the notebook? Who's the pit interview lead? |
| **Morning of event** | Notebook on pit table. Robot accessible. Parents briefed to step back. |
| **During event** | Behave as if judges are watching all day (they are). Help other teams when you can. |
| **After judging** | Debrief: what question caught you off guard? Document for next time. |
