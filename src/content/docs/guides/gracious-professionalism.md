"Gracious Professionalism" is one of the most-used phrases in FLL — and one of the most vague. Judges ask about it. Coaches remind kids about it. But what does it actually *look like* when it's happening?

This guide gives concrete, real-world examples across practices, tournaments, and day-to-day team life.

:::note[FIRST's definition]
FIRST defines Gracious Professionalism® as a way of doing things that encourages high-quality work, emphasizes the value of others, and respects individuals and the community. It's trademarked by FIRST. For their full description, see the [FIRST website](https://www.firstinspires.org/about/vision-and-mission). This page is a practical companion, not a substitute for the official description.
:::

## The short version

Gracious Professionalism is two things at once:
- **Gracious:** You treat other people well — including competitors — even under pressure.
- **Professional:** You do your work seriously, take pride in it, and hold yourself to a high standard.

Both parts matter. Being gracious but sloppy is not GP. Being technically excellent but unkind is not GP. The goal is both, simultaneously.

---

## What GP looks like at practice

### With your own team

- You disagree with a teammate's idea, so you ask "can we try it and see what happens?" instead of dismissing it
- Your robot fails a mission five runs in a row — you say "okay, what specifically changed between run 3 and run 4?" instead of blaming the programmer
- Someone makes a mistake that sets the team back — your first response is "what do we do now?" not "whose fault was this?"
- You notice a quieter teammate hasn't spoken in 20 minutes — you ask them directly: "What do you think about this?"
- You did most of the work on one section — you make sure others understand it too, so it's a team asset, not yours alone

### What it is NOT at practice

- Winning every argument, even if you're right
- Doing all the work yourself because it's faster
- Rolling your eyes when someone suggests something you think won't work
- Saying "I told you so" when your prediction was correct

## What GP looks like at tournaments

### With other teams

**In the pits:**
- A team nearby is struggling with a sensor issue you recognize — you walk over and mention what worked for your team. You don't fix it for them; you share what you know.
- Another team asks to see your robot — you show them, including how an attachment works. You're not required to; you do it because that's the culture.
- A team you've been competing against all day asks how your run went — you tell them honestly, including what failed.

**During matches:**
- You watch another team's run and applaud when they complete a mission, even if they're your direct competition.
- Your team has a perfect run; you celebrate with your team, not in the direction of another team.
- Another team has a terrible run — you don't comment on it or laugh.

**After results:**
- Your team places lower than expected — you congratulate the teams that placed higher, specifically. "Your robot was really consistent all day" is better than a generic "good job."
- Your team wins an award — you acknowledge publicly which other teams you thought were great.

### With judges and volunteers

- A judge asks a question your team doesn't know — you say "we're not sure, but here's what we do know" instead of guessing or going quiet.
- A volunteer makes a call that goes against your team — you accept it and move on. You can ask for clarification politely after the fact; you don't argue in the moment.
- At the end of the day, your team says thank you to the volunteers running the event. Specifically. "Thank you for running the practice table — it helped a lot."

### What it is NOT at tournaments

- Refusing to share information with other teams because you think it gives them an advantage
- Celebrating loudly near a team that just had a bad run
- Arguing with referees or judges about scoring decisions during the event
- "Strategically" leaving your robot parked in a way that interferes with another team's run (this is also a rule violation)

## What GP looks like for coaches and parents

Kids model what they see. If the adults around them behave graciously and professionally, that's the culture of the team.

**Gracious coaching:**
- You don't correct kids in front of other teams in a way that embarrasses them
- When a run fails, you start with "what happened?" not "you didn't do what we practiced"
- You thank opposing coaches after matches
- You tell your team what other teams did well — not just what your own team did

**Professional coaching:**
- You prepare. Kids who show up to a chaotic practice learn that preparation doesn't matter.
- You're honest about what your team can and can't do. Over-promising to kids sets them up to fail.
- You hold the team to commitments — if someone agreed to research a topic, you follow up.

**What parents can do:**
- Cheer for your own team; don't comment on other teams' performance
- Let kids handle their own interactions with judges and other teams — your job is support, not management
- After a tough result, lead with curiosity ("what was that like?") before fixing mode ("here's what you should have done")

## Explaining GP to kids by age

**Ages 9–11 (FLL Challenge beginners):**
> "Gracious Professionalism means you work really hard AND you're kind to everyone — even the teams you're competing against. It's being a good sport AND doing your best work. Both at the same time."

**Ages 12–14:**
> "GP is about how you act when it's hard. When your robot fails, when another team beats you, when you disagree with a teammate — how do you handle it? That's where GP shows up. Anyone can be nice when things are going well."

**Ages 15–16 (experienced competitors):**
> "GP is what makes this community worth being in. If you hoard knowledge, celebrate other teams failing, or cut corners — you win one tournament and make the whole program worse. If you share, mentor, and compete hard AND fairly, you build something that lasts."

## GP moments worth calling out in real time

Coaches: when you see GP in action, name it. "That was gracious professionalism — you helped that team even though they're competing against us." Real-time recognition builds culture faster than any lecture.

Moments worth naming:
- A kid shares a solution with another team unprompted
- A kid accepts a judge's feedback without getting defensive
- A kid acknowledges a teammate's contribution publicly
- A kid steps back so a quieter teammate can take the lead
- A kid loses gracefully — congratulates the other team, no sulking

Moments worth privately addressing (not calling out in front of others):
- Eye-rolling at another team's presentation
- Blaming a teammate loudly after a failed run
- Refusing to engage with a judge's question

## Quick reference: GP in one sentence per situation

| Situation | GP response |
|---|---|
| Another team asks how your robot works | Show them — competitors sharing knowledge is the FLL culture |
| Your team loses an award you expected to win | "Congratulations" to the winners; debrief privately with your team later |
| A judge gives critical feedback | "Thank you — that's something we'll work on" |
| Your robot fails in front of everyone | "We'll figure out what happened and come back stronger" |
| A teammate made a mistake that cost the team | "What do we need to do now?" — not attribution |
| Another team is struggling nearby | Offer what you know; let them decide whether to use it |
| A referee makes a call you disagree with | Accept it; ask politely for clarification after the run |
| Your team wins | Acknowledge the other teams specifically; don't minimize their effort |
