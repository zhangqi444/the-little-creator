Two coaches on one team is common. Done well, it's one of the best structures in youth robotics — students get more bandwidth, coaches get less burnout, and there's always a second perspective when something goes wrong.

Done poorly, it creates exactly the opposite: confused students who play coaches against each other, coaches who undercut each other in front of the team, and a season that feels twice as exhausting as going solo.

The difference is almost never about the individual coaches. It's about whether the two coaches have explicitly designed how they'll work together.

---

## The core problem co-coaches don't solve up front

Most co-coaching relationships start with implicit role division: one person takes charge, the other fills in. This works for the first few sessions. It stops working when:

- A student asks the secondary coach a direct question and gets a different answer
- Both coaches notice the same problem at the same time and respond differently
- One coach thinks the team is ready; the other thinks they're not
- One coach wants to push harder; the other wants to protect the students

None of these are character problems. They're coordination problems. The solution is designing the coordination in advance, not waiting for conflict to force it.

---

## Role division: make it explicit

There's no single right way to divide coaching roles. What matters is that you've agreed on a structure before the season starts — and that students know it.

### Option A: Domain split

Each coach owns specific domains. Students know who to go to for what.

Examples:
- Coach 1 owns robot design and hardware; Coach 2 owns programming and software
- Coach 1 owns Innovation Project / outreach; Coach 2 owns robot and competition prep
- Coach 1 is primary during build sessions; Coach 2 is primary during judging prep

**Works best when:** Coaches have genuinely different skill sets and can stay in their lanes.

**Watch for:** Domain overlap on mixed tasks (e.g., autonomous involves both code and mechanism); students routing around the "wrong" coach.

### Option B: Session lead rotation

One coach leads each session; the other supports. The lead role rotates on a schedule.

**Works best when:** Both coaches have similar skill sets and want to share the full experience.

**Watch for:** Lead/support lines blurring in the moment; the support coach jumping in when the lead is handling something.

### Option C: Primary + specialist

One coach is the day-to-day primary; the other plays a specialist role (e.g., comes in for judging prep, programming help, or tournament day only).

**Works best when:** One coach has more availability and one has a specific expertise to contribute.

**Watch for:** The specialist feeling marginalised; the primary burning out from running the team alone between specialist appearances.

### Option D: Age/group split (larger teams)

Each coach takes responsibility for a subset of the team — often by age, year of experience, or role.

**Works best when:** The team is large (8+ students) and naturally divides into sub-groups.

**Watch for:** Sub-groups becoming siloed; students on one side not knowing what the other side is doing.

## Talking about authority in front of students

Students will test the co-coaching structure. Not maliciously — they're doing what humans do: looking for the fastest path to a "yes."

If Coach 1 says "not yet" and the student knows Coach 2 might say "sure, go ahead," they'll ask Coach 2. This is natural. It becomes a problem if it works.

Prevent it with one rule: **when one coach has made a call, the other coach backs it in public and discusses it in private.**

This doesn't mean silent agreement on everything. It means:

1. If you disagree with a call your co-coach just made, don't show it to students.
2. Address it with your co-coach between sessions or in a team pause — not by overriding in the moment.
3. If a student is actively trying to play you against each other, name it calmly: "That's a question for Coach A — they're handling that."

The one exception: safety. If your co-coach is about to let something unsafe happen, intervene immediately. Safety overrides coaching unity.

## Pre-session sync

The single highest-value practice for co-coaches is a brief pre-session check-in — even 5 minutes before the team arrives.

What to cover:
- What's the focus today? (One specific thing, not "we'll see how it goes")
- Who's leading? (Explicit, not assumed)
- Any follow-up from last session the other coach doesn't know about?
- Any student situations to flag? (A student having a hard week, a conflict from last time)
- Any decisions that might come up today that we should agree on now?

This is boring. Do it anyway. It prevents the majority of in-session co-coaching friction.

---

## Post-session debrief

Brief is fine — even a 5-minute message exchange works. What matters is that both coaches leave with the same read on where the team is.

Cover:
- What went well?
- What didn't?
- Any student situations to track?
- What needs to happen before next session?
- Any unresolved decisions?

If you're in different time zones or have different schedules, a shared note or session log (see [Team Documentation System](/for-educators/team-documentation-system/)) works as well as a conversation.

## When you disagree

You will disagree. That's part of what makes co-coaching valuable — two perspectives. The goal isn't to eliminate disagreement; it's to handle it in a way that doesn't damage the team's trust in their coaches.

**In-session disagreement:** Hold it. Finish the session. Discuss after.

If the disagreement is about something immediate — a decision that has to be made now — one of you makes the call and the other backs it. Agree in advance who gets the deciding vote on which types of decisions (see role division above).

**Recurring disagreement:** Take it seriously. If you're regularly landing on different sides of the same question (how much to push the team, whether the robot is ready, how to handle a specific student), you have a fundamental coaching philosophy difference. Read through the [Coaching Philosophy guide](/for-educators/coaching-philosophy/) together and surface where your approaches differ. You don't have to agree on everything, but you need to know where you differ and have a protocol for it.

**Disagreement about a student situation:** These are the most sensitive. If one coach thinks a student is struggling and needs more support and the other thinks they need to be pushed harder, the risk of getting it wrong is high. Default to: check in with the student directly, separately if needed. Then align on an approach before the next session.

## What students should experience

From a student's perspective, a well-functioning co-coaching pair should feel like:

- Two adults who agree on the rules and expectations
- Two adults with slightly different styles and strengths they can draw on
- A consistent experience from session to session, regardless of which coach is leading

What they should not experience:
- Confusion about who to ask
- Getting different answers on the same question
- Tension between coaches
- Feeling like they have to manage the coaches' dynamic

If students start managing the coaches — mediating between them, choosing information to share with one but not the other, acting differently depending on who's present — that's a signal the co-coaching structure needs repair.

## Program-specific notes

| Program | Common co-coaching structure | Watch for |
|---|---|---|
| **FLL / VEX IQ** | One lead coach, one assistant; often parent volunteer + teacher | Parent volunteer may defer too much or too little; align on authority before season |
| **FTC** | Robot lead + project/portfolio lead split is natural | Portfolio coach sometimes feels secondary; make both tracks visibly valued |
| **FRC** | Lead mentor + sub-team mentor structure; multiple co-coaches common | Mentors from different sub-teams may have different standards; align on non-robot expectations (meetings, notebook, Impact) |
| **VEX V5 / VRC** | Often head coach + assistant from same club or school | Familiarity can mean less explicit role design — still do it |
| **VEX U** | Faculty advisor + graduate student lead is common | Faculty advisor may be distant from day-to-day; define the floor for involvement |

## Handling co-coach burnout

Co-coaching should reduce burnout — both people share the load. If one person is carrying more than their share, name it early.

Signs of imbalanced load:
- One coach does all the pre-session communication with parents
- One coach runs every session while the other "supports"
- One coach is the de facto single point of contact for students between sessions
- One coach does all the administrative tasks (registration, forms, equipment logistics)

These imbalances are usually invisible to the person not carrying the load. They need to be surfaced explicitly — not as complaints, but as design questions. "I've noticed I've been handling all the parent emails — can we split that?"

---

## Ending a co-coaching relationship

Sometimes a co-coaching pair doesn't work out. One person leaves, the team dynamic shifts, schedules change.

Handle it directly with students: "Coach [Name] won't be able to continue with us after [date]. We'll [describe what's changing]." Don't over-explain. Don't involve students in the reasons.

If you're the one leaving mid-season: document everything you know. Session notes, student context, where the robot is in development, any decisions in progress. Give your co-coach the best possible handoff. See [Team Documentation System](/for-educators/team-documentation-system/) for structure.

---

## Quick-start checklist

Before the season starts, co-coaches should answer these together:

- [ ] How are we dividing domains or session leads? (Pick Option A, B, C, or D above)
- [ ] Who has final say when we disagree on a given type of decision?
- [ ] How will we handle it if a student gets different answers from each of us?
- [ ] When/how will we sync before and after sessions?
- [ ] Who is the primary contact for parents and external communication?
- [ ] Who handles administrative tasks (registration, equipment, forms)?
- [ ] How will we flag student welfare concerns to each other?
- [ ] What's our plan if one of us can't make a session?
