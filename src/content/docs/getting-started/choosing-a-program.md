Six youth competitive robotics programs exist across the FIRST and VEX families — each designed for different ages, commitment levels, and goals. This guide helps you figure out which one fits your kid, your team, or your classroom.

If you already know which program you want, jump straight to [What is FLL?](/getting-started/what-is-fll/), [What is FTC?](/getting-started/what-is-ftc/), [What is FRC?](/getting-started/what-is-frc/), [What is VEX IQ?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-iq/), [What is VEX V5 / VRC?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-v5/), or [What is VEX U?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-u/).

## Start with age

The fastest filter is your child's current age.

**Ages 4–6 — FLL Discover**
LEGO DUPLO-based activities focused on first STEM exposure. No competition or scores — families and classrooms do this for the joy of building. There is no wrong answer here; if your child is curious about robots, start here.

**Ages 6–10 — FLL Explore or VEX IQ**
FLL Explore uses familiar LEGO bricks and is a gentle, team-based intro. VEX IQ starts at age 8 and introduces a snap-together metal-look system with proper gears and motors — slightly higher floor, but also a higher ceiling.

**Ages 9–14 — FLL Challenge or VEX IQ (or both)**
This is the most common overlap range. Both programs run at the same schools and clubs, and some teams do both in the same year. See the "FLL Challenge vs VEX IQ" section below for how to choose.

**Ages 12–18 — FTC or VEX V5 / VRC**
Once kids are in middle or high school they are ready for metal construction, larger robots, and more complex game strategies. FTC and VEX V5 / VRC are direct competitors in this age range. See the "FTC vs VEX V5" section below.

**Ages 14–18 — FRC**
FRC is available to high-schoolers, but it's significantly more demanding than FTC or VEX V5 — typical teams put in 10–20 hours per week during the six-week build season. It's best suited to students who want a near-professional engineering experience.

**18+ (college) — VEX U**
VEX U / VURC is a college competition with no age ceiling. If a student loved VEX V5 in high school, VEX U is the natural continuation. See [What is VEX U?](/getting-started/what-is-vex-u/).

## FLL Challenge vs VEX IQ

These two programs serve overlapping ages (9–14) and both run in schools and clubs. Here's how to choose:

**Choose FLL Challenge if:**
- Your child loves LEGO and you already own LEGO bricks — you'll feel at home
- A balance of **robotics + research + teamwork judging** appeals (FLL has an Innovation Project and Core Values component; VEX IQ does not)
- The FIRST community and its Core Values philosophy resonate with you
- You want a strong global community and extensive coach resources
- You're OK with fully autonomous competition (no driver control during matches)

**Choose VEX IQ if:**
- Your child wants **driver control** — VEX IQ has both a driver-operated Teamwork Challenge and an autonomous Autonomous Coding Skills event
- Head-to-head **alliance matches** (two teams cooperating per match) feel more exciting than solo timed runs
- Your child may want to continue into VEX V5 / VRC later — VEX IQ is the on-ramp
- You prefer a metal-and-screws building system with more mechanical engineering depth

**Both programs at once:** Some teams run both simultaneously. FLL Challenge and VEX IQ have different registration timelines and hardware, but they use similar calendar windows (Aug–Dec season). It's more work but absolutely done by dedicated clubs.

## FTC vs VEX V5 / VRC

Both serve ages 12–18, involve metal construction, larger robots, and multi-team alliance matches. The communities have different cultures and different hardware ecosystems.

**Choose FTC if:**
- Your student is already in the FIRST family (FLL background) — FTC is the natural next step
- **Java programming** is the direction of travel (FTC uses Java/Kotlin or Blocks via the Android-based REV Control Hub)
- A strong emphasis on **judged components** (Engineering Portfolio, outreach, community impact) matters alongside robot performance
- You want access to the well-developed **Game Manual 0 community resource** and an active mentor network
- Your team is smaller (FTC works well at 3–15 members)

**Choose VEX V5 / VRC if:**
- Your student is already in the VEX family (VEX IQ background) — VEX V5 is the natural next step
- **C++ / Python** through VEXcode or PROS is the direction of travel
- **Skills Challenge** (individual team runs on an empty field) matters — it's a way to qualify for events independently of alliance partners
- You want a very active global community with strong regional support in the United States and internationally
- Your team is smaller — VEX V5 teams are typically 3–10 students, often a single classroom

**Can my student do both?** It's uncommon to compete in both FTC and VEX V5 simultaneously because the seasons overlap and both require significant weekly time. If a student must choose, go with whichever matches their existing hardware, community connections, or strongest interest.

## FRC vs everything else

FRC is a category unto itself. It's significantly more intensive than any other program on this list:

- **Six-week build season** (Jan–Feb) typically requires 15–20 hours per week
- **Robots can weigh up to 125 lb** (57 kg) — this is serious mechanical engineering
- **Teams of 25–150 students** — more of a school organization than a small team
- **Cost:** $10,000–$50,000+ per season depending on team resources
- **Reward:** unmatched real-world engineering experience; many FRC alumni attribute internships and careers directly to FRC

**FRC is right for your student if:**
- They are 14–18, already have some robotics background (1–2 seasons of FTC, FLL, or VEX V5)
- They want the experience of building a competition robot used in an arena alongside 40 other teams
- They can commit to the demanding timeline and team culture
- Their school or local community has an existing team (starting a brand-new FRC team is possible but much harder than joining one)

**FRC may not be the right fit if:**
- Your child is brand-new to robotics and younger — start with FLL or VEX IQ
- Time commitment is a real constraint — FTC or VEX V5 offer competitive robotics at lower weekly time cost
- You want to start small and scale — FTC is widely recommended as the stepping stone before FRC

## Thinking about commitment

Realistic time expectations per week, during the active season:

| Program | Min (recreational) | Typical | Competitive |
|---|---|---|---|
| FLL Discover / Explore | 1 h | 1–2 h | 2 h (not competitive) |
| FLL Challenge | 2 h | 3–5 h | 6–8 h (chasing championship) |
| VEX IQ | 2 h | 3–5 h | 6–10 h (Skills ranking) |
| FTC | 3 h | 5–8 h | 10–15 h |
| VEX V5 / VRC | 3 h | 5–8 h | 10–15 h |
| FRC | — | 10–15 h | 15–20 h (build season) |
| VEX U | — | 10–15 h | 15–25 h |

A first-year team at any level should aim for the middle column. The "competitive" column describes what teams chasing world championship slots often do — it's not required for an enjoyable, educational experience.

## Thinking about goals

Different families and educators come to robotics with different goals. Match your goal to the program:

**"We just want to try robotics — low pressure."**
→ FLL Discover (ages 4–6), FLL Explore (ages 6–10), or VEX IQ (ages 8–14) as a club. All are forgiving of first-year mistakes.

**"We want a well-rounded experience: engineering + teamwork + research."**
→ FLL Challenge. The Innovation Project and Core Values judging components make this a whole-child program, not just a robot-building club.

**"We want the most competitive experience available at this age."**
→ FRC (high school) or VEX V5 (middle/high school). Both have high ceilings and passionate competitive communities.

**"We want to build coding skills specifically."**
→ FTC (Java) or VEX V5 with PROS (C++). Both have strong programming depth. VEX U adds Python and advanced autonomous libraries.

**"We're a classroom teacher integrating robotics into curriculum."**
→ FLL Challenge or VEX IQ for middle school; FTC or VEX V5 for high school. All four have curriculum starter guides in our [For Educators](/for-educators/) section.

**"My student wants to continue into college."**
→ FRC alumni often report the strongest university engineering-career connection. VEX U is the direct college continuation of the VEX path. Both are strong choices for a student planning an engineering degree.

## The two program families at a glance

**FIRST family** (FLL, FTC, FRC) — run by [FIRST](https://www.firstinspires.org/):
- Stronger emphasis on holistic STEM education, community impact, and gracious professionalism
- Judged components (team presentations, outreach, portfolio) matter alongside robot performance
- Very large global community; extensive regional support structure
- FLL → FTC → FRC is a well-documented progression path

**VEX family** (VEX IQ, VEX V5, VEX U) — run by [RECF](https://www.roboticseducation.org/) and [VEX Robotics](https://www.vexrobotics.com/):
- More competition-forward; judging exists but is less central
- Skills Challenge (solo qualifier events) provides extra pathways to advance
- Tight hardware ecosystem: VEX IQ → VEX V5 → VEX U all use VEXcode / PROS progression
- Very active online community (VEX Forum, RobotEvents)

Choosing "FIRST or VEX" is often a matter of what your local school or club already runs. Both are excellent programs. If your area has a strong club in one family, joining that community is usually more valuable than starting from scratch in the other.

## Still unsure?

A few practical suggestions:

1. **Attend a local event as a spectator first.** Watching a live FLL qualifying tournament or VEX V5 competition for 30 minutes will tell you more than any guide can. Check [RobotEvents](https://www.robotevents.com) (VEX) or [FIRST's event finder](https://www.firstinspires.org/find-an-event) (FIRST) for events near you.

2. **Find your local PDO.** Each region has a Partner / PDO (Program Delivery Partner) who can answer questions specific to your area — teams available, events scheduled, mentors who can help. See the [FLL Resource Map](/resources/fll-resource-map/) or [VEX IQ Resource Map](/resources/vex-iq-resource-map/) for your region's contact.

3. **Join an existing team first.** Most teams welcome students as mid-season observers. A season spent watching how an experienced team operates is worth more than a solo first-year struggle.

4. **Read the progression guide.** If your child is young and you're thinking long-term, the [Progression Guide](/getting-started/progression-guide/) explains the typical FLL → FTC → FRC and VEX IQ → VEX V5 → VEX U ladders in detail.
