Talking to a real expert is one of the most memorable parts of FLL — and one of the most valuable. A 20-minute conversation with someone who works in your problem area teaches you things no website can.

But it only works if you prepare. An unprepared interview wastes the expert's time and leaves the team with vague impressions instead of useful knowledge.

This guide walks you through before, during, and after the interview.

---

## Before: prepare like a journalist

### Do your research first

Don't ask the expert to explain things you could have learned from a Wikipedia article. Arrive knowing:
- The basic facts about the problem you're studying
- Key vocabulary in the field (so you can follow their answers)
- What you already think your solution might be

This lets you ask better questions — and it shows the expert you're serious.

### Write 6–10 questions in advance

Not "Tell us about [topic]" — those produce vague answers. Good questions are specific:

**Instead of:** "Can you tell us about water pollution?"
**Ask:** "What's the most common source of water contamination in rivers near agricultural areas?"

**Instead of:** "What do you think of our idea?"
**Ask:** "We're proposing [specific solution]. What's the most significant obstacle to implementing something like this?"

**Structure your questions:**
1. Two or three about the problem (confirm your understanding, learn what you might be missing)
2. Two or three about current solutions (what already exists, what works, what doesn't)
3. Two or three about your proposed solution (is it feasible? What would need to be true?)
4. One "what would you tell a team of kids working on this?" — often produces great quotes for the presentation

### Assign roles

Don't have one kid ask all the questions while the others stare. Assign:
- **Lead questioner:** reads questions and guides the flow
- **Note-taker:** writes key answers and direct quotes (ask the expert if you can take notes — almost everyone says yes)
- **Follow-up asker:** listens for details to dig into ("You said X — can you explain more about Y?")
- **Thank-you closer:** says thank you at the end and confirms any follow-up

If you're doing a video call, one extra person can manage the screen and recording (with permission).

### Send questions in advance (optional but appreciated)

For a 20-minute conversation with a busy professional, emailing your top 4–5 questions a few days before helps them prepare useful answers. It also signals that your team takes this seriously.

## During: listen, follow threads, stay curious

### Start with a brief introduction

"Hi, we're [team name], a FIRST LEGO League team of [ages] from [city]. We're researching [topic] and we're so glad you could talk with us today."

Two sentences. Then ask your first question.

### Follow interesting threads

Your prepared questions are a starting point, not a script. If the expert says something unexpected or surprising, follow it:

- "That's interesting — we hadn't thought about that. Can you explain more?"
- "You mentioned [X] — is that a bigger problem than [Y]?"
- "We assumed [Z] was true. Are we thinking about this wrong?"

The best insights often come from the unexpected tangents.

### Handle "I don't know"

Experts sometimes say they don't know something, or that it's more complicated than your question assumes. This is useful data — write it down. "Our expert said the evidence on this is mixed and there isn't a clear answer yet" is an honest, credible thing to say in your presentation.

### Watch the time

Keep an eye on the clock. Leave time at the end for your "what would you tell a team of kids working on this?" question — it usually lands well and produces quotable answers.

End on time. Say thank you specifically: "Thank you — the part about [X] was really useful, we hadn't thought about that angle."

## After: capture and use what you learned

### Write it up within 24 hours

Memory fades fast. Within a day of the interview, have the team write a summary:
- The expert's name, title, and how you found them
- Date and format (call, in-person, email)
- The 3–5 most important things they said
- Any direct quotes worth using in the presentation
- What you now know that you didn't know before
- How this changes (or confirms) your proposed solution

This becomes your notebook entry for this interaction.

### Let it change your thinking

If the expert identified a flaw in your proposed solution — that's valuable. The team that says "We originally planned X, but our expert Dr. [Name] pointed out Y, so we changed our approach to Z" demonstrates exactly the kind of thinking FLL judges are looking for.

Don't paper over the contradictions. Show the revision.

### Follow up if needed

If the expert said "I'd be happy to look at your final design" or "let me know if you have more questions" — follow up. A brief thank-you email with a link to your final presentation is also a nice touch, and occasionally leads to ongoing connections the team values.

## What judges look for

In the Innovation Project judging session, judges specifically look for:
- **Evidence of real stakeholder contact** — who did you talk to? what did they tell you?
- **Specific information, not generalities** — names, data, specific feedback
- **How the research influenced the solution** — did you learn something that changed your approach?

An interview with one real expert, documented specifically, is worth more than three generic "we read websites" citations.

**What to say in the judging session:**
- "We talked to [Name], a [title] at [organization], who told us that [specific insight]."
- "After talking with our expert, we realized [specific thing] and changed our solution from [X] to [Y]."
- "Our expert said the biggest obstacle to our solution would be [Z], so we looked into [how you addressed it]."

## Finding an expert (if you haven't yet)

See [Finding Mentors and Technical Experts](/guides/finding-mentors/) for a full guide. Quick pointers specific to Innovation Project experts:

- **LinkedIn direct message:** "I'm a coach for a FIRST LEGO League team of 10-year-olds. We're researching [topic]. Could one of your team members spend 20 minutes on a video call answering questions from the kids?" — high response rate.
- **University extension programs:** designed to connect academics with the community
- **Local nonprofits and businesses** in your theme area: they often welcome student outreach
- **One targeted contact beats five cold ones:** do a little research on the person before emailing
